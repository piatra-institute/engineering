# Volume XI: Design

**Working chapter count**: 13
**Working page total**: ~1,080
**Half-life of the contents**: durable for the design-process core; medium-life for digital-twin and modern-prototyping specifics.

## Scope

Design is what engineering is, when seen from the engineer's chair. The previous ten volumes gave the reader the tools, the materials, the mathematics, the failure literacy. This book gives the reader the practice of design itself: how a working engineer takes a need from the world, turns it into a requirement, turns the requirement into a concept, turns the concept into a detailed design, simulates and prototypes it, tests it, reviews it, certifies it, and signs the drawing that says it is done.

Design under risk is the central theme. The reader has finished Volume X knowing how things fail; design is what is left when failure is taken seriously. Ethics under risk (settled question 47) is treated here as a constitutive feature of design, not a postscript.

## Arc within the book

Chapter 1 defines design. Chapter 2 covers requirements and specification. Chapter 3 covers user research and human-centred design, placed before conceptual work because requirements without user research are brittle and the front end of design is the hardest part to teach late. Chapter 4 covers conceptual design. Chapter 5 covers detailed design and analysis. Chapter 6 covers simulation and digital twins. Chapter 7 covers prototyping. Chapter 8 covers testing and validation. Chapter 9 covers reviews, certification, sign-off. Chapter 10 covers manufacturability, maintainability, sustainability, accessibility (the fourth concern is now explicit so accessibility lives as engineering practice rather than an afterthought). Chapter 11 covers ethics under risk. Chapter 12 is the integrated design studio: case studies done at full depth. Chapter 13 is the capstone, where the reader takes the entire work and applies it to one problem.

## Substantial book project

The capstone. The reader produces a complete design pack for an engineering problem of meaningful scale: a small bridge for a community, a community-scale water-purification system, a small-batch manufacturing line, an open-source medical device, a robotic-aid for a disability, a renewable installation, a public-data platform with safety implications. The deliverable is judged against the same rubric a professional design panel would apply: requirements, concept selection, detailed design, analysis, simulation, prototype evidence, test plan, review-board package, manufacturability, ethics statement.

The capstone is the moment the book stops asking the reader to learn and starts asking the reader to act. Volumes I-X taught the reader to think like an engineer; Volume XI asks the reader to design like one.
