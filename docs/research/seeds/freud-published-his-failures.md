# Freud only published his failures

## The claim

Freud's case histories, the canonical Freudian literature that taught the world what psychoanalysis was, are a literature of failures. Dora terminates her analysis abruptly and Freud publishes the fragment as a fragment. The Rat Man's treatment is interrupted by the First World War and the patient dies before resolution. The Wolf Man's analysis is re-opened decades later by another analyst and his later life remains contested. Schreber is written from a memoir of a patient Freud never met. The cases Freud published are the cases that did not close. Todd McGowan's reading is that this is not a record of a career's misfortunes but the structure of the discipline: psychoanalytic knowledge is constituted by the failure case, because the successful case is the case in which the patient stops returning and there is nothing left to write down. The publishable, durable record of psychoanalysis is the symptom that refused to resolve.

## Why it matters here

Engineering shares this structure more than it admits. The bridges that stand, the planes that fly, the codebases that ship without incident, the reactors that run for forty years and are decommissioned on schedule, these vanish into routine. They are not the literature. The literature of engineering, the body of texts that engineers actually read and cite and teach from, is the post-mortem: Tacoma Narrows, the Comet airliner, Challenger, Therac-25, Bhopal, Mars Climate Orbiter, Hyatt Regency, Volkswagen, Boeing MCAS. The named-accident registry that anchors this volume is, in this reading, the project's equivalent of Freud's *Studies on Hysteria* and the five major case histories. It is the place where engineering knowledge becomes transmissible.

The seed authorises Volume X to open with a stronger claim than "failure is also important." The claim is that Volume X is not a *negative* of the rest of the book, not a cautionary appendix to the constructive volumes, but the lane in which the discipline writes down what it has learned. Engineering, like psychoanalysis, publishes its failures because those are the cases where the discipline learns something it can record. The successful case is unpublishable in both fields for the same reason: in the successful case nothing is contested, nothing surprises, nothing forces the engineer or the analyst to revise their model. The failure is where the model meets the world.

The frame should sit in Ch 1 (Discipline of failure), specifically section 1.5 ("Failure as data, not as shame"), and should be picked back up in Ch 16 (Post-mortem as practice), specifically section 16.1 ("The post-mortem as engineering practice"). The frame is also load-bearing for Ch 12 (Forensic engineering) and Ch 13 (Near-misses and weak signals), because both chapters depend on the prior commitment that failure is a *first-class subject of study*, not a sub-discipline of project management. The frame is consistent with the settled positions of Q29 (failure section in every chapter), Q30 (named accidents allowed and encouraged), and Q31 (software failure at the same depth as physical failure).

What the frame disqualifies: any chapter prose that treats Volume X as a list of cautionary tales, that uses the word "lesson" in the trivial sense, or that frames failure as the absence of success. Failure is the presence of a specific kind of evidence, and the volume is the place where that evidence is read carefully.

- Freud, Wolf Man (1918): the published account is followed, decades later, by Ruth Mack Brunswick's continuation analysis (1928) and by the Wolf Man's own late-life memoir (Gardiner, ed., 1971), all of which keep the case in active contestation rather than closing it. The canonical published case is by construction unfinished. Bibkey placeholders: `text:freud-wolfman-1918`, with secondary `text:gardiner-wolfman-1971` if the secondary reading is needed.

- Freud, Schreber (1911): the most extreme case of the pattern. The analyst never meets the patient; the analysis is conducted entirely on the basis of Schreber's *Memoirs of My Nervous Illness* (1903). The case enters the canon as a textual analysis of an unobtainable subject. Bibkey placeholder: `text:freud-schreber-1911`, secondary `text:schreber-memoirs-1903`.
