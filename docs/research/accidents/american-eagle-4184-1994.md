# American Eagle Flight 4184, 1994

## Date(s) and location

American Eagle Flight 4184, an Avions de Transport Régional ATR 72-212 (registration N401AM) operated by Simmons Airlines, crashed at approximately 16:00 local time on 31 October 1994 in a soybean field near Roselawn, Indiana, while holding at 10,000 ft pending sequencing for arrival at Chicago O'Hare International Airport \cite{acc:ntsb-aar96-01}. The accident killed all 64 passengers and 4 crew. The hold had been conducted in freezing precipitation for approximately 30 minutes before the loss of control.

## Technical mechanism

The NTSB investigation identified the proximate mechanism as the formation of a ridge of ice on the upper wing surface aft of the deicing boots, in a location the boots were not designed to protect against, during the hold in freezing-drizzle conditions \cite{acc:ntsb-aar96-01}. The ice ridge altered the wing's local pressure distribution sufficiently to produce aileron hinge-moment reversal at the aircraft's flap configuration and angle of attack during the hold. When the autopilot disconnected and the flying pilot's natural roll input attempted to correct a right roll, the hinge-moment reversal caused the aileron to deflect in the opposite direction from the pilot's input, producing an uncommanded right roll past 90 degrees of bank. The aircraft entered a steep descent from which recovery was not achieved before ground impact.

The ATR 72's design certification had relied on icing-tunnel testing and flight testing that did not include the freezing-drizzle condition (large supercooled droplets in cloud temperatures in the range $-2$ to $-15\,\degC$) that produced the Roselawn ice ridge. The NTSB's investigation identified that the ATR 42 and ATR 72 had been involved in multiple prior in-service icing incidents in similar conditions, with anomalous handling behaviour reported by crews but no flight-control loss leading to accident; these incidents constituted weak signals that the icing certification basis was incomplete.

## Organisational / regulatory mechanism

The NTSB found that the ATR 72 had a history of in-service icing incidents in freezing-drizzle conditions across multiple operators, with reports of anomalous handling that included autopilot disconnects, uncommanded roll inputs, and crew reports of "control snatching" \cite{acc:ntsb-aar96-01}. The reports were known to the manufacturer (Aérospatiale-Alenia, the ATR consortium) and to the certification authorities (DGAC in France; FAA in the US); the institutional response prior to Roselawn had not led to flight-manual restrictions, additional crew procedures, or design changes that would have prevented the accident. The NTSB identified the institutional response to the prior incidents as a contributing cause: the weak signals had been treated as crew-handling issues or as one-off events rather than as indications of a systematic gap in the icing certification basis.

The post-accident response included: an immediate FAA Airworthiness Directive prohibiting ATR 42 and ATR 72 operation in known freezing-drizzle conditions pending modifications; the manufacturer's development of extended deicing boots that covered the upper-wing-surface ice-accretion area; the FAA's revision of icing-certification requirements through the Appendix O process (which added the supercooled-large-droplet envelope to the certification icing-envelope definitions); and the broader regulatory recognition that the original Appendix C icing envelope was incomplete and required supplementation.
