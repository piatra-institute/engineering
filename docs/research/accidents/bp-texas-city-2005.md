# BP Texas City refinery explosion, 2005

## Date(s) and location

On 23 March 2005, at approximately 13:20 local time, an explosion occurred at the BP Products North America Texas City refinery's isomerisation unit during a startup operation. The Texas City refinery, located approximately 60 km southeast of Houston, was at the time the third-largest refinery in the United States by capacity. Fifteen workers were killed; 180 were injured. Most fatalities occurred in temporary trailers that had been located within the unit's blast envelope to support contractor activity. The unit was permanently shut down following the accident; the refinery was sold to Marathon Petroleum in 2013 and rebuilt under new ownership.

## Technical mechanism

The U.S. Chemical Safety and Hazard Investigation Board investigation, published as CSB Report 2005-04-I-TX, identified the immediate sequence of failures \cite{acc:csb-bp-texas-city-2007}. During a planned startup of the isomerisation unit's raffinate splitter tower (a 50-metre distillation column processing light hydrocarbons), operators introduced raffinate to the tower at a rate that exceeded the system's outflow capacity. The tower's liquid-level instrumentation was unreliable and was being monitored only through a single transmitter; the operators were not aware that the actual liquid level in the tower was rising far above the design operating range. The startup procedure called for a withdrawal of liquid at a higher rate than was practiced; the procedure deviation was longstanding and had been normalised in the unit's operating practice.

Eventually, the tower over-pressurised; the safety relief valves opened and discharged liquid hydrocarbon to the unit's blowdown drum. The blowdown drum, of 1950s-era design, was equipped with an open atmospheric stack rather than a flare; the relief discharge exceeded the drum's capacity, and a geyser of flammable hydrocarbon liquid and vapour erupted from the stack to atmosphere. The resulting vapour cloud drifted across the unit and was ignited by an idling truck approximately 30 metres from the stack. The vapour-cloud explosion produced peak overpressures of approximately 35 kPa at the trailers located approximately 35 metres from the centre of the cloud, destroying the trailers and killing their occupants.

## Organisational / regulatory mechanism

The CSB investigation identified the systemic causes as deficiencies in BP's process-safety culture, in the maintenance of the instrumentation and controls that should have detected the developing over-fill, in the budget and staffing levels at the Texas City site, and in the policy that permitted occupied temporary trailers within the unit's blast envelope. The investigation found that the company's safety performance had been measured by personal-injury metrics (lost-time accidents, recordable injuries) rather than by process-safety leading indicators (instrumented system reliability, mechanical integrity inspection backlog, procedure-deviation frequency); the personal-injury metrics had been good while the process-safety performance had been degrading.

The accident triggered the OSHA Petroleum Refinery Process Safety Management National Emphasis Program (2007), the BP-commissioned independent panel review by James A. Baker III (the Baker Panel Report, 2007), and revised industry practice on siting of occupied buildings (API Recommended Practice 752, "Management of Hazards Associated with Location of Process Plant Buildings"). BP paid criminal and civil penalties, restructured its refining safety governance, and ultimately divested the Texas City refinery.
