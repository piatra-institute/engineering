# International prototype of the kilogram mass drift, 1889-2019

## Date(s) and location

The international prototype of the kilogram (IPK), known by its
French designation "le grand K", was a cylinder of platinum-iridium
alloy (90\,\% Pt, 10\,\% Ir, by mass) sanctioned as the world's
primary mass standard by the 1st General Conference on Weights and
Measures (CGPM) in 1889 and held in the BIPM pavilion at S{\`e}vres,
France. The IPK ceased to be the definition of the kilogram on 20 May
2019, when the SI redefinition fixed the numerical value of the
Planck constant and the kilogram became a derived realisation. The
formal verifications of the IPK against its sister copies took place
in 1889 (sanction), 1939--1953 (first periodic verification),
1939--1953 (extension), 1988--1992 (third periodic verification), and
2014 (extraordinary calibration before redefinition).

## Technical mechanism

The IPK and its official copies were the same alloy, manufactured to
the same nominal dimensions and treated with the same cleaning
protocol. By the 1980s, comparisons among the IPK, its six "{\'e}talons
t{\'e}moins" held alongside it at BIPM, and the national prototypes
distributed to member states were showing apparent mass differences
of order tens of micrograms over decades, with most national
prototypes drifting upward relative to the IPK by roughly $50 \,\si
{\micro\gram}$ over a hundred years. The Third Periodic Verification
of 1988--1992 quantified the divergence: among the cohort of
prototypes, the standard deviation of the drifts was several tens of
micrograms, and the IPK could not be claimed to be uniquely stable
within the scatter (\cite{paper:girard-ipk-1994}, sec.~7). The
candidate physical mechanisms include adsorption and desorption of
contaminants on the platinum-iridium surfaces (gain and loss of
several micrograms per cleaning cycle had been demonstrated),
diffusion of hydrogen and other gases into the bulk metal, and
mechanical wear from periodic handling. None of these mechanisms
could be unambiguously ruled in or out as the dominant contributor,
because the IPK was, by definition, exactly one kilogram, leaving no
external reference against which to measure its absolute drift
(\cite{paper:stock-kibble-2018}, sec.~3.2; \cite{web:bipm-kilogram-history}).

This is a length-and-mass measurement case as much as a mass case
alone. The IPK's surface was finished to optical quality so that the
contamination layer, of order nanometres thick, could be removed by
the standardised washing protocol; the dimensional and mass-loss
behaviour during washing was tracked in micrometres and in
micrograms. The artefact's $39 \,\si{\milli\meter}$ diameter and
height were chosen to minimise surface area for a given volume,
because surface adsorption was already recognised in 1889 as a
limiting mechanism.

## Organisational / regulatory mechanism

The IPK governance structure made the artefact's primacy
self-fulfilling. By the 1875 Metre Convention and the 1889 CGPM
sanction, the IPK was, by definition, the unit of mass. Its drift
relative to any other artefact was, in principle, the other
artefact's drift relative to the IPK; the IPK had no external
reference. Periodic verifications were intended to detect divergence
across the cohort and to flag the case in which the IPK was the
outlier, but no procedural mechanism existed to declare a definitional
change in the SI kilogram on the basis of a verification result. The
1948--1979 period of metrological research into electrical analogues
of mass (notably the watt balance / Kibble balance work originating
with Bryan Kibble's NPL proposal in 1975) was the necessary
prerequisite for a definition that did not depend on a single
artefact. The CGPM resolutions of 2011 (resolution 1) and 2018
(resolution 1) approved the SI redefinition once the multiple
independent realisations of the Kibble balance and the X-ray crystal
density (XRCD) method had been brought into agreement at the
$10^{-8}$ relative uncertainty level
(\cite{paper:stock-kibble-2018}, sec.~5;
\cite{web:bipm-mise-en-pratique-kilogram-2019}, sec.~2).

## Lessons by scale

- Volume I, Chapter 2 ("Units and dimensions"): an artefact-based
  definition has no external reference against which to detect its
  own drift; the definition's authority is also the definition's
  blind spot.
- Volume I, Chapter 3 ("Calibration and traceability"): the
  traceability chain from a national prototype to the IPK carries the
  IPK's drift uncertainty into every downstream calibration; a
  fixed-constant definition makes the chain shorter and the
  uncertainty source explicit.
- Volume I, Chapter 7 ("Length, area, volume, mass"): mass
  realisation as a measurement problem; the move from artefact to
  fixed-constant definition as the closing of a wrong-ruler loop at
  the metrology-institute scale.
- Volume X, Chapter 11 ("Forensic engineering and root-cause
  analysis"): the IPK case as a multi-decade scientific investigation
  whose root cause was structural, not technical, and whose
  resolution required a definitional change rather than a fix.

## Short-form summaries

\textbf{One sentence}: From its sanction in 1889 to its retirement in
2019, the international prototype of the kilogram, a
platinum-iridium artefact held at the BIPM at S{\`e}vres, was the
world's mass reference; periodic verifications against its official
copies showed apparent drifts of order tens of micrograms among the

\textbf{Two sentences}: The international prototype of the kilogram
(IPK), a platinum-iridium cylinder sanctioned by the 1st CGPM in
1889, was the world's mass reference for 130 years; periodic
verifications, most carefully in 1988--1992, showed apparent drifts
of order tens of micrograms between the IPK and its sister copies,
with no external standard against which to identify the drifter. The
2019 SI redefinition replaced the artefact with a definition that
fixes the Planck constant, allowing the kilogram to be realised by
any laboratory capable of operating a Kibble balance or an XRCD
silicon-sphere experiment.

\textbf{One paragraph}: The international prototype of the kilogram
(IPK), known as "le grand K", was a cylinder of platinum-iridium
alloy (90\,\% Pt, 10\,\% Ir) sanctioned by the 1st General Conference
on Weights and Measures in 1889 as the world's reference mass. The
IPK and its six BIPM-held witness copies, along with the national
prototypes distributed to member states, were compared periodically;
the third verification of 1988--1992 documented apparent drifts of
order tens of micrograms among the artefacts over the century since
sanction. Because the IPK was, by definition, exactly one kilogram,
no external standard existed against which to identify the drifter.
The ambiguity, combined with three decades of metrological progress
in Kibble-balance and X-ray-crystal-density realisations of the
kilogram via the Planck constant, motivated the 2019 SI
redefinition, which fixed the Planck constant and replaced the
artefact-based definition with a procedural one that any sufficiently
equipped national metrology institute can realise.
