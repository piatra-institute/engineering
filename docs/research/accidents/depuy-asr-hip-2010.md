# DePuy ASR metal-on-metal hip implant, 2010

## Date(s) and location

The DePuy ASR XL Acetabular System (a total hip replacement) and the DePuy ASR Hip Resurfacing System (a resurfacing prosthesis) were manufactured by DePuy Orthopaedics, a Johnson \& Johnson subsidiary based in Warsaw, Indiana, and were sold worldwide between 2003 (resurfacing system, ex-United States) and 2010. The ASR XL was cleared in the United States in 2005 via the FDA 510(k) pathway (predicate: prior metal-on-metal acetabular components), while the ASR resurfacing prosthesis was sold outside the United States only, having never received FDA clearance. DePuy issued a worldwide voluntary recall on 26 August 2010. Approximately 93,000 ASR units had been implanted worldwide by recall.

## Technical mechanism

The ASR was a metal-on-metal (MoM) bearing: both the femoral head and the acetabular cup were made of high-carbon cobalt-chromium-molybdenum alloy (ASTM F1537 or equivalent). The bearing was designed with low diametral clearance (approximately 80 to 150 micrometres) and a low cup-rim coverage angle (approximately 144 degrees), the latter chosen to reduce the cup wall thickness and allow larger femoral heads in patients with smaller anatomy. The design intent was that the small clearance would, in normal gait, support a hydrodynamic lubrication regime with very low wear (\cite{paper:v6c12-langton-asr-2011}).

In service, the lubrication regime depended sensitively on cup inclination angle, anteversion, and bearing geometry. When the cup was implanted at a steeper inclination (above approximately 50 degrees from the horizontal), or when anteversion fell outside a narrow window, the contact patch migrated toward the cup rim. Edge loading then occurred during routine gait, the lubrication film thinned or broke down, and metal-on-metal contact produced cobalt and chromium wear particles in the submicrometre size range. The local tissue response included aseptic lymphocytic vasculitis-associated lesions (ALVAL), pseudotumours (sterile fluid- and tissue-mass lesions), bone resorption around the implant, and elevated whole-blood cobalt and chromium ion concentrations (cobalt frequently above 7 micrograms per litre, with case reports above 1000 micrograms per litre in extreme failure) (\cite{paper:v6c12-cohen-mom-bmj-2012}).

Registry data accumulated faster than DePuy or the regulators recognised the signal. The National Joint Registry of England and Wales (NJR) reported the ASR's five-year cumulative revision rate at approximately 12 to 13 percent in its 2010 report, against an expected rate of one to two percent for a successful total hip replacement at the same horizon. The Australian Orthopaedic Association National Joint Replacement Registry (AOANJRR) had flagged elevated ASR revision rates from its 2007 report onward (\cite{paper:v6c12-smith-asr-revision-2012}). Long-term follow-up (10 to 13 years) has confirmed cumulative revision rates well above contemporary non-MoM alternatives.

## Organisational / regulatory mechanism

The ASR XL was cleared in the United States via the FDA's 510(k) substantial-equivalence pathway. The predicate device chain extended back to earlier metal-on-metal components that had not been subject to a long-term clinical-outcome registry comparison. The 510(k) review did not require new clinical-outcome data, did not require finite-element or hydrodynamic-lubrication analysis of the small-clearance geometry, and did not require a registry-based post-market study. The 510(k) clearance pathway as it existed in 2005 treated the ASR as substantially equivalent to its predicate; the failure mode that emerged in service (edge-loading-driven metal-ion release) was not predicted from the predicate device's record.

Outside the United States, the United Kingdom MHRA issued a medical-device alert in April 2010 recommending annual blood-metal-ion testing for symptomatic patients with MoM implants, followed by a 2012 alert tightening surveillance (\cite{web:v6c12-mhra-mom-2012}). The FDA issued a safety communication on metal-on-metal hip implants in January 2013 (\cite{web:v6c12-fda-mom-orthopaedic-2013}) and required post-market surveillance studies for the broader MoM device class.

The DePuy ASR recall was the largest medical-device recall in orthopaedic history by patient count at the time. Subsequent multi-district litigation in the United States resulted in settlements totalling more than \$2.5 billion (initial 2013 settlement) plus subsequent extensions. The case became one of the canonical demonstrations of the 510(k) pathway's blind spot for design features whose failure mode is not present in the predicate's failure record, and contributed to the FDA's 2011 and 2013 reconsideration of the 510(k) pathway for high-risk Class III devices (the 515(i) reclassification process for MoM hip implants moved them from 510(k) eligibility to mandatory PMA in 2016).

No single official accident-investigation report exists. The closest-equivalent canonical source is the FDA's 2010 ASR recall record, supplemented by the UK MHRA medical-device alerts, the peer-reviewed BMJ review by Cohen (2012), and the joint-registry reports.
