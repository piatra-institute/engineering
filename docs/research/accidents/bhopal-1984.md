# Bhopal disaster, 1984

## Date(s) and location

The release occurred on the night of 2-3 December 1984 at the Union Carbide India Limited (UCIL) pesticide plant in Bhopal, Madhya Pradesh, India. The plant was located adjacent to densely populated residential neighbourhoods. Estimates of the immediate death toll have ranged from a few thousand to more than ten thousand depending on source and time-frame; long-term excess mortality and morbidity have been documented in epidemiological studies for decades since.

## Technical mechanism

Tank 610 of the plant's methyl-isocyanate (MIC) storage system contained approximately 40 tonnes of MIC at the time of the release. Water entered the tank through a route that has remained contested across investigations; the Indian government's reconstruction has been that water entered through plant pipework during line-washing operations, while Union Carbide's reconstruction has been that water was deliberately introduced via direct sabotage. The Indian Council of Medical Research's technical report and subsequent academic literature treat the line-washing route as the consensus mechanism while noting the contested nature of the source \cite{acc:icmr-bhopal-2004}.

The water-MIC reaction is highly exothermic. The reaction in Tank 610 raised the tank's temperature and pressure, vapourising the MIC and forcing release through the tank's relief valve. The release entered the atmosphere over a period of approximately two hours, forming a low-lying gas cloud that drifted over the surrounding city under inversion conditions.

Three engineered safeguards that should have mitigated the release were inoperative or under-capacity at the time:

\begin{itemize}
  \item The vent-gas scrubber, designed to neutralise MIC released from the tank, was on standby (not in active mode) and its caustic-soda capacity had been depleted.
  \item The flare tower, designed to incinerate vented gas, was offline for maintenance.
  \item The refrigeration system, designed to keep the MIC tanks at low temperature to slow reaction kinetics, had been switched off as a cost-reduction measure approximately five months earlier.
\end{itemize}

Each of these defects had been documented in plant records before the release; none had been corrected.

## Organisational / regulatory mechanism

The plant had been operating under cost-reduction pressure for several years before the release, with documented reductions in maintenance, training, staffing, and safety equipment. Plant-level decisions had moved several engineered safeguards to a state where they would not have been effective even at design release rates. Worker-level reports of safety concerns had not been escalated within UCIL's management chain, and the regulatory framework in India at the time did not require external safety auditing of large chemical-processing facilities at the depth that contemporary process-safety regulation does.

The Indian Council of Medical Research's long-term epidemiological studies have documented chronic respiratory, ophthalmic, and reproductive health effects in exposed populations across the four decades since the release \cite{acc:icmr-bhopal-2004}. Civil litigation, criminal proceedings, and corporate-restructuring debates extended for decades. The case has become canonical in process-safety regulation, particularly in the framing of the EPA's risk-management programme rule and the OSHA process-safety-management standard, both of which were passed in part in response to Bhopal.

The provisional designation reflects two facts: the technical mechanism's water-entry route remains contested in primary sources; and a single canonical investigation report comparable to the NTSB / NASA reports for aerospace cases does not exist. The ICMR technical report on long-term health effects is the closest available canonical source on chronic effects; the technical mechanism is canonical in academic process-safety literature but not in a single official report.
