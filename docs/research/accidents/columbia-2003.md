# Space Shuttle Columbia, 2003

## Date(s) and location

Space Shuttle Columbia broke up at approximately 09:00 EST on 1 February 2003 over Texas during entry from the STS-107 mission. The vehicle disintegrated at an altitude of approximately $63 \,\si{\kilo\meter}$ travelling at approximately Mach 18. All seven crew members died.

## Technical mechanism

The Columbia Accident Investigation Board (CAIB) identified the cause as a breach in the orbiter's left wing leading edge produced by a foam strike during ascent \cite{acc:caib-columbia}.

At $T + 81.7 \,\si{\second}$ during ascent, a piece of insulating foam approximately $50 \,\si{\centi\meter} \times 30 \,\si{\centi\meter}$ separated from the external tank's left bipod ramp and struck the orbiter's left wing in the vicinity of reinforced carbon-carbon (RCC) panel 8. The piece's mass was approximately $0.76 \,\si{\kilogram}$ and its relative velocity at impact was approximately $250 \,\si{\meter\per\second}$. The strike's kinetic energy was sufficient to breach the RCC panel.

During entry on 1 February 2003, hot plasma at temperatures approaching $1{,}650 \,\si{\degreeCelsius}$ entered the wing through the breach. The hot-gas ingress progressively destroyed the wing's internal structure (spars, ribs, wiring, and hydraulic systems). Sensor data from the vehicle's modular auxiliary data system, recovered from the debris field, recorded a sequence of off-nominal temperatures, hydraulic-system anomalies, and wheel-well sensor failures consistent with progressive internal damage to the left wing. At approximately $T + 13 \,\si{\minute}$ into entry, the wing failed structurally; the orbiter's aerodynamic stability was lost; the vehicle broke up.

## Organisational / regulatory mechanism

The CAIB report's organisational findings were extensive and parallel in many ways to the Rogers Commission's findings on Challenger seventeen years earlier \cite{acc:caib-columbia}. The CAIB identified two primary organisational mechanisms.

The first was the progressive reclassification of foam shedding. Foam separation from the external tank had been observed on many prior flights, including foam strikes on the orbiter. The hazard had originally been classified as a flight-safety issue requiring resolution before flight; over time, repeated occurrences without catastrophic consequences led to a progressive reclassification as "in-family" (within the experienced range of behaviour) rather than as evidence that the design had not met its specification. The CAIB used the term "normalization of deviance" (drawing on Vaughan's analysis of Challenger) to describe this pattern.

The second was the on-orbit imaging request. After the foam strike was identified in launch imagery review, several NASA engineers expressed concern about possible wing damage and recommended that the Department of Defense or other on-orbit imaging assets be requested to characterise the damage. The request chain stalled at multiple levels within NASA management; the request was not made; the crew was told that the foam strike had been analysed and posed no flight-safety concern. The CAIB found that imaging would have been technically feasible and would have characterised the damage in time for either an emergency rescue mission or an attempt at on-orbit repair (both options were assessed in the CAIB's annexes as having been within marginal feasibility had they been initiated immediately after launch).

The CAIB's recommendations included foam-shedding-prevention design changes, on-orbit thermal-protection-system inspection and repair capability, restructured flight-safety review processes, and a renewed independent safety, reliability, and quality assurance organisation. Most were implemented during the Return-to-Flight programme that culminated in the STS-114 launch in July 2005.

- Volume V (materials chapters): RCC failure under thermal and impact loading; insulating foam degradation and shedding mechanics.
- Volume IX (control and reliability chapters): the CAIB's framing of organisational risk as a system-level failure mode.
- Volume X, Chapter 11 ("Forensic engineering and root-cause analysis"): the CAIB's reconstruction from sensor data, debris analysis, and impact testing as a model of contemporary aerospace forensic engineering.
- Volume X, Chapter 12 ("Regulation, certification" or similar): the parallel between the Rogers Commission and CAIB findings, and the question of why post-Challenger reforms had not prevented Columbia.
- Volume XI, Chapter 11 ("Ethics under risk and uncertainty"): the on-orbit imaging request and the engineering escalation chain.
