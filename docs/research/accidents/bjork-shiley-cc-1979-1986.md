# Bjork-Shiley convexo-concave heart valve, 1979-1986

## Date(s) and location

The Bjork-Shiley convexo-concave (BSCC) heart valve was a tilting-disc prosthetic mechanical valve manufactured by Shiley Inc. (a Pfizer subsidiary, Irvine, California) between 1979 and the company's voluntary withdrawal of the device from the United States market in November 1986. Approximately 86,000 valves were implanted worldwide during that window, principally in the aortic and mitral positions. The first reported outlet-strut fracture occurred during pre-market clinical use of the redesigned convexo-concave geometry; the cumulative count of confirmed strut fractures reached 619 in published epidemiology by 2003 and continues to grow at a low rate as the implanted cohort ages.

## Technical mechanism

The BSCC valve consists of a pyrolytic-carbon occluder disc, a Haynes-25 (cobalt-chromium-tungsten) metal ring, and two welded struts that retain the disc within the ring. The convexo-concave design (introduced in 1979) opened the disc to a larger angle (60 to 70 degrees, versus 60 degrees for the earlier flat-disc model) to reduce regurgitation and improve hemodynamic performance. The outlet strut, the smaller of the two retention legs, was welded to the ring at two locations. Under the cyclic bending load applied by the disc on every cardiac cycle (approximately 35 to 40 million cycles per year), fatigue cracks initiated at the weld toes and propagated until the strut fractured in a single ligament. Once the second leg failed, the disc embolised, the patient suffered acute massive regurgitation, and mortality from a single-leg-then-strut-fracture (SLF) event was approximately 70 percent without immediate emergency valve replacement (\cite{paper:v6c12-blot-bjork-shiley-2005}, Table 2).

The fracture risk was strongly size-dependent: the 29 mm and larger mitral valves with 70-degree opening angle showed annual strut-fracture incidence of 0.20 to 0.50 percent per patient-year, while smaller aortic valves with 60-degree opening showed rates an order of magnitude lower (\cite{paper:v6c12-blot-bjork-shiley-2005}, Section 3). The mechanism was confirmed by metallurgical examination of explanted valves and by in vitro pulse-duplicator testing, both of which identified the weld toe as the consistent fatigue-crack initiation site (\cite{paper:v6c12-vanderlugt-strut-fracture-1993}).

A subset of valves carried documented weld defects. Internal Shiley records, later released in litigation discovery, showed that quality-control inspections identified out-of-specification welds and that some valves with documented rework or weld anomalies were not segregated from the production stream. The defect rate inferred from these records was not uniform across production lots, and certain manufacturing-date cohorts showed elevated fracture rates consistent with concentrated defects (\cite{web:v6c12-doj-shiley-1994-settlement}).

## Organisational / regulatory mechanism

Shiley introduced the convexo-concave variant in 1979 under the FDA's then-current Investigational Device Exemption (IDE) and Pre-Market Approval (PMA) regime; the BSCC was approved as a supplement to the existing Bjork-Shiley PMA rather than as a new device requiring fresh trials. The first strut fractures were reported to FDA in 1980. Shiley withdrew the larger 70-degree valves in 1983 and the smaller 60-degree valves in 1986, but never formally recalled the implanted cohort: the recommendation for implanted patients remained surveillance rather than prophylactic explantation, because the per-event mortality of elective re-operation in older patients with comorbidities was, for many cohorts, comparable to or higher than the per-year fracture risk.

In 1992 the FDA accepted a consent agreement under which Pfizer/Shiley funded the Supplemental Surgical Mortality Risk (SSMR) calculator and a patient-notification programme. In 1994 the United States Department of Justice settled a civil case for \$10.75 million arising from allegations that Shiley had not disclosed weld-defect records during the PMA process (\cite{web:v6c12-doj-shiley-1994-settlement}). A separate \$165 million class settlement (1992) and roughly \$215 million in subsequent settlements compensated patients and surviving families. The Bjork-Shiley case became one of the founding episodes in modern medical-device post-market surveillance and contributed to the 1990 Safe Medical Devices Act, which strengthened FDA authority to require post-market studies and to mandate adverse-event reporting from hospitals and nursing homes.

No single official accident-investigation report exists. The closest-equivalent canonical source is the FDA's 1992 recall and consent-agreement documentation, supplemented by the peer-reviewed epidemiology of Blot, Ibrahim, and Inglesby (2005), the metallurgical reconstruction of van der Lugt and others (1993), and the U.S. Department of Justice 1994 settlement record. The closest-equivalent designation rests on the absence of an NTSB-style independent investigator for medical devices in this era and on the fragmentary, litigation-driven public record.
