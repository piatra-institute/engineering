# Macchiarini synthetic-trachea transplants, 2008-2014

## Date(s) and location

The first event of the case sequence was the 2008 implantation of a decellularised cadaveric tracheal scaffold seeded with the recipient's own bone-marrow mesenchymal cells into a thirty-year-old woman with end-stage tuberculosis-related airway disease, performed in Barcelona at Hospital Cl\'inic and reported in The Lancet that November as a successful tissue-engineering proof of principle \cite{paper:v6c05-macchiarini-2008-trachea}. The case sequence that became the scandal began on 9 June 2011 at Karolinska University Hospital (Stockholm), where Macchiarini implanted the first of three synthetic-polymer scaffolds in a thirty-six-year-old man with recurrent tracheal cancer; two further patients received synthetic scaffolds at Karolinska in 2011 and 2012. Additional synthetic-scaffold implantations followed at Krasnodar Regional Hospital (Russia) between 2012 and 2014 and at the University of Illinois (Peoria) in 2013. Of the eight patients implanted with synthetic scaffolds across these sites, seven died within months to four years; the surviving recipient required scaffold explantation and reconstruction with native tissue.

## Technical mechanism

The 2011-2014 implants used a polyethylene-terephthalate-and-polyurethane scaffold electrospun on a computed-tomography-derived mould of each patient's trachea, then seeded in a rotating bioreactor for forty-eight hours with bone-marrow mononuclear cells from the recipient before implantation. The design hypothesised that the seeded cells would attract host vascular endothelium, regenerate a respiratory epithelium on the luminal surface, and remodel the synthetic polymer into a viable airway segment. The hypothesis had no preclinical foundation: the bioreactor seeding protocol had not been validated in any animal model approaching the size or geometry of a human trachea, the synthetic polymer had no track record of integrating with airway tissue, and the proposed in-situ vascularisation mechanism had not been demonstrated for any synthetic scaffold of comparable thickness ($\sim 2\,\text{mm}$ wall, $\sim 12\,\text{cm}$ length) (\cite{paper:v6c05-karolinska-heckscher-2018}, sections 4 and 7).

The post-implant course of every Karolinska patient was consistent with the predicted failure modes of a thick, avascular synthetic construct. The scaffolds remained acellular through their bulk on post-mortem histology; the luminal surface developed granulation tissue and fistulas to the surrounding mediastinum rather than respiratory epithelium; the scaffold-host interface separated as the recipient's connective tissue failed to bond to the polymer; mediastinitis, mediastinal abscesses, and vascular erosion into the great vessels accumulated as complications over the first six to twelve post-operative months \cite{paper:v6c05-delaere-2014-thorax, paper:v6c05-vogel-2017-science-investigation}. The peer-reviewed reports that appeared in The Lancet in 2011 and Lancet Respiratory Medicine in 2014 described post-operative recoveries that subsequent investigations found to have been materially inconsistent with the contemporaneous clinical records, including misrepresented endoscopy findings, omitted complications, and altered timelines (\cite{paper:v6c05-karolinska-heckscher-2018}, section 5; \cite{paper:v6c05-jungebluth-2011-lancet-retraction}).

## Organisational / regulatory mechanism

No single official accident-investigation report exists; the closest-equivalent canonical source is the 2018 final external review commissioned by the Karolinska Institute board and delivered by the former Swedish Supreme Court justice Sten Heckscher and colleagues \cite{paper:v6c05-karolinska-heckscher-2018}. The Heckscher review identifies a chain of organisational failures: the Karolinska University Hospital approved the 2011 implant as compassionate-use surgery on a single-patient basis rather than as a clinical trial, bypassing the institutional review board and the Swedish Medical Products Agency; subsequent implants were approved on the same single-patient basis without an aggregated safety review across cases; the surgeon was simultaneously a Karolinska Institute professor and a Karolinska University Hospital surgeon, with no clear line of accountability between the research and clinical roles; senior colleagues' written objections to the second and third implants were not escalated; whistle-blower reports from four clinical colleagues (Matthias Corbascio, Karl-Henrik Grinnemo, Oscar Simonson, Thomas Fux) submitted to the hospital and the institute in 2014 were initially dismissed; an external misconduct review by Bengt Gerdin in 2015 found scientific misconduct in six papers; Karolinska Institute initially rejected the Gerdin findings, then reversed in 2016 after the Swedish television documentary \emph{Experimenten} aired and the scandal became public. The institute's vice-chancellor, the dean, the hospital director, and the board chair resigned in 2016; the Heckscher review was commissioned to reconstruct the decisions and was delivered in September 2018 \cite{paper:v6c05-karolinska-heckscher-2018, paper:v6c05-vogel-2017-science-investigation}.

The regulatory mechanism includes: the compassionate-use exemption that permitted single-patient experimental implantation without trial-stage safety review; the absence of an institutional framework for aggregating single-patient experimental cases into a recognisable trial that would have triggered formal regulatory oversight; the inadequacy of the journal peer-review process for catching misrepresented patient outcomes when the underlying clinical records were not made available to reviewers; and the slow response of the institutional misconduct review process to whistle-blower reports. The Lancet papers were retracted in 2018 (the 2011 paper) and subsequent years; six Macchiarini papers had been retracted by 2020.

## Short-form summaries

\textbf{One sentence}: Between 2011 and 2014, eight patients at Karolinska University Hospital and partner institutions received synthetic-polymer tracheal scaffolds seeded with autologous bone-marrow cells; seven died from scaffold-related complications because the avascular synthetic constructs failed to vascularise, did not regenerate respiratory epithelium, and eroded into mediastinal structures, and the underlying publications were subsequently found to have misrepresented the preclinical evidence and the patient course.

\textbf{Two sentences}: The Macchiarini synthetic-trachea programme of 2011-2014 implanted polyethylene-terephthalate-and-polyurethane scaffolds, electrospun on patient-specific moulds and bioreactor-seeded with bone-marrow cells for forty-eight hours, into eight patients without preclinical animal-model validation of either the seeding protocol or the synthetic-polymer choice. The scaffolds remained acellular through their bulk, did not vascularise, did not regenerate mucosa, eroded into the great vessels, and led to seven patient deaths; the supporting publications were retracted after whistle-blower reports were validated by the 2018 Heckscher external review.

\textbf{One paragraph}: Between June 2011 and 2014, the thoracic surgeon Paolo Macchiarini implanted synthetic-polymer tracheal scaffolds, seeded for forty-eight hours in a bioreactor with autologous bone-marrow mononuclear cells, into eight patients at Karolinska University Hospital (three patients) and at partner institutions in Krasnodar and Peoria. The clinical programme rested on the hypothesis that the seeded cells would recruit host vasculature, regenerate respiratory epithelium on the luminal surface, and remodel the polymer into a viable airway. The hypothesis had no preclinical foundation. On post-mortem histology, the scaffolds remained acellular through their bulk; the luminal surface developed granulation tissue and fistulas rather than mucosa; the scaffold-host interface separated; mediastinitis, abscesses, and vascular erosion accumulated. Seven of the eight patients died within months to four years. The supporting publications, including the 2011 Lancet report, were subsequently found to have misrepresented the preclinical evidence, the post-operative course, and the consent process; the papers were retracted; the Karolinska vice-chancellor, dean, hospital director, and board chair resigned in 2016; the 2018 Heckscher external review reconstructed the chain of organisational failures (compassionate-use exemption used to bypass trial-stage safety review, insufficient escalation of whistle-blower reports, inadequate aggregation of single-patient cases). The case is the canonical failure of synthetic-scaffold whole-organ engineering at clinical scale and is the working example of vascularisation failure and host-integration failure under skipped preclinical validation.
