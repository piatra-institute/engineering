# Genzyme Cerezyme Vesivirus 2117 contamination, 2009

## Date(s) and location

On 16 June 2009 Genzyme Corporation announced the detection of Vesivirus 2117 in a 2000 L Chinese hamster ovary (CHO) cell-culture bioreactor at its Allston Landing manufacturing facility (Allston, Massachusetts, U.S.A.). The facility manufactured two enzyme-replacement therapies for lysosomal storage diseases: imiglucerase (Cerezyme), for Gaucher disease type 1, and agalsidase beta (Fabrazyme), for Fabry disease. Genzyme suspended the bioreactor train, drained and sanitised the affected vessels and adjacent equipment, and restarted production on a phased schedule through late 2009 and into 2010. A second, unrelated regulatory action followed in 2010 when the U.S. Food and Drug Administration issued a Warning Letter citing additional good-manufacturing-practice (cGMP) deficiencies observed during a follow-up inspection at the Allston facility.

## Technical mechanism

Vesivirus 2117 is a small (\textasciitilde 35 nm) non-enveloped single-stranded RNA virus of the family Caliciviridae, related to vesiviruses associated with disease in pinnipeds and feline calicivirus; the 2117 strain was first reported by Victoria and coworkers in 2006 as a calf-serum-borne contaminant of CHO cell cultures, producing cytopathic effect and reduced productivity (\cite{paper:v6c10-vesivirus-2117-victoria-2006}). The virus does not infect humans and is not known to cause human disease, so the regulatory issue at Allston was process integrity, not patient infection from contaminated product. The mechanism of contamination is generally attributed to virus introduced through animal-derived raw materials, most plausibly bovine-origin medium components (fetal bovine serum, bovine peptones, or other serum-derived supplements), entering the upstream cell-culture train and replicating in the CHO host. Once replicating, the virus interferes with CHO growth and recombinant-protein expression and contaminates the bioreactor environment and downstream surfaces.

The Allston facility's remediation involved draining, decontaminating, and revalidating the affected bioreactor train; switching to virus-free chemically defined media where possible; tightening raw-material qualification (animal-component-free supplements, gamma-irradiated or high-temperature short-time-treated serum where used, supplier audits); and adding nucleic-acid-based screening assays for Vesivirus and related caliciviruses to the in-process control plan. The downstream purification trains for Cerezyme and Fabrazyme include nanofiltration and chromatographic clearance steps that are validated to clear small non-enveloped viruses by orders of magnitude, so the patient-safety risk from any product released before the contamination was identified was limited; nonetheless, FDA and the European Medicines Agency required additional virus-clearance data before lots manufactured during the relevant window could be released (\cite{web:v6c10-fda-warning-letter-genzyme-2010}).

The aftermath unfolded over more than two years. Genzyme's 2009 SEC filings disclosed roughly US\$300 million in lost revenue from the suspended production and the consequent patient-supply rationing; the 2010 FDA Warning Letter and a subsequent Consent Decree (May 2010) required Genzyme to transfer fill-finish operations off the Allston site and to remediate the facility under third-party oversight. The shortage led to documented disease progression in some Gaucher and Fabry patients on reduced dosing schedules during 2009-2010 (\cite{paper:v6c10-genzyme-shortage-2010}). Sanofi acquired Genzyme in 2011 for US\$20.1 billion, with the Allston remediation programme inherited as a continuing obligation.

## Organisational / regulatory mechanism

Genzyme operated Allston as a single-site, single-train manufacturing source for two products with no equivalent alternative supply: at the time of the contamination, Cerezyme had no biosimilar approved, and Fabrazyme had only one approved competitor (agalsidase alfa, Replagal, Shire) which itself faced supply constraints. The decision to concentrate two life-saving enzyme-replacement therapies in a single facility, with a single bioreactor train and shared media-preparation infrastructure, created a supply-chain failure mode that the contamination then exercised. The 2010 FDA Warning Letter and Consent Decree explicitly cited the absence of redundant manufacturing capacity, the inadequacy of viral-clearance demonstration for the specific contamination event, and the broader cGMP deficiencies (cleaning validation, change control, environmental monitoring) observed at the site. The Consent Decree required US\$175 million in disgorgement plus 18.5 percent of future Allston-produced Cerezyme and Fabrazyme revenue until specified remediation milestones were met. Sanofi's 2011 acquisition was partly motivated by Genzyme's depressed valuation following the Allston episode and the associated investor concern about Genzyme's manufacturing risk.

## Short-form summaries

\textbf{One sentence}: In June 2009 Genzyme detected Vesivirus 2117 in a CHO bioreactor at its Allston, Massachusetts facility, suspending production of Cerezyme and Fabrazyme and incurring more than US\$300 million in lost revenue plus a 2010 FDA Consent Decree.

\textbf{Two sentences}: In June 2009 Genzyme Corporation detected Vesivirus 2117, a calicivirus most plausibly introduced through bovine-origin media components, in a 2000 L Chinese hamster ovary bioreactor at its Allston Landing, Massachusetts facility producing the enzyme-replacement therapies Cerezyme (imiglucerase) and Fabrazyme (agalsidase beta). The contamination forced a multi-month production suspension and multi-year supply shortage, cost roughly US\$300 million in lost revenue, drove a 2010 FDA Warning Letter and Consent Decree requiring US\$175 million in disgorgement, and contributed to the depressed valuation that supported Sanofi's US\$20.1 billion 2011 acquisition of Genzyme.

\textbf{One paragraph}: On 16 June 2009 Genzyme Corporation announced that Vesivirus 2117 had been detected in a 2000 L CHO bioreactor at its Allston Landing, Massachusetts manufacturing site, which served as the sole production facility for two enzyme-replacement therapies for lysosomal storage disease, Cerezyme (imiglucerase, for Gaucher disease type 1) and Fabrazyme (agalsidase beta, for Fabry disease). Vesivirus 2117 is a non-enveloped single-stranded RNA virus of the family Caliciviridae, characterised in 2006 as a calf-serum-borne contaminant of CHO cultures; it does not infect humans, so the regulatory concern at Allston was process integrity rather than direct patient infection. Genzyme suspended the affected bioreactor train, drained and sanitised the facility, and restarted production on a phased schedule. The episode cost roughly US\$300 million in lost 2009 revenue, triggered patient-supply rationing for Gaucher and Fabry patients across multiple countries, drew a 2010 FDA Warning Letter, and culminated in a Consent Decree requiring US\$175 million in disgorgement plus 18.5 percent of future Allston Cerezyme and Fabrazyme revenue until remediation milestones were met. Sanofi acquired Genzyme in 2011 for US\$20.1 billion against the backdrop of the Allston remediation programme. The technical lesson is that bovine-origin raw materials are part of the sterile boundary; the operational lesson is that single-site, single-train manufacture of a sole-source biologic is a supply-chain failure mode that contamination can exercise. No single official investigation report exists; the canonical record is built from Genzyme's SEC 8-K filings, FDA Form 483 inspectional observations and Warning Letters, the Consent Decree, and peer-reviewed analyses of the patient-supply consequences.
