# Tacoma Narrows Bridge, 1940

## Date(s) and location

The original Tacoma Narrows Bridge, a suspension bridge spanning the Tacoma Narrows strait of Puget Sound in Washington State, opened on 1 July 1940 and collapsed on 7 November 1940. The collapse occurred under a steady wind of approximately $19 \,\si{\meter\per\second}$ ($42 \,\text{mph}$). The bridge's main span was $853 \,\si{\meter}$.

## Technical mechanism

The Federal Works Agency report, prepared by O.\ H.\ Ammann, Theodore von K\'arm\'an, and Glenn B.\ Woodruff and dated 28 March 1941, identified the cause as aeroelastic instability of the bridge deck under steady wind \cite{acc:tacoma-narrows-1941}. The deck had a shallow plate-girder cross-section approximately $2.4 \,\si{\meter}$ deep, contributing a girder-to-span depth ratio of about $1{:}350$, considerably more slender than contemporary suspension-bridge practice. Under wind, the deck cross-section behaved as a bluff body with a strongly pitching-moment-dependent flow separation pattern.

At low wind speeds the deck oscillated in a vertical heaving mode driven by vortex shedding. At the critical wind speed of about $19 \,\si{\meter\per\second}$, a torsional self-excited oscillation developed, in which the angular displacement of the deck modified the aerodynamic moment in a manner that fed energy back into the deck's torsional motion. The amplitude of the torsional oscillation grew until structural members failed; the centre span tore from its suspension cables and dropped into the strait. The mode of instability is now classified as torsional flutter, the steady-wind dual of classical bending-torsion flutter familiar from aircraft wing analysis.

The bridge had been designed for static wind loads using practice consistent with the period: a horizontal pressure of about $2.4 \,\si{\kilo\pascal}$ ($50 \,\text{psf}$) applied to the deck's projected vertical area. No analysis had been done of the deck cross-section as a body in steady flow, of its critical flutter speed, or of its aerodynamic stiffness and damping derivatives.

## Organisational / regulatory mechanism

The Federal Works Agency report identified the design culture of the period as the underlying mechanism. Suspension-bridge practice in the 1930s had been moving toward shallower and more slender deck profiles for aesthetic and economic reasons. The aerodynamic consequences of those choices had not been brought into routine design analysis; the engineering profession's bridge-design canon treated wind as a static distributed load. The report did not assign blame to the bridge's designer (Leon Moisseiff, also a designer of the George Washington and Golden Gate bridges) but observed that the design had followed the period's accepted practice and that the practice itself was inadequate for slender decks. The recommendations called for incorporating aerodynamic analysis into suspension-bridge design and for wind-tunnel testing of deck sections \cite{acc:tacoma-narrows-1941}.

The Tacoma Narrows replacement bridge, opened in 1950, used a deeper truss-stiffened deck and explicit aerodynamic analysis. Subsequent suspension-bridge design has carried the lesson forward; deck-section wind-tunnel testing is now standard practice.
