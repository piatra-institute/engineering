# Texas February 2021 Grid Failure

## Date(s) and location

The event spanned 10-19 February 2021 across Texas and parts of the South Central United States. The most severe impacts were within the ERCOT (Electric Reliability Council of Texas) interconnection, which serves most of Texas and is largely isolated from the Eastern and Western US interconnections.

## Technical mechanism

A multi-day cold weather event produced temperatures across Texas substantially below historical winter design conditions for the regional energy infrastructure. Natural gas wellhead freeze-offs, frozen instrumentation at power plants, frozen wind-turbine blades, and reduced grid imports combined to produce a generation shortfall against load. ERCOT executed rolling outages from 15 February to manage the imbalance; the outages persisted for days in many areas as equipment damage and the continuing cold delayed restoration. Approximately $4.5$ million customers lost power for periods up to several days; the loss of water-pressure (from pump-station outages) compounded the disruption.

## Organisational / regulatory mechanism

The cold-weather preparedness of the ERCOT generation fleet and the natural-gas supply chain was substantially below the level required for the event's severity. Prior cold-weather events (notably 2011) had produced similar but smaller-scale failures; recommendations from FERC/NERC after the 2011 event had been only partially implemented. The ERCOT interconnection's relative isolation from neighbouring grids limited the import option during the shortfall. The post-event FERC/NERC inquiry documented the technical and institutional contributing factors and made recommendations for cold-weather preparedness.

## Short-form summaries

A multi-day winter storm in February 2021 produced widespread generation outages on the ERCOT grid in Texas; over $4$ million customers lost power for periods up to several days, and $\sim 200$ deaths were attributed to the outages and related conditions.

The February 2021 cold-weather event in Texas exceeded the cold-weather design preparedness of the regional generation fleet and natural-gas supply chain; the resulting generation shortfall produced rolling outages lasting up to several days, with cascading effects on water supply and other infrastructure dependent on electric power.
