# Therac-25, 1985-1987

## Date(s) and location

Therac-25 was a radiation-therapy machine manufactured by Atomic Energy of Canada Limited (AECL) and sold to clinical-radiotherapy facilities in Canada and the United States starting in 1982. The known overdose accidents occurred between June 1985 and January 1987 at six sites: Kennestone Regional Oncology Center (Marietta, Georgia), Ontario Cancer Foundation (Hamilton, Ontario), Yakima Valley Memorial Hospital (Yakima, Washington), East Texas Cancer Center (Tyler, Texas; two events), and again at Yakima. Three of the six events were fatal. The machine was withdrawn after the second Tyler event in January 1987.

## Technical mechanism

Therac-25 supported two beam modes: a low-current electron mode for surface treatment, and a high-current X-ray mode in which a metal scattering target and tungsten flattener intervened between a high-energy electron beam and the patient. Switching between modes required mechanical movement of the target into or out of the beam path. The control software's race conditions allowed the operator console to indicate the X-ray mode while the mechanical target had not yet moved into position, or to allow the high-energy electron beam to fire when the operator believed the lower-energy electron mode was selected. In those conditions, the patient received a high-energy electron beam without the target's scattering, a factor of approximately $10^{2}$ higher than the prescribed dose at the entry surface \cite{paper:leveson-turner1993}.

The race condition was not detected by the machine's safety interlocks. The interlocks had been migrated from earlier Therac models (Therac-6, Therac-20) which had implemented hardware interlocks for the same hazard. Therac-25 replaced the hardware interlocks with software, on the assumption that the underlying software had been validated by the prior models' service history. That assumption was incorrect: prior models had hardware backups for the same software, masking software errors that became unmasked when Therac-25 removed the hardware.

Operator displays showed messages such as "MALFUNCTION 54" without a safety interpretation. After several failed treatment attempts, operators in some sites used a "P" command to proceed past the malfunction, on the basis that prior Therac machines' malfunction codes had typically indicated a recoverable beam-positioning error. In the Therac-25 cases, "Malfunction 54" indicated a dose anomaly, and proceeding through the malfunction allowed the beam to fire again with the same misconfiguration. The Tyler dose display, in at least one case, showed an underdose indication while the patient had received a massive overdose; the operator restarted the treatment without realising the patient had already been irradiated \cite{paper:leveson-turner1993}.

## Organisational / regulatory mechanism

No single official accident-investigation report exists for Therac-25. The Food and Drug Administration (FDA) issued correction notices and required design changes following each event, but FDA's role was as a regulator monitoring AECL's response, not as an independent investigator. The Canadian Health Protection Branch acted similarly. The closest-equivalent canonical source is Leveson and Turner's 1993 IEEE Computer paper, "An Investigation of the Therac-25 Accidents," which reconstructs the events from FDA records, depositions in subsequent litigation, AECL technical correspondence, the user manuals, and direct interviews with hospital staff. Leveson's 2011 book \emph{Engineering a Safer World} extends the analysis with additional documents and a system-theoretic safety framing \cite{paper:leveson-turner1993, text:leveson2011}.

The closest-equivalent designation rests on the absence of a single official investigation: Therac-25 belongs to a class of medical-device failures where regulatory action is per-event and where civil litigation produces fragmentary public records. Leveson and Turner are treated as canonical here because their reconstruction is peer-reviewed, draws on FDA records and primary correspondence, and has been the consensus reference in the safety-critical software community for three decades.

The organisational mechanisms identified include: AECL's slow recognition of a recurring software fault despite multiple incident reports; the absence of a hazard analysis that recognised the removal of hardware interlocks as a safety-critical change; the absence of independent software review for safety-critical paths; the inadequacy of operator-display design for distinguishing routine malfunctions from dose anomalies; and the regulatory framework's reliance on manufacturer self-reporting for software-induced events.
