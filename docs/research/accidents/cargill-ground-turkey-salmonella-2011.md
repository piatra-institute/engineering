# Cargill ground turkey Salmonella Heidelberg recall, 2011

## Date(s) and location

The first recall, USDA FSIS Recall 090-2011, was announced on 3 August 2011 and covered approximately $35{,}900{,}000$ pounds ($16{,}300{,}000$ kg) of fresh and frozen ground turkey products produced at the Cargill Meat Solutions plant in Springdale, Arkansas, between 20 February and 2 August 2011. The second recall, FSIS Recall 094-2011, was announced on 11 September 2011 and covered an additional $185{,}000$ pounds ($83{,}900$ kg) of ground turkey produced at the same plant on 23, 24, 30, and 31 August 2011 after production resumed. Both recalls were classified Class I (reasonable probability that the use of the product will cause serious adverse health consequences or death). The CDC's confirmed-case count at the close of the outbreak investigation was 136 illnesses across 34 states with at least 37 hospitalisations and one death \cite{acc:ch01t3b-cdc-mmwr-salmonella-heidelberg-2013}.

## Technical mechanism

The outbreak strain was \emph{Salmonella enterica} serotype Heidelberg, characterised by a pulsed-field gel electrophoresis (PFGE) pattern designated JF6X01.0223 in the CDC PulseNet database \cite[p.~46]{acc:ch01t3b-cdc-mmwr-salmonella-heidelberg-2013}. The strain showed resistance to ampicillin, streptomycin, tetracycline, and gentamicin, and reduced susceptibility to ceftriaxone, complicating clinical management of severe cases. CDC and state public-health laboratories linked the strain to ground turkey purchased by case-patients through PulseNet pattern matching; FSIS traceback investigations linked four positive retail-sample isolates to the Springdale plant, three of which carried the outbreak PFGE pattern \cite[p.~3]{acc:ch01t3b-usda-fsis-recall-090-2011}.

The measurement-protocol failure that delayed detection was structural. FSIS's pre-2011 ground-turkey sampling under the Pathogen Reduction and Hazard Analysis and Critical Control Points (PR/HACCP) framework collected composite samples at the establishment level on a frequency tied to the plant's production volume and inspector schedule, with each sample treated as representative of the entire production day at the plant rather than of any individual consumer-package lot \cite[pp.~12--18]{acc:ch01t3b-gao-fsis-sampling-2012}. A plant of Springdale's output rate (approximately $400{,}000$ pounds per day) produced thousands of consumer-package lots per production day; a single composite sample drawn from one production-day stream could clear the day's output regardless of contamination confined to a particular line, shift, or vendor of incoming birds. The outbreak strain had been isolated from Cargill ground turkey samples collected by USDA-FSIS, the National Antimicrobial Resistance Monitoring System (NARMS), and retail testing at multiple points during 2011 before the recall, but no single sample triggered a recall because each was treated as a stand-alone establishment-level finding rather than as one entry in a longitudinal record tied to lot-level traceback \cite[p.~22]{acc:ch01t3b-gao-fsis-sampling-2012}.

The financial cost was reported by Cargill in regulatory and industry disclosures at approximately $\$30 \,\text{million}$ in direct recall expenses, with downstream costs (lost production at the idled plant, brand recovery, litigation reserves) raising the total to a higher figure that the company did not separately publish. The total recalled mass of approximately 36 million pounds was, at the time, the largest meat recall in US history measured in product mass.

## Organisational / regulatory mechanism

The regulatory mechanism that defined the measurement protocol was the USDA Food Safety and Inspection Service's PR/HACCP rule, codified in 9 CFR Parts 304, 308, 310, 320, 327, 381, 416, and 417 (the cross-Part HACCP rule), which had been in force since 1996 and which left the granularity of sampling to the establishment-level operating definitions of the inspector and the plant. The Government Accountability Office's 2012 review of FSIS sampling found that the protocol's level of aggregation (plant-day, not consumer-lot) had been a known limitation in the academic and trade literature since the early 2000s but had not been changed because lot-level sampling would have multiplied inspection cost and required plant cooperation that the existing rule did not mandate \cite[pp.~22--28]{acc:ch01t3b-gao-fsis-sampling-2012}.

Following the recall, FSIS issued updated guidance on ground-poultry sampling that moved toward more granular sampling streams, tighter linkage between PulseNet pattern matches and recall triggers, and a 2013 published outbreak-response framework requiring earlier coordination between FSIS, CDC, and state health departments when PulseNet detected an antibiotic-resistant Salmonella cluster. The case became canonical in food-engineering and process-safety literature for the principle that sampling granularity must match the granularity at which the product is consumed and at which it can fail, not the granularity at which it is produced.

- Volume I, Chapter 1 ("Why we measure"): canonical case for measurement-protocol granularity. The lots that should have been sampled as consumer-package lots were sampled at plant-day granularity, so contamination confined to a fraction of the day's output cleared the test and reached consumers.

\textbf{One paragraph}: Between 20 February and 2 August 2011, Cargill Meat Solutions' Springdale, Arkansas plant produced approximately 36 million pounds of ground turkey contaminated, intermittently, with a multidrug-resistant strain of \emph{Salmonella enterica} serotype Heidelberg (PulseNet PFGE pattern JF6X01.0223). The CDC linked 136 cases of human illness across 34 states (37 hospitalisations, 1 death) to the strain through PulseNet pattern matching. USDA FSIS announced Recall 090-2011 on 3 August 2011, covering the full plant output for the period; a second recall (094-2011) on 11 September 2011 added 185,000 pounds produced after the plant resumed operation. The reason the contamination had not been caught in routine sampling was structural: FSIS's PR/HACCP sampling protocol collected composite samples at the plant-day level, treated each sample as representative of the entire day's output, and did not link consecutive samples into a longitudinal record at consumer-package lot granularity. A plant of Springdale's volume produced thousands of consumer-package lots per day; a single composite sample drawn from one production-day stream could clear the day's output regardless of contamination confined to a particular line, shift, or vendor of incoming birds. The case is the canonical example for the principle that sampling granularity must match the granularity at which the product can fail, and led to USDA FSIS revising its ground-poultry sampling guidance and its coordination with CDC PulseNet on antibiotic-resistant Salmonella clusters.
