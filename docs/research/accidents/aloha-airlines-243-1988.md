# Aloha Airlines Flight 243, 1988

## Date(s) and location

Aloha Airlines Flight 243, a Boeing 737-200, registration N73711, departed Hilo, Hawaii, on the afternoon of 28 April 1988 bound for Honolulu. At approximately 13:48 local time, while climbing through $24{,}000\,\text{ft}$ over the channel between Maui and Molokai, the aircraft suffered an explosive structural failure of the upper forward fuselage. A section of skin and supporting structure roughly $5.5\,\text{m}$ in length and extending from aft of the cockpit to the forward edge of the wing departed the aircraft. The flight crew descended and landed at Kahului Airport, Maui. One flight attendant was lost overboard during the decompression; 65 of the remaining 95 occupants were injured \cite{acc:ntsb-aar94-04}.

## Technical mechanism

The National Transportation Safety Board, in NTSB/AAR-89/03, attributed the structural failure to fatigue cracking that initiated at the rivet holes of cold-bonded fuselage lap joints on the upper forward fuselage \cite{acc:ntsb-aar94-04}. The joints used in the early 737 production were assembled with an epoxy adhesive in addition to the row of rivets; the design intent was that the bond would carry a substantial share of the joint load and reduce fatigue at the rivet holes. In service, the bond degraded by a combination of moisture ingress, salt-spray exposure, and corrosion of the underlying aluminium, leaving the rivet rows as the only load path.

The fatigue cracks initiated at the upper rivet row of the lap joint along stringer S-10L, propagated as a row of short cracks at adjacent rivet holes, and joined to form a continuous crack. The mechanism is the canonical multiple-site damage (MSD) pattern: many small, geometrically aligned cracks whose individual lengths remain below the inspection threshold, but whose combined length exceeds the residual-strength capacity of the structure once they link. The aircraft had accumulated $89{,}680$ flight cycles at the time of the accident, well above the original design service objective of $75{,}000$ cycles, and the aircraft operated on short island-hopping legs that pressurised the fuselage on every flight. The combination of cyclic pressurisation, MSD growth at disbonded lap joints, and corrosion accelerated the link-up failure into the service life of the airframe.

## Organisational / regulatory mechanism

The NTSB found contributory factors in the maintenance and inspection regime. The visual inspection of fuselage lap joints required by then-current FAA airworthiness directives could detect cracks of sufficient length, but the MSD pattern produces many short cracks rather than one long crack, and the visual technique was poorly matched to the failure mode. The disbond between the upper and lower skin of the lap joint hid the rivet-hole cracking from external visual inspection. Aloha's maintenance procedures had documented unresolved disbonding and corrosion on the accident aircraft but had not removed the airframe from service for the relevant fuselage section to be opened up.

The accident triggered the FAA's Aging Aircraft Program and led to the National Aging Aircraft Research Programme, which produced new airworthiness directives mandating cold-bond-joint inspections by eddy-current and ultrasonic methods, fuselage skin replacement programmes for high-cycle 737-200 aircraft, and a re-framed approach to the design and inspection of pressurised airframes against multiple-site damage. The case is the canonical aerospace example of a fatigue-plus-corrosion failure mode that the original design intent (cold-bonded joints carrying load in parallel with rivets) did not survive in service \cite{acc:ntsb-aar94-04}.
