# Flint water crisis, 2014-2019

## Date(s) and location

The Flint water supply was switched from the Detroit Water and Sewerage Department (which sourced treated Lake Huron water) to the Flint River, treated at the Flint Water Plant, on 25 April 2014 \cite{acc:fwatf-2016}. The switch had been planned as a temporary measure pending Flint's connection to the new Karegnondi Water Authority pipeline; the temporary period was extended through the contamination period. Residents and pediatricians began documenting evidence of elevated lead in 2014-2015; the State of Michigan acknowledged the contamination in October 2015; the supply was switched back to Detroit-system water on 16 October 2015. The remediation (replacement of lead service lines; ongoing water-quality monitoring; public-health response) continued through 2019 and beyond.

## Technical mechanism

The Flint Water Advisory Task Force identified the proximate technical mechanism as the absence of corrosion-control treatment in the Flint River water as supplied through the city's distribution system \cite{acc:fwatf-2016}. The city's distribution system included a substantial inventory of lead service lines (the small-diameter pipes connecting the city water mains to individual homes; many of Flint's older homes had lead service lines installed before the practice was discontinued in the 1980s). Lead service lines in normal operation develop a protective scale of lead-corrosion products and (in systems with orthophosphate or other corrosion-control treatment) phosphate-based protective films; the protective scale and films keep lead concentrations in delivered water at acceptable levels.

The Flint River water, as supplied without corrosion-control treatment, was substantially more corrosive than the Detroit-system water it replaced. The Flint River water's chloride content was approximately 8 times higher than the Detroit-system water's; the chloride-to-sulfate mass ratio (a recognised predictor of lead corrosion in service lines) was substantially above the threshold associated with elevated lead release. The aggressive chemistry stripped the protective scale from the lead service lines; lead corrosion accelerated; lead concentrations in delivered water rose to levels well above the federal action level of 15 parts per billion (and in some homes to levels above 100 parts per billion).

In parallel, the same water-quality changes (combined with the city's water-distribution-system temperature, residence-time, and disinfection-treatment changes) contributed to a Legionnaires' disease outbreak in Genesee County in 2014-2015. The outbreak's connection to the water-system changes was identified retrospectively and is supported by epidemiological analysis; the outbreak killed at least 12 people.

## Organisational / regulatory mechanism

The Flint Water Advisory Task Force investigation identified contributing factors at multiple levels \cite{acc:fwatf-2016}. The City of Flint had been operating under a state-appointed emergency manager who had broad authority over financial decisions; the decision to switch water supply was made in part to reduce water-supply costs as part of the city's financial emergency. The Michigan Department of Environmental Quality (MDEQ), the state regulator with primacy over Safe Drinking Water Act enforcement, had advised that corrosion-control treatment was not required for the Flint River water because it was the first of two six-month monitoring periods; the advice was inconsistent with EPA's Lead and Copper Rule and was identified by the EPA's later review as incorrect. The MDEQ's response to the early evidence of elevated lead (including pediatricians' reports of elevated blood lead in Flint children) was contested at the time and identified by the Task Force as inadequate.

The institutional response over the following years included: criminal prosecutions of state officials, MDEQ staff, and emergency managers; the resignation of MDEQ leadership; substantial federal and state funding for service-line replacement and for affected residents' health monitoring; broader EPA regulatory revisions to the Lead and Copper Rule (final rule in 2021); and the broader recognition of the structural conditions that produced the failure (state emergency-manager authority that overrode local democratic processes; the fragmented federal-state regulatory regime under the Safe Drinking Water Act; the underfunding of water-quality regulators relative to the technical complexity of distribution-system chemistry).

## Short-form summaries

\textbf{One sentence}: The Flint, Michigan water supply was switched from Detroit-system water (treated and corrosion-controlled) to Flint River water (treated but without corrosion control) on 25 April 2014; the absence of corrosion-control treatment caused lead from the city's lead service lines to leach into drinking water, exposing approximately 100,000 residents to elevated lead levels for approximately 18 months before the contamination was acknowledged and the supply was switched back; the public-health consequences included elevated blood-lead levels in children, a Legionnaires' disease outbreak that killed at least 12 people, and substantial subsequent water-infrastructure costs.

\textbf{One paragraph}: The technical mechanism was the absence of corrosion-control treatment in the more-corrosive Flint River water, which stripped the protective scale from the city's lead service lines and produced elevated lead concentrations in delivered water (well above the federal action level of 15 ppb in many homes). The organisational mechanism was a chain of decisions and non-decisions by the state-appointed emergency manager (whose financial-recovery priorities drove the supply switch), the Michigan Department of Environmental Quality (which advised that corrosion-control treatment was not required during a monitoring period, an advice inconsistent with EPA's Lead and Copper Rule), and the broader state and federal regulatory framework (which had funded MDEQ's drinking-water program below the level required for substantive oversight). Pediatricians' reports of elevated blood lead in Flint children, independent water-quality testing by Virginia Tech researchers, and other early signals were contested and dismissed by state officials before being acknowledged; the supply was switched back to Detroit-system water in October 2015. The remediation continued for years (service-line replacement; long-term health monitoring; legal and regulatory responses).
