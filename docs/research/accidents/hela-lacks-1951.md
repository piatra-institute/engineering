# HeLa cells (Henrietta Lacks), 1951

## Date(s) and location

A cervical-tumour biopsy was taken from Henrietta Lacks at the Johns Hopkins Hospital "coloured ward" on 8 February 1951 during the diagnostic work-up for an aggressive adenocarcinoma. George Gey's tissue-culture laboratory at Johns Hopkins received a sample of the tumour the same day. The HeLa line was established within days and the first culture-method paper (Gey, Coffman, Kubicek 1952) appeared in *Cancer Research* in April 1952. Henrietta Lacks died on 4 October 1951. The line has been propagated continuously in laboratories worldwide ever since and was the first human cell line to achieve indefinite replication outside the body.

## Technical mechanism

HeLa cells are an aneuploid epithelial line derived from a cervical adenocarcinoma later identified as driven by integrated human papillomavirus type 18 (HPV-18) sequence. Integration of HPV-18 E6 and E7 oncoproteins inactivates the p53 and Rb tumour-suppressor pathways respectively, removing the dominant cell-cycle restraints; HeLa cells also express high levels of telomerase reverse transcriptase (TERT), avoiding the telomere-shortening barrier to replication. The combination of these features supports indefinite proliferation under standard tissue-culture conditions, in contrast to the Hayflick limit of approximately 50 divisions for primary human fibroblasts. The HeLa karyotype is approximately hypertriploid (median chromosome count ~82, range 70-164) with extensive structural rearrangements; the high genomic instability has produced multiple substrains over the line's history and contributes to the cross-contamination problem that has confused decades of cell-biology results. Adey et al. (2013) reported the first full HeLa genome sequence; Landry et al. (2013) corrected and extended it.

## Organisational / regulatory mechanism

No tissue-acquisition consent was sought or obtained for the 1951 biopsy. The practice at Johns Hopkins and at most US tertiary hospitals in that era was to retain surgical specimens for research without explicit patient consent, on the rationale that the tissue was a by-product of clinical care. The first Code of Federal Regulations on human-subjects research (45 CFR 46, the Common Rule) was promulgated in 1974, twenty-three years after the HeLa biopsy. The Lacks family was not informed of the existence of the cell line until 1973, when researchers contacted the family seeking blood samples to clarify the HeLa contamination problem; the family learned the commercial scale of HeLa-related research only over the following decade. Skloot's 2010 book *The Immortal Life of Henrietta Lacks* prompted a 2013 NIH agreement with the Lacks family (Hudson and Collins, *Nature* 2013) that requires investigators submitting HeLa genome data to a controlled-access NIH archive to obtain approval from a six-member advisory committee that includes Lacks family representatives. The agreement does not impose retroactive royalties on past research and does not constrain non-NIH-funded use of HeLa.

The case has become canonical in research-ethics teaching because it combines a clear consent failure with a long latency between the failure and the public reckoning, a structural racial inequity in mid-twentieth-century US medical research, an evolving genomic-privacy problem (publishing a cell line's full genome reveals heritable information about the donor's living relatives), and a global open-science benefit (HeLa-based research has supported polio-vaccine development, in vitro fertilisation methodology, the AIDS-drug pipeline, and the Nobel-prize-winning discoveries of the HPV-cervical cancer link and of telomerase). No single moral conclusion captures the case; the engineering reading is that consent processes for biological samples are a regulatory function whose absence has scientific, economic, and ethical consequences that compound over decades.

## Short-form summaries

\textbf{One sentence}: HeLa cells, the first continuously propagated human cell line, were derived in February 1951 at Johns Hopkins Hospital from a cervical-tumour biopsy of Henrietta Lacks without her or her family's consent and became the basis of decades of biomedical research and a recurring case in research ethics.

\textbf{Two sentences}: HeLa cells were derived in February 1951 from a cervical-tumour biopsy of Henrietta Lacks at Johns Hopkins Hospital; no consent was sought, in accordance with the practice of the era. The line supported polio-vaccine development, in vitro fertilisation methodology, the HPV-cervical cancer link, and the discovery of telomerase, while the consent failure remained publicly unaddressed until the family was contacted in 1973 and the 2013 NIH-Lacks family agreement formalised controlled access to HeLa genome data.

\textbf{One paragraph}: In February 1951 a cervical-tumour biopsy was taken from Henrietta Lacks at Johns Hopkins Hospital. The sample was passed to George Gey's tissue-culture laboratory the same day; within weeks, Gey's group had established the first continuously propagated human cell line. The HeLa line was made possible by the combination of HPV-18 integration (inactivating the p53 and Rb pathways) and high telomerase expression, both downstream consequences of the underlying adenocarcinoma. Henrietta Lacks died in October 1951; no consent was sought for the biopsy and no consent process existed at the institution. The Lacks family was not informed of the cell line's existence until 1973. The case combines a clear consent failure with a long-latency public reckoning, a structural racial inequity in mid-twentieth-century US medical research, an evolving genomic-privacy problem, and a global open-science benefit; it is the canonical example for tissue-ownership and informed-consent debates in modern research ethics. The 2013 NIH-Lacks family agreement formalised controlled access to HeLa genome data through a six-member advisory committee including Lacks family representatives.
