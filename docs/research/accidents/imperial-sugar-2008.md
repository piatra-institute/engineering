# Imperial Sugar refinery dust explosion, 2008

## Date(s) and location

On the evening of 7 February 2008, at approximately 19:15 local time, a series of dust explosions destroyed much of the Imperial Sugar Company refinery at Port Wentworth, Georgia. The refinery processed approximately $4{,}000{,}000$ kg of granulated sugar per week and had been in operation since 1917. Fourteen workers died and thirty-six were injured; multiple buildings within the refinery were destroyed or rendered uninhabitable. The plant did not return to operation in the same physical configuration.

## Technical mechanism

The U.S. Chemical Safety and Hazard Investigation Board investigation, published as CSB Report 2008-05-I-GA, identified the immediate failure mode as a primary dust explosion inside an enclosed steel conveyor belt operating beneath three large sugar storage silos \cite{acc:csb-imperial-sugar-2009}. The conveyor enclosure, installed approximately one year before the explosion to control dust emissions to the workspace, had also created a confined volume in which airborne sugar dust could accumulate to within the flammable concentration range. The ignition source for the primary explosion was not definitively identified; candidate sources included an overheated bearing in the conveyor, a static electricity discharge from accumulated sugar particles, and friction sparks from foreign metal objects on the belt.

The primary explosion inside the conveyor enclosure produced a pressure pulse that ruptured the enclosure and propelled accumulated sugar dust on overhead structural members, pipework, and elevated walkways into airborne suspension throughout the surrounding spaces. The suspended dust, now dispersed at concentrations within the flammable range, was ignited by the flame front from the primary explosion and the hot combustion products. The resulting secondary dust explosions propagated through interconnected spaces in the packaging and palletising buildings and caused most of the fatalities and structural damage. The investigation found accumulated sugar dust on overhead surfaces in many parts of the plant that significantly exceeded the depths at which deflagration propagation is sustained.

## Organisational / regulatory mechanism

The CSB investigation identified a long-term pattern of accumulated combustible dust in the refinery as the systemic precondition for the secondary explosions. Routine housekeeping, sufficient to maintain dust accumulation below the depth at which secondary explosions propagate, had not been performed at the required frequency on the elevated surfaces. The investigation found that combustible-dust hazards were not consistently identified, evaluated, or controlled under the plant's process-safety management practices, and that OSHA's pre-2008 regulatory framework for combustible dust in general manufacturing was significantly weaker than the comparable framework for the grain-handling industry.

The accident led to renewed OSHA emphasis on combustible-dust hazards, the proposed (and unfinalised as of 2025) federal Combustible Dust Standard, and revised industry practice through the National Fire Protection Association's NFPA 652 (Standard on the Fundamentals of Combustible Dust) and NFPA 61 (Standard for the Prevention of Fires and Dust Explosions in Agricultural and Food Processing Facilities). Sugar refining and similar industries (corn milling, flour, wood products, metal powders) revised housekeeping practices, installed explosion isolation and venting on enclosed conveyors, and added dust-collection systems with downstream isolation.
