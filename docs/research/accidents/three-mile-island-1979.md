# Three Mile Island, 1979

## Date(s) and location

The accident occurred at Three Mile Island Unit 2 (TMI-2), a pressurised-water reactor operated by Metropolitan Edison Company near Middletown, Pennsylvania. The initiating event began at approximately 04:00 local time on 28 March 1979. Loss of feedwater progressed to partial core uncovery over the following hours; the core damage was characterised over subsequent days. No immediate fatalities occurred; the long-term radiological impact has been debated.

## Technical mechanism

The Kemeny Commission report identified a sequence beginning with the loss of feedwater to the steam generators \cite{acc:kemeny-tmi-1979}. The condensate-polishing system tripped, removing main feedwater. Auxiliary feedwater pumps started but were ineffective because their discharge valves had been left closed after maintenance, a fact not displayed on the control-room boards. With no feedwater removing heat from the secondary side, the primary coolant heated and pressurised. A pilot-operated relief valve (PORV) on the pressuriser opened to relieve pressure, as designed.

The PORV stuck open. The control-room indication for the PORV showed only the valve's command signal, not its actual position; the indication read "closed" while the valve was open. Primary coolant began escaping through the open PORV into the reactor coolant drain tank. The reactor's emergency core cooling system (ECCS) injected coolant in response to falling primary pressure. Operators, reading the indication as "PORV closed," interpreted the rising pressuriser level (caused by ECCS flow into a system losing inventory through the PORV) as evidence of "going solid" with water and reduced ECCS flow. With ECCS reduced and the PORV continuing to discharge, primary coolant inventory fell over several hours; the core was partially uncovered, and core damage occurred.

The Kemeny Commission's reconstruction identified the key technical failure as the misleading PORV indicator combined with the operators' training in pressuriser-level interpretation, which had taught operators to maintain pressuriser level within bounds without distinguishing the case where level rose because of inventory addition versus the case where level rose because of voiding in a depressurised system.

## Organisational / regulatory mechanism

The Kemeny Commission's report on TMI-2 was sweeping in its institutional findings \cite{acc:kemeny-tmi-1979}. The Commission found that the Nuclear Regulatory Commission (NRC), the operating utility, the reactor vendor, and the industry's professional culture all bore significant responsibility for an accident-prone state of the U.S.\ commercial nuclear industry: licensing focused on hardware rather than on operational discipline; operator training was inadequate for the diagnostic complexity of the actual control-room environment; emergency planning around plants assumed accident scenarios that did not match the actual TMI sequence; the NRC's organisational structure made it difficult for any single body within the regulator to "see" reactor safety as a whole.

Specific recommendations included: restructuring the NRC; instituting symptom-based emergency procedures (procedures that respond to plant symptoms rather than requiring operators to diagnose the underlying scenario); upgrading control-room human factors; requiring full-scope training simulators for operating crews; and instituting more rigorous emergency planning. The Institute of Nuclear Power Operations (INPO) was created in 1979 in response to TMI as an industry-driven peer-review body; the NRC instituted post-TMI rule-making over the following decade.
