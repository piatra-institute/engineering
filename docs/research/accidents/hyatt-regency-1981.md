# Hyatt Regency walkway collapse, 1981

## Date(s) and location

The Hyatt Regency Hotel in Kansas City, Missouri, hosted a tea dance in its multi-storey atrium on the evening of 17 July 1981. The atrium was spanned by three suspended walkways at the second, third, and fourth floor levels. The fourth-floor walkway hung directly above the second-floor walkway. At approximately 19:05 local time, the connection at the fourth-floor walkway failed; the fourth-floor walkway fell onto the second-floor walkway, and both then fell to the atrium floor. There were 114 fatalities and more than 200 injuries.

## Technical mechanism

The National Bureau of Standards investigation, led by Edward O.\ Pfrang and Richard D.\ Marshall and published as NBS Building Science Series 143 in 1982, identified the cause as a connection failure at the fourth-floor walkway's hanger-rod attachment \cite{acc:nbs-hyatt-1982}.

The original design used a single continuous threaded rod from the atrium ceiling, passing through the box-beam ends of both the fourth-floor and second-floor walkways. Under that design, each walkway's box-beam connection carried only its own walkway's load: the fourth-floor connection carried the fourth-floor walkway only, and the second-floor connection carried the second-floor walkway only.

During fabrication, the design was modified to use two separate threaded rods: one rod from the ceiling to the fourth-floor walkway, and a second rod from the fourth-floor walkway to the second-floor walkway. The modification was made because the original single-continuous-rod design was difficult to install; the second-floor walkway nut would have had to be threaded onto the rod over its full length. The modified design transferred the second-floor walkway's load through the fourth-floor walkway's box-beam connection, doubling the load on that connection. The connection had not been redesigned for the doubled load.

Calculations performed during the NBS investigation indicated that the original single-rod design's connection had a factor of safety of approximately 1.6 against the design code, near the lower limit of acceptable practice. The modified two-rod design's connection had a factor of safety of approximately 0.6 against the design code: the connection was overloaded under the design load alone, and the addition of a tea-dance crowd produced loads that exceeded the connection's strength by a substantial margin.

The connection failed by tear-out: the box-beam's lower flange was pulled through by the washer and nut. The fourth-floor walkway dropped onto the second-floor walkway, whose connection had not been doubled; the second-floor connection failed by overload; both walkways fell.

## Organisational / regulatory mechanism

The NBS investigation found that the design modification had been made through a sequence of conversations and shop-drawing changes between the structural-steel fabricator (Havens Steel Company) and the structural-engineering firm (G.\ C.\ E.\ International, later Jack D.\ Gillum and Associates). The final detailed design that fabrication followed was a shop drawing produced by the fabricator and stamped by the structural engineer of record. The shop drawing showed the modified two-rod connection. The structural engineer of record stamped the drawing without recalculating the connection's capacity for the doubled load.

The investigation identified the underlying organisational mechanism as a breakdown in the design-review chain: the modification crossed the boundary from designer to fabricator, but the design-load recalculation that the modification required did not. Subsequent licensure proceedings in Missouri revoked the structural engineer of record's license. The case became a canonical example in engineering ethics curricula of the limits of the engineer's stamp as a verification mechanism \cite{acc:nbs-hyatt-1982}.
