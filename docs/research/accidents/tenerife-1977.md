# Tenerife airport disaster, 1977

## Date(s) and location

The collision occurred at 17:06:50 local time on 27 March 1977 on the runway at Los Rodeos Airport (now Tenerife North), Canary Islands, Spain \cite{acc:ciaiac-tenerife-1978}. Both aircraft were Boeing 747s: KLM Flight 4805 (registration PH-BUF, "Rijn") and Pan American World Airways Flight 1736 (registration N736PA, "Clipper Victor"). Both had been diverted to Los Rodeos from Gran Canaria after a bomb incident closed Las Palmas; both were awaiting permission to depart for Las Palmas when fog reduced runway visibility below the threshold for normal operations. The crash killed all 248 occupants of the KLM 747 and 335 of the 396 occupants of the Pan Am 747, total 583, the highest accident toll in aviation history.

## Technical mechanism

The Spanish Subsecretaría de Aviación Civil investigation identified the proximate cause as KLM beginning its takeoff roll without takeoff clearance from air traffic control while Pan Am was still backtracking on the same runway in conditions of dense fog \cite{acc:ciaiac-tenerife-1978}. The runway was the only runway at Los Rodeos and was being used for both backtracking (aircraft taxiing in the direction opposite to takeoff) and takeoff. KLM had been instructed to backtrack down the runway, perform a 180-degree turn at the end, and hold for takeoff clearance; Pan Am had been instructed to backtrack along the same runway and exit at a specified taxiway. Pan Am was still on the runway when KLM began the takeoff roll.

The investigation identified that KLM had requested and received from the tower an ATC clearance, which specified the route after takeoff. The KLM captain, immediately after receiving the route clearance, advanced throttles. The first officer transmitted "we are now at takeoff" or "we are now uh taking off," a non-standard phraseology whose meaning was ambiguous (an announcement of intent or a statement of action in progress). The tower responded "OK $\ldots$ stand by for takeoff, I will call you," a transmission that overlapped with a Pan Am transmission asking whether the runway was clear ("We're still taxiing down the runway, the Clipper one seven three six"). The simultaneous transmissions produced a heterodyne squeal in the KLM cockpit; the KLM crew heard only the "OK" portion of the tower's response and did not hear the Pan Am transmission at all. KLM continued the takeoff roll; the collision occurred when KLM had achieved rotation speed and was airborne by a few meters; the KLM's main gear struck the Pan Am's fuselage as Pan Am attempted to clear the runway via a sharp left turn.

## Organisational / regulatory mechanism

The investigation identified contributing factors beyond the immediate communication failure \cite{acc:ciaiac-tenerife-1978}. The KLM captain was a senior pilot, head of the KLM 747 training department, and one of the most senior pilots in the airline. Crew resource management as a discipline did not yet exist; the cockpit-voice-recorder transcript records the KLM first officer voicing concern (about the takeoff clearance, about the question of whether ATC had cleared takeoff) and not pressing the concern when the captain proceeded. The KLM flight was approaching its crew-duty-time limit, which under Dutch regulations of the period would have required either a return to the Netherlands or a long delay; the captain had reason to want to depart quickly. The KLM had refuelled at Los Rodeos for the leg back from Las Palmas, which added an hour to the ground time and reduced the takeoff performance margin (a heavier aircraft requires more runway).

The institutional response in the years following Tenerife was the development and dissemination of crew resource management (CRM) training, which gave first officers explicit framing and language for challenging captains' decisions and gave captains explicit framing for inviting and receiving challenges. The non-standard radio phraseology used in the accident also drove a tightening of ICAO standard phraseology for takeoff clearance; "cleared for takeoff" became the only acceptable formulation, and "takeoff" was eliminated from other communications (where formerly "ready for takeoff" was accepted, the standard became "ready for departure").
