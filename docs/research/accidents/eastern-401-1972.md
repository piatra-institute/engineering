# Eastern Air Lines Flight 401, 1972

## Date(s) and location

Eastern Air Lines Flight 401, a Lockheed L-1011-1 (registration N310EA), crashed in the Florida Everglades approximately 18 miles west-northwest of Miami International Airport at approximately 23:42 EST on 29 December 1972 \cite{acc:ntsb-aar73-14}. The flight had departed New York JFK at 21:20 with 163 passengers and 13 crew aboard. The accident killed 99 of the 163 passengers and all but one of the flight crew (the second officer survived) plus several cabin crew; the total dead was 101.

## Technical mechanism

The NTSB investigation identified the proximate cause as crew preoccupation with a malfunction of the nose-landing-gear position indicator (a burnt-out bulb that produced an unlit indicator instead of the expected green light when the gear was confirmed down) while the autopilot ceased maintaining the 2000 ft cruise altitude assigned by air traffic control \cite{acc:ntsb-aar73-14}. The aircraft had been holding west of Miami while the crew worked the indicator problem. The captain had instructed the second officer to enter the avionics bay below the cockpit to visually confirm the landing-gear position through a small viewport. During the troubleshooting, the autopilot's altitude-hold mode was disengaged inadvertently (the most likely scenario identified by the investigation was that the captain bumped the control column with enough force to trip the altitude-hold disconnect, after which the autopilot reverted to a pitch-attitude mode that did not maintain altitude). The aircraft began a gradual descent at approximately 200 feet per minute, undetectable in night cruise without instrument scan.

An audible altitude-deviation warning sounded in the cockpit when the aircraft descended approximately 250 feet below the assigned altitude; the warning was the C-chord aural alert located at the second officer's station (then unoccupied because the second officer was in the avionics bay) and was not noticed by the remaining cockpit crew. The aircraft continued the gradual descent for approximately 80 seconds before impact. The first verbal indication of the descent in the cockpit-voice-recorder transcript is the first officer saying "we did something to the altitude," followed seconds later by impact. Miami approach control did notice the altitude deviation on radar (the aircraft was at 900 ft instead of 2000 ft) and queried "Eastern uh four oh one how are things comin' along out there"; the query was insufficiently specific to alert the crew to the altitude problem, and the controller did not follow up before impact.

## Organisational / regulatory mechanism

The NTSB identified the accident's primary causal factor as the failure of the flight crew to monitor the flight instruments during the final four minutes of flight, attributing this to crew distraction by the landing-gear indicator malfunction \cite{acc:ntsb-aar73-14}. The institutional response was foundational for crew resource management as a discipline: NASA convened a workshop in 1979 ("Resource Management on the Flight Deck") that drew on Eastern 401 as a canonical case for the gap between individual technical skill and crew coordination under workload. The workshop and its successors produced CRM as a training discipline now codified internationally; United Airlines was the first major US carrier to mandate CRM training in 1981, with industry-wide adoption through the 1980s and 1990s.

The technical response included improvements in autopilot mode awareness (clearer annunciation when modes change, harder requirements for explicit re-engagement after disconnect), the relocation or duplication of the altitude-deviation aural alert so that it sounds at all crew stations, and the broader adoption of cockpit task-management protocols (one pilot flies; one pilot manages the issue; explicit verbal confirmation when control transfers).
