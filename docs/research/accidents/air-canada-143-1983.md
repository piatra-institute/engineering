# Air Canada Flight 143, 1983

## Date(s) and location

Air Canada Flight 143 departed Montreal on 23 July 1983 bound for Edmonton via Ottawa. The aircraft, a Boeing 767-233 registered C-GAUN, exhausted its usable fuel at approximately $41{,}000\,\text{ft}$ over Red Lake, Ontario. The crew glided the aircraft to an emergency landing on the former Royal Canadian Air Force base at Gimli, Manitoba. There were 61 passengers and 8 crew aboard; no fatalities occurred.

## Technical mechanism

The Boeing 767 was Air Canada's first metric-instrumented aircraft. The fuel-quantity processor and load-calculation workflow used kilograms, while fuel itself was loaded by volume in litres. Conversion required multiplication by the fuel's density. On the day of the flight, the aircraft's fuel-quantity processor was unserviceable, so the load was determined by an abnormal procedure using drip sticks (mechanical fuel-volume gauges) plus a hand calculation involving fuel density. The calculation was performed using a factor near $1.77 \,\text{lb/L}$, the appropriate factor for pounds-per-litre, where the kilogram-based calculation required approximately $0.80 \,\si{\kilogram\per\liter}$. The result overstated fuel mass by approximately a factor of $2.2$. The aircraft departed with about half the fuel its flight plan required \cite{acc:lockwood-gimli1985}.

In flight, the fuel-monitoring system reported low fuel and the engines flamed out from fuel exhaustion before the aircraft reached Winnipeg. The crew used the 767's ram air turbine for partial hydraulics and selected Gimli as a former military airfield with a usable runway. The captain executed a sideslip manoeuvre, the first officer (a former glider pilot) located the airfield, and the aircraft landed without engine power.

## Organisational / regulatory mechanism

The Lockwood Board of Inquiry, established by Order in Council under Justice George H.\ Lockwood, identified contributory factors beyond the unit conversion. The fuel-quantity processor was unserviceable in a manner that had been carried as a non-routine deferred defect. Procedural shortcuts had been authorised under conditions that did not match the day's actual maintenance state. Documentation accompanying the metric-fuel transition assigned responsibility for fuel-load verification across multiple roles in a way that allowed each role to assume another role had verified the conversion. The dispatch chain trusted the fuelling crew's arithmetic without an independent cross-check \cite{acc:lockwood-gimli1985}.

## Lessons by scale

- Volume I, Chapter 1 ("Why we measure"): unit confusion at an interface (drip-stick volume to fuel-mass) under a procedure that did not enforce the conversion as an audited step.
- Volume I, Chapter 2 ("Units and dimensions"): the same interface seen at the dimensional level (mass vs.\ volume conversion through density).
- Volume X, Chapter 9 ("Human factors and operator error"): the procedure's distribution of responsibility across roles such that no single role audited the conversion.
- Volume X, Chapter 14 ("Maintenance, observability, life-cycle" or equivalent): the deferred-defect entry and its interaction with the abnormal fuel-load procedure.
