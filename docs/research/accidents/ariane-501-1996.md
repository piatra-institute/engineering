# Ariane 5 Flight 501, 1996

## Date(s) and location

The maiden flight of the Ariane 5 launcher (Flight 501), carrying the four Cluster scientific satellites, lifted off from the Centre Spatial Guyanais at Kourou, French Guiana, on 4 June 1996. The vehicle began to disintegrate at about $H_{0} + 39 \,\si{\second}$ (where $H_{0}$ is the start of main engine ignition), corresponding to about $31 \,\si{\second}$ after lift-off. The launch failed; all four satellites were lost.

## Technical mechanism

The Inquiry Board chaired by Jacques-Louis Lions, reporting on 19 July 1996, identified the cause as an unhandled software exception in the inertial reference system (SRI). The exception occurred during conversion of a 64-bit floating-point representation of an internal horizontal-bias variable (BH) to a 16-bit signed integer. The result exceeded the integer's range $[-32768, 32767]$ and the conversion overflowed. The exception was not caught; the SRI shut down, and the back-up SRI, running the same software, shut down for the same reason. With both SRIs offline, the on-board computer interpreted diagnostic output from the SRI as flight data, computed extreme attitude corrections, and the launcher commanded large nozzle deflections. Aerodynamic loads exceeded structural limits and the vehicle broke up; the self-destruct system then activated \cite{acc:ariane501-ifr}.

The conversion occurred inside an alignment function whose purpose was to align the inertial reference platform on the launch pad before lift-off. For Ariane 4, the function had been kept active for several seconds after lift-off as part of a pre-launch-recovery requirement. For Ariane 5, the same function had no operational purpose after lift-off, but the software was reused from Ariane 4 unchanged. The Ariane 5 trajectory developed horizontal velocity at a rate that took the BH variable outside the 16-bit integer range; the Ariane 4 trajectory had not. The variable had been one of seven that the Ariane 4 software protected against overflow; the other three, including BH, had been left unprotected on the basis that the Ariane 4 trajectory could not produce out-of-range values. That analysis was not redone for Ariane 5.

## Organisational / regulatory mechanism

The Inquiry Board found the failure to be a verification-domain problem: software qualified for one launcher was reused on another whose flight envelope had not been re-analysed against the reused code. The board observed that the Ariane 5 qualification had treated the reused SRI software as already qualified by Ariane 4 service history, rather than as new software whose flight envelope must be re-verified. The board recommended explicit reuse-with-modification verification, including range analysis of all internal variables against the new flight envelope, defensive programming for arithmetic operations on flight-critical values, and software-architecture review for reused components in qualification \cite{acc:ariane501-ifr}.
