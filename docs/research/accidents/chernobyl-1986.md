# Chernobyl, 1986

## Date(s) and location

The accident occurred at Reactor Unit 4 of the Chernobyl Nuclear Power Plant in the Ukrainian Soviet Socialist Republic (now Ukraine), near the city of Pripyat. The initiating power excursion occurred at 01:23 local time on 26 April 1986. The reactor's power rose to many times its rated thermal output within seconds; the resulting steam explosion destroyed the reactor and the upper part of the reactor building. A graphite fire burned for several days after the initial explosion; fission products were released into the atmosphere over approximately ten days.

## Technical mechanism

The reactor was an RBMK-1000, a graphite-moderated, water-cooled, pressure-tube design. The IAEA's INSAG-1 (1986) and INSAG-7 (1992) reports identify the technical mechanism as a runaway power excursion produced by the interaction of the reactor's positive void coefficient with the design of its control rods, under operating conditions outside the reactor's safety envelope \cite{acc:iaea-insag1-1986, acc:iaea-insag7-1992}.

The accident occurred during a planned safety test intended to demonstrate that, on loss of off-site power, the kinetic energy of the still-spinning main turbine could power the main coolant pumps long enough for backup diesel generators to start. The test required the reactor to operate at reduced power during a turbine coastdown.

In preparation for the test, operators inserted control rods to reduce power. Power dropped further than intended, into a regime where xenon-135 buildup poisoned the core. To recover power, operators withdrew most of the control rods, leaving only a small operational reactivity margin. The test then proceeded with the reactor in a state outside its operational safety envelope: low power, low operational reactivity margin, multiple safety systems disabled to allow the test to complete without trip.

When the operators initiated the test by closing the turbine steam-stop valves, coolant flow began to reduce. The RBMK's positive void coefficient meant that as coolant in the pressure tubes converted from liquid to steam (voiding), reactivity rose. The control rods, designed with graphite displacers at their tips, initially \emph{added} reactivity when inserted from their fully-withdrawn position before subtracting it: the graphite tips displaced water in the pressure tubes' lower regions, increasing reactivity locally before the absorber sections reached the core.

When operators initiated SCRAM (full control-rod insertion) in response to a power transient, the control-rod insertion's initial positive-reactivity contribution combined with the existing positive void coefficient to produce a prompt-criticality power excursion. Power rose by several orders of magnitude in seconds. Fuel pellets fragmented; coolant flashed to steam; the resulting pressure pulse blew the reactor's upper biological shield off; oxygen reached the now-exposed graphite moderator and the moderator burned for nine days.

## Organisational / regulatory mechanism

The IAEA's INSAG-1 report in 1986 was issued in the immediate aftermath and based largely on Soviet authorities' presentation; it emphasised operator error. INSAG-7 in 1992, with full access to the reactor's documentation, revised the analysis substantially toward design and regulatory mechanisms \cite{acc:iaea-insag7-1992}.

INSAG-7's findings included: the RBMK's positive void coefficient at low power was a known design characteristic that had been documented in Soviet design literature but had not been incorporated into operator training or operating procedures; the control-rod design's positive-reactivity insertion at full withdrawal was similarly known but not communicated; the test procedure that the operating crew followed had been written and approved without independent safety review; the regulatory framework in the Soviet nuclear industry separated safety review from operating-organisation incentives in a manner that allowed the test to proceed with safety systems disabled.

Subsequent regulatory and industrial response was global. The IAEA's nuclear-safety standards were revised; the World Association of Nuclear Operators (WANO) was founded in 1989 as an industry peer-review body modelled in part on the U.S.\ INPO. The RBMK reactors that remained in operation were retrofitted with redesigned control rods and reduced void coefficient. The accident shaped public attitudes toward nuclear power across Europe and beyond for decades.
