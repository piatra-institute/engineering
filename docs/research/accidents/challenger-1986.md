# Space Shuttle Challenger, 1986

## Date(s) and location

Space Shuttle Challenger lifted off from Kennedy Space Center at 11:38 EST on 28 January 1986 on mission STS-51-L. The vehicle broke up at $T + 73 \,\si{\second}$ at an altitude of approximately $14 \,\si{\kilo\meter}$ over the Atlantic Ocean. All seven crew members died.

## Technical mechanism

The Rogers Commission's report identified the cause as the failure of an O-ring seal in the right solid rocket booster's (SRB's) aft field joint \cite{acc:nasa-challenger}. The SRB's segmented case was joined by tang-and-clevis field joints sealed by two redundant O-rings of fluorocarbon elastomer. At normal operating temperatures, the O-rings deformed under the joint's pressure-driven rotation and maintained the seal. At low temperatures, the elastomer's resilience decreased; the O-ring's response time to joint rotation increased; the seal could fail to engage during the brief window after motor ignition during which the joint's geometry changed.

The ambient temperature at launch was approximately $-2 \,\si{\degreeCelsius}$ ($28 \,{}^{\circ}\mathrm{F}$), the lowest at which the Space Shuttle had ever launched and well below temperatures at which prior O-ring erosion had been observed. At ignition, the right SRB's aft field joint failed to seal completely; hot combustion gas escaped from the joint, leaving a transient flame visible in launch photography. The leak appeared to seal partially during boost (likely as combustion products solidified in the gap), but at $T + 58 \,\si{\second}$ wind shear loosened the temporary seal. A persistent flame burned through the SRB's lower attachment strut to the external tank and, simultaneously, through the external tank's aft dome. The hydrogen tank failed; thrust from the SRB rotated the aft dome rupture; the orbiter, still under aerodynamic loads, broke up.

## Organisational / regulatory mechanism

The Rogers Commission's findings on the launch decision became one of the most-studied events in engineering ethics and organisational decision-making. Engineers at Morton Thiokol, the SRB contractor, had identified the O-ring's temperature-dependent resilience as a concern and had recommended against launch the night before mission STS-51-L. Under management discussion within Morton Thiokol and a teleconference with NASA, the recommendation was reversed; Morton Thiokol's management voted unanimously to approve launch over the engineers' objections. The Rogers Commission identified this reversal as a critical failure point: the engineers' technical concerns had been correctly recognised at the time but were not preserved through the decision chain \cite{acc:nasa-challenger}.

Diane Vaughan's 1996 book \emph{The Challenger Launch Decision} provides a sociological reanalysis of the same documents and identifies what she terms "the normalization of deviance": a pattern in which signals of departure from design specification (O-ring erosion observed on prior flights) were progressively reinterpreted as within acceptable risk, rather than as evidence that the original specification had not been met. Vaughan's analysis is the canonical secondary source on the organisational mechanism \cite{hist:vaughan1996}.

The Rogers Commission's recommendations included redesign of the SRB field joints, an independent safety, reliability, and quality assurance organisation within NASA, restructured launch-decision protocols, and crew-escape provisions for ascent. Most were implemented before the Space Shuttle's return to flight in September 1988.
