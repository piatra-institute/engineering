# Comair Flight 5191, 2006

## Date(s) and location

Comair Flight 5191 (operating as Delta Connection 5191), a Bombardier CL-600-2B19 (CRJ-100, registration N431CA), attempted takeoff at approximately 06:07 local time on 27 August 2006 from Lexington Blue Grass Airport (LEX), Kentucky \cite{acc:ntsb-aar07-05}. The crew taxied to and lined up on runway 26 instead of the assigned runway 22, began the takeoff roll, did not achieve flying speed before the runway end, and crashed in a field beyond the runway, with the wreckage striking trees and a perimeter fence. The accident killed 47 passengers, both flight attendants, and the captain; the first officer survived with serious injuries.

## Technical mechanism

The proximate cause was that the crew taxied to and attempted takeoff from a runway too short to support the aircraft's takeoff requirements \cite{acc:ntsb-aar07-05}. Runway 26 at LEX is 3500 ft long and is used for general-aviation operations; runway 22 is 7000 ft long and is the runway for commercial operations. The CRJ-100's takeoff distance at the flight's weight and ambient conditions required substantially more than 3500 ft. The aircraft used essentially all of runway 26 in the takeoff roll, did not achieve $V_R$ (rotation speed), and entered the unprepared terrain beyond the runway end.

The cockpit-voice-recorder transcript shows the crew followed the assigned-runway clearance ("Comair 191 cleared for takeoff runway 22") but, in taxi to position, turned onto and lined up on runway 26. The taxi route from the ramp to the runway 22 hold-short line crossed runway 26; the airport's lighting configuration that night (runway 26 was unlit; runway 22's lights were on) and the relative geometry of the taxi path created conditions in which the crew's mistaken alignment was not externally corrected before takeoff began. The crew did not perform a final orientation check against runway heading versus aircraft heading before commencing the takeoff roll; the CRJ-100's electronic flight displays did show runway alignment information that the crew did not consult.

## Organisational / regulatory mechanism

The NTSB investigation identified contributing factors at multiple levels \cite{acc:ntsb-aar07-05}. The Lexington tower was staffed by a single controller at the time of the takeoff (FAA staffing requirements at the time allowed single-controller operation during low-traffic periods); the controller cleared 5191 for takeoff and then turned to handle administrative work, missing the visual indication that the aircraft was on the wrong runway. The airport's signage and lighting configuration (a construction project was in progress that had altered taxi routes; an unlit runway 26 was in the taxi path to runway 22) had been identified in pre-accident safety assessments as creating possible confusion, but the assessments had not led to interim mitigations. The crew was on the second leg of a duty period that had started early; circadian-rhythm effects on attention at the 06:00 hour were a contributing factor identified by the NTSB.

The post-accident response included: FAA changes to tower-staffing requirements (mandating two controllers during operations with commercial flights at certain category airports); FAA changes to runway-incursion-avoidance procedures (mandatory verification of runway alignment against expected heading before takeoff); industry adoption of cockpit-procedure changes that incorporate explicit runway-identification callouts; and the broader regulatory recognition that airport configurations under construction or altered taxi routing require enhanced crew briefings and signage.

Contributing factors included the airport's lighting configuration that night (runway 26 unlit, runway 22 lit) which masked the alignment error in the taxi route; the single-controller tower configuration that allowed the controller to miss the visual indication of the wrong-runway alignment; the crew's omission of a final orientation check against runway heading; an airport construction project that had altered taxi routing; and circadian-rhythm effects at the 06:00 takeoff hour.
