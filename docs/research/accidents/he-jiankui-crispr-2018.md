# He Jiankui CRISPR-edited human embryos, 2018

## Date(s) and location

In November 2018 He Jiankui, then an associate professor at Southern University of Science and Technology in Shenzhen, China, announced via a YouTube video and a series of interviews with the Associated Press that twin girls (referred to as "Lulu" and "Nana") had been born in late October 2018 from human embryos he had edited with CRISPR-Cas9 to introduce a putative HIV-resistance allele at the CCR5 locus. The announcement was timed to coincide with the Second International Summit on Human Genome Editing in Hong Kong (27-29 November 2018), where He presented the work in person. A third child from the programme was born subsequently. In December 2019 He was convicted by the Nanshan District People's Court (Shenzhen) of "illegal medical practice" and sentenced to three years' imprisonment plus a fine of 3 million yuan; he was released in April 2022.

## Technical mechanism

He's group used CRISPR-Cas9 to attempt to disrupt the CCR5 gene in single-cell-stage human embryos created by in vitro fertilisation, on the rationale that the naturally occurring CCR5-$\Delta$32 allele in humans confers resistance to HIV-1 infection. The editing did not reproduce the natural $\Delta$32 deletion. Instead, the embryos accumulated novel insertions and deletions in CCR5: in Lulu, a 15-base-pair deletion in one allele and a 4-base-pair frame-shifting deletion in the other; in Nana, a 1-base-pair insertion in one allele and an unedited second allele (Musunuru 2019, reconstructing the data presented at the Hong Kong summit). The embryos were also mosaic (different cells in the same embryo carrying different edits), reflecting Cas9 activity that continued after the first cleavage division. The resulting genotypes do not reliably model the natural CCR5-$\Delta$32 resistance allele, do not necessarily confer HIV resistance, and do introduce off-target risk at homologous loci across the genome that were not adequately characterised before implantation. The clinical rationale was also weak: HIV transmission from infected fathers to embryos created by sperm washing is near zero with standard clinical practice, so the children faced no plausible risk of HIV that would justify the intervention.

## Organisational / regulatory mechanism

He's work bypassed Chinese national regulation. The 2003 Ministry of Health Ethical Guiding Principles on Human Embryonic Stem Cell Research explicitly prohibit genetic manipulation of human embryos that will be implanted, and successive guidance from the Chinese Ministry of Science and Technology and from the National Health and Family Planning Commission reaffirmed the prohibition. Local institutional review at the Shenzhen Harmonicare Women's and Children's Hospital approved a research protocol whose ethics-committee documentation, examined after the November 2018 announcement, was found by the Chinese investigation to be partly forged. Patient-consent documents, also examined later, did not adequately describe the experimental nature of the work or the off-target risks. The 2018 announcement was made directly to the media without prior peer review and outside the protocol on file. Three independent reviews followed: the Chinese government investigation (concluded January 2019) found He responsible for violating the Ministry of Health guidance and ordered him barred from research and fertility-clinic practice; the World Health Organization established an Expert Advisory Committee on Developing Global Standards for Governance and Oversight of Human Genome Editing, which issued reports in 2021; and the International Commission on the Clinical Use of Human Germline Genome Editing (a joint US National Academy of Medicine, US National Academy of Sciences, and Royal Society project) issued a 2020 report establishing the consensus that heritable human genome editing should not proceed until standards for safety, efficacy, and societal authorisation have been established.

The mechanism of failure spans three layers. The technical mechanism is that CRISPR-Cas9 was deployed without the validation and target characterisation that the safety profile required; mosaicism, off-target editing, and the failure to reproduce the natural $\Delta$32 allele were predictable and were not adequately addressed. The organisational mechanism is that a single investigator at a single institution can, given access to in vitro fertilisation infrastructure, conduct heritable human-genome editing without national approval, suggesting that the prohibitions then in place were not enforceable against a determined defector. The regulatory mechanism is that international scientific norms (the 2015 Hinxton Group statement, the 2017 NAS report, the 2018 Second Summit's consensus statement) did not have legal force in China and were not adequate to deter the work; the case has since become the canonical example used to argue for binding international standards rather than purely advisory ones.

\textbf{One paragraph}: In November 2018 He Jiankui, then at Southern University of Science and Technology in Shenzhen, announced the birth of twin girls and one subsequent child from human embryos he had edited with CRISPR-Cas9 to disrupt CCR5, the cell-surface co-receptor used by HIV-1. The clinical rationale was that the natural CCR5-$\Delta$32 deletion confers HIV resistance and that the edited children would inherit protection; the rationale was weak because sperm-washing protocols reduce vertical HIV transmission risk to near zero in the clinical setting that the parents faced. The work bypassed Chinese national regulation explicitly prohibiting genetic manipulation of implanted embryos; the ethics-committee documentation was later found to be partly forged. The case has become the canonical example of a normative-consensus-without-enforcement failure in biotechnology governance.
