# de Havilland Comet, 1954

## Date(s) and location

The Comet 1 (G-ALYP) was lost in the Mediterranean Sea near the island of Elba on 10 January 1954, with 35 fatalities. The Comet 1 (G-ALYY) was lost near Naples, Italy on 8 April 1954, with 21 fatalities. The investigations of both accidents were carried out by the Royal Aircraft Establishment (RAE) at Farnborough, with the public reports issued by the Cohen Court of Inquiry in 1955.

## Technical mechanism

The Comet's cabin was pressurised. Each flight cycle subjected the fuselage to one full pressure cycle: from atmospheric at ground level to approximately $0.55\,\si{\bar}$ differential at cruise altitude, and back. The cabin skin saw cyclic stress at this rate. The Royal Aircraft Establishment's investigation, including the famous water-tank fatigue test of an intact Comet airframe with simulated pressure cycles \cite{acc:cohen-comet-report-1955}, identified fatigue-crack initiation at the corners of the rectangular automatic-direction-finder (ADF) antenna cutouts on the cabin roof. The cutouts had been designed with relatively small fillet radii; the corners produced stress concentrations that exceeded the material's fatigue limit under the repeated pressure cycling.

Once a crack initiated, it propagated through the thin aluminium skin during pressure cycling. When the crack length exceeded the critical length for unstable propagation (which depends on the material's fracture toughness and the stress level), the crack ran across the fuselage, the cabin depressurised explosively, and the aircraft disintegrated.

The water-tank test reproduced the failure: after approximately $1\,830$ simulated pressure cycles (against a design life of $10\,000$), the test airframe developed a crack at one of the cutout corners and ruptured \cite{acc:cohen-comet-report-1955}. The number of cycles before failure was consistent with the service histories of the lost aircraft.

## Organisational / regulatory mechanism

The case was the first major demonstration of fatigue as a design-driver for pressurised aircraft. The pre-Comet design culture had treated fatigue primarily as a concern for vibrating components (engine, propeller) rather than for primary structure. The Comet's design and certification had not included fatigue testing of the pressurised cabin at scale; static pressure tests had verified the design ultimate strength but had not exercised the cyclic-load regime.

The post-Comet regulatory response was widespread: the introduction of full-airframe fatigue testing as a certification requirement, the formal recognition of safe-life and (later) damage-tolerant design philosophies, and the establishment of structural-integrity programs across the aviation industry. The Cohen Court of Inquiry's recommendations were rapidly incorporated into UK Civil Aviation Authority and FAA standards.
