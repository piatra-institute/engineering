% the bearing reactions have components in both transverse directions and
% the problem does not reduce to a single plane.

\caption[Shaft on two bearings carrying two pulleys]{A horizontal power
shaft supported on two bearings $A$ and $B$ and carrying two pulleys $C$
and $D$, the worked three-dimensional statics example. The belt pull on
pulley $C$ acts in the vertical ($y$) plane while the belt pull on pulley
$D$ acts in the horizontal ($x$) plane, so the two transverse directions do
not collapse into one and the analysis is genuinely three-dimensional. Each
bearing supplies two transverse reaction components and no moment about the
shaft axis, four reaction unknowns in all, resolved by the four transverse
equilibrium equations (force balance and moment balance in each of the two
transverse planes). The moment balance about the shaft axis is a separate
equation that the driving torque and the belt-tension differences satisfy;
the bearings carry no axial-axis moment, which is why the shaft turns.}
\label{fig:vol03ch02:shaft-bearings}
\end{figure}
