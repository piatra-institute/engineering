\noindent
A beam is a one-dimensional structural element; a plate is the
two-dimensional generalisation, a shell is the curved two-dimensional
generalisation, and three-dimensional elasticity is the parent theory
beneath all three. The chapter develops Kirchhoff-Love plate theory
in the small-deflection limit, the membrane and bending theory of
thin shells, the equations of three-dimensional linear elasticity in
the form the finite-element method (FEM) uses them, the working
discipline of mesh generation and convergence checking, and the
verification, validation, and uncertainty quantification that turn
an FEM output into a load-bearing engineering claim. The failure
section names the mechanisms by which simulation results mislead
engineers who trust them without verifying them.

\section{Plate theory}\pathtag{core}

A \engterm{plate} is a structural element whose thickness is small
compared to its in-plane dimensions and which carries transverse load
primarily by bending. The plate analogue of the Euler-Bernoulli beam
theory of chapter 8 is the Kirchhoff-Love plate theory, due to
Gustav Kirchhoff (1850) and later refined by A.\ E.\ H.\ Love. The
theory rests on three kinematic assumptions, parallel to those of
slender-beam theory:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Straight lines normal to the midsurface before deformation
    remain straight and normal after deformation.
  \item These normals do not change length: the plate's thickness is
    preserved.
  \item The transverse normal stress $\sigma_{zz}$ through the
    thickness is small compared to the in-plane stresses.
\end{itemize}

The first two assumptions, the \engterm{Kirchhoff hypothesis},
neglect transverse shear deformation and are accurate when the
plate's thickness $h$ is small compared to its smallest in-plane
dimension (the working rule is $h \le L/20$). For thicker plates
the Mindlin-Reissner theory (section 9.3) keeps transverse shear and
gives more accurate results.

Under the Kirchhoff hypothesis, the displacement field of a plate
with midsurface deflection $w(x, y)$ is
\[
u(x, y, z) = -z \frac{\partial w}{\partial x}, \quad
v(x, y, z) = -z \frac{\partial w}{\partial y}, \quad
w(x, y, z) = w(x, y),
\]
where $z$ is measured from the midsurface and $(u, v, w)$ are the
displacement components in the $(x, y, z)$ directions. The strains
follow:
\[
\epsilon_{xx} = -z \frac{\partial^2 w}{\partial x^2}, \quad
\epsilon_{yy} = -z \frac{\partial^2 w}{\partial y^2}, \quad
\gamma_{xy} = -2 z \frac{\partial^2 w}{\partial x \partial y},
\]
linear in $z$, as in beams. The in-plane stresses, by Hooke's law
for an isotropic plate in plane stress (Young's modulus $E$,
Poisson's ratio $\nu$), are
\begin{align*}
\sigma_{xx} &= \frac{E}{1 - \nu^2}\left(\epsilon_{xx} + \nu
\epsilon_{yy}\right), \\
\sigma_{yy} &= \frac{E}{1 - \nu^2}\left(\epsilon_{yy} + \nu
\epsilon_{xx}\right), \\
\tau_{xy} &= \frac{E}{2(1+\nu)} \gamma_{xy}.
\end{align*}

The bending moments per unit length of the plate's midsurface are
the first moments of the stresses through the thickness,
\[
M_{xx} = \int_{-h/2}^{h/2} z \sigma_{xx} \,dz, \quad
M_{yy} = \int_{-h/2}^{h/2} z \sigma_{yy} \,dz, \quad
M_{xy} = \int_{-h/2}^{h/2} z \tau_{xy} \,dz.
\]
Performing the integrals,
\[
M_{xx} = -D \left(\frac{\partial^2 w}{\partial x^2} + \nu
\frac{\partial^2 w}{\partial y^2}\right), \quad
M_{yy} = -D \left(\frac{\partial^2 w}{\partial y^2} + \nu
\frac{\partial^2 w}{\partial x^2}\right),
\]
\[
M_{xy} = -D(1-\nu) \frac{\partial^2 w}{\partial x \partial y},
\]
where the constant
\[
D = \frac{E h^3}{12(1 - \nu^2)}
\]
is the plate's \engterm{flexural rigidity}, the two-dimensional
analogue of the beam's $EI$. The cube on thickness is structurally
identical to the cube on depth in the beam: a plate twice as thick
has eight times the flexural rigidity.

Transverse force equilibrium of an infinitesimal plate element,
combined with the moment-curvature relations above, gives the
\engterm{plate bending equation}, the two-dimensional analogue of
the Euler-Bernoulli beam equation $EI \,y'''' = w(x)$:
\[
D \nabla^4 w = q(x, y),
\]
with $\nabla^4 = \nabla^2 \nabla^2$ the biharmonic operator and
$q(x, y)$ the distributed transverse load per unit area. The
equation is a fourth-order linear PDE in two spatial variables; the
boundary conditions on the plate's edge (two conditions per edge)
close the problem.

\input{volumes/03-force/ch09-plates-shells-solid-mechanics/figures/fig-plate-element}

The moment resultants of figure~\ref{fig:vol03ch09:plate-element} are
the bookkeeping device that turns the three-dimensional stress field
into a two-dimensional field of stress couples per unit length. Once
$w(x,y)$ is known, the through-thickness stress at any depth $z$
recovers from the moments by the linear distribution
\[
\sigma_{xx}(x,y,z) = \frac{12 M_{xx}}{h^3}\, z, \qquad
\sigma_{xx}^{\max} = \pm \frac{6 M_{xx}}{h^2}\ \text{at}\ z = \pm h/2,
\]
the plate analogue of the beam flexure formula $\sigma = Mc/I$ with
$c = h/2$ and $I = h^3/12$ per unit width. The surface stress, not the
midsurface stress (which is zero in pure bending), is the quantity to
compare against the material's allowable.

The standard plate boundary conditions, on an edge $x = a$:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Simply supported (pin or roller along the edge):
    $w = 0$, $M_{xx} = 0$. The plate's edge deflection vanishes;
    the moment normal to the edge vanishes.
  \item Clamped (built-in along the edge): $w = 0$, $\partial w/
    \partial x = 0$. The deflection and the slope normal to the
    edge both vanish.
  \item Free: $M_{xx} = 0$ and the Kirchhoff effective shear $V_{xx}
    = \partial M_{xx}/\partial x + 2 \partial M_{xy}/\partial y = 0$.
    The effective shear combines the transverse shear with the
    twisting moment's gradient because the Kirchhoff hypothesis does
    not admit independent twist; see \cite[ch.~2]{
    text:timoshenko-plates-shells} for the derivation.
\end{itemize}

\engterm{Closed-form solutions} exist for a handful of canonical
cases. A simply supported rectangular plate of dimensions $a \times
b$ under uniform load $q_0$ has the Navier double-sine series
solution
\[
w(x, y) = \frac{16 q_0}{\pi^6 D} \sum_{m, n \text{ odd}}
\frac{\sin(m\pi x/a) \sin(n\pi y/b)}{m n \left[(m/a)^2 + (n/b)^2
\right]^2}.
\]
The series converges rapidly; the first term ($m = n = 1$) typically
gives the centre deflection to within a few percent. For a square
plate of side $a$, $w_{\max} \approx 0.00406 q_0 a^4/D$, a useful
working number.

A clamped circular plate of radius $a$ under uniform load $q_0$ has
the axisymmetric closed-form solution
\[
w(r) = \frac{q_0 (a^2 - r^2)^2}{64 D}, \quad
w_{\max} = w(0) = \frac{q_0 a^4}{64 D},
\]
the standard reference case for circular-plate analysis (Timoshenko
\cite[ch.~3, eqn.~62]{text:timoshenko-plates-shells}). The
maximum bending moment occurs at the clamped edge, where
$M_{rr} = -q_0 a^2/8$.

\input{volumes/03-force/ch09-plates-shells-solid-mechanics/figures/fig-circular-plate-deflection}

Figure~\ref{fig:vol03ch09:circular-plate-deflection} contrasts the
clamped and simply supported deflection shapes. The simply supported
circular plate carries the same load with about four times the centre
deflection, because its edge rotates freely instead of being held flat.
The clamped plate pays for its smaller deflection with a large edge
moment, which is why a clamped circular plate of brittle material
tends to crack at the rim, while a simply supported one of the same
material tends to crack at the centre. The collected deflection and
moment coefficients for the canonical plate cases are tabulated in
\repopath{data/plate_coefficients.csv} for quick design reference.

For non-canonical geometries, the working tool is the finite-element
method (section 9.5); the closed-form solutions remain the reference
against which the FEM result is verified.

\begin{principle}[Plates carry transverse load with thickness cubed]
For a thin isotropic plate of thickness $h$ in pure bending, the
flexural rigidity scales as $D = E h^3/[12(1-\nu^2)]$. The thickness
appears cubed because the bending moment per unit length is the
first moment of the linearly varying stress through the thickness,
and the integral picks up an additional factor of $h$ from the
moment arm. Doubling the plate's thickness multiplies its bending
stiffness by eight; halving the thickness divides the stiffness by
eight. The thickness is therefore the most powerful parameter in
plate design, exactly as the depth is the most powerful parameter
in beam design.
\end{principle}

\section{Shell structures}\pathtag{standard}

A \engterm{shell} is a curved plate: a thin structural element whose
midsurface is a curved surface and which carries load by both
bending and membrane action. The fundamental difference between a
plate and a shell is that a shell can carry transverse load by
in-plane (membrane) stresses, with bending playing only a secondary
role. An eggshell carries the weight of a hen by membrane
compression; a folded paper carries far more transverse load than a
flat paper because the folds give it shell-like geometry.

For a thin shell of midsurface radius $R$ and thickness $h$, with
$h/R \ll 1$, the stress state at any point is decomposed into
membrane stresses (independent of $z$ through the thickness, in
equilibrium with the applied load) and bending stresses (linear in
$z$, vanishing on the midsurface). The decomposition is exact for
the linear-elastic shell.

\engterm{Membrane theory} treats the shell as a curved surface that
carries only in-plane stress resultants, $N_{xx}, N_{yy}, N_{xy}$
(forces per unit length of midsurface), with no bending. For a
shell of revolution under axisymmetric load (a pressure vessel, a
water tower, a dome), membrane theory gives the meridional and
circumferential stress resultants directly from force equilibrium
on a strip of the shell. The standard examples:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \engterm{Thin spherical pressure vessel} of radius $R$,
    thickness $h$, internal gauge pressure $p$:
    \[
    \sigma_{\text{meridional}} = \sigma_{\text{circumferential}} =
    \frac{pR}{2h}.
    \]
    Both principal membrane stresses are equal; the sphere is the
    most efficient pressure-containing geometry.
  \item \engterm{Thin cylindrical pressure vessel} of radius $R$,
    thickness $h$, length $L \gg R$, internal gauge pressure $p$:
    \[
    \sigma_{\text{circumferential (hoop)}} = \frac{pR}{h}, \quad
    \sigma_{\text{axial}} = \frac{pR}{2h}.
    \]
    The hoop stress is twice the axial stress; the cylinder is
    twice as efficient in axial as in hoop, and this is why
    pressure-vessel design uses the hoop stress as the governing
    quantity.
  \item \engterm{Conical shell} of half-angle $\alpha$ under
    internal pressure $p$, at distance $s$ along the slant from the
    apex:
    \[
    \sigma_{\text{circumferential}} = \frac{p s \tan\alpha}{h},
    \quad \sigma_{\text{meridional}} = \frac{p s \tan\alpha}{2 h}.
    \]
    The stresses grow linearly with distance from the apex; the
    cone is therefore most heavily stressed at its base.
\end{itemize}

\input{volumes/03-force/ch09-plates-shells-solid-mechanics/figures/fig-cylinder-stresses}

\input{volumes/03-force/ch09-plates-shells-solid-mechanics/figures/fig-vessel-efficiency}

The three membrane results above share a single algebraic skeleton:
the membrane stress is the product of the pressure, a length scale set
by the local radius of curvature, and the reciprocal of the wall
thickness. The sphere splits the load between two equal principal
curvatures and so carries it at half the stress of the cylinder, whose
hoop direction has finite curvature while the axial direction is
straight. Figure~\ref{fig:vol03ch09:vessel-efficiency} turns this into
the design statement an engineer uses: for the same radius and
allowable stress, a sphere needs half the wall thickness of a
cylinder. The membrane sizing and its inversion to a required wall
thickness are implemented in \repopath{code/pressure_vessel.py}.

The membrane-theory result is exact within the membrane hypothesis
(no bending, no transverse shear, no thickness through-stress) and
is accurate for thin shells away from edge constraints, point loads,
or geometric discontinuities. Near such locations, bending stresses
that are not small compared to the membrane stresses arise as a
\engterm{boundary layer} of bending in the shell. The bending decays
away from the discontinuity over a length of order $\sqrt{Rh}$, narrow
for a thin shell, and inside that band it can reach a sizeable fraction
of the membrane stress (figure~\ref{fig:vol03ch09:discontinuity-stress};
the governing equation and its damped-oscillator structure are derived
in the mastery box below). Every welded junction, supported edge, and
nozzle in a real vessel carries this discontinuity stress, which is why
the membrane sizing of a vessel is necessary but never sufficient. The
working tool for the full boundary-layer analysis is the
Donnell-Mushtari-Vlasov equation set, beyond the scope of this chapter
(see \cite[ch.~14]{text:timoshenko-plates-shells}).

\input{volumes/03-force/ch09-plates-shells-solid-mechanics/figures/fig-discontinuity-stress}

\engterm{Shell buckling}. A thin shell in compression buckles at a
load far below the equivalent flat-plate buckling load and far below
the prediction of linear shell-buckling theory. The classical Euler
buckling result for a thin spherical shell of radius $R$ under
external pressure is
\[
p_{\text{cr}} = \frac{2 E h^2}{R^2 \sqrt{3(1-\nu^2)}},
\]
but experiment shows that real spherical shells buckle at $20$ to
$40\,\%$ of this value because of geometric imperfections. A small
flat spot, a small dimple, or a small out-of-roundness reduces the
buckling load substantially. The companion result for a long
cylindrical shell under uniform external pressure, the case of a
submarine hull or a vacuum-loaded tank between widely spaced
stiffeners, is the ring-collapse formula
\[
p_{\text{cr}} = \frac{E h^3}{4(1-\nu^2) R^3},
\]
which follows from treating a unit-length ring of the cylinder as an
Euler ring and which falls off as the cube of the thinness ratio
$h/R$. A cylinder twice as thin (at fixed radius) loses a factor of
eight in external-pressure capacity, the same thickness-cubed scaling
that governs plate bending. The same imperfection knock-down applies:
the working capacity is a fraction of this linear value, and the
design adds ring stiffeners to shorten the unsupported length when the
bare shell cannot carry the external pressure (worked example in the
exercises, and the external-pressure step of the air-receiver case
study below). The shell-buckling literature calls
this the \engterm{imperfection sensitivity} of shells, and it is the
single most important practical issue in shell design. Design
codes for shells (ASME Boiler and Pressure Vessel Code, Section VIII)
use reduced buckling capacities calibrated to extensive test data;
no engineer designs a shell against its linear-theory buckling load.

\input{volumes/03-force/ch09-plates-shells-solid-mechanics/figures/fig-shell-buckling}

The contrast with the flat plate is the instructive one.
A flat plate in compression buckles close to its linear-theory load,
within ten or twenty percent, because the post-buckling path is stable
and small imperfections only nudge the result. A shell buckles on an
unstable post-buckling path, where the first dimple relieves the
membrane stress that was holding the neighbouring material, which
dimples in turn. The result is the steep knock-down of
figure~\ref{fig:vol03ch09:shell-buckling} and the worked design
factors collected in \repopath{data/shell_knockdown.csv}: the axially
compressed cylinder, the most sensitive case, is designed to roughly
one fifth of its classical load.

\engterm{The Brazier effect}. A thin-walled tube under bending
ovalises: the originally circular cross-section flattens into an
ellipse at the section of maximum bending moment, because the
compression-side fibres push inward while the tension-side fibres
pull outward. The ovalisation reduces the section's effective
bending stiffness and triggers a nonlinear failure mode at a moment
significantly below the linear prediction. Brazier (1927) gave the
classical analysis; modern thin-tube design (bicycle frames, aircraft
fuselages, oil pipelines) accounts for the Brazier effect in the
design rules for tube wall thickness.

\begin{mastery}
\textbf{The shell bending boundary layer.} Membrane theory satisfies
force equilibrium but cannot, on its own, satisfy the displacement
conditions at an edge, a support, or a junction between two shells of
different stiffness. A spherical head welded to a cylindrical shell is
the standard case: under pressure the two free shells want to grow
radially by different amounts (worked example 8), and the weld forces
them to share an edge. The shell reconciles the mismatch by developing
a self-equilibrating set of edge shears and moments that decay away
from the discontinuity. Linearising the axisymmetric shell equations
about the membrane state gives, for the radial displacement
perturbation $w$, an equation of the damped-oscillator form
\[
\frac{d^4 w}{dx^4} + 4\beta^4 w = 0, \qquad
\beta^4 = \frac{3(1-\nu^2)}{R^2 h^2},
\]
whose decaying solutions are $w(x) = e^{-\beta x}(C_1\cos\beta x +
C_2\sin\beta x)$. The structure is identical to the beam on an elastic
foundation of chapter 8: the membrane stiffness of the shell plays the
role of the foundation, and the bending stiffness plays the role of the
beam. The decay length $1/\beta$ scales as $\sqrt{R h}$, so the bending
boundary layer is narrow for a thin shell, a few times $\sqrt{Rh}$
wide. Inside that band the bending stress can reach a sizeable fraction
of the membrane stress and must be added to it; outside it the membrane
solution is accurate. The practical consequences run through every
pressure-vessel detail: the knuckle radius of a torispherical head, the
thickness taper at a nozzle, the reinforcing ring at a support saddle,
each exists to keep the boundary-layer stress within the allowable. The
same mathematics governs the edge of a long cylindrical tank under
hydrostatic load and the junction of a chimney to its foundation. A
finite-element model resolves the boundary layer only if the mesh
places several elements within one decay length of the discontinuity;
a mesh coarser than $\sqrt{Rh}$ at a junction reports the membrane
stress and misses the very stress that governs the detail.
\end{mastery}

\section{Three-dimensional elasticity and the FEM equations}\pathtag{standard}

The Kirchhoff plate theory and the membrane shell theory are
\engterm{reduced models}: they collapse one or more spatial
dimensions by imposing kinematic assumptions on the displacement
field. The full \engterm{three-dimensional linear elasticity}
equations make no such assumption; they govern the displacement
field $\vec{u}(x, y, z) = u\hat{\imath} + v\hat{\jmath} + w\hat{k}$
of an arbitrary solid body under load.

The three governing equation sets, in compact tensor notation:

\engterm{Kinematics} (strain-displacement, infinitesimal-strain
limit):
\[
\epsilon_{ij} = \tfrac{1}{2}\left(\frac{\partial u_i}{\partial x_j}
+ \frac{\partial u_j}{\partial x_i}\right).
\]
The strain tensor $\epsilon_{ij}$ is symmetric ($\epsilon_{ij} =
\epsilon_{ji}$) and has six independent components in three
dimensions; the relation to displacement is the same kinematic
identity introduced in chapter 3 of this volume.

\input{volumes/03-force/ch09-plates-shells-solid-mechanics/figures/fig-stress-element-3d}

The companion object is the stress tensor of
figure~\ref{fig:vol03ch09:stress-element-3d}, also symmetric and also
six-component. The symmetry $\sigma_{ij} = \sigma_{ji}$ is not an
assumption; it is the statement of moment equilibrium of the
infinitesimal element, an unbalanced shear couple would give the
element an infinite angular acceleration. Six strains, six stresses,
three displacements: these fifteen fields at every point of the body,
tied by the six kinematic relations, the six constitutive relations,
and the three equilibrium equations, are exactly the unknowns and
equations the finite-element method discretises.

\engterm{Constitutive law} (Hooke's law, isotropic linear-elastic
material):
\[
\sigma_{ij} = \lambda \delta_{ij} \epsilon_{kk} + 2 \mu
\epsilon_{ij},
\]
with $\lambda, \mu$ the Lam{\'e} parameters ($\mu$ also the shear
modulus $G$, and $\lambda + 2\mu/3 = K$ the bulk modulus). The
material constants are two: any pair of $(E, \nu)$, $(E, G)$,
$(K, G)$, or $(\lambda, \mu)$ uniquely determines the others by
algebraic identities. Representative values of $E$, $\nu$, $G$, yield
strength, and density for the materials this chapter uses are
collected in \repopath{data/elastic_constants.csv}.

\engterm{Equilibrium} (Cauchy stress, no body force besides
gravity):
\[
\frac{\partial \sigma_{ij}}{\partial x_j} + \rho g_i = 0,
\]
with $\rho$ the mass density and $g_i$ the gravitational
acceleration. The equation is the three-dimensional analogue of
$dM/dx = V$, $dV/dx = -w$ in beams: equilibrium of an
infinitesimal element.

The boundary conditions on a body of volume $\Omega$ with boundary
$\partial\Omega$ are imposed in two parts:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \engterm{Displacement (Dirichlet) boundary conditions} on
    $\Gamma_u \subset \partial\Omega$: $\vec{u} = \vec{u}_0$ on
    $\Gamma_u$. The body is held in place along that part of the
    boundary.
  \item \engterm{Traction (Neumann) boundary conditions} on
    $\Gamma_t = \partial\Omega \setminus \Gamma_u$: $\sigma_{ij}
    n_j = t_i$ on $\Gamma_t$, with $n_j$ the outward unit normal
    and $t_i$ the applied surface traction.
\end{itemize}

The displacement and traction boundary conditions together
constitute the \engterm{boundary-value problem} of elasticity. The
problem is well-posed (existence and uniqueness of the solution)
under mild regularity conditions on the geometry, loads, and
material constants.

\engterm{Plane stress and plane strain}. Two two-dimensional
reductions of the three-dimensional elasticity problem are widely
used.

\emph{Plane stress} applies to thin in-plane-loaded plates, where
the through-thickness stresses are negligible: $\sigma_{zz} =
\sigma_{xz} = \sigma_{yz} = 0$. The displacement is in the $(x, y)$
plane, with the through-thickness strain $\epsilon_{zz}$ determined
by the constitutive equations.

\emph{Plane strain} applies to long prismatic bodies loaded
transversely, where the through-thickness displacement is
constrained: $\epsilon_{zz} = \epsilon_{xz} = \epsilon_{yz} = 0$.
A long pipe under internal pressure (away from the ends), a tunnel
in soil, a long retaining wall: all are plane strain.

The two reductions differ in their effective elastic constants. For
plane stress, $E^* = E$, $\nu^* = \nu$. For plane strain, $E^* =
E/(1 - \nu^2)$, $\nu^* = \nu/(1-\nu)$. The two-dimensional
biharmonic equation $\nabla^4 \phi = 0$ for the Airy stress
function $\phi$ (defined by $\sigma_{xx} = \partial^2\phi/\partial
y^2$, $\sigma_{yy} = \partial^2\phi/\partial x^2$, $\sigma_{xy} =
-\partial^2\phi/\partial x\partial y$) governs both cases; the
difference is in the boundary conditions and in the recovery of
the through-thickness stresses or strains.

\engterm{The variational principle}. The boundary-value problem of
linear elasticity is equivalent to the minimisation of the
\engterm{total potential energy}
\[
\Pi[\vec{u}] = \int_\Omega \tfrac{1}{2} \sigma_{ij} \epsilon_{ij}
\,dV - \int_\Omega \rho g_i u_i \,dV - \int_{\Gamma_t} t_i u_i \,dA
\]
over all kinematically admissible displacement fields (those that
satisfy the displacement boundary conditions). The displacement
field that minimises $\Pi$ is the unique solution to the
boundary-value problem. The variational formulation is the
foundation of the finite-element method, which discretises the
admissible-displacement space and finds the minimum on the
discretised space.

\subsection{From stress tensor to a yield check}

A solved elasticity problem returns the stress tensor at every point.
The design question is whether that multiaxial stress state will yield
the material, and the answer requires collapsing the six stress
components into a single scalar that can be compared against the
uniaxial yield strength $\sigma_Y$ measured in a tension test. The
two criteria in working use for ductile metals are von Mises and
Tresca.

The \engterm{von Mises criterion} states that yield begins when the
distortion energy reaches the value it has at uniaxial yield. In terms
of the principal stresses $\sigma_1, \sigma_2, \sigma_3$,
\[
\sigma_{\text{vm}} = \sqrt{\tfrac{1}{2}\left[(\sigma_1-\sigma_2)^2 +
(\sigma_2-\sigma_3)^2 + (\sigma_3-\sigma_1)^2\right]},
\]
and yield occurs when $\sigma_{\text{vm}} = \sigma_Y$. In terms of the
full stress tensor the same quantity is
\[
\sigma_{\text{vm}} = \sqrt{\tfrac{1}{2}\left[(\sigma_{xx}-\sigma_{yy})^2
+ (\sigma_{yy}-\sigma_{zz})^2 + (\sigma_{zz}-\sigma_{xx})^2\right]
+ 3\left(\tau_{xy}^2 + \tau_{yz}^2 + \tau_{zx}^2\right)}.
\]
The von Mises stress is the quantity a finite-element post-processor
plots as its default stress contour; the von Mises computation, with a
plane-stress specialisation, is implemented in
\repopath{code/von_mises.py}.

The \engterm{Tresca criterion} states that yield begins when the
maximum shear stress reaches the value at uniaxial yield, $\tau_{\max}
= (\sigma_{\max} - \sigma_{\min})/2 = \sigma_Y/2$. Tresca is the more
conservative of the two; its hexagon is inscribed in the von Mises
ellipse.

\input{volumes/03-force/ch09-plates-shells-solid-mechanics/figures/fig-von-mises-envelope}

Figure~\ref{fig:vol03ch09:von-mises-envelope} plots both envelopes in
the plane of the two in-plane principal stresses. The largest gap
between them, a factor of $2/\sqrt{3} \approx 1.155$, occurs in pure
shear; for most biaxial states the two criteria agree to within ten
percent. An engineer reading an FEM von Mises plot is reading the
distance of the stress state from the boundary of this ellipse.

\section{Anisotropy preview}\pathtag{mastery}

The isotropic constitutive law $\sigma_{ij} = \lambda \delta_{ij}
\epsilon_{kk} + 2\mu \epsilon_{ij}$ assumes that the material's
elastic response is independent of orientation. The assumption
fails for many engineering materials: wood (orthotropic), composite
laminates (orthotropic at the lamina, more general at the
laminate), single crystals (cubic, hexagonal, or lower symmetry),
rolled steel (mild orthotropy from rolling texture), and biological
tissues (anisotropy that varies with loading history).

The general linear-elastic constitutive law for an anisotropic
material has the form
\[
\sigma_{ij} = C_{ijkl} \epsilon_{kl},
\]
where $C_{ijkl}$ is the fourth-rank elasticity tensor with
$3^4 = 81$ components. The symmetries $\sigma_{ij} = \sigma_{ji}$,
$\epsilon_{kl} = \epsilon_{lk}$, and (from the existence of a
strain-energy potential) $C_{ijkl} = C_{klij}$ reduce the number
of independent components to $21$ in the most general triclinic
case. Material symmetries further reduce the count: orthotropic
materials have $9$ independent constants; tetragonal have $6$;
hexagonal (or transversely isotropic) have $5$; cubic have $3$;
isotropic have $2$.

\engterm{Wood} is approximately orthotropic with three principal
directions: longitudinal (along the grain), radial, and tangential
to the growth rings. The longitudinal Young's modulus exceeds the
radial and tangential moduli by factors of $20$ or more; the shear
moduli are smaller again. The structural engineer who designs in
wood designs in the orthotropic frame and respects the
grain-direction-dependent allowable stresses (NDS for the United
States, Eurocode 5 for the European Union).

\engterm{Composite laminates} are constructed from layers of
unidirectional fibre-reinforced material stacked at chosen
orientations. A typical carbon-fibre-epoxy lamina has
longitudinal (fibre-direction) Young's modulus of $130$ to $200\,
\text{GPa}$, transverse Young's modulus of $7$ to $10\,\text{GPa}$,
in-plane shear modulus of $4$ to $7\,\text{GPa}$, and major
Poisson's ratio of $0.25$ to $0.35$. The laminate's effective
elastic properties depend on the stacking sequence, layer
thicknesses, and ply orientations; the working tool is classical
laminate plate theory, which assembles the laminate stiffness
matrix from the lamina stiffness matrices and the ply-orientation
rotations.

The design discipline for anisotropic materials starts with the
correct material model. An isotropic FEM analysis of a composite
laminate, or a wooden beam loaded across the grain analysed as
isotropic, gives wrong stresses by factors that can exceed two and
unsafe predictions of failure. Composite design is the subject of
Volume V chapters 11 and 12; the present chapter notes only that
the FEM machinery extends to anisotropic materials by replacing
the isotropic elasticity tensor with the anisotropic tensor,
nothing more.

\begin{mastery}
\textbf{The elasticity tensor in Voigt notation.} The fourth-rank
tensor $C_{ijkl}$ with its $21$ independent constants is awkward to
carry around, so engineering practice flattens the symmetric stress
and strain tensors into six-vectors and writes the constitutive law as
a $6\times 6$ symmetric matrix. The \engterm{Voigt convention} maps the
index pairs $(11,22,33,23,31,12) \to (1,2,3,4,5,6)$, so that
\[
\begin{pmatrix} \sigma_1 \\ \sigma_2 \\ \sigma_3 \\ \sigma_4 \\
\sigma_5 \\ \sigma_6 \end{pmatrix}
= \begin{pmatrix}
C_{11} & C_{12} & C_{13} & C_{14} & C_{15} & C_{16} \\
       & C_{22} & C_{23} & C_{24} & C_{25} & C_{26} \\
       &        & C_{33} & C_{34} & C_{35} & C_{36} \\
       &        &        & C_{44} & C_{45} & C_{46} \\
       &        &        &        & C_{55} & C_{56} \\
\text{sym} & & & & & C_{66}
\end{pmatrix}
\begin{pmatrix} \epsilon_1 \\ \epsilon_2 \\ \epsilon_3 \\
\epsilon_4 \\ \epsilon_5 \\ \epsilon_6 \end{pmatrix},
\]
where the shear strains in the strain six-vector are the engineering
shears $\gamma_{ij} = 2\epsilon_{ij}$. The matrix has $21$ independent
entries in the triclinic case. For an orthotropic material aligned with
the coordinate axes, the off-diagonal shear couplings $C_{14}$ through
$C_{56}$ vanish and the upper-left $3\times 3$ block plus three
diagonal shear terms leave $9$ constants. For isotropy the matrix
collapses to two: $C_{11} = C_{22} = C_{33} = \lambda + 2\mu$,
$C_{12} = C_{13} = C_{23} = \lambda$, $C_{44} = C_{55} = C_{66} = \mu$.
Every commercial finite-element code stores the material as this
$6\times 6$ matrix; choosing the right one for the material at hand is
the single most consequential material-model decision, because an
isotropic matrix applied to a composite laminate is wrong by the ratio
of the longitudinal to transverse modulus, a factor that often exceeds
ten.
\end{mastery}

\section{Finite-element method: introduction}\pathtag{core}

The \engterm{finite-element method} (FEM) is a numerical method
for solving partial differential equations on arbitrary geometries.
For linear elasticity, the FEM discretises the domain into
elements, approximates the displacement field within each element
as a low-order polynomial in the element's local coordinates,
assembles the global stiffness matrix from the elements, and
solves the resulting linear system for the nodal displacements.
The method was developed in the 1950s and 1960s for aerospace
structural analysis (Turner, Clough, Martin, Topp, 1956) and has
become the dominant analytical tool for structural mechanics
across every engineering discipline. The standard engineering
reference is Zienkiewicz, Taylor, and Zhu
\cite[chs.~1--3]{text:zienkiewicz-fem}.

The method's working steps, in the order an engineer performs
them:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item \engterm{Discretise the geometry.} Divide the body into
    elements: triangles or quadrilaterals in two dimensions;
    tetrahedra, hexahedra, prisms, or pyramids in three dimensions.
    The collection of elements is the \engterm{mesh}; the points at
    which elements connect are the \engterm{nodes}.
  \item \engterm{Choose the element type.} The element type fixes
    the polynomial order of the displacement field within the
    element. Linear (first-order) elements have nodes only at
    corners; quadratic (second-order) elements have additional
    nodes at edge midpoints. Higher-order elements give more
    accuracy per element but more computation per element.
  \item \engterm{Assemble the global stiffness matrix.} Each
    element contributes a small stiffness matrix $K_e$ (typically
    $6 \times 6$ to $30 \times 30$ depending on element type and
    dimension) that relates the element's nodal forces to its
    nodal displacements. The global stiffness matrix $K$ is
    assembled by summing the element contributions at the shared
    nodes; the result is a large sparse symmetric positive
    semidefinite matrix.
  \item \engterm{Impose boundary conditions.} Displacement
    boundary conditions are imposed by removing the corresponding
    rows and columns of $K$ and the corresponding entries of the
    force vector $\vec{F}$. Traction boundary conditions become
    nodal forces by integrating the traction over the element
    boundary.
  \item \engterm{Solve the linear system} $K \vec{u} = \vec{F}$
    for the nodal displacements $\vec{u}$. The solver is either
    direct (sparse Cholesky factorisation) for problems up to
    perhaps $10^7$ degrees of freedom, or iterative (conjugate
    gradient with preconditioning) for larger problems.
  \item \engterm{Recover element stresses and strains} from the
    nodal displacements by differentiating the element's
    interpolated displacement field. The stresses are typically
    most accurate at the Gauss points (interior integration
    points) of the element and less accurate at the nodes and
    edges.
\end{enumerate}

The method is general: the same six steps work for plates, shells,
three-dimensional solids, plane stress, plane strain, axisymmetric
problems, linear and nonlinear materials, static and dynamic loads.
Every commercial finite-element package (Abaqus, ANSYS, NASTRAN,
LS-DYNA, COMSOL, the open-source Code\_Aster and CalculiX) implements
the same machinery with different element libraries and different
solvers. A teaching-grade implementation of the six steps for
plane-stress problems with linear triangular elements, short enough to
read in one sitting, is in \repopath{code/cst_fem.py}; running it on
its own patch test reproduces the analytical uniaxial strain
$\epsilon = \sigma/E$ and makes the assembly-and-solve loop concrete
before any commercial package hides it.

\engterm{Convergence}. The FEM solution depends on the mesh: refining
the mesh changes the answer, and a well-posed problem produces a
sequence of FEM solutions that converges to the exact solution as
the mesh is refined. The standard refinement strategies:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \engterm{h-refinement}: keep the element order fixed, reduce
    the element size. The error decreases as $h^p$, where $p$ is
    the polynomial order of the element and $h$ is the
    characteristic element size.
  \item \engterm{p-refinement}: keep the mesh fixed, increase the
    element order. The error decreases exponentially in $p$ for
    smooth solutions.
  \item \engterm{hp-refinement}: refine the mesh near features
    (corners, point loads, material discontinuities) and increase
    the element order in smooth regions. The combination gives the
    best convergence rate but requires more sophisticated mesh
    management.
\end{itemize}

\engterm{Mesh-convergence study}. A working FEM analysis is not a
single run on a single mesh; it is a sequence of runs on progressively
refined meshes, with the engineer verifying that the quantity of
interest (peak stress, maximum deflection, natural frequency) is
converging to a stable value as the mesh is refined. A typical study
runs the analysis on three or four meshes with element-size ratios
of $2:1$, plots the quantity of interest against the inverse element
size, and extrapolates to zero element size by Richardson
extrapolation. A result that has not been checked against mesh
convergence is a result that depends on the mesh, and is therefore
not a result.

\begin{mastery}
\textbf{Where the element stiffness matrix comes from.} The element
stiffness matrix $K_e$ is not a black box; it is the discretised form
of the variational principle. Within an element, the displacement field
is interpolated from the nodal displacements $\vec{d}_e$ by the
\engterm{shape functions} $N_a(\vec{x})$,
\[
\vec{u}(\vec{x}) = \sum_a N_a(\vec{x})\,\vec{d}_{e,a}
= \mathbf{N}(\vec{x})\,\vec{d}_e .
\]
Differentiating gives the strain in terms of the nodal displacements
through the \engterm{strain-displacement matrix} $\mathbf{B} =
\partial \mathbf{N}$, so $\boldsymbol{\epsilon} = \mathbf{B}\,
\vec{d}_e$. Substituting into the strain energy
$\tfrac{1}{2}\int_{\Omega_e} \boldsymbol{\epsilon}^{\mathsf T}
\mathbf{D}\,\boldsymbol{\epsilon}\,dV$ and minimising over $\vec{d}_e$
yields
\[
K_e = \int_{\Omega_e} \mathbf{B}^{\mathsf T}\,\mathbf{D}\,\mathbf{B}
\,dV ,
\]
with $\mathbf{D}$ the material matrix above. For a constant-strain
triangle, $\mathbf{B}$ is constant over the element and the integral is
just $\mathbf{B}^{\mathsf T}\mathbf{D}\,\mathbf{B}$ times the element
area and thickness, which is exactly the arithmetic implemented in
\repopath{code/cst_fem.py}. For higher-order elements $\mathbf{B}$
varies over the element and the integral is evaluated numerically at
Gauss points, which is why element stresses are most accurate there.
Assembly is the statement that the displacement is continuous across
element boundaries: the global $K$ is the sum of element $K_e$ with
shared nodes overlapped. Every commercial code does precisely this; the
sophistication is in the element library ($\mathbf{B}$ formulations
that avoid locking) and the sparse solver, not in the recipe.
\end{mastery}

\begin{archetype}[Stability]
The archetype of plate, shell, and three-dimensional elasticity is
\emph{stability}: the load capacity of the structure is set not by
material strength but by the geometric stability of the loaded
configuration. A thin spherical shell buckles at $20\,\%$ of its
linear-theory critical load because a small dimple destabilises
the membrane equilibrium. A thin-walled tube under bending
ovalises and triggers the Brazier failure at a moment below the
linear prediction. A plate under in-plane compression buckles
out-of-plane at a load far below the material's yield. The
discipline of stability-dominated design is to compute not only
the static stress state but also the bifurcation point of the
loaded equilibrium, and to design with a stability margin large
enough to absorb the imperfections that real structures carry.
\end{archetype}

\section{Mesh quality and the engineer's responsibility}\pathtag{core}

A finite-element solution is only as good as the mesh on which it
is computed. A poor mesh produces wrong answers, and the wrongness
is not signalled by the solver: the analysis runs, the colours on
the screen look reassuring, and the numbers are wrong. The
engineer's responsibility is to verify the mesh before trusting
the result.

The diagnostics of mesh quality, in order of importance:

\engterm{Element aspect ratio.} The ratio of the element's longest
to its shortest edge. For linear tetrahedra and triangles, aspect
ratios above $\sim 5$ produce unreliable stress recovery;
ratios above $\sim 20$ produce wrong results. The mesh generator
must control aspect ratio everywhere, including near boundaries
and geometric features where automatic mesh refinement may
inadvertently produce thin slivers.

\engterm{Element skewness.} The deviation of the element's
internal angles from their ideal values ($60^{\circ}$ for
equilateral triangles, $90^{\circ}$ for quadrilaterals, the
corresponding values for three-dimensional elements). Skewed
elements have poorly conditioned stiffness matrices and produce
inaccurate stresses. The working rule is to keep all interior
angles within $\pm 30^{\circ}$ of the ideal.

\engterm{Element warpage} (for quadrilateral and hexahedral
elements). A quadrilateral element whose four corner nodes are
not coplanar is warped; the warpage produces an additional
mode in the element's stiffness matrix that distorts the
displacement field. The working rule is to keep the warpage
below $5\,\%$ of the element's characteristic size.

\engterm{Mesh refinement near stress concentrations.} A hole, a
sharp corner, a material discontinuity, a point load, or any
location of high stress gradient requires fine mesh refinement
locally. A coarse mesh at a stress concentration under-predicts
the peak stress by factors that can exceed two; an FEM analysis
that misses a stress concentration is an FEM analysis that
predicts a non-failure for a member that will fail.

\engterm{Convergence of the quantity of interest.} The peak stress
at a stress concentration may converge slowly with mesh
refinement, or it may not converge at all if the geometry has a
re-entrant corner (a singular stress field). The engineer must
verify convergence on the quantity of interest, not on any
quantity. A converged maximum-deflection result is not a
converged maximum-stress result; the two quantities have different
convergence rates and may require different meshes.

\engterm{Material-discontinuity meshing.} Elements should not span
material discontinuities. A composite laminate is meshed with
separate element layers for each ply; a steel-concrete composite
beam is meshed with separate elements for the steel and the
concrete, connected at the interface by either continuous
displacement or a contact-mechanics interface.

\engterm{Element selection.} For thin structures (plates, shells)
the engineer uses shell elements rather than solid elements,
because solid elements with high aspect ratio in the thickness
direction are prone to \engterm{shear locking}, a numerical
artifact in which the element's bending stiffness is overestimated
by a factor that can exceed ten. The shell element formulation
handles the thin-structure kinematics correctly. Similarly, for
nearly incompressible materials ($\nu \to 0.5$, as in rubber or
nearly-saturated soil), the engineer uses elements formulated with
mixed displacement-pressure interpolation to avoid
\engterm{volumetric locking}.

\input{volumes/03-force/ch09-plates-shells-solid-mechanics/figures/fig-shear-locking}

Figure~\ref{fig:vol03ch09:shear-locking} shows the mechanism of shear
locking, the most common way a beginner's solid-element model of a
thin part comes back far too stiff. A single linear element through the
thickness has straight edges and cannot bend; it manufactures a
parasitic transverse shear strain that soaks up energy and reports a
deflection that can be a half or a third of the truth. The diagnosis
is to compare against the analytical Kirchhoff prediction, the cure is
shell elements or a stack of quadratic elements.

\engterm{The mesh-generation workflow.} A working FEM analyst
proceeds:

\begin{enumerate}
  \item Generate a first coarse mesh on the entire domain, with
    element size comparable to the smallest geometric feature.
  \item Refine locally near stress concentrations, geometric
    discontinuities, and material interfaces.
  \item Check element quality (aspect ratio, skewness, warpage)
    against the analysis software's quality metrics. Repair
    poor-quality elements either manually or by remeshing.
  \item Run the analysis on the first mesh; record the quantity
    of interest.
  \item Refine the mesh by a factor of two; rerun; record the
    quantity of interest.
  \item Compare the two runs. If they agree to within the
    required tolerance, the analysis is converged; if not,
    refine again. Continue until convergence is confirmed.
\end{enumerate}

A finite-element analysis whose mesh-convergence study is not in
the report is not a finite-element analysis whose results can be
trusted.

\section{Verification, validation, uncertainty quantification}\pathtag{standard}

A finite-element analysis produces numerical answers. Whether
those numerical answers correspond to the physical system the
engineer wishes to predict is a separate question, addressed by
the disciplines of \engterm{verification}, \engterm{validation},
and \engterm{uncertainty quantification} (VVUQ).

\engterm{Verification} asks: does the simulation solve the
mathematical model correctly? It is the discipline of confirming
that the software, the mesh, the solver, and the numerical
parameters together produce the solution of the boundary-value
problem the engineer intended to solve. Verification is internal
to the simulation; it does not require any comparison with physical
reality. The standard verification tools:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \engterm{Code verification}. Run the software on test
    problems with known analytical solutions and confirm that the
    numerical solution converges to the analytical solution at
    the expected rate. Commercial codes are verified by their
    vendors against benchmark suites; the engineer should be
    aware of the verified element types and physical models in
    use.
  \item \engterm{Calculation verification}. Run a mesh-convergence
    study (section 9.6) on the actual problem. Confirm that the
    quantity of interest converges to a stable value as the mesh
    is refined.
  \item \engterm{Order-of-accuracy verification}. Confirm that the
    convergence rate matches the theoretical rate for the element
    type. A linear element should give $O(h^2)$ convergence in
    energy norm; a quadratic element should give $O(h^4)$. A
    convergence rate below the theoretical rate signals a problem
    (mesh quality, material model, boundary condition
    implementation).
\end{itemize}

\engterm{Validation} asks: does the mathematical model describe
the physical system? It is the discipline of comparing the
simulation result to experimental measurement on the physical
system. Validation is external to the simulation; it requires
physical data. The standard validation methods:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \engterm{Validation against a controlled test}. A simple
    component is tested under controlled load; the test measures
    deflection, strain at instrumented points, and failure load.
    The simulation predicts the same quantities; the agreement
    (or disagreement) is recorded.
  \item \engterm{Validation hierarchy}. A complex system is
    validated by validating subcomponents at increasing levels of
    complexity: material coupons, structural elements, sub-
    assemblies, and finally the full system. The hierarchy gives
    confidence that the simulation captures the relevant physics
    at every scale.
  \item \engterm{Validation under representative loading}. The
    validation test loads the component in conditions that
    represent the intended service: temperature range, strain
    rate, environment. A validation under static room-temperature
    conditions does not validate the simulation for use at high
    temperature or under impact loading.
\end{itemize}

\engterm{Uncertainty quantification} asks: how much do the inputs
vary, and how much does the output vary in response? It is the
discipline of propagating input uncertainties (material constants,
geometric tolerances, load magnitudes) through the simulation to
produce a distribution of outputs. The working tools:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \engterm{Sensitivity analysis}. Compute the partial
    derivatives of the quantity of interest with respect to each
    input. The largest derivatives identify the inputs that most
    affect the output.
  \item \engterm{Monte Carlo sampling}. Sample the input
    distributions, run the simulation for each sample, and
    accumulate the output distribution. Monte Carlo gives the
    output distribution exactly in the limit of many samples but
    is expensive for slow simulations.
  \item \engterm{Polynomial chaos expansion}. Represent the
    output as a polynomial expansion in the input random variables
    and compute the expansion coefficients from a small number of
    simulations. The method is far more efficient than Monte Carlo
    for smooth input-output relationships.
\end{itemize}

The VVUQ discipline is the discipline that turns a finite-element
result into a quantitative engineering claim. A simulation without
verification produces a number; a simulation with verification but
without validation produces a number that solves the wrong problem;
a simulation with verification and validation but without
uncertainty quantification produces a number whose error bars are
unknown. The full VVUQ pipeline produces a number with error bars,
calibrated against physical reality. The full pipeline is what
the regulatory authority will require for any safety-critical
structural certification.

\begin{estimation}
\textbf{Estimate the central deflection of a $4 \,\text{m} \times 4
\,\text{m}$ square aluminium plate, $5 \,\si{\milli\meter}$ thick,
simply supported on all four edges, carrying a uniform pressure of
$1\,\text{kPa}$.}

\smallskip
\textit{Estimate, before reading on.}

\smallskip
For a simply supported square plate of side $a$ under uniform
pressure $q_0$, the centre deflection from the Navier series is
$w_{\max} \approx 0.00406 q_0 a^4/D$, where $D = E h^3/[12(1-\nu^2)]$.

For aluminium ($E \approx 70\,\text{GPa}$, $\nu \approx 0.33$),
\[
D = \frac{70 \times 10^9 \times (5 \times 10^{-3})^3}{12 \times
(1 - 0.33^2)} \approx \frac{70 \times 10^9 \times 1.25 \times 10^{-7}}{
10.7} \approx 818\,\text{N}\cdot\text{m}.
\]
With $q_0 = 10^3\,\text{Pa}$, $a = 4\,\text{m}$, $a^4 = 256\,
\text{m}^4$,
\[
w_{\max} \approx 0.00406 \times \frac{10^3 \times 256}{818}
\approx 1.27\,\text{m}.
\]
That answer is absurd: a deflection of more than a metre at the
centre of a plate $5\,\text{mm}$ thick spanning $4\,\text{m}$ violates
the small-deflection assumption of Kirchhoff theory by orders of
magnitude. The estimate flags the design as a non-starter: the
plate is far too thin for its span.

The postmortem: the estimate has done its job by showing that
linear plate theory does not apply. The plate is in the
membrane-action regime, in which the deflected midsurface stretches
and carries the load by tensile in-plane stresses rather than by
bending. The membrane analysis of a thin square plate gives a
deflection of order $w_{\max} \sim a (q_0 a/(Eh))^{1/3}$. With the
above numbers, $w_{\max} \sim 4 \times (10^3 \times 4/(70 \times
10^9 \times 5 \times 10^{-3}))^{1/3} \approx 4 \times (1.14 \times
10^{-5})^{1/3} \approx 4 \times 0.022 \approx 0.09\,\text{m}$. The
plate deflects $\sim 90\,\si{\milli\meter}$ at the centre,
which is plausible for a $5\,\si{\milli\meter}$ membrane spanning
$4\,\text{m}$ under $1\,\text{kPa}$, but is still likely beyond the
serviceability limit for any building application. The honest
design specifies a thicker plate, stiffeners, or both.

The deeper lesson: a thin plate's linear bending analysis is valid
only when the deflection is less than the plate's thickness. A
deflection comparable to or larger than the thickness moves the
plate into the membrane regime, and a different model is required.
A finite-element analysis run in linear mode on this geometry would
give the same absurd answer as the formula; an analyst who trusted
the result without checking against the small-deflection criterion
would specify a plate that fails in service. The deflection-to-
thickness ratio is the load-bearing diagnostic.
\end{estimation}

\section{Worked examples}\pathtag{standard}

The formulas of the preceding sections become engineering only when
they are pushed through to numbers with units, checked for plausibility,
and turned into a decision. The examples below work the canonical cases
end to end: a plate deflection, a pressure-vessel wall sizing, a
multiaxial yield check, a stress concentration, and a shell-buckling
margin. Each is the kind of calculation that a working engineer
performs on the back of a free-body diagram before any finite-element
package is opened.

\subsection*{Worked example 1: deflection of a simply supported plate}

\textbf{Problem.} A simply supported square steel plate, side
$a = 1\,\si{\meter}$, thickness $h = 10\,\si{\milli\meter}$, $E =
200\,\text{GPa}$, $\nu = 0.3$, carries a uniform pressure $q_0 =
5\,\text{kPa}$. Find the centre deflection and the maximum surface
stress, and confirm that linear plate theory applies.

\textbf{Solution.} The flexural rigidity is
\[
D = \frac{E h^3}{12(1-\nu^2)} = \frac{200\times 10^9 \times (0.01)^3}
{12(1-0.09)} = \frac{200\times 10^9 \times 10^{-6}}{10.92}
= 1.832\times 10^4\,\text{N}\cdot\text{m}.
\]
For a simply supported square plate the centre deflection is
$w_{\max} = 0.00406\, q_0 a^4/D$, so
\[
w_{\max} = 0.00406 \times \frac{5\times 10^3 \times 1^4}
{1.832\times 10^4} = 1.11\times 10^{-3}\,\text{m} = 1.11\,\si{\milli\meter}.
\]
The maximum bending moment per unit length at the centre is
$M_{\max} \approx 0.0479\, q_0 a^2 = 0.0479\times 5000\times 1 =
240\,\text{N}\cdot\text{m}/\text{m}$. The surface stress follows from
$\sigma_{\max} = 6M_{\max}/h^2$:
\[
\sigma_{\max} = \frac{6\times 240}{(0.01)^2} = 1.44\times 10^7\,
\text{Pa} = 14.4\,\text{MPa},
\]
comfortably below the $250\,\text{MPa}$ yield of structural steel,
giving a yield margin of about $17$. The deflection-to-thickness ratio
is $1.11/10 = 0.11$, well under unity, so the small-deflection
Kirchhoff theory is valid and the plate is in the bending regime, not
the membrane regime. The Navier-series cross-check, summed in
\repopath{code/navier_plate.py}, reproduces the $0.00406$ coefficient
to four figures.

\subsection*{Worked example 2: sizing a cylindrical pressure vessel}

\textbf{Problem.} A cylindrical steel pressure vessel of inner radius
$R = 0.4\,\si{\meter}$ must hold an internal gauge pressure
$p = 4\,\text{MPa}$ with a safety factor of $3$ against yield
($\sigma_Y = 250\,\text{MPa}$). Size the cylindrical wall, then size a
hemispherical end cap, and compare.

\textbf{Solution.} The governing stress in the cylinder is the hoop
stress $\sigma_\theta = pR/h$. The allowable stress is $\sigma_{\text{
allow}} = \sigma_Y/3 = 83.3\,\text{MPa}$. Setting $\sigma_\theta =
\sigma_{\text{allow}}$ and solving for the wall thickness,
\[
h_{\text{cyl}} = \frac{pR}{\sigma_{\text{allow}}} = \frac{4\times 10^6
\times 0.4}{83.3\times 10^6} = 0.0192\,\text{m} = 19.2\,\si{\milli\meter}.
\]
The hemispherical cap carries $\sigma = pR/2h$, half the cylinder hoop
for the same wall, so
\[
h_{\text{cap}} = \frac{pR}{2\sigma_{\text{allow}}} = \frac{4\times 10^6
\times 0.4}{2\times 83.3\times 10^6} = 9.6\,\si{\milli\meter},
\]
exactly half the cylinder wall. The thinner cap is the structural
reason hemispherical and ellipsoidal heads are preferred over flat
ones, and the reason the cylinder-to-head junction, where the two wall
thicknesses and the two stress states meet, is the location of a
bending boundary layer that the membrane theory alone does not
capture; a real design adds a knuckle radius and a local thickness
transition there. The sizing arithmetic is in
\repopath{code/pressure_vessel.py}.

\subsection*{Worked example 3: a multiaxial yield check}

\textbf{Problem.} A point in the wall of the cylinder above carries
hoop stress $\sigma_\theta = 125\,\text{MPa}$, axial stress $\sigma_z =
62.5\,\text{MPa}$, and a small radial compression averaging
$\sigma_r \approx -2\,\text{MPa}$ across the wall. Compute the von
Mises stress and the safety factor against the $250\,\text{MPa}$
yield. Compare with the Tresca prediction.

\textbf{Solution.} The three values are principal stresses (the wall
state is biaxial with a small radial term), so
\[
\sigma_{\text{vm}} = \sqrt{\tfrac{1}{2}\left[(125-62.5)^2 +
(62.5-(-2))^2 + ((-2)-125)^2\right]}\ \text{MPa}.
\]
The bracket is $\tfrac{1}{2}[62.5^2 + 64.5^2 + 127^2] =
\tfrac{1}{2}[3906 + 4160 + 16129] = 12098$, so $\sigma_{\text{vm}} =
\sqrt{12098} = 110\,\text{MPa}$. The safety factor against yield is
$250/110 = 2.27$. The Tresca prediction uses the maximum shear:
$\tau_{\max} = (\sigma_{\max} - \sigma_{\min})/2 = (125-(-2))/2 =
63.5\,\text{MPa}$, so the Tresca equivalent stress is $2\tau_{\max} =
127\,\text{MPa}$ and the Tresca safety factor is $250/127 = 1.97$.
Tresca is the more conservative of the two, as expected from its
inscribed hexagon. An engineer reporting a margin should state which
criterion was used; the same stress state is at safety factor $2.27$ by
von Mises and $1.97$ by Tresca. The computation matches
\repopath{code/von_mises.py}.

\subsection*{Worked example 4: stress concentration at a hole}

\textbf{Problem.} A wide steel plate carries a remote uniaxial tension
of $\sigma_\infty = 80\,\text{MPa}$ and contains a small circular hole.
What is the peak stress at the hole, and how far from the hole does the
disturbance decay to within five percent of the remote field?

\textbf{Solution.} The Kirsch solution gives a stress-concentration
factor of $K_t = 3$ at the sides of the hole (the points transverse to
the load, $\theta = 90^\circ$ in figure~\ref{fig:vol03ch09:kirsch-hole}),
so the peak hoop stress is
\[
\sigma_{\max} = K_t\,\sigma_\infty = 3\times 80 = 240\,\text{MPa},
\]
which exceeds the $250\,\text{MPa}$ yield by only a small margin; a
hole that triples the local stress turns a comfortably elastic member
into one on the edge of local yielding. The Kirsch field shows the
hoop stress along the transverse axis decaying as $\sigma_\theta/
\sigma_\infty = 1 + \tfrac{1}{2}(a/r)^2 + \tfrac{3}{2}(a/r)^4$ for
$r \ge a$. At $r = 3a$ this gives $1 + 0.056 + 0.0185 = 1.074$, still
seven percent high; at $r = 4a$ it is $1 + 0.031 + 0.0059 = 1.037$,
within four percent. The disturbance is local to within three or four
hole radii, the quantitative basis for the rule that a hole far from
an edge can be analysed as if the plate were infinite. A coarse FEM
mesh that places fewer than several elements within one radius of the
hole will miss this peak entirely.

\subsection*{Worked example 5: a shell-buckling margin}

\textbf{Problem.} A thin steel hemisphere of radius $R = 1\,\si{\meter}$
and wall thickness $h = 5\,\si{\milli\meter}$ ($E = 200\,\text{GPa}$,
$\nu = 0.3$) is subjected to external pressure. Find the linear-theory
critical pressure, apply a realistic imperfection knock-down, and state
the working buckling pressure.

\textbf{Solution.} The classical buckling pressure of a complete
spherical shell under external pressure is
\[
p_{\text{cr}} = \frac{2E h^2}{R^2\sqrt{3(1-\nu^2)}}
= \frac{2\times 200\times 10^9 \times (0.005)^2}
{1^2 \times \sqrt{3\times 0.91}}.
\]
The numerator is $2\times 200\times 10^9 \times 2.5\times 10^{-5} =
1.0\times 10^7$; the denominator is $\sqrt{2.73} = 1.652$, so
$p_{\text{cr}} = 6.05\times 10^6\,\text{Pa} = 6.05\,\text{MPa}$. This is
the perfect-shell value. Applying a knock-down factor of $0.3$, typical
for a sphere under external pressure (\repopath{data/shell_knockdown.csv}),
the working buckling pressure is $0.3\times 6.05 = 1.82\,\text{MPa}$, a
little under $18$ atmospheres. A vacuum failure ($\Delta p \le 0.1\,
\text{MPa}$) is nowhere near this margin, but a hemisphere that has to
resist several megapascals of external pressure would be designed
against the knocked-down value, never against the $6\,\text{MPa}$
linear figure. The factor-of-three penalty between linear theory and
working capacity is the price of imperfection sensitivity, and an
analyst who quotes the linear value as the capacity has overstated the
strength of the shell by a factor of three.

\subsection*{Worked example 6: a rectangular plate and the aspect-ratio effect}

\textbf{Problem.} Two simply supported steel plates carry the same
uniform pressure $q_0 = 4\,\text{kPa}$ and have the same thickness
$h = 8\,\si{\milli\meter}$ ($E = 200\,\text{GPa}$, $\nu = 0.3$). The
first is square, side $a = 1.2\,\si{\meter}$. The second is a long
rectangle, $a = 1.2\,\si{\meter}$ by $b = 2.4\,\si{\meter}$. Compare
the centre deflections and explain what the aspect ratio does to the
load path.

\textbf{Solution.} The flexural rigidity is common to both:
\[
D = \frac{E h^3}{12(1-\nu^2)} = \frac{200\times 10^9\times (0.008)^3}
{12\times 0.91} = 9.38\times 10^3\,\text{N}\cdot\text{m}.
\]
For a simply supported rectangular plate the centre deflection is
$w_{\max} = \alpha\, q_0 a^4/D$, where $a$ is the shorter side and the
coefficient $\alpha$ rises from $0.00406$ for a square toward the
one-way-strip limit $0.0130$ as the plate grows long. Tabulated values
give $\alpha = 0.00406$ at $b/a = 1$ and $\alpha = 0.01013$ at $b/a =
2$ (the collected coefficients are in
\repopath{data/plate_coefficients.csv}). With $a^4 = 1.2^4 = 2.074\,
\text{m}^4$ and $q_0 a^4/D = 4000\times 2.074/9380 = 0.884\,\text{m}$,
\[
w_{\text{square}} = 0.00406\times 0.884 = 3.59\times 10^{-3}\,\text{m}
\approx 3.6\,\si{\milli\meter},
\]
\[
w_{\text{long}} = 0.01013\times 0.884 = 8.96\times 10^{-3}\,\text{m}
\approx 9.0\,\si{\milli\meter}.
\]
The long plate deflects about $2.5$ times as much, although the short
span is identical. The square plate sends load to all four edges by
two-way bending, so each direction carries roughly half; the long
plate carries almost the whole load across the short span in one-way
bending, with the long edges contributing little once $b/a$ exceeds
about two. The lesson is the working rule that a rectangular plate of
aspect ratio above two behaves as a one-way strip spanning the short
direction, and is designed as such. The deflection-to-thickness ratios
are $0.45$ and $1.1$, so the long plate is at the edge of the
small-deflection regime and a more careful analysis would check whether
membrane stiffening has begun to help.

\subsection*{Worked example 7: a stiffened panel by smeared stiffness}

\input{volumes/03-force/ch09-plates-shells-solid-mechanics/figures/fig-stiffened-panel}

\textbf{Problem.} A steel deck panel, $2\,\si{\meter}\times 1\,
\si{\meter}$, is a $6\,\si{\milli\meter}$ skin reinforced by
longitudinal blade stiffeners $50\,\si{\milli\meter}$ deep and $6\,
\si{\milli\meter}$ thick at a pitch of $250\,\si{\milli\meter}$
(figure~\ref{fig:vol03ch09:stiffened-panel}). Estimate the effective
bending rigidity in the stiffener direction and compare it to the bare
skin, then state the separate check the smeared model cannot supply.

\textbf{Solution.} The bare-skin rigidity per unit width is $D_{\text{
skin}} = E h^3/[12(1-\nu^2)] = 200\times 10^9\times (0.006)^3/[12\times
0.91] = 3.96\times 10^3\,\text{N}\cdot\text{m}$. The stiffener adds
second moment of area along its length. Treat one stiffener plus its
tributary skin strip (width $b = 0.25\,\si{\meter}$) as a tee section.
The skin strip contributes area $A_s = 0.25\times 0.006 = 1.5\times
10^{-3}\,\text{m}^2$; the blade contributes $A_b = 0.05\times 0.006 =
3.0\times 10^{-4}\,\text{m}^2$. The neutral axis sits a distance
$\bar{y}$ below the skin midplane,
\[
\bar{y} = \frac{A_b\,(0.003 + 0.025)}{A_s + A_b}
= \frac{3.0\times 10^{-4}\times 0.028}{1.8\times 10^{-3}}
= 4.67\times 10^{-3}\,\text{m},
\]
about $4.7\,\si{\milli\meter}$ below the skin midplane. The section
second moment about that axis, summing the skin and blade own-inertias
and their parallel-axis terms, is approximately
\[
I = 1.5\times 10^{-3}\,(4.67\times 10^{-3})^2 + 3.0\times 10^{-4}\,
(0.0233)^2 + \tfrac{1}{12}(0.006)(0.05)^3 \approx 2.6\times 10^{-7}\,
\text{m}^4
\]
per stiffener bay. The smeared rigidity in the stiffener direction is
$D_x = E I/b = 200\times 10^9\times 2.6\times 10^{-7}/0.25 = 2.08\times
10^5\,\text{N}\cdot\text{m}$, about $53$ times the bare-skin rigidity.
The transverse rigidity $D_y$ stays near the bare-skin value, so the
panel is strongly orthotropic, $D_x \gg D_y$, and the global deflection
analysis uses the orthotropic plate equation $D_x w_{,xxxx} + 2H
w_{,xxyy} + D_y w_{,yyyy} = q$ rather than the isotropic biharmonic.

The check the smeared model cannot supply is the local one. Smearing
replaces the discrete skin-and-stiffener geometry with a continuum, so
it predicts the panel's global deflection and the stress carried into
the stiffeners, but it knows nothing about the skin bay between two
stiffeners. That bay, $250\,\si{\milli\meter}\times$ (its length),
$6\,\si{\milli\meter}$ thick, is a small plate in its own right and can
buckle locally under in-plane compression or deflect excessively under
pressure long before the global panel is in trouble. A complete design
runs the smeared model for the global response and a separate local
plate analysis for the worst skin bay. The smeared-versus-discrete
split is the plate-and-shell instance of the multiscale discipline:
one model for the structure, another for the detail.

\subsection*{Worked example 8: discontinuity stress at a shell junction}

\textbf{Problem.} A cylindrical shell, radius $R = 0.6\,\si{\meter}$,
wall $h = 12\,\si{\milli\meter}$, internal pressure $p = 3\,\text{MPa}$,
is closed by a hemispherical head of the same radius but wall $h/2 =
6\,\si{\milli\meter}$, the membrane-efficient thickness. Estimate the
radial growth mismatch at the junction and the axial length over which
the bending boundary layer decays.

\textbf{Solution.} Under pressure each shell grows radially. The
cylinder hoop strain is $\epsilon_\theta = (\sigma_\theta - \nu
\sigma_z)/E$ with $\sigma_\theta = pR/h$ and $\sigma_z = pR/2h$, so
\[
\delta_{\text{cyl}} = R\,\epsilon_\theta = \frac{R}{E}\,
\frac{pR}{h}\left(1 - \tfrac{\nu}{2}\right)
= \frac{0.6}{200\times 10^9}\,\frac{3\times 10^6\times 0.6}{0.012}\,
(1 - 0.15).
\]
The membrane hoop stress is $pR/h = 3\times 10^6\times 0.6/0.012 =
150\,\text{MPa}$, so $\delta_{\text{cyl}} = (0.6/200\times 10^9)\times
150\times 10^6\times 0.85 = 3.83\times 10^{-4}\,\text{m}$, about
$0.38\,\si{\milli\meter}$. The hemisphere carries equal biaxial stress
$pR/2h_{\text{sph}} = 3\times 10^6\times 0.6/(2\times 0.006) = 150\,
\text{MPa}$ as well (the half thickness exactly offsets the factor of
two), and its radial growth is $\delta_{\text{sph}} = (R/E)\,\sigma\,
(1-\nu) = (0.6/200\times 10^9)\times 150\times 10^6\times 0.7 = 3.15
\times 10^{-4}\,\text{m}$, about $0.32\,\si{\milli\meter}$.

The two free shells want to grow by different amounts, a mismatch of
about $0.07\,\si{\milli\meter}$, but the junction holds them together.
The shells satisfy continuity by developing shear and bending at the
edge, a self-equilibrating set of edge forces that produces the local
\engterm{discontinuity stress}. The bending decays over the
characteristic length of the shell boundary layer,
\[
\ell \approx \frac{\pi}{\beta}, \qquad
\beta = \left[\frac{3(1-\nu^2)}{R^2 h^2}\right]^{1/4},
\]
which for the cylinder gives $\beta = [3\times 0.91/(0.6^2\times
0.012^2)]^{1/4} = [2.73/(5.18\times 10^{-5})]^{1/4} = (5.27\times
10^4)^{1/4} = 15.1\,\text{m}^{-1}$, so the bending is confined to about
$1/\beta = 66\,\si{\milli\meter}$, of order $\sqrt{R h} = \sqrt{0.6
\times 0.012} = 0.085\,\si{\meter}$. The boundary-layer stress can add
a meaningful fraction of the membrane stress at the junction
(figure~\ref{fig:vol03ch09:discontinuity-stress}), which is why the
junction is detailed with a thickness taper or a knuckle radius and is
never sized on the membrane formula alone. The estimate sets the scale:
the local detail matters over a band a few times $\sqrt{R h}$ wide, and
nowhere else.

\section{Case study: sizing an air receiver end to end}\pathtag{standard}

The membrane formulae, the head selection, the discontinuity check, and
the buckling check are five separate calculations in the preceding
sections. A real vessel design carries one set of numbers through all of
them and ends in a verdict. We size a compressed-air receiver from its
service requirement to its specified wall and head, with every number
carried forward, and state at each step which check governs and why.

\textbf{The requirement.} A plant needs an air receiver to buffer a
reciprocating compressor: storage volume about $3\,\text{m}^3$, maximum
allowable working pressure $p = 1.6\,\text{MPa}$ gauge (a common
$16\,\text{bar}$ rating), service temperature near ambient, working
fluid dry compressed air. The vessel is a horizontal cylinder closed by
two heads, with a single top nozzle for the relief valve and outlet
(figure~\ref{fig:vol03ch09:vessel-layout}). The material is a plain
carbon-steel plate of yield $\sigma_Y = 250\,\text{MPa}$ and a
code-style allowable stress $S = 138\,\text{MPa}$ (a representative
value for this class of steel at modest temperature; a real design
reads $S$ from the material tables of the governing code). We adopt a
joint efficiency $E_j = 0.85$, the value for a spot-radiographed
double-welded butt seam.

\input{volumes/03-force/ch09-plates-shells-solid-mechanics/figures/fig-vessel-layout}

\textbf{Step 1: geometry from the volume.} Choose an inner diameter
$D_i = 1.2\,\si{\meter}$, so $R = 0.6\,\si{\meter}$. The cylindrical
shell of tangent length $L$ holds $\pi R^2 L$; the two hemispherical-
equivalent heads add roughly $\tfrac{4}{3}\pi R^3$ between them. Setting
the total to $3\,\text{m}^3$,
\[
\pi (0.6)^2 L + \tfrac{4}{3}\pi (0.6)^3 = 3
\;\Rightarrow\;
1.131\,L + 0.905 = 3
\;\Rightarrow\;
L = 1.85\,\si{\meter}.
\]
Round the tangent length up to $L = 3.0\,\si{\meter}$ to leave margin
and to suit standard plate, giving a generous $\sim 4.3\,\text{m}^3$;
the geometry of figure~\ref{fig:vol03ch09:vessel-layout} is fixed.

\textbf{Step 2: cylindrical shell thickness (hoop governs).} The hoop
stress is the governing membrane stress. Sizing on the code-style
relation $t = pR/(S E_j)$, which is the membrane hoop formula with the
allowable reduced by the joint efficiency,
\[
t_{\text{shell}} = \frac{p R}{S E_j}
= \frac{1.6\times 10^6\times 0.6}{138\times 10^6\times 0.85}
= \frac{9.6\times 10^5}{1.173\times 10^8}
= 8.19\times 10^{-3}\,\text{m}
= 8.2\,\si{\milli\meter}.
\]
Add a corrosion allowance of $1.5\,\si{\milli\meter}$ and round to a
standard plate: specify $t_{\text{shell}} = 10\,\si{\milli\meter}$. The
as-specified hoop stress on the corroded wall ($8.5\,\si{\milli\meter}$
after allowance) is $\sigma_\theta = pR/t = 1.6\times 10^6\times 0.6/
(8.5\times 10^{-3}) = 113\,\text{MPa}$, comfortably under the
$138\,\text{MPa}$ allowable.

\textbf{Step 3: head selection and thickness.} A hemispherical head
carries $pR/2t$ and would size at $t = pR/(2 S E_j) = 4.1\,\si{
\milli\meter}$, half the shell. A hemisphere is deep and costly to
form, though, so we choose a $2{:}1$ ellipsoidal head, the common
compromise. The ellipsoidal head's governing thickness, from the code
relation $t = pD_i/(2 S E_j - 0.2 p)$, is
\[
t_{\text{head}} = \frac{1.6\times 10^6\times 1.2}
{2\times 138\times 10^6\times 0.85 - 0.2\times 1.6\times 10^6}
= \frac{1.92\times 10^6}{2.346\times 10^8 - 3.2\times 10^5}
= 8.2\times 10^{-3}\,\text{m},
\]
the same as the shell thickness to two figures. Specify the head at the same
$10\,\si{\milli\meter}$ as the shell, which also eases the welding of
two equal-thickness edges. The thickness ordering across head types is
the one shown in figure~\ref{fig:vol03ch09:head-types}: the
hemispherical head is thinnest, the ellipsoidal and torispherical heads
sit near the shell thickness, and a flat head would be several times
thicker because it carries the pressure in bending.

\input{volumes/03-force/ch09-plates-shells-solid-mechanics/figures/fig-head-types}

\textbf{Step 4: head-to-shell junction.} The ellipsoidal head and the
cylinder grow by slightly different radial amounts under pressure, the
discontinuity-stress mechanism of worked example 8. With $R = 0.6\,
\si{\meter}$ and $t = 10\,\si{\milli\meter}$, the boundary-layer width
is of order $\sqrt{R t} = \sqrt{0.6\times 0.01} = 0.077\,\si{\meter}$,
so the local bending is confined to a band about $80\,\si{\milli\meter}$
from the junction. We detail the junction with the head's own knuckle
radius (the $2{:}1$ ellipsoidal profile supplies it) and avoid any
abrupt thickness step, keeping the two welded edges at equal thickness.
No extra wall is needed here for a vessel of this modest pressure; the
detail is the fix.

\textbf{Step 5: nozzle reinforcement.} The top nozzle cuts a hole in
the shell, and a hole removes load-carrying metal, exactly the
stress-concentration mechanism of worked example 4. The area-
replacement rule of pressure-vessel codes requires that the metal
removed by the opening be replaced by reinforcement within a defined
zone around the nozzle. For a nozzle of inner diameter $d = 100\,
\si{\milli\meter}$, the area removed is $A_{\text{rem}} = d\,t_{\text{
required}} = 0.100\times 8.2\times 10^{-3} = 8.2\times 10^{-4}\,
\text{m}^2$ (using the required, not the specified, wall). The excess
wall already present (the specified $10\,\si{\milli\meter}$ against the
$8.2\,\si{\milli\meter}$ required, over the reinforcement zone) supplies
part of this; the nozzle neck supplies more; a reinforcing pad makes up
any shortfall. We note the check rather than work the full area
balance: the design intent is that the opening not lower the vessel's
pressure capacity, achieved by replacing the removed area locally.

\textbf{Step 6: external-pressure and buckling check.} An air receiver
is an internal-pressure vessel, but it can see external pressure during
a vacuum event (a blocked inlet during pump-out, or steam condensation
if it were ever steamed and sealed). The relevant failure is inward
buckling, not yield. The long-cylinder ring-buckling pressure is
$p_{\text{cr}} = E t^3/[4(1-\nu^2)R^3]$; with $E = 200\,\text{GPa}$,
$t = 8.5\times 10^{-3}\,\si{\meter}$ (corroded), $R = 0.6\,\si{\meter}$,
\[
p_{\text{cr}} = \frac{200\times 10^9\times (8.5\times 10^{-3})^3}
{4\times 0.91\times (0.6)^3}
= \frac{200\times 10^9\times 6.14\times 10^{-7}}{0.786}
= 1.56\times 10^5\,\text{Pa}.
\]
A $0.3$ imperfection knock-down brings this to about $47\,\text{kPa}$,
below a full atmosphere of external differential ($101\,\text{kPa}$).
The bare shell would not survive a hard vacuum. The verdict at this
step is to fit a vacuum-breaker (anti-vacuum valve) sized so the
credible external differential cannot exceed the knocked-down capacity,
the standard and cheap fix; ring stiffeners would be the alternative if
a vacuum-rated vessel were required.

\textbf{Verdict.} The vessel is a $1.2\,\si{\meter}$ inner-diameter,
$3.0\,\si{\meter}$ tangent-length carbon-steel cylinder, $10\,\si{
\milli\meter}$ wall, with two $2{:}1$ ellipsoidal heads also at
$10\,\si{\milli\meter}$, a top nozzle reinforced by the local excess
wall plus a pad as the area balance requires, equal-thickness welds at
the head-to-shell junctions, full radiography downgraded to spot with
$E_j = 0.85$, a $1.5\,\si{\milli\meter}$ corrosion allowance, and a
vacuum-breaker against external-pressure collapse. The governing check
is the cylindrical hoop stress; the head, junction, and nozzle checks
each refine the detail without changing the wall; the external-pressure
check changes nothing structural but adds a protective device. The
membrane sizing arithmetic and the head and external-pressure relations
are implemented in \repopath{code/pressure_vessel.py}. A reader who
takes the membrane hoop result as the whole design ships a vessel that
collapses on the first vacuum event; the discipline is to carry the
numbers through every check, not only the one that sizes the wall.

\section{Failure: when finite-element analyses lie}\pathtag{core}

A finite-element analysis is a numerical method; its output is a
set of numbers. The numbers are correct only if the model
(geometry, material properties, boundary conditions, load
distribution), the discretisation (mesh, element type), the solver
(numerical parameters, convergence tolerances), and the
interpretation (which output quantity, at which point, in which
unit) are all correct. The failure section of this chapter names
the classes of error that produce wrong FEM results and the
diagnostics that catch them.

\subsection{Modelling errors}

The simulation solves the wrong problem.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \engterm{Wrong material model.} An anisotropic material is
    analysed as isotropic; a nonlinear material is analysed as
    linear; a temperature-dependent material is analysed at the
    wrong temperature; the elastic constants are taken from a
    handbook for the wrong alloy. The diagnostic is a coupon test
    on the actual material under the actual conditions.
  \item \engterm{Wrong boundary conditions.} A bolted joint is
    modelled as a rigid weld; a contact surface is modelled as
    bonded; a frictional interface is modelled as frictionless. A
    structure that is hinged in reality but modelled as
    moment-resisting will be predicted as stiffer and stronger
    than the real structure.
  \item \engterm{Wrong load distribution.} A pressure load is
    applied uniformly when the real pressure has a gradient; a
    point load is applied at a node when the real load is spread
    over a contact area; a dynamic load is analysed statically
    when inertia or rate effects matter. The diagnostic is a
    careful free-body diagram (chapter 1) of the system, applied
    before the finite-element analysis is built.
  \item \engterm{Wrong geometry.} The CAD model differs from the
    as-built geometry (chamfers omitted, fillets simplified,
    tolerances not represented). The diagnostic is to compare the
    CAD-to-mesh transcription against the manufacturing drawing
    and against the as-built measurements.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Discretisation errors}

The simulation solves the right problem incorrectly.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \engterm{Coarse mesh at a stress concentration.} The peak
    stress is under-predicted; the predicted location of failure
    moves away from the actual location. The diagnostic is mesh
    refinement and a mesh-convergence study.
  \item \engterm{Linear elements on a bending-dominated thin
    structure.} Shear locking causes linear tetrahedra and
    hexahedra to over-predict the stiffness of thin plates and
    shells. The diagnostic is to use shell elements, reduced-
    integration solid elements, or quadratic solid elements.
  \item \engterm{Volumetric locking in nearly incompressible
    material.} Standard displacement elements over-predict the
    stiffness of nearly incompressible materials. The diagnostic
    is to use mixed displacement-pressure elements.
  \item \engterm{Poor element quality at automatic mesh
    refinement.} The mesh generator produces thin sliver
    elements at curved boundaries or near point loads. The
    diagnostic is element-quality checks (aspect ratio,
    skewness, warpage) on every mesh.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Interpretation errors}

The simulation produces correct numbers that the engineer reads
incorrectly.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \engterm{Reading a stress value at a node where it is
    inaccurate.} Element stresses are typically most accurate at
    the Gauss points, less accurate at the element edges, and
    least accurate at the nodes (where they are computed by
    averaging or extrapolation from adjacent elements). A node
    stress at a stress concentration, in a coarse mesh, can be
    badly inaccurate.
  \item \engterm{Failing to recognise a singularity}. A
    re-entrant corner produces an unbounded stress in the
    elastic-theory limit; the finite-element peak stress at the
    corner increases without bound as the mesh is refined.
    Interpreting the corner stress as a failure criterion gives a
    progressively more conservative design as the mesh is refined,
    which is the opposite of convergence to a sensible answer. The
    engineer must recognise the singularity (the corner is a
    geometric feature with no physical analogue at sufficient
    resolution) and apply a different failure criterion (J-integral,
    notch-stress, structural-stress methods).
  \item \engterm{Unit confusion}. Forces in newtons, stresses in
    pascals, lengths in millimetres; or forces in pounds, stresses
    in psi, lengths in inches. A unit confusion gives results
    wrong by factors of $10^3$ or $10^6$. The diagnostic is to
    check units on every input and every output, and to verify
    that the units of the result are the units the engineer
    expected.
  \item \engterm{Wrong factor of safety}. The finite-element peak
    stress is taken as the design stress, without considering the
    stress concentration's locality, the material's fatigue
    properties, or the load uncertainty. The diagnostic is the
    full code-based design check (chapter 8) applied to the FEM
    result.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{The thin-mirror analogue: Hubble's metrology chain}

The Hubble Space Telescope primary mirror, fabricated by
Perkin-Elmer between 1979 and 1981, is the canonical case study
of a precision-fabrication failure in which the test instrument
itself was wrong \cite{acc:nasa-hubble-optical-systems-1990}.
The mirror was figured to the wrong shape because the reflective
null corrector used to measure the figure during polishing was
misassembled: a field lens was mispositioned by about $1.3\,
\si{\milli\meter}$ along the optical axis, the result of a
misinterpretation of a measurement target on a metering rod
during assembly. The error in the test instrument propagated into
the polished mirror's figure: the outer edge was about $2\,\si{
\micro\meter}$ too flat, an error large enough to ruin
diffraction-limited imaging.

The Hubble case is not a finite-element failure; the mirror was
fabricated mechanically and measured optically, with no
finite-element analysis in the verification chain. Its relevance
to the present chapter is the parallel discipline: a numerical
result is only as good as the verification chain that established
its trustworthiness. The reflective null corrector was treated as
the authoritative figure measurement, with no independent
verification of the test instrument itself; two auxiliary optical
tests showed discrepancies and were set aside. The engineering
discipline that should have caught the error was the
\engterm{independent verification of the test instrument} (in FEM
language: code verification of the simulation). The discipline
that should have caught the residual error was the
\engterm{anomaly resolution} that takes seriously every
discrepancy between an authoritative result and an auxiliary
check (in FEM language: validation against an independent
analysis or test).

The thin-mirror connection to plate and shell mechanics is also
direct. The Hubble primary is a $2.4 \,\text{m}$ diameter ULE
glass shell with a mass of about $830 \,\si{\kilogram}$ and an
average thickness of about $300\,\si{\milli\meter}$; in
ground-based optics the figure error from gravity sag and
mounting forces is computed by FEM analysis of the shell as a
thin curved plate. Modern lightweight space telescopes use even
thinner mirrors (the Herschel primary was $3.5 \,\text{m}$
diameter and only $\sim 20\,\si{\milli\meter}$ thick), and the
mechanical analysis of figure error under thermal and gravity
load is wholly an FEM exercise. The discipline that catches
errors in those analyses is the same VVUQ discipline named in
section 9.7.

\subsection{The square-corner analogue: the Comet cabin cutouts}

The stress-concentration discipline of this chapter has a structural
counterpart as sharp as the Hubble metrology case. The de Havilland
Comet, the first jet-powered passenger airliner, suffered two in-flight
cabin disintegrations in 1954, traced by the Royal Aircraft
Establishment to fatigue cracks that initiated at the square-cornered
cutouts in the pressurised cabin skin and propagated under repeated
pressurisation cycles \cite{acc:cohen-comet-report-1955}. The cabin
was a thin cylindrical pressure vessel; each flight took it through one
full pressure cycle of about $0.55\,\si{\bar}$ differential. The
cutouts had small fillet radii, and a small fillet radius at the edge
of a cutout in a loaded plate produces exactly the stress
concentration that figure~\ref{fig:vol03ch09:kirsch-hole} quantifies
for a circular hole, except that the square corner concentrates the
stress more sharply than a round hole does. The local peak exceeded
the material's fatigue limit; a crack initiated, grew under cycling,
reached its critical length, and ran.

The connection to FEM practice is the singularity error of section
9.7. A re-entrant or sharp corner produces an unbounded stress in the
elastic limit; an FEM model of the Comet cutout meshed coarsely would
have reported a finite, reassuring peak that rose without bound as the
mesh refined. The discipline that catches it is the one named earlier:
recognise the geometric singularity, refine the corner to a real
fillet radius, and apply a fatigue criterion rather than a static
peak-stress check. The post-Comet response, full-airframe fatigue
testing and the generous radiusing of every cutout, is the structural
analogue of the mesh-convergence and singularity discipline of this
chapter. A round window is a stress-concentration fix you can see from
your seat.

\begin{principle}[A FEM result is a hypothesis, not a fact]
A finite-element output is a numerical prediction of how the
modelled system would respond to the modelled load. The
prediction is a fact about the model; it is a hypothesis about
the physical system. The discipline that turns the hypothesis
into a fact about the physical system is verification (the
solver solves the model correctly), validation (the model
captures the physics), and uncertainty quantification (the
inputs propagate to bounded outputs). A FEM output without
verification, validation, and uncertainty quantification is a
colourful screenshot, not an engineering claim.
\end{principle}

\section{Project}\pathtag{core}

\begin{project}[Analytical plus FEM, with reconciliation]
The reader solves a two-dimensional elasticity problem by hand,
solves the same problem with a finite-element package, compares
the two results, identifies the source of any disagreement, and
reports the reconciliation. The deliverable is a thirty- to
fifty-page engineering report.

\textbf{Track}. Analytical plus simulation. No physical build.
\textbf{Hazard class}: none.

\textbf{Problem}. A flat steel plate, $200\,\si{\milli\meter}
\times 100 \,\si{\milli\meter}$, $5\,\si{\milli\meter}$ thick, with
a central circular hole of $20\,\si{\milli\meter}$ diameter, is
loaded uniaxially in the long direction with a uniform tension of
$50\,\text{MPa}$ applied to its short edges. The long edges and
the hole boundary are traction-free. Find the maximum stress at
the hole.

\input{volumes/03-force/ch09-plates-shells-solid-mechanics/figures/fig-kirsch-hole}

\textbf{Analytical solution}. The Kirsch (1898) solution for an
infinite plate with a circular hole under remote uniaxial tension
gives a stress concentration factor of $3.0$ at the hole
(figure~\ref{fig:vol03ch09:kirsch-hole}). With the
applied stress of $50\,\text{MPa}$, the peak hoop stress at the
hole is $150\,\text{MPa}$. The reader writes out the Kirsch
solution from first principles (chapter 3 of this volume, section
on plane stress), verifies the stress concentration factor, and
notes the role of the plate's finite width in modifying the
analytical result (the finite-width correction for a $D/W = 0.2$
ratio raises the stress concentration factor to approximately
$3.13$).

\textbf{FEM solution}. The reader meshes the plate (taking
advantage of the two-fold symmetry to model only a quarter of the
plate), runs the analysis on three progressively refined meshes,
and records the peak stress at the hole on each mesh. The
mesh-convergence study should show convergence to within a few
percent of the analytical result on the finest mesh.

\textbf{Reconciliation}. The reader writes a one-page section
identifying:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item The peak stress from the analytical solution.
  \item The peak stress from the converged FEM solution.
  \item The percentage difference and its sources (finite-width
    correction, mesh resolution at the hole, element type and
    order).
  \item The mesh-convergence study (a plot of peak stress versus
    inverse element size at the hole) and the inferred converged
    value.
  \item The implication for engineering practice: at what mesh
    resolution would the FEM peak stress have been wrong by more
    than $10\,\%$?
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Deliverable}. The analytical derivation, the FEM model
files (mesh, input deck, output), the mesh-convergence plot, the
peak-stress comparison, and a short conclusion paragraph on the
reconciliation. The reader is encouraged to use an open-source
FEM package (CalculiX, Code\_Aster, FEniCS) so the workflow is
reproducible.

\textbf{Time}. Two to four hours for the analytical solution,
four to eight hours for the FEM setup and mesh-convergence study,
two to four hours of write-up. The project is suitable as a
weekend exercise once the reader is comfortable with the FEM
software.
\end{project}

\subsection*{Calculation}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
A simply supported square steel plate of side $1\,\si{\meter}$ and
thickness $10\,\si{\milli\meter}$ ($E = 200\,\text{GPa}$, $\nu =
0.3$) carries a uniform pressure of $5\,\text{kPa}$. Compute the
flexural rigidity $D$ and the centre deflection.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} $D = Eh^3/[12(1-\nu^2)] = 200\times 10^9
\times (0.01)^3/[12\times 0.91] = 1.83\times 10^4\,\text{N}\cdot\text{m}$.
For a simply supported square plate $w_{\max} = 0.00406\, q_0 a^4/D =
0.00406\times 5000\times 1/(1.83\times 10^4) = 1.11\times 10^{-3}\,
\text{m} \approx 1.1\,\si{\milli\meter}$. The ratio $w/h = 0.11 < 1$
confirms linear theory applies. This is Worked example 1.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A clamped circular aluminium plate of radius $0.5\,\si{\meter}$
and thickness $4\,\si{\milli\meter}$ ($E = 70\,\text{GPa}$, $\nu =
0.33$) carries a uniform pressure of $2\,\text{kPa}$. Compute the
centre deflection and the maximum bending moment at the clamped
edge.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} $D = 70\times 10^9\times (0.004)^3/
[12(1-0.33^2)] = 70\times 10^9\times 6.4\times 10^{-8}/10.69 =
419\,\text{N}\cdot\text{m}$. For a clamped circular plate $w_{\max} =
q_0 a^4/(64D) = 2000\times 0.5^4/(64\times 419) = 2000\times 0.0625/
26816 = 4.66\times 10^{-3}\,\text{m} \approx 4.7\,\si{\milli\meter}$.
The edge moment is $M_{rr} = -q_0 a^2/8 = -2000\times 0.25/8 =
-62.5\,\text{N}\cdot\text{m}/\text{m}$; the surface stress there is
$6|M|/h^2 = 6\times 62.5/(0.004)^2 = 23.4\,\text{MPa}$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A thin spherical pressure vessel of radius $0.6\,\si{\meter}$,
wall thickness $4\,\si{\milli\meter}$, carries an internal gauge
pressure of $2\,\text{MPa}$. Compute the membrane stress in the
wall.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} A sphere has equal meridional and
circumferential membrane stress $\sigma = pR/(2h) = 2\times 10^6
\times 0.6/(2\times 0.004) = 1.2\times 10^6/0.008 = 1.5\times 10^8\,
\text{Pa} = 150\,\text{MPa}$. Both principal stresses are equal, so
the von Mises stress equals $150\,\text{MPa}$ as well; against a
$250\,\text{MPa}$ yield the margin is $1.67$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A thin cylindrical steel pressure vessel of inner radius $0.5\,
\si{\meter}$ and wall thickness $6\,\si{\milli\meter}$ carries an
internal gauge pressure of $1.5\,\text{MPa}$. Compute the hoop
stress, the axial stress, and the ratio of the two.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Hoop $\sigma_\theta = pR/h = 1.5\times
10^6\times 0.5/0.006 = 1.25\times 10^8\,\text{Pa} = 125\,\text{MPa}$.
Axial $\sigma_z = pR/(2h) = 62.5\,\text{MPa}$. Ratio $\sigma_\theta/
\sigma_z = 2$, the universal thin-cylinder result; the hoop governs.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A conical steel shell of half-angle $30^{\circ}$ and uniform wall
thickness $5\,\si{\milli\meter}$ carries an internal gauge pressure
of $0.5\,\text{MPa}$. Compute the circumferential and meridional
membrane stresses at a slant distance of $1\,\si{\meter}$ from
the apex.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} With $\tan 30^\circ = 0.577$, $s = 1\,
\si{\meter}$, $h = 0.005\,\si{\meter}$: circumferential $\sigma_\theta
= p s\tan\alpha/h = 0.5\times 10^6\times 1\times 0.577/0.005 = 5.77
\times 10^7\,\text{Pa} = 57.7\,\text{MPa}$. Meridional $\sigma_s =
p s\tan\alpha/(2h) = 28.9\,\text{MPa}$, half the circumferential. The
stresses grow linearly with $s$, so the base is the critical section.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A long aluminium beam ($E = 70\,\text{GPa}$, $\nu = 0.33$) of
square cross-section $40 \times 40\,\si{\milli\meter}$ is loaded
in plane strain with an in-plane uniaxial stress of $100\,\text{MPa}
$. Compute the effective two-dimensional Young's modulus and
Poisson's ratio.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Plane strain uses $E^* = E/(1-\nu^2) =
70/(1-0.33^2) = 70/0.891 = 78.6\,\text{GPa}$ and $\nu^* = \nu/(1-\nu)
= 0.33/0.67 = 0.493$. The effective modulus is stiffer and the
effective Poisson's ratio approaches $0.5$ because the through-thickness
constraint prevents the lateral contraction that a free bar would
have; the constraint makes the material behave as if nearly
incompressible in the plane.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A simply supported rectangular steel plate of dimensions $1.5\,
\si{\meter} \times 1\,\si{\meter}$ and thickness $8\,\si{\milli
\meter}$ carries a uniform pressure of $3\,\text{kPa}$. Use the
Navier double-sine series, retaining the four smallest terms
($m, n = 1, 3$), to compute the centre deflection. Compare to the
single-term approximation.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} $D = 200\times 10^9\times (0.008)^3/
[12\times 0.91] = 9.38\times 10^3\,\text{N}\cdot\text{m}$. At the
centre $\sin(m\pi/2)\sin(n\pi/2) = \pm 1$ for odd $m,n$. With $a =
1.5$, $b = 1$, the term denominators $mn[(m/a)^2+(n/b)^2]^2$ are:
$(1,1)$ gives $1\times[0.444+1]^2 = 2.085$ with sign $+$; $(1,3)$
gives $3\times[0.444+9]^2 = 267.6$ sign $-$; $(3,1)$ gives $3\times
[4+1]^2 = 75$ sign $-$; $(3,3)$ gives $9\times[4+9]^2 = 1521$ sign
$+$. Sum $= 1/2.085 - 1/267.6 - 1/75 + 1/1521 = 0.4796 - 0.00374 -
0.01333 + 0.00066 = 0.4632$. Then $w = (16 q_0/\pi^6 D)\times 0.4632 =
(16\times 3000/(961\times 9380))\times 0.4632 = 5.33\times 10^{-3}
\times 0.4632 = 2.47\times 10^{-3}\,\text{m}$. The single term alone
gives $5.33\times 10^{-3}\times 0.4796 = 2.56\,\si{\milli\meter}$, so
the four-term value $2.47\,\si{\milli\meter}$ is about three percent
lower; the series converges quickly. Cross-check with
\repopath{code/navier_plate.py}.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A long thin-walled aluminium cylinder of radius $0.8\,\si{\meter}$
and wall thickness $3\,\si{\milli\meter}$ is loaded by external
hydrostatic pressure. Estimate the buckling pressure from the
classical shell-buckling formula, then apply a knock-down factor
of $0.3$ for imperfection sensitivity. Compare to the
gauge-pressure equivalent of a $20\,\si{\meter}$ depth of water.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} For a long cylinder under external
pressure the classical critical pressure is $p_{\text{cr}} = E h^3/
[4(1-\nu^2)R^3]$ (the long-cylinder ring formula). With $E = 70\times
10^9$, $h = 0.003$, $R = 0.8$, $\nu = 0.33$: $p_{\text{cr}} = 70\times
10^9\times 2.7\times 10^{-8}/[4\times 0.891\times 0.512] = 1890/1.825
= 1.04\times 10^3\,\text{Pa} \approx 1.0\,\text{kPa}$. After the $0.3$
knock-down, $\approx 0.31\,\text{kPa}$. The pressure at $20\,\si{\meter}$
of water is $\rho g h = 1000\times 9.81\times 20 = 1.96\times 10^5\,
\text{Pa} = 196\,\text{kPa}$, more than two orders of magnitude above
the buckling capacity: this thin cylinder would collapse almost
immediately at depth. It needs ring stiffeners or a far thicker wall.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Derivation}\pathtag{standard}

\begin{exercise}
Derive the plate flexural rigidity $D = E h^3/[12(1-\nu^2)]$ from
the Kirchhoff kinematic assumptions and the plane-stress Hooke's
law. State which integral is responsible for the $h^3$ dependence.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} The Kirchhoff kinematics give
$\epsilon_{xx} = -z\,\partial^2 w/\partial x^2$ (and similarly for
$yy$, $xy$): strains linear in $z$. Plane-stress Hooke's law makes
$\sigma_{xx} = [E/(1-\nu^2)](\epsilon_{xx}+\nu\epsilon_{yy})$, also
linear in $z$. The bending moment is the first moment $M_{xx} =
\int_{-h/2}^{h/2} z\,\sigma_{xx}\,dz$. Substituting, the integrand
carries $z\times z = z^2$, and $\int_{-h/2}^{h/2} z^2\,dz = h^3/12$.
That integral is the source of the cube: one factor of $h$ comes from
the strain's linear $z$ dependence, one from the moment arm $z$, and
one from the range of integration. The result is $M_{xx} = -D(\partial^2
w/\partial x^2 + \nu\,\partial^2 w/\partial y^2)$ with $D = Eh^3/
[12(1-\nu^2)]$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Derive the plate bending equation $D \nabla^4 w = q$ from
transverse force equilibrium of an infinitesimal plate element
and the moment-curvature relations.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Transverse force balance on a $dx\,dy$
element gives $\partial Q_x/\partial x + \partial Q_y/\partial y + q =
0$. Moment balance about the two in-plane axes relates the shears to
the moment gradients: $Q_x = \partial M_{xx}/\partial x + \partial
M_{xy}/\partial y$ and $Q_y = \partial M_{yy}/\partial y + \partial
M_{xy}/\partial x$. Substituting eliminates the shears, leaving
$\partial^2 M_{xx}/\partial x^2 + 2\,\partial^2 M_{xy}/\partial x
\partial y + \partial^2 M_{yy}/\partial y^2 = -q$. Now insert the
moment-curvature relations $M_{xx} = -D(w_{,xx}+\nu w_{,yy})$,
$M_{yy} = -D(w_{,yy}+\nu w_{,xx})$, $M_{xy} = -D(1-\nu)w_{,xy}$. The
$\nu$ terms cancel in pairs and the twist term supplies the cross
derivative, collecting to $D(w_{,xxxx} + 2w_{,xxyy} + w_{,yyyy}) = q$,
i.e.\ $D\nabla^4 w = q$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Derive the membrane stresses in a thin spherical pressure vessel
under internal pressure from force equilibrium on a hemisphere.
State the assumption about wall thickness that the derivation
uses.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Cut the sphere along a diameter. The
internal pressure $p$ acting on the projected circular area $\pi R^2$
gives a net axial force $p\pi R^2$ pushing the two halves apart. The
wall resists it over the cut circumference $2\pi R$ with thickness $h$,
carrying membrane stress $\sigma$: resisting force $= \sigma\times 2\pi
R h$. Equilibrium $p\pi R^2 = \sigma\,2\pi R h$ gives $\sigma = pR/(2h)$.
By symmetry every great circle gives the same result, so the
meridional and circumferential stresses are equal. The assumption is
that $h \ll R$, so the stress is uniform through the wall (membrane,
no through-thickness bending) and $R$ is taken as the midsurface
radius.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Derive the hoop and axial stresses in a thin cylindrical pressure
vessel from force equilibrium on a half-cylinder slice and on a
circular end cap, respectively.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} \emph{Hoop:} slice a length $L$ of the
cylinder along a diametral plane. The pressure on the projected
rectangle $2RL$ gives force $p\,2RL$; the two longitudinal cut edges
(each $Lh$) resist with hoop stress $\sigma_\theta$: resisting force
$2\sigma_\theta L h$. Equilibrium $p\,2RL = 2\sigma_\theta Lh$ gives
$\sigma_\theta = pR/h$. \emph{Axial:} cap the cylinder and balance the
end thrust $p\pi R^2$ against the axial stress over the wall
cross-section $2\pi R h$: $p\pi R^2 = \sigma_z\,2\pi R h$, so $\sigma_z
= pR/(2h)$. Hence $\sigma_\theta = 2\sigma_z$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Show that, for an isotropic linear-elastic material, the
constitutive law $\sigma_{ij} = C_{ijkl} \epsilon_{kl}$ has only
two independent elastic constants, by enumerating the symmetries
of $C_{ijkl}$.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Start from $81$ components. Stress
symmetry $\sigma_{ij}=\sigma_{ji}$ forces $C_{ijkl}=C_{jikl}$ (minor
symmetry in $ij$), strain symmetry forces $C_{ijkl}=C_{ijlk}$ (minor
in $kl$); together these reduce $81$ to $36$. The existence of a
strain-energy potential gives the major symmetry $C_{ijkl}=C_{klij}$,
reducing $36$ to $21$ (the symmetric $6\times 6$ Voigt matrix).
Imposing full isotropy, the tensor must be invariant under all
rotations, and the only isotropic fourth-rank tensors with the right
symmetries are built from $\delta_{ij}\delta_{kl}$ and $(\delta_{ik}
\delta_{jl}+\delta_{il}\delta_{jk})$. Their two coefficients are the
Lam{\'e} parameters $\lambda$ and $\mu$, giving $C_{ijkl}=\lambda
\delta_{ij}\delta_{kl}+\mu(\delta_{ik}\delta_{jl}+\delta_{il}
\delta_{jk})$: two independent constants.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Estimation}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the central deflection of a $2 \,\text{m} \times 2\,\text{m}$
glass window pane, $6\,\si{\milli\meter}$ thick, simply supported
on all four edges, under a uniform wind pressure of $500\,\text{Pa}$.
State assumptions about glass elastic properties and check that
the deflection is within the linear-theory range.

\paragraph{Reference estimate.} Take glass $E \approx 70\,\text{GPa}$,
$\nu \approx 0.22$. $D = 70\times 10^9\times (0.006)^3/[12(1-0.048)] =
70\times 10^9\times 2.16\times 10^{-7}/11.42 = 1.32\times 10^3\,
\text{N}\cdot\text{m}$. For a simply supported square plate $w_{\max}
= 0.00406\,q_0 a^4/D = 0.00406\times 500\times 16/1320 = 0.0246\,
\text{m} \approx 25\,\si{\milli\meter}$. The ratio $w/h = 25/6 \approx
4 > 1$, so the pane is already past the linear-theory range and into
membrane action; the true deflection is smaller than $25\,\si{\milli
\meter}$ but the linear estimate flags that a $6\,\si{\milli\meter}$
pane spanning $2\,\si{\meter}$ is marginal and would normally be
thicker or smaller in practice.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the burst pressure of a thin-walled aluminium soda can
($R \approx 33\,\si{\milli\meter}$, wall thickness $\approx 0.1\,
\si{\milli\meter}$, ultimate tensile strength $\approx 250\,\text{
MPa}$). Compare to a typical internal pressure ($\sim 300\,\text{
kPa}$) for a carbonated drink.

\paragraph{Reference estimate.} Treat the can wall as a thin cylinder;
the hoop stress governs, $\sigma_\theta = pR/h$. Setting $\sigma_\theta
= \sigma_{\text{ult}} = 250\,\text{MPa}$ and solving for the burst
pressure: $p_{\text{burst}} = \sigma_{\text{ult}}\,h/R = 250\times 10^6
\times 1\times 10^{-4}/0.033 = 2.5\times 10^4/0.033 = 7.6\times 10^5\,
\text{Pa} \approx 0.76\,\text{MPa}$. The carbonation pressure of about
$0.3\,\text{MPa}$ is roughly a factor of $2.5$ below burst, which
matches the lived experience that a shaken can can fail but a normal
one does not. The estimate also explains why the can ends are domed:
the flat-end stress would govern instead.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the natural frequency of a clamped circular drumhead of
radius $0.15\,\si{\meter}$, thickness $0.2\,\si{\milli\meter}$,
material density $1400\,\si{\kilogram\per\meter\cubed}$,
Young's modulus $\sim 3\,\text{GPa}$.

\paragraph{Reference estimate.} The fundamental frequency of a clamped
circular plate is $f = (\lambda^2/2\pi)\sqrt{D/(\rho h a^4)}$ with
$\lambda^2 = 10.21$ for the lowest mode. $D = 3\times 10^9\times
(2\times 10^{-4})^3/[12(1-0.16)] \approx 3\times 10^9\times 8\times
10^{-12}/10.08 = 2.38\times 10^{-3}\,\text{N}\cdot\text{m}$ (using
$\nu \approx 0.4$). Mass per area $\rho h = 1400\times 2\times 10^{-4}
= 0.28\,\si{\kilogram\per\meter\squared}$. Then $D/(\rho h a^4) =
2.38\times 10^{-3}/(0.28\times 0.15^4) = 2.38\times 10^{-3}/(0.28\times
5.06\times 10^{-4}) = 16.8\,\text{s}^{-2}$, $\sqrt{\cdot} = 4.1$. So
$f = (10.21/6.28)\times 4.1 \approx 6.7\,\text{Hz}$ if read as plate
bending. A real drumhead is membrane-tensioned, which raises this
substantially; the bending estimate is a lower bound and shows the
plate stiffness alone gives only a few hertz, so the audible pitch
must come from tension, not flexural rigidity.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the buckling pressure of a thin steel hemisphere of
radius $1\,\si{\meter}$ and wall thickness $5\,\si{\milli\meter}$
under external pressure. State the linear-theory critical
pressure and the realistic value after the imperfection knock-down.

\paragraph{Reference estimate.} $p_{\text{cr}} = 2E h^2/[R^2\sqrt{3
(1-\nu^2)}]$ with $E = 200\,\text{GPa}$, $h = 0.005$, $R = 1$, $\nu =
0.3$. Numerator $2\times 200\times 10^9\times 2.5\times 10^{-5} =
1.0\times 10^7$; denominator $\sqrt{3\times 0.91} = 1.65$, so
$p_{\text{cr}} = 6.05\,\text{MPa}$ (linear). After a $0.3$ knock-down,
$\approx 1.8\,\text{MPa}$. This is Worked example 5; the factor of
three between theory and capacity is the imperfection penalty.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the peak stress concentration factor at the corner of a
square cutout in a flat plate under uniaxial tension. State why
the geometric singularity produces an unbounded prediction in
the elastic-theory limit and what design discipline replaces
the peak-stress check.

\paragraph{Reference estimate.} A truly sharp re-entrant corner (zero
radius) has a stress that grows as $r^{-\lambda}$ with $\lambda \approx
0.46$ for a $90^\circ$ notch, so the elastic stress is unbounded as
$r\to 0$ and the stress-concentration factor is formally infinite. The
FEM peak rises without bound as the mesh refines, so there is no
converged number to report. The design discipline that replaces the
peak-stress check is fracture or notch mechanics: characterise the
notch by its stress-intensity factor or by a finite notch radius from
the manufacturing process (a real corner always has a small radius),
and apply a fatigue or fracture criterion. The practical estimate for
a fillet of radius $\rho$ at a corner is $K_t \approx 1 + 2\sqrt{t/\rho}$
for a notch of depth $t$, finite only because $\rho > 0$.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Simulation}\pathtag{mastery}

\begin{exercise}
Using an open-source finite-element package, mesh a square plate
with a central circular hole. Apply uniaxial tension to two
opposite edges. Run the analysis on three meshes (coarse, medium,
fine) and tabulate the peak stress at the hole on each mesh.
Estimate the converged value by Richardson extrapolation.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} The peak stress should rise from below
toward $K_t\sigma_\infty$ as the mesh refines; for a small hole and
remote tension $\sigma_\infty$, the converged value is $3\sigma_\infty$.
With three results such as the $128$, $139$, $146\,\text{MPa}$ sequence
of \repopath{data/mesh_convergence.csv} at refinement ratio $r = 2$,
Richardson extrapolation gives $p = \ln[(f_1-f_2)/(f_2-f_3)]/\ln r =
\ln(11/7)/\ln 2 \approx 0.65$ and $f_{\text{exact}} = f_3 + (f_3-f_2)/
(r^p-1) \approx 146 + 7/0.57 \approx 158\,\text{MPa}$ from this coarse
triple; refining further tightens it toward the analytical $150\,
\text{MPa}$. The key reported quantities are the converged value, the
observed order, and the grid-convergence index; see
\repopath{code/richardson_convergence.py}.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Implement a hand-coded two-dimensional plane-stress finite-element
solver using linear triangular elements. Verify on a uniaxial
tension test against the analytical solution.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} The solver needs four pieces: the
plane-stress material matrix $\mathbf{D} = [E/(1-\nu^2)]\,[[1,\nu,0],
[\nu,1,0],[0,0,(1-\nu)/2]]$; the constant strain-displacement matrix
$\mathbf{B}$ built from the triangle's nodal coordinates; the element
stiffness $K_e = t\,A\,\mathbf{B}^{\mathsf T}\mathbf{D}\,\mathbf{B}$;
and assembly plus a constrained solve $K\vec{u}=\vec{F}$. The patch
test loads a single (or two-triangle) square in uniaxial tension and
checks that the recovered strain equals $\sigma/E$ and the stress
field is uniform. A complete reference implementation that passes this
test is \repopath{code/cst_fem.py}; a correct solver reproduces $\sigma
= E\epsilon$ to machine precision on the patch test, which is the
minimum bar an element formulation must clear.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Using a finite-element package, model a cantilevered thin square
plate of side $200\,\si{\milli\meter}$ and thickness $2\,\si{
\milli\meter}$ as both (a) a shell element model and (b) a
single-layer brick element model. Compare the tip deflection
under a $10\,\text{N}$ tip load with the analytical Kirchhoff
plate prediction. Identify the source of any discrepancy in the
brick-element model.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} The Kirchhoff cantilever-strip estimate
treats the plate as a beam of width $b = 0.2\,\si{\meter}$, second
moment $I = bh^3/12 = 0.2\times (0.002)^3/12 = 1.33\times 10^{-10}\,
\text{m}^4$, length $L = 0.2$, $E = 200\,\text{GPa}$: tip deflection
$\delta = PL^3/(3EI) = 10\times 0.008/(3\times 200\times 10^9\times
1.33\times 10^{-10}) = 0.08/79.8 = 1.0\times 10^{-3}\,\text{m}\approx
1\,\si{\milli\meter}$. The shell-element model should match this to a
few percent. The single-layer brick model returns roughly half to a
third of it because of shear locking
(figure~\ref{fig:vol03ch09:shear-locking}): one linear element through
the thickness cannot bend and manufactures parasitic shear strain. The
cure is shell elements, several quadratic bricks through the thickness,
or reduced integration.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Design}\pathtag{standard}

\begin{exercise}
Design a flat steel plate floor of a small workshop, $3\,\si{
\meter} \times 4\,\si{\meter}$, simply supported on its four
edges by perimeter beams, to carry a uniform live load of $5\,
\text{kPa}$. Specify the plate thickness and verify the
small-deflection assumption.

\paragraph{Model-answer sketch.} The short span $a = 3\,\si{\meter}$
governs. Set a serviceability deflection limit, say $w \le L/250 =
12\,\si{\milli\meter}$. For a rectangular plate near the one-way limit
the deflection coefficient is around $0.01$, so $w \approx 0.01\,q_0
a^4/D$ and the required $D \ge 0.01\times 5000\times 81/0.012 = 3.4\times
10^6\,\text{N}\cdot\text{m}$. With steel $D = Eh^3/[12(1-\nu^2)] =
1.83\times 10^{10}h^3$, this needs $h^3 \ge 1.85\times 10^{-4}$, so
$h \ge 57\,\si{\milli\meter}$, which is implausibly thick for a bare
plate. The honest answer is that a flat plate alone is the wrong
structure for a $3\times 4\,\si{\meter}$ floor; the design adds
stiffening ribs or a grillage so each plate panel spans under a metre,
or uses a composite deck. The exercise teaches that the thickness-cubed
penalty makes large unstiffened plates uneconomic, and verifies $w/h$
is small only after stiffening reduces the panel span.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Design a thin-walled cylindrical steel pressure vessel of inner
radius $0.4\,\si{\meter}$ to carry an internal pressure of $4\,
\text{MPa}$ with a safety factor of $3$ on hoop stress. Specify
the wall thickness and a code-style longitudinal weld detail.

\paragraph{Model-answer sketch.} Allowable stress $\sigma_{\text{allow}}
= \sigma_Y/3$. Taking $\sigma_Y = 250\,\text{MPa}$, $\sigma_{\text{
allow}} = 83.3\,\text{MPa}$. Hoop governs: $h = pR/\sigma_{\text{allow}}
= 4\times 10^6\times 0.4/83.3\times 10^6 = 19.2\,\si{\milli\meter}$;
specify $h = 20\,\si{\milli\meter}$. The longitudinal weld sees the
full hoop stress, so it is the governing seam; a code design specifies
a full-penetration double-welded butt joint with a joint efficiency
$E_j$ (ASME applies $E_j \approx 0.85$ for spot-radiographed,
$1.0$ for fully radiographed), increasing the required thickness to
$h = pR/(\sigma_{\text{allow}}E_j)$. With $E_j = 0.85$, $h = 22.6\,
\si{\milli\meter}$; specify $24\,\si{\milli\meter}$ and full
radiography of the longitudinal seam. The circumferential seam, at
half the stress, can use a lower joint efficiency.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Design a hemispherical pressure-vessel end cap to match a
cylindrical body of inner radius $0.4\,\si{\meter}$, internal
pressure $4\,\text{MPa}$, with the same safety factor. Specify
the end-cap thickness and note the comparison to the cylindrical
wall thickness.

\paragraph{Model-answer sketch.} The hemispherical cap carries $\sigma
= pR/(2h)$, so $h_{\text{cap}} = pR/(2\sigma_{\text{allow}}) = 4\times
10^6\times 0.4/(2\times 83.3\times 10^6) = 9.6\,\si{\milli\meter}$;
specify $10\,\si{\milli\meter}$. This is half the cylinder wall
($\approx 20\,\si{\milli\meter}$ from the previous exercise), the
quantitative statement that a hemispherical head is the most efficient
closure. Note the discontinuity: the thin cap meets the thicker
cylinder at the junction, where the mismatch in radial growth under
pressure induces a bending boundary layer. A real design either tapers
the thickness transition over a few wall thicknesses or uses a knuckle
radius (a torispherical or ellipsoidal head) to soften the
discontinuity.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Design a finite-element mesh strategy for analysing the stress
concentration at a fillet between a shaft and its mounting
flange, with the fillet radius equal to one-tenth the shaft
radius. Specify the element type, the minimum element size at
the fillet, and the criteria for accepting the converged peak
stress.

\paragraph{Model-answer sketch.} Use quadratic (second-order)
elements: tetrahedra if the geometry forces an unstructured mesh,
hexahedra if a swept mesh is possible, because linear elements
under-predict the fillet peak. The element size at the fillet should
resolve the radius with several elements across it; with a fillet
radius $\rho = R_{\text{shaft}}/10$, set the minimum element size to
about $\rho/4$ to $\rho/6$, giving four to six elements through the
fillet arc. Accept the peak stress only after a mesh-convergence study
of three meshes shows the peak changing by less than a chosen tolerance
(say $2\%$) between the two finest meshes, with the convergence rate
consistent with the element order. Because a fillet has a finite
radius, the peak is bounded and converges (unlike a sharp corner), so
a converged value exists and is the design stress; apply the fatigue
check from chapter 8 to it.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Diagnosis and reverse engineering}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
A finite-element analysis of a bolted bracket predicts a peak
stress of $400\,\text{MPa}$ at a node on a sharp re-entrant
corner. The mesh refinement is doubled; the predicted peak rises
to $580\,\text{MPa}$. Identify the underlying issue and the
diagnostic that catches it. State the engineering criterion that
should replace the peak-stress check.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} The peak stress rising with mesh
refinement is the signature of a geometric stress singularity at the
sharp re-entrant corner: the elastic stress is unbounded there, so the
FEM peak diverges and never converges. The diagnostic is the
mesh-convergence study itself, which shows the peak increasing rather
than stabilising. The corner stress is not a physical failure
criterion; the criterion that replaces it is a fracture- or
notch-mechanics check (stress-intensity factor, the structural-stress
or notch-stress method, or a finite fillet radius from the actual
manufactured part). A real corner has a small radius; modelling that
radius bounds the stress and restores convergence.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A thin spherical pressure vessel is observed to buckle inward
under external vacuum loading at a pressure differential
significantly below the linear-theory critical pressure. Identify
the dominant cause and state the design discipline that should
have anticipated the failure.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} The dominant cause is imperfection
sensitivity: a real spherical shell buckles at $20$ to $40\%$ of the
linear-theory critical pressure because small geometric imperfections
(a dimple, a flat spot, out-of-roundness) destabilise the membrane
equilibrium. The design discipline that anticipates it is to apply a
knock-down factor calibrated to test data, never to design against the
linear value; design codes (ASME Section VIII, the NASA shell-buckling
monographs) supply the reduced capacities. The fix is a thicker wall,
ring or stringer stiffeners, or a tighter manufacturing tolerance on
out-of-roundness.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A cantilevered FEM model of a thin metal panel returns a tip
deflection that is approximately half of the closed-form
prediction from Kirchhoff plate theory. The model uses linear
brick elements with one element through the thickness. Identify
the numerical artifact and the corrective action.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} The artifact is shear locking. A single
linear brick through the thickness has straight edges and cannot
represent the curvature of pure bending; it develops a parasitic
transverse shear strain that absorbs energy and makes the panel report
far too stiff, hence a deflection roughly half the Kirchhoff value
(figure~\ref{fig:vol03ch09:shear-locking}). The corrective action is to
switch to shell elements, use several quadratic bricks through the
thickness, or use reduced-integration solid elements with hourglass
control. A through-thickness mesh-refinement study would also expose it.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A wooden floor beam analysed as isotropic with the wood's
longitudinal Young's modulus predicts deflections half those
actually observed. Identify the modelling error and the
corrective material model.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} The error is treating wood as isotropic
with its largest (longitudinal, along-grain) modulus, which overstates
the bending stiffness when the beam is loaded so that the radial or
tangential modulus matters, or when shear deformation along the grain
is significant. Wood is orthotropic: the longitudinal modulus exceeds
the radial and tangential moduli by a factor of $20$ or more, and the
shear moduli are smaller still. The corrective model is an orthotropic
constitutive law with the grain direction aligned to the beam axis and
the radial and tangential moduli and the relevant shear modulus
entered correctly; design then follows the grain-direction allowables
(NDS or Eurocode 5). Doubling of the predicted-versus-observed
deflection is consistent with a missing shear-deflection contribution
and an overstated effective modulus.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Failure analysis}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
Re-read the NASA Optical Systems Failure Report on the Hubble
primary mirror \cite{acc:nasa-hubble-optical-systems-1990}.
Identify the parallel between the metrology-chain failure in
Hubble fabrication and the VVUQ failure modes of section 9.7.
State the specific verification step that, if applied to the
reflective null corrector, would have caught the error before
the mirror was polished to its final figure.

\paragraph{Model-answer sketch.} The parallel is the unverified test
instrument. In Hubble the reflective null corrector was treated as the
authoritative figure measurement, exactly as a beginner treats an FEM
result as authoritative; both are predictions whose trustworthiness
depends on a verification chain that was skipped. The specific step
that would have caught it is independent verification of the test
instrument: an end-to-end check of the null corrector against a second,
independent metrology method (the auxiliary refractive null corrector
and the inverse-null test that did show discrepancies but were set
aside). In FEM terms this is code verification (does the tool solve the
intended problem) plus anomaly resolution (take every discrepancy
between the authoritative result and an independent check seriously).
The discipline is to verify the instrument, not only the part it
measures.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A thin-walled aluminium aircraft fuselage panel, designed by
finite-element analysis as a flat plate in plane stress, develops
visible buckles under combined cabin pressurisation and bending
during pre-service ground testing. Argue, in $200$ to $300$ words,
which class of FEM error (modelling, discretisation, interpretation)
is most likely responsible and what verification should have
caught it.

\paragraph{Model-answer sketch.} The most likely class is a modelling
error: a linear static plane-stress analysis cannot predict
out-of-plane buckling, because buckling is a stability (eigenvalue)
problem, not a stress problem. The flat-plate plane-stress idealisation
discards the very degree of freedom (transverse displacement) in which
the panel buckles. A panel can be well below its yield stress and still
buckle. The verification that should have caught it is a linear
buckling (eigenvalue) analysis followed by a geometrically nonlinear
post-buckling analysis with an imperfection seeded, and validation
against a coupon or sub-component compression test. A converged stress
plot from a linear static run says nothing about stability; the
discipline is to recognise the loading as stability-driven and to run
the buckling analysis the static model omitted. A good answer names the
class as modelling, identifies the missing physics as stability, and
prescribes a buckling eigenvalue analysis plus imperfection-sensitive
nonlinear check.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A thin spherical storage tank for liquefied gas, designed against
the linear-theory buckling pressure with a factor of safety of
$1.5$, collapses inward when a vapour-return valve fails closed
during pump-out. Argue, in $200$ to $300$ words, which factor of
the design was responsible for the failure and what design
discipline catches the imperfection-sensitivity issue.

\paragraph{Model-answer sketch.} The responsible factor is designing
against the linear-theory buckling pressure with only a $1.5$ safety
factor. Real shells buckle at $20$ to $40\%$ of the linear value, so a
$1.5$ factor against the linear capacity leaves an actual margin below
unity once imperfection sensitivity is accounted for; an external
pressure the design treated as within margin was in fact above the true
buckling capacity. The valve failing closed during pump-out created an
unanticipated external pressure differential (partial vacuum inside),
the loading the inward-buckling mode responds to. The discipline that
catches it is a calibrated knock-down factor on the linear buckling
load, per ASME Section VIII or the NASA shell-buckling monographs, plus
a vacuum-breaker or relief device sized so the credible valve-failure
pressure cannot exceed the knocked-down capacity. A good answer names
the linear buckling basis as the fault and the empirical knock-down
(with a vacuum breaker) as the cure.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Judgment}\pathtag{standard}

\begin{exercise}
A junior engineer presents a FEM analysis of a structural
component to the design review meeting, with no mesh-convergence
study and no validation against any analytical or experimental
data. The peak stress is below the material's yield strength,
and the engineer recommends the design be approved. Write a
one-paragraph reply that identifies what is missing and what
verification the design review should require before approval.

\paragraph{Model-answer sketch.} What is missing is the entire
verification-and-validation chain: a single FEM run on a single mesh
gives a number whose error is unknown, and a peak stress below yield is
not a result if it has not been shown to be mesh-independent. The
review should require, before approval, a mesh-convergence study on the
quantity of interest with the observed convergence order reported; a
check that the peak is not at a geometric singularity (where no
converged value exists); validation against an analytical result or a
test for at least one representative case; and a proper code-based
design check applying the right factor of safety, not a bare
stress-versus-yield comparison. The reply should say plainly that
"below yield on one mesh" is a colourful screenshot, not an engineering
claim, and name the convergence study as the minimum gate.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A senior colleague argues that commercial FEM software is
sufficiently mature that engineers no longer need to understand
the underlying mathematics. Write a one-paragraph reply that
identifies the specific knowledge a working FEM analyst must
retain and the failure modes that opaque software use admits.

\paragraph{Model-answer sketch.} The reply should grant that solvers
and element libraries are mature, then state what no maturity can
substitute for: the analyst must understand the governing physics
(stress versus stability, linear versus nonlinear, static versus
dynamic), the element-formulation pitfalls (shear and volumetric
locking, singularities at sharp corners, stress accuracy at Gauss
points versus nodes), the boundary-condition and load idealisations,
and the verification-and-validation discipline. The failure modes that
opaque use admits are exactly the ones in section 9.7: an isotropic
model of an anisotropic material, a linear static run where buckling
governs, a coarse mesh that under-predicts a concentration, a
singularity read as a failure stress, unit confusion, and a peak read
at an inaccurate node. Mature software runs all of these to convincing
colour plots. The knowledge the analyst retains is the knowledge of
when the software is solving the wrong problem.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Identify a thin-walled or plate-like structural component in
your immediate environment whose loading you have never explicitly
analysed (a metal cabinet panel, a glass tabletop, a sheet-metal
appliance casing, a thin polystyrene shelf). State, in $200$ to
$300$ words, the loads it carries, the dominant deformation mode,
the relevant simplified theory (plate, shell, membrane, or
three-dimensional elasticity), and the failure mode you would
inspect for first.

\paragraph{Model-answer sketch.} A good answer picks one component and
carries it through the chapter's framework. For a sheet-metal appliance
casing panel, for instance: the loads are its own weight, the press
fit of fasteners, and incidental hand pressure; the dominant
deformation mode is plate bending of the unsupported spans between
stiffening ribs or fold lines; the relevant theory is Kirchhoff plate
theory for the flat panels and shell or fold geometry where the metal
is bent (a fold is a stiffener because it gives the sheet local
curvature). The failure mode to inspect first is local buckling or
oil-canning of the largest unsupported flat span under modest pressure,
since a thin flat panel is far more flexible than the same metal folded.
The answer is judged on correct identification of the deformation mode
and the matching simplified theory, not on the specific object.
\end{exercise}
