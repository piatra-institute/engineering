\noindent
A mechanical system that returns toward an equilibrium when displaced
oscillates around that equilibrium. The oscillation is the system's
vibration. Vibrations carry energy, transmit force, radiate sound,
fatigue materials, and at the wrong frequency, destroy what they act
on. The chapter develops the engineering theory of vibration: the
oscillator's natural frequency, its damping, its forced response, its
modal structure as the system grows from one degree of freedom to
many to a continuum, and the diagnostic discipline that turns
vibration measurements into engineering decisions.

\section{Single-degree-of-freedom oscillators}\pathtag{core}

The simplest vibrating system is a mass on a spring. A mass $m$
connected to a wall by a linear spring of stiffness $k$, on a
frictionless horizontal surface, has displacement $x(t)$ measured
from the spring's equilibrium length. Newton's second law (or the
Lagrangian formulation of chapter 5) gives the equation of motion
\[
m\ddot{x} + kx = 0.
\]
The equation is linear, homogeneous, and second-order. Its general
solution is
\[
x(t) = A\cos(\omega_n t) + B\sin(\omega_n t) = X\cos(\omega_n t -
\phi),
\]
where
\[
\omega_n = \sqrt{\frac{k}{m}}
\]
is the \engterm{natural angular frequency}, and the constants $A,
B$ (or equivalently $X, \phi$) are set by the initial conditions
$x(0)$ and $\dot{x}(0)$. The natural frequency is the rate at which
the system oscillates when displaced from equilibrium and left to
itself; it depends only on the stiffness and the mass, not on the
amplitude of motion. The natural frequency in Hz is $f_n = \omega_n /
(2\pi)$, and the natural period is $T_n = 1/f_n = 2\pi/\omega_n$.

The energy of the oscillator is
\[
E = \tfrac{1}{2} m \dot{x}^2 + \tfrac{1}{2} k x^2,
\]
which is constant in time for the undamped system. The kinetic
energy is maximum at the equilibrium position ($x = 0$, $\dot{x} =
\pm X\omega_n$); the potential energy is maximum at the extremes ($x
= \pm X$, $\dot{x} = 0$). Energy oscillates between the two forms
at frequency $2 \omega_n$ (twice the displacement frequency, because
each form peaks once per quarter-period).

Many real systems reduce to the single-degree-of-freedom (SDOF)
oscillator in their simplest analysis. A pendulum of length $L$
linearised at small amplitude has $\omega_n = \sqrt{g/L}$. A rigid
body of mass moment of inertia $I$ on a torsional spring of
stiffness $K$ has $\omega_n = \sqrt{K/I}$. A floor joist supporting
a load $m$ at midspan has, for small vertical vibrations, $\omega_n
= \sqrt{k_{\text{joist}}/m}$ with $k_{\text{joist}} = 48 EI/L^3$
the joist's flexural stiffness at midspan. The same equation, with
different physical interpretations of $m$ and $k$, recurs across
domains.

\engterm{Equivalent stiffness and equivalent mass}. The reduction of
a complex structure to a single equivalent SDOF system is the first
step in many vibration analyses. For a long beam vibrating in its
fundamental mode, the equivalent mass is not the full beam mass but
the modal-effective fraction (typically $0.5$ to $0.6$ for common
boundary conditions); the equivalent stiffness is the modal
stiffness corresponding to that mass. The same approach reduces a
multi-storey building's lowest sway mode to an SDOF system, a
spinning rotor's first whirl mode to an SDOF system, or a complex
machine's principal vibration mode to an SDOF system. The reduction
is exact only for the mode it represents; other modes need their
own reductions.

\section{Damped vibration}\pathtag{core}

Real systems lose energy to friction, internal-material hysteresis,
air resistance, and acoustic radiation. The simplest model adds a
linear viscous damper of damping coefficient $c$:
\[
m\ddot{x} + c\dot{x} + kx = 0.
\]
The equation is still linear; its solutions depend on the damping
strength relative to the system's natural frequency. The
\engterm{damping ratio} is
\[
\zeta = \frac{c}{2\sqrt{mk}} = \frac{c}{2 m \omega_n},
\]
a dimensionless quantity. Three regimes:

\engterm{Underdamped} ($\zeta < 1$): the system oscillates with
exponentially decaying amplitude. The solution is
\[
x(t) = X_0 e^{-\zeta\omega_n t} \cos(\omega_d t - \phi),
\]
where
\[
\omega_d = \omega_n\sqrt{1 - \zeta^2}
\]
is the damped angular frequency. For lightly damped systems
($\zeta \lesssim 0.1$), $\omega_d \approx \omega_n$, and the period
is approximately the undamped period. Each successive amplitude
peak is reduced from the previous one by the factor $e^{-2\pi\zeta/
\sqrt{1-\zeta^2}}$; the logarithm of this ratio is the
\engterm{logarithmic decrement} $\delta = 2\pi\zeta/\sqrt{1-\zeta^2}$,
a standard experimental measure of damping.

\engterm{Critically damped} ($\zeta = 1$): the system returns to
equilibrium in the shortest time without oscillating. The solution
is
\[
x(t) = (A + Bt) e^{-\omega_n t}.
\]
Critical damping is the dividing line between oscillatory and
non-oscillatory return; many engineering systems (vehicle shock
absorbers, instrument needles, door closers) are designed near
critical damping.

\engterm{Overdamped} ($\zeta > 1$): the system returns to equilibrium
without oscillating, but more slowly than at critical damping. The
solution is a sum of two decaying exponentials with different rates.
Overdamped systems are sluggish; they appear in heavily-damped
isolators and in some control-loop responses.

Typical damping ratios for engineering systems, current as of 2025
per the standard structural-dynamics references \cite{text:den-hartog-vibrations}:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Welded steel structures: $\zeta \approx 0.005$ to $0.01$.
  \item Bolted steel structures: $\zeta \approx 0.01$ to $0.02$.
  \item Reinforced concrete buildings: $\zeta \approx 0.02$ to $0.05$.
  \item Soils under cyclic loading: $\zeta \approx 0.05$ to $0.20$.
  \item Vehicle suspensions (designed): $\zeta \approx 0.2$ to $0.7$.
  \item Automotive shock absorbers (alone, in compression): $\zeta
    \approx 0.3$.
  \item Pendulum clock: $\zeta \approx 10^{-4}$ (very lightly damped,
    by design).
\end{itemize}

The numerical values vary widely by structure, material, joint
detailing, amplitude, and frequency; published values are working
approximations, and a specific design uses the measured damping of
the actual structure when it matters.

\engterm{Sources of damping}. Viscous damping is the simplest
mathematical model and represents only a few real dissipation
mechanisms (motion through a fluid at low Reynolds number, properly
designed dashpots). Real damping in structures is dominated by
material hysteresis, frictional sliding at joints, and acoustic
radiation; these mechanisms are nonlinear and amplitude-dependent in
detail but are commonly modelled as equivalent viscous damping for
engineering analysis. The reduction is accurate near resonance for
small amplitudes and increasingly approximate elsewhere.

\section{Forced response and resonance}\pathtag{core}

A vibrating system subjected to a time-varying applied force
$F(t)$ has the equation of motion
\[
m\ddot{x} + c\dot{x} + kx = F(t).
\]
The general solution is the sum of the homogeneous (free) response,
which decays in time for $\zeta > 0$, and a particular (steady-state)
response driven by $F(t)$. The free response is determined by initial
conditions and dies out; the steady-state response continues
indefinitely so long as $F(t)$ is applied.

For a sinusoidal driving force $F(t) = F_0 \cos(\omega t)$, the
steady-state response is
\[
x_{\text{ss}}(t) = X(\omega) \cos(\omega t - \phi(\omega)),
\]
where the response amplitude and phase are
\[
X(\omega) = \frac{F_0/k}{\sqrt{(1 - r^2)^2 + (2\zeta r)^2}}, \quad
\tan\phi(\omega) = \frac{2\zeta r}{1 - r^2},
\]
with $r = \omega/\omega_n$ the dimensionless frequency ratio. The
quantity $F_0/k$ is the static deflection the spring would
experience under a constant force $F_0$; the ratio $X/(F_0/k)$ is
the \engterm{dynamic amplification factor}.

At $\omega \ll \omega_n$ ($r \ll 1$), the amplification is
approximately $1$: the system follows the driving force quasi-
statically. At $\omega \gg \omega_n$ ($r \gg 1$), the amplification
approaches $1/r^2$: the system's mass-inertia dominates, and the
response amplitude is small compared to the static deflection. At
$\omega = \omega_n$ ($r = 1$), \engterm{resonance}, the amplification
is $1/(2\zeta)$, which can be very large for lightly damped systems.
A structure with $\zeta = 0.01$ at resonance has an amplification of
$50$; with $\zeta = 0.001$, it is $500$.

The resonance amplification is the working tool and the working
hazard of structural dynamics. A lightly damped natural frequency
at the frequency of a driving force amplifies the system's response
by $1/(2\zeta)$, which can exceed the structure's capacity even when
the static force is small. A driving force at any frequency well
away from $\omega_n$ produces a response no larger than $F_0/k$,
the static deflection. The design discipline is to either keep the
driving frequencies well separated from the natural frequencies, or
to add damping to limit the resonance amplification.

The phase $\phi$ has a corresponding pattern. At $\omega \ll
\omega_n$, the response is in phase with the force; at $\omega =
\omega_n$, the response lags by $90^{\circ}$ (the force is in phase
with the velocity, the displacement lags); at $\omega \gg \omega_n$,
the response lags by $180^{\circ}$ (the response is out of phase
with the force). The phase shift at resonance is the signature that
identifies a measured resonance experimentally: the displacement
and velocity are $90^{\circ}$ apart, and the driving force is in
phase with the velocity.

\engterm{Transmissibility}. A vibrating support transmits its
motion to a mounted mass. The dimensionless ratio of the mass's
amplitude to the support's amplitude is the transmissibility:
\[
\text{TR}(\omega) = \sqrt{\frac{1 + (2\zeta r)^2}{(1 - r^2)^2 + (2
\zeta r)^2}}.
\]
The transmissibility crosses unity at $r = \sqrt{2}$ and approaches
zero as $r \to \infty$. The principle of vibration isolation is
based on this: a mounted mass that is to be isolated from support
vibration must be mounted on springs and dampers whose combined
natural frequency $\omega_n$ is well below the lowest driving
frequency $\omega_{\text{drive}}$, so that $r > \sqrt{2}$ and TR <
1. The further above $\sqrt{2}$, the better the isolation. The
isolation is independent of the magnitude of the support motion; it
depends only on the frequency ratio.

The flip side is that an isolated mass that experiences a transient
disturbance (an impulse, a startup) is set into oscillation at its
own $\omega_n$. The transient response decays at the system's
damping rate. Designing an isolator with low $\omega_n$ for steady-
state isolation can therefore produce poor transient performance;
the trade-off is the standard design balance in vibration isolation.

\begin{principle}[Resonance is the hazard and the tool]
The amplification of a forced response at the natural frequency,
$1/(2\zeta)$, is both the engineering hazard (a small force at the
right frequency produces a destructive response) and the
engineering tool (a tuned system responds preferentially to one
frequency and rejects others). Every dynamic system in the book is
designed either to avoid resonance (suspension bridges, building
columns, machine shafts) or to exploit it (musical instruments,
filters, resonance-based sensors). The design choice rests on the
ratio of driving-force amplitude, structural damping, and the
relative cost of the two failure modes.
\end{principle}

\section{Multi-degree-of-freedom systems and modal analysis}
\pathtag{standard}

A system with $n$ generalised coordinates has $n$ equations of
motion. For a linear system with mass matrix $M$, damping matrix $C$,
and stiffness matrix $K$ (all $n \times n$, symmetric, positive-
definite in the standard case), the equations of motion are
\[
M\ddot{\vec{q}} + C\dot{\vec{q}} + K\vec{q} = \vec{F}(t),
\]
a coupled system in the displacement vector $\vec{q}(t)$.

The undamped free vibration ($\vec{F} = 0$, $C = 0$) admits solutions
of the form $\vec{q}(t) = \vec{\phi} \cos(\omega t - \phi_0)$, with
$\vec{\phi}$ a constant vector. Substituting,
\[
(K - \omega^2 M)\vec{\phi} = \vec{0}.
\]
For non-trivial $\vec{\phi}$, the determinant must vanish:
\[
\det(K - \omega^2 M) = 0.
\]
This is the \engterm{generalised eigenvalue problem}. It has $n$
solutions for $\omega^2$, the \engterm{natural angular frequencies}
$\omega_1^2 \leq \omega_2^2 \leq \cdots \leq \omega_n^2$, with
corresponding eigenvectors $\vec{\phi}_1, \vec{\phi}_2, \ldots,
\vec{\phi}_n$, the \engterm{mode shapes}. Each mode shape describes
the relative displacements of the $n$ generalised coordinates when
the system oscillates purely in that mode; the natural frequency
gives the rate of oscillation of that mode.

The mode shapes are orthogonal with respect to the mass and
stiffness matrices: $\vec{\phi}_i^T M \vec{\phi}_j = 0$ and
$\vec{\phi}_i^T K \vec{\phi}_j = 0$ for $i \neq j$. The orthogonality
lets the engineer decouple the system: defining \engterm{modal
coordinates} $\eta_k$ by $\vec{q}(t) = \sum_k \eta_k(t) \vec{\phi}_k$
and substituting, the equations of motion separate into $n$
independent SDOF equations,
\[
m_k \ddot{\eta}_k + c_k \dot{\eta}_k + k_k \eta_k = \vec{\phi}_k^T
\vec{F}(t),
\]
with $m_k = \vec{\phi}_k^T M \vec{\phi}_k$, $k_k = \vec{\phi}_k^T K
\vec{\phi}_k$, and $\omega_k^2 = k_k/m_k$. The decoupling is exact for
the undamped case and approximate for damping that satisfies the
classical-damping condition (commonly the Rayleigh model $C = \alpha
M + \beta K$ for scalars $\alpha, \beta$).

Modal analysis is the working procedure for any structure with more
than two degrees of freedom: solve the eigenvalue problem once,
extract the natural frequencies and mode shapes, transform the
forcing to modal coordinates, solve $n$ SDOF problems, and transform
the response back to physical coordinates. The procedure is the basis
of structural dynamics, earthquake engineering, machinery vibration
analysis, and the modal-superposition method in finite-element
software.

\engterm{Participation factors}. Not every mode contributes equally
to the response to a given excitation. For a base excitation (the
support moves) on a many-degree-of-freedom system, each mode's
contribution is weighted by its participation factor $\Gamma_k =
\vec{\phi}_k^T M \vec{r} / m_k$, where $\vec{r}$ is the rigid-body
unit vector in the direction of the base motion. Modes with small
participation factors contribute little; in seismic analysis it is
standard to retain only enough modes to capture $90\,\%$ of the
total participating mass and to ignore the rest.

\section{Continuous systems}\pathtag{mastery}

A vibrating string, beam, plate, or shell has infinitely many
degrees of freedom, indexed by position along (or across) the body.
The equations of motion are partial differential equations, and the
modal analysis generalises to a continuous-spectrum problem with
infinitely many natural frequencies and mode shapes.

\subsection{The vibrating string}

A taut string of length $L$, mass per unit length $\rho$, and
tension $T$ has the wave equation
\[
\frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial t^2} = c^2 \frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial
x^2}, \quad c = \sqrt{T/\rho},
\]
with $y(x, t)$ the lateral displacement and $c$ the wave speed
along the string. For a string fixed at both ends, the natural
modes are
\[
y_n(x, t) = A_n \sin\left(\frac{n\pi x}{L}\right) \cos(\omega_n t -
\phi_n),
\]
with natural frequencies
\[
\omega_n = \frac{n\pi c}{L} = \frac{n\pi}{L}\sqrt{T/\rho}, \quad n =
1, 2, 3, \ldots.
\]
The frequencies are an integer harmonic series; the fundamental is
$\omega_1 = \pi c/L$, the next harmonics are at $2\omega_1, 3\omega_1,
\ldots$. The integer harmonic structure is what makes vibrating
strings musically pleasing, the subject of chapter 7 of this volume.

\subsection{The Euler-Bernoulli beam}

A slender beam of length $L$, mass per unit length $\rho A$ (with
$\rho$ the material density and $A$ the cross-sectional area),
flexural rigidity $EI$, has the equation of motion for small
transverse vibrations
\[
\rho A \frac{\partial^2 y}{\partial t^2} + EI \frac{\partial^4 y}{
\partial x^4} = w(x, t),
\]
with $w(x, t)$ the distributed transverse force per unit length and
$y(x, t)$ the transverse deflection. The fourth-spatial-derivative
makes the beam dispersive: the wave speed depends on wavelength.
The boundary conditions at the beam's ends fix the natural-frequency
spectrum.

For a simply supported beam of length $L$, the natural frequencies
are
\[
\omega_n = (n\pi)^2 \sqrt{\frac{EI}{\rho A L^4}}, \quad n = 1, 2, 3,
\ldots.
\]
The frequency ratio is $1 : 4 : 9 : 16 : \cdots$, not harmonic, which
is why a struck beam sounds like a clang rather than a pitch.

For a cantilever beam, the natural-frequency-determining equation
is transcendental ($\cos\beta_n L \cosh\beta_n L + 1 = 0$, with
$\omega_n = \beta_n^2 \sqrt{EI/\rho A}$); the first few roots give
$\beta_1 L \approx 1.875$, $\beta_2 L \approx 4.694$, $\beta_3 L
\approx 7.855$, $\beta_4 L \approx 10.996$. The frequencies are not
in any simple ratio.

\subsection{Plates, shells, and three-dimensional bodies}

Two-dimensional and three-dimensional continuous systems have
correspondingly richer modal spectra. The mode shapes of a
rectangular plate, simply supported on all sides, are products of
sine functions in the two directions, with natural frequencies
proportional to $(m/L_x)^2 + (n/L_y)^2$ for integer mode numbers
$m, n$. The mode shapes of a circular drum head are Bessel functions
times sines or cosines. The mode shapes of a wine glass include
ring modes that are the basis of the "singing wineglass" experiment.
Chapter 9 of this volume develops plate and shell vibration in
more detail.

\section{Random vibration}\pathtag{mastery}

Real loadings are often not sinusoidal but random: turbulent wind on
a building, road roughness on a vehicle, ocean waves on an offshore
platform, jet-engine pressure on an aircraft skin. The driving
function $F(t)$ is then a random process, and the system response
is itself a random process. The analysis tool is the
\engterm{frequency response function} $H(\omega)$ of the system
combined with the \engterm{power spectral density} $S_F(\omega)$ of
the forcing.

For a linear SDOF system, the steady-state response to a stationary
random input has power spectral density
\[
S_x(\omega) = |H(\omega)|^2 S_F(\omega),
\]
where $|H(\omega)|^2 = 1/[k^2 ((1-r^2)^2 + (2\zeta r)^2)]$ is the
squared magnitude of the system's transfer function. The mean-square
response is the integral
\[
\langle x^2 \rangle = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} S_x(\omega) \,d\omega.
\]
For white-noise input ($S_F(\omega) = S_0$ constant) and small
damping, the integral is dominated by the resonance region and
evaluates to
\[
\langle x^2 \rangle \approx \frac{\pi S_0}{2 k^2 \zeta \omega_n} =
\frac{\pi S_0}{2 c k}.
\]
The mean-square response is inversely proportional to the damping
$c$. For Gaussian forcing, the response is Gaussian, and the
probability of exceeding any threshold can be computed from
$\langle x^2 \rangle$ and the threshold's level.

Random vibration analysis is the working tool of aerospace structural
design (jet-engine and aerodynamic-buffet response), automotive
suspension design (road-roughness response), offshore platform
design (ocean-wave response), and any engineering domain where the
load is irreducibly stochastic. The chapter touches the framework
only at introduction level; the full treatment is the subject of
chapter 14 of Volume X (maintenance failure under random loading)
and chapter 8 of Volume IX (system response to random inputs).

\section{Vibration measurement and signature analysis}\pathtag{standard}

A measurement of a vibrating system's response yields a time history
$x(t)$ from which the engineer can extract the system's modal
properties (natural frequencies, mode shapes, damping ratios) and
identify the operating regime. The standard tools:

\engterm{Accelerometers} are the most common sensors: a small
seismic mass on a piezoelectric or capacitive transducer, producing
a voltage proportional to acceleration over a defined bandwidth.
Piezoelectric accelerometers have flat response from a few Hz to
tens of kHz; capacitive accelerometers measure down to DC and are
the choice for low-frequency structural vibration (typically below
$50\,\si{\hertz}$). Range and resolution vary widely with the
specific sensor; modern industrial accelerometers report from
$\sim 10^{-6}\,g$ to $\sim 10^4\,g$ depending on model.

\engterm{Spectral analysis} converts the time history to its
frequency content. The fast Fourier transform (FFT) of $x(t)$ gives
the spectrum $X(\omega)$; the magnitude $|X(\omega)|^2$ is the
power spectrum. Peaks in the power spectrum identify natural
frequencies; the width of each peak (the "Q" of the resonance)
identifies damping. The frequency resolution of an FFT is the
inverse of the sample-record length; a $1\,\si{\second}$ record gives
$1\,\si{\hertz}$ resolution, a $10\,\si{\second}$ record $0.1\,\si{
\hertz}$.

\engterm{Modal identification} extracts mode shapes from
simultaneous measurements at many points. Excitation by impact
hammer (an instrumented hammer that produces a broadband impulse) or
by shaker (a controlled force at a chosen frequency or chirp)
produces a frequency response function $H(\omega)$ at each
measurement point. The peaks of $|H(\omega)|$ at each point identify
the natural frequencies; the relative amplitudes at the peak
identify the mode shape. Commercial modal-analysis software
performs this routinely on hundreds of measurement points.

\engterm{Signature analysis} compares a vibration spectrum to a
baseline (the same machine when it was new, the same structure
when it was first commissioned) and flags deviations. Standard
machinery faults have standard signatures:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \engterm{Imbalance}: a peak at the rotation frequency $1
    \times$, possibly with overtones at $2\times$, $3\times$.
  \item \engterm{Misalignment}: peaks at $1\times$ and $2\times$
    with $2\times$ comparable to or larger than $1\times$.
  \item \engterm{Bearing wear}: broad-band noise increase, plus
    specific peaks at the bearing's race-defect frequencies
    (computed from the bearing's geometry).
  \item \engterm{Looseness}: subharmonic peaks at $0.5\times$ or
    impulsive responses with multiple harmonics.
  \item \engterm{Resonance}: a peak that doesn't move with rotation
    speed and is sharp (small bandwidth).
\end{itemize}

The signatures let the maintenance engineer diagnose the fault from
a vibration measurement without disassembling the machine. The
discipline of routine vibration monitoring, with computer-stored
spectra trended over months and years, is now standard practice
for critical industrial machinery and is the basis of
condition-based maintenance.

\begin{estimation}
\textbf{Estimate the natural frequency of a $300\,\si{\milli\meter}$
metre stick clamped at one end as a cantilever, made of pine wood
of cross-section $25\,\si{\milli\meter} \times 6\,\si{\milli\meter}$.}

\smallskip
\textit{Estimate, before reading on.}

\smallskip
For a cantilever of length $L$, mass per unit length $\rho A$, and
flexural rigidity $EI$, the first natural angular frequency is
\[
\omega_1 = (1.875)^2 \sqrt{\frac{EI}{\rho A L^4}}.
\]
For pine, $E \approx 10\,\text{GPa}$, density $\rho \approx 500\,\si{
\kilogram\per\meter\cubed}$. The cross-section is $b \times h = 25
\times 6\,\si{\milli\meter}$, so $A = 1.5 \times 10^{-4}\,\si{\meter
\squared}$, and assuming the load is in the strong direction
(perpendicular to the $25\,\si{\milli\meter}$ width), $I = bh^3/12 =
0.025 \times (0.006)^3/12 = 4.5 \times 10^{-10}\,\si{\meter\tothe{4}}$.
Then
\[
EI = 10^{10} \times 4.5 \times 10^{-10} = 4.5\,\si{\newton\meter\squared},
\]
\[
\rho A = 500 \times 1.5 \times 10^{-4} = 0.075\,\si{\kilogram\per\meter},
\]
\[
\frac{EI}{\rho A L^4} = \frac{4.5}{0.075 \times (0.3)^4} = \frac{4.5}{
6.07 \times 10^{-4}} \approx 7{,}400\,\si{\per\second\squared}.
\]
So $\omega_1 \approx (3.516) \sqrt{7{,}400} \approx 3.516 \times 86
\approx 303\,\si{\radian\per\second}$, and $f_1 = \omega_1/(2\pi)
\approx 48\,\si{\hertz}$.

Estimate: roughly $50\,\si{\hertz}$.

The postmortem: the answer is sensitive to the orientation of the
cross-section (strong or weak direction), to the elastic modulus
(pine varies from $8$ to $14\,\text{GPa}$ depending on species and
grain direction), and to the exact length. A measurement on a real
metre stick clamped in a vice typically returns a fundamental in
the range $30$ to $60\,\si{\hertz}$, in agreement. The project of
this chapter is to measure this for the reader's own apparatus and
compare to the prediction.
\end{estimation}

\section{Failure: Tacoma Narrows, Millennium Bridge, machinery
resonance}\pathtag{core}

Vibration failures share a structure: a system has a lightly-damped
natural mode at a frequency near the dominant excitation, and the
amplification at resonance produces motion the structure cannot
sustain. The chapter's failure section names the canonical cases.

\subsection{Tacoma Narrows: aeroelastic flutter, revisited}

Chapter 5 of this volume introduced the original Tacoma Narrows
Bridge collapse of 7 November 1940 as the canonical case of a
missing degree of freedom in an analytical-mechanics analysis. The
vibration perspective on the same event is that the bridge had a
lightly-damped torsional natural mode whose interaction with a
steady wind produced negative aerodynamic damping above a critical
wind speed of approximately $19\,\si{\meter\per\second}$; the
effective total damping became negative; the torsional vibration
grew exponentially until the deck's structural capacity was exceeded
\cite{acc:tacoma-narrows-1941}.

The aerodynamic-damping mechanism is the key. The deck section in
steady wind experiences a pitching moment that depends on the angle
of attack. When the deck oscillates torsionally, the angle of attack
changes; the resulting pitching moment has both a component in phase
with the angular displacement (an aerodynamic stiffness) and a
component in phase with the angular velocity (an aerodynamic
damping). At low wind speeds, the aerodynamic damping is positive
and adds to the structural damping. Above a critical wind speed, the
aerodynamic damping turns negative; at the critical speed, the
total damping (structural plus aerodynamic) reaches zero; above the
critical speed, the total damping is negative, and the natural mode
becomes self-excited.

In the engineering-vibration framework of this chapter, Tacoma
Narrows is a case of a structurally lightly-damped natural mode
($\zeta \approx 0.005$, typical of welded-steel bridge structures)
that loses its damping to an aerodynamic feedback term. The
remediation in subsequent bridge designs is either higher structural
damping (truss-stiffened decks add internal friction at the truss
joints) or aerodynamic-section shapes whose damping does not turn
negative (streamlined deck sections used on modern long-span
bridges).

\subsection{Millennium Bridge: synchronous lateral excitation}

The London Millennium Footbridge across the Thames opened on 10
June 2000 and was closed two days later, on 12 June 2000, when
crowds of pedestrians produced visible lateral oscillations of the
deck of approximately $70\,\si{\milli\meter}$ amplitude at
frequencies near $1\,\si{\hertz}$. The mechanism is
\engterm{synchronous lateral excitation}: pedestrians instinctively
adjust their gait to maintain balance when the deck moves laterally,
locking their per-step lateral footfall forces in phase with the
deck's lateral velocity and feeding energy into the bridge's
lateral mode. Above a critical pedestrian density of approximately
$1.3\,\text{persons}/\si{\meter\squared}$, the coherent forcing
exceeds the deck's structural damping and the lateral mode becomes
self-excited \cite{acc:dallard-millennium-2001}.

The mechanism is novel in two ways. First, the forcing is itself a
function of the motion, like the aerodynamic feedback in Tacoma
Narrows, but generated by human balance reflexes rather than
fluid-structure interaction. Second, the random-phase
incoherent-forcing model used in classical pedestrian-load analysis
(which underestimates the response by $\sim\sqrt{N}$ rather than
$\sim N$ in the pedestrian count) does not apply once the
synchronisation sets in. The Arup engineering team led by Pat Dallard
identified the mechanism in the year following the closure and
remediated the bridge by installing $26$ fluid-viscous dampers and
$52$ tuned mass dampers, adding approximately $20\,\%$ critical
damping to the lateral modes and moving the critical pedestrian
density above any plausible loading. The bridge reopened on 22
February 2002.

In the engineering-vibration framework, the Millennium Bridge is a
case of a structurally lightly-damped natural mode whose damping is
overcome by a load-coupled feedback term. The same diagnostic
principle as Tacoma Narrows applies: an apparently inadequately
analysed structure exhibits a vibration failure mode in service that
the design analysis did not include. The structural-vibration
analysis was correct for the mode it represented; the analysis did
not represent the mode that was excited.

\subsection{Machinery resonance: less famous but more common}

Industrial machinery (pumps, fans, compressors, turbines, motors,
generators) almost always has some component whose natural
frequency falls in the operating-speed band of the machine. When the
operating speed coincides with the component's natural frequency,
the component's response is amplified by $1/(2\zeta)$ and may
exceed its design capacity. The result is rapid wear, fatigue
failure of bolts or shafts, audible drumming, or in extreme cases
sudden fracture.

The standard machinery-resonance failure modes:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \engterm{Critical speed of rotating machinery}. A spinning
    shaft has bending natural frequencies (whirl modes); when the
    rotation speed coincides with a whirl natural frequency, the
    shaft's centroid orbits the geometric axis at large amplitude.
    Designers either keep the operating speed below the first
    critical (subcritical operation, conservative but heavy) or
    accelerate quickly through the criticals to a supercritical
    operating point (lighter, but transient critical-speed crossings
    must be managed).
  \item \engterm{Piping resonance}. Process plant piping vibrates
    when the fluid pulsation frequency (from positive-displacement
    pumps, compressors, or flow-induced turbulence) coincides with
    the piping's mechanical natural frequency. The vibration
    fatigues the piping at supports and at branch connections, often
    leaking after months or years rather than failing catastrophically.
  \item \engterm{Foundation resonance}. A machine's foundation,
    treated as a rigid structure in much design analysis, has its
    own modes. When a machine's vibration excites a foundation mode,
    the machine-foundation system's response can exceed the
    machine-on-stiff-foundation prediction by factors of $10$ or
    more. The mitigation is foundation stiffening, foundation
    isolation, or operating-speed change.
  \item \engterm{Blade-pass resonance in turbomachinery}. A rotor
    blade passes a stator vane at a frequency equal to (rotor speed)
    $\times$ (number of stator vanes). If this frequency coincides
    with a blade's natural frequency, the blade vibrates at large
    amplitude and fatigues at its hub fillet. Design discipline is
    to detune the blade so the resonance falls outside the operating
    range.
\end{itemize}

The remediation in every case is the same: change the natural
frequency (stiffness or mass modification), change the driving
frequency (operating-speed change), or add damping. The choice
depends on the cost and the design constraints.

\begin{principle}[Vibration failure is a frequency-coincidence
failure]
A vibration-related structural or machinery failure occurs when (1)
a lightly-damped natural mode of the system coincides in frequency
with (2) a driving excitation, and (3) the resulting resonance
amplification exceeds the structure's capacity. Mitigating any one
of the three (moving the natural frequency, removing or detuning
the excitation, adding damping, or strengthening the structure)
prevents the failure. The design discipline is to identify the
natural frequencies, the expected excitation spectrum, the damping,
and to verify that no coincidence in the operating envelope
produces a response above capacity.
\end{principle}

\section{Project}\pathtag{core}

\begin{project}[Characterise the vibration response of a household
object]
The reader instruments a household structural object (a metre stick
clamped as a cantilever, a wooden beam supported as a simply-
supported member, a metal ruler, a guitar string, or similar),
measures its frequency response under impact excitation, extracts
the lowest natural frequency and the damping ratio, and compares
to a beam- or string-vibration theoretical prediction. The
deliverable is a twenty- to thirty-page engineering report.

\textbf{Track}. Physical measurement plus analysis. \textbf{Hazard
class}: none. Impact excitation by a light mallet or by snapping the
finger against the object.

\textbf{Apparatus}. A measurement device: a smartphone with a free
accelerometer-logging app (most smartphones have built-in
accelerometers logged at $50$ to $200\,\si{\hertz}$), or a contact
microphone attached to the object, or a strain-gauge bridge as in
the Chapter 3 project. The reader's chosen vibrating object. A
clamp or support fixture. A timing reference (the smartphone's
clock).

\textbf{Procedure}. The reader sets up the vibrating object as a
known geometric configuration (cantilever, simply supported, free-
free), excites it by a light impact at one end, and records the
time history of the response. The reader records at least five
different excitation locations and at least three different impact
strengths.

\textbf{Analysis}. From each recorded time history, the reader:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Estimates the fundamental natural period from the time
    between successive zero-crossings of the response.
  \item Computes the natural frequency in Hz.
  \item Estimates the damping ratio from the exponential decay of
    successive amplitude peaks (the logarithmic-decrement method).
  \item Computes the FFT of the recorded signal and identifies the
    first three peaks; reports their frequencies and the ratios
    among them.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Theoretical prediction}. The reader computes the
theoretical fundamental natural frequency from the object's geometry
and material properties using the cantilever-beam formula, the
simply-supported-beam formula, or the string formula as
appropriate.

\textbf{Comparison}. The reader compares the measured fundamental
frequency to the predicted fundamental frequency and discusses
sources of discrepancy.

\textbf{Reflection ($1{,}500$ to $3{,}000$ words)}. The reader
addresses:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Which sources of damping (material hysteresis, joint
    friction, air drag, acoustic radiation) dominate at the
    measured damping ratio.
  \item Whether the higher-frequency peaks in the FFT match the
    theoretical higher modes (in their ratios) or are something
    else (room acoustics, sensor artifacts, electrical noise).
  \item One feature of the measurement (resolution, accelerometer
    noise floor, finite record length) that limits the experiment's
    accuracy.
  \item One quantitative prediction (next mode's natural frequency,
    response to a different boundary condition) the reader could
    make from the measured data and verify with a second
    experiment.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Deliverable}. The recorded time histories, the FFT plots,
the natural-frequency and damping estimates, the theoretical
predictions, the comparison, and the reflection.

\textbf{Time}. Two to four hours of measurement, four to eight hours
of analysis (FFT, log-decrement extraction), four to eight hours of
write-up.
\end{project}

\section{Exercises}

The exercises mix calculation, derivation, estimation, simulation,
design, diagnosis, failure analysis, and judgment.

\subsection*{Calculation}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
A mass of $2\,\si{\kilogram}$ on a spring of stiffness $200\,\si{
\newton\per\meter}$ has no damping. Find the natural frequency in Hz
and the period.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A pendulum has period $T = 1.5\,\si{\second}$ for small swings. Find
its length, assuming $g = 9.81\,\si{\meter\per\second\squared}$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A SDOF system has $m = 5\,\si{\kilogram}$, $k = 500\,\si{\newton\per
\meter}$, and damping ratio $\zeta = 0.05$. Find the damped natural
frequency. State the damping coefficient $c$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A SDOF system with natural frequency $\omega_n = 10\,\si{\radian\per
\second}$ and damping ratio $\zeta = 0.02$ is driven at $\omega =
\omega_n$ by a force of amplitude $F_0 = 50\,\text{N}$. Find the
steady-state response amplitude.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A vibration isolator has natural frequency $f_n = 5\,\si{\hertz}$.
Find the transmissibility at a driving frequency of $20\,\si{
\hertz}$, with $\zeta = 0.1$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A simply-supported beam of length $4\,\si{\meter}$, density $\rho =
7{,}800\,\si{\kilogram\per\meter\cubed}$, cross-section $50\,\si{
\milli\meter} \times 25\,\si{\milli\meter}$ (depth direction
$25\,\si{\milli\meter}$), and $E = 200\,\text{GPa}$. Find the first
three natural frequencies.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A guitar string of length $0.65\,\si{\meter}$ and mass per unit length
$5\,\si{\gram\per\meter}$ has tension $T = 100\,\text{N}$. Find its
fundamental frequency and the next two harmonics.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A two-degree-of-freedom system has $M = \text{diag}(2, 1)\,\si{
\kilogram}$ and $K$ matrix $\begin{pmatrix} 3 & -1 \\ -1 & 1
\end{pmatrix}\,\si{\newton\per\meter}$. Find the two natural
frequencies and the corresponding mode shapes.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Derivation}\pathtag{standard}

\begin{exercise}
Derive the equation of motion of a SDOF system from Newton's second
law for a mass on a horizontal spring. State the assumption about
the spring's linearity and the meaning of the displacement variable.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Derive the natural frequency $\omega_n = \sqrt{g/L}$ for a simple
pendulum at small amplitude, starting from the work-energy theorem
or the Lagrangian.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Derive the logarithmic decrement $\delta = 2\pi\zeta/\sqrt{1-\zeta^2}$
from the underdamped solution $x(t) = X_0 e^{-\zeta\omega_n t}\cos(
\omega_d t - \phi)$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Derive the resonance amplification factor $1/(2\zeta)$ for a
forced SDOF system at $\omega = \omega_n$, starting from the
frequency response function $X(\omega)/(F_0/k)$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Derive the natural frequencies of a string fixed at both ends from
the wave equation $\partial^2 y/\partial t^2 = c^2 \partial^2 y/
\partial x^2$ with boundary conditions $y(0, t) = y(L, t) = 0$.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Estimation}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the natural frequency of a chair when shaken side-to-side
by a sitting person. State the dominant mass (the chair, the
person, both) and the stiffness mechanism.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the fundamental natural frequency of a residential wood
floor joist of $4\,\si{\meter}$ span carrying a uniform live load
of $2\,\text{kN}/\text{m}$. State assumptions about the joist's
cross-section and material; compare to the typical resonant
frequency band of walking.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the damping ratio of a swing set in a park, measured by
the rate at which a swing's amplitude decays after the rider stops
pumping. State the dominant damping source.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the natural frequency of a tall office building's first
sway mode. State assumptions about the building's height, mass per
unit height, and effective stiffness.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the natural frequency of a soda bottle when partially
filled with water and tapped at the rim. State the analogy with a
Helmholtz resonator and what happens to the frequency as the water
level rises.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Simulation}\pathtag{mastery}

\begin{exercise}
Simulate the response of an undamped SDOF system to a sinusoidal
forcing at $\omega/\omega_n = 0.5$, $0.99$, $1.01$, $1.5$, $2.0$.
Plot the response over twenty periods of forcing and identify the
beat frequencies for the cases close to resonance.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Simulate the response of a damped SDOF system with $\zeta = 0.01$,
$0.1$, $0.5$ to a sinusoidal forcing at $\omega = \omega_n$.
Estimate the time required for each system to reach steady state.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Implement a two-degree-of-freedom modal-analysis solver. Input: mass
matrix $M$, stiffness matrix $K$. Output: natural frequencies and
mode shapes. Verify on the system of Calculation 8.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Design}\pathtag{standard}

\begin{exercise}
Design a passive vibration isolator for a $100\,\si{\kilogram}$
machine that vibrates at $50\,\si{\hertz}$ with $0.1\,\si{\milli
\meter}$ amplitude. Specify the isolator natural frequency, the
damping ratio, the spring stiffness, and the resulting
transmissibility.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Design a tuned mass damper for a tall building whose lowest sway
mode is at $0.5\,\si{\hertz}$. Specify the damper's mass (as a
fraction of the building's first-mode modal mass), its natural
frequency, and its damping ratio. State the expected reduction in
the building's response amplitude.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Design a measurement campaign to identify the first three natural
frequencies of an industrial centrifugal pump. Specify the
accelerometer placement, the excitation method, and the signal-
processing chain.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Design a vibration-monitoring threshold table for a critical
industrial fan: green/yellow/red alert levels for the $1\times$,
$2\times$, and broad-band components of the bearing vibration
signature. State the rationale for each threshold.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Diagnosis and reverse engineering}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
An industrial fan exhibits a vibration spectrum with a peak at
$1\times$ rotation frequency that has grown over six months. Identify
the most probable cause, the corrective action, and the maintenance
discipline that would have caught the trend earlier.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A residential building exhibits an audible humming during high
winds at a frequency of $\sim 4\,\si{\hertz}$. Identify the most
probable mechanism and the verification measurement.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A pedestrian bridge exhibits visible vertical oscillation at
$\sim 2\,\si{\hertz}$ when groups of pedestrians cross. Identify
whether this is a synchronous-vertical-excitation phenomenon
analogous to the Millennium Bridge's lateral mode, or a different
mechanism, and state the diagnostic measurement.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A residential dishwasher in operation rattles the cabinet at a
particular speed during the spin cycle. Identify the most probable
dynamic mechanism (rotor imbalance, foundation resonance,
shipping-restraint pin not removed, or other) and the diagnostic
procedure.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Failure analysis}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
Re-read the Federal Works Agency report on the Tacoma Narrows
Bridge collapse \cite{acc:tacoma-narrows-1941}. State the
structural-damping ratio of the deck's torsional mode estimated by
the report, the aerodynamic-damping term that turned the total
damping negative, and the critical wind speed at which the
transition occurred.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Re-read the Arup analysis of the Millennium Bridge lateral
oscillation \cite{acc:dallard-millennium-2001}. Identify the
critical pedestrian density, the lateral natural frequency of the
bridge, and the per-pedestrian lateral force coefficient that the
report uses to predict the onset of synchronous excitation.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A high-speed turbine has lost a fan blade after a few hundred hours
of service. The blade's fracture surface shows fatigue striations.
State whether blade-pass resonance is a plausible cause, what
calculation would verify, and what design discipline (detuning,
damping, inspection interval) would have prevented the failure.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Judgment}\pathtag{standard}

\begin{exercise}
A junior engineer argues that vibration analysis is overkill for a
small consumer product and that designing the product to be "stiff
enough" is sufficient. Write a one-paragraph reply that takes the
argument seriously and states when a vibration analysis is required.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A senior colleague proposes to mount a vibrating machine on
soft springs to reduce transmitted vibration. The machine's lowest
operating speed is $5\,\si{\hertz}$. Write a one-paragraph reply
that addresses the trade-off between steady-state isolation and
transient response, and states a defensible recommendation for the
isolator natural frequency.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Identify a vibrating system in your immediate environment whose
natural frequencies you have never measured (a desk, a window, a
ceiling fan, a bookshelf). State, in $200$ to $300$ words, the
mode shapes you would expect, the natural-frequency band, and the
mechanism by which the system might fail in service.
\end{exercise}
