\noindent
The previous chapter treated the flow as inviscid: zero viscous
stress, no boundary layer, no separation, no turbulent dissipation.
Real fluids are viscous, and viscosity acts in a thin region
adjacent to solid surfaces and inside shear layers. This chapter
develops the Navier-Stokes equation for incompressible viscous
flow, derives the laminar exact solutions that close pipe flow
and Couette flow, develops Prandtl's boundary-layer theory, and
treats the transition to turbulence and its consequences for drag.
The reader leaves able to compute the Reynolds number for any
flow, identify whether the flow is laminar or turbulent, estimate
the boundary-layer thickness and skin-friction coefficient on a
surface, and recognise separation, stall, and the conditions under
which a viscous flow's drag departs from the inviscid prediction.

\section{Newton's law of viscosity}\pathtag{core}

A fluid resists shear deformation. If two parallel plates separated
by a thin layer of fluid are moved relative to one another at
constant velocity $U$ across a gap $h$, the steady-state velocity
profile in the gap is linear, and the shear stress required to
maintain the motion is proportional to the velocity gradient:
\[
\tau = \mu \frac{du}{dy}.
\]
The relation defines the \engterm{dynamic viscosity} $\mu$, a
material property of the fluid. A fluid obeying this linear
relation is a \engterm{Newtonian fluid}; air, water, and most
common liquids and gases at engineering conditions are Newtonian to
a very good approximation.

The dynamic viscosity has SI units of $\si{\pascal\second}$, or
equivalently $\si{\kilogram\per\meter\per\second}$. Representative
values at $20\,\degC$, current as of 2025 per
\textcite{text:white-fluid-mechanics}:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Air: $\mu \approx 1.8\times 10^{-5}\,\si{\pascal\second}$.
  \item Water: $\mu \approx 1.0\times 10^{-3}\,\si{\pascal\second}$.
  \item Glycerin: $\mu \approx 1.5\,\si{\pascal\second}$.
  \item Honey (room temperature): $\mu \approx 10\,\si{\pascal\second}$,
    variable with sugar content and water content.
  \item Glass at room temperature: $\mu \sim 10^{21}\,\si{\pascal
    \second}$ (a solid, by the engineering definition of flow over
    relevant time scales).
\end{itemize}

Viscosity is strongly temperature dependent. Gases become more
viscous as they heat (faster molecular motion produces more
momentum transfer between layers); liquids become less viscous as
they heat (the molecular cohesion weakens). Engine oil drops by a
factor of $100$ in viscosity between $0\,\degC$ and $100\,\degC$,
which is why oil ratings carry temperature qualifiers (SAE 5W-30
specifies viscosity at both cold-start and operating temperatures).

The \engterm{kinematic viscosity} is
\[
\nu = \frac{\mu}{\rho},
\]
with SI units of $\si{\meter}^2/\si{\second}$. The kinematic
viscosity is the diffusivity of momentum in the fluid: it has the
same dimensions as a thermal diffusivity or a mass diffusivity, and
it characterises how rapidly velocity disturbances spread by
viscous action. For air at room conditions, $\nu \approx 1.5\times
10^{-5}\,\si{\meter}^2/\si{\second}$; for water, $\nu \approx
1.0\times 10^{-6}\,\si{\meter}^2/\si{\second}$. Water has lower
kinematic viscosity than air, despite higher dynamic viscosity,
because water is much denser.

\engterm{Non-Newtonian fluids} are those for which the
stress-strain-rate relation is nonlinear. Important classes
include:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \engterm{Shear-thinning} (pseudoplastic): viscosity decreases
    with shear rate. Paint, blood, and many polymer solutions.
  \item \engterm{Shear-thickening} (dilatant): viscosity increases
    with shear rate. Concentrated cornstarch in water; certain
    body-armour fluids.
  \item \engterm{Bingham plastic}: behaves as a rigid solid below a
    yield stress, as a Newtonian fluid above. Toothpaste, drilling
    mud, fresh concrete.
  \item \engterm{Viscoelastic}: combines viscous and elastic
    responses. Polymer melts, biological tissues, silly putty.
\end{itemize}

Non-Newtonian fluids are the subject of \engterm{rheology}; we
treat them only in passing here. The Navier-Stokes equation of the
next section assumes a Newtonian fluid; rheologically complex
fluids require constitutive extensions.

\section{The Navier-Stokes equation}\pathtag{core}

Adding the viscous stress to the Euler equation of chapter 11
gives the \engterm{Navier-Stokes equation} for incompressible
Newtonian flow:
\[
\rho\frac{D\vec{u}}{Dt} = -\nabla p + \mu\nabla^2\vec{u} +
\rho\vec{g}.
\]
The viscous term $\mu\nabla^2\vec{u}$ replaces the surface-stress
contribution that the inviscid analysis ignored. The equation was
derived independently by Claude-Louis Navier in 1822 (from a
molecular argument) and by George Gabriel Stokes in 1845 (from a
continuum argument); both names are attached.

Combined with the incompressible continuity equation $\nabla\cdot
\vec{u} = 0$, the Navier-Stokes system is four equations in four
unknowns ($u_x$, $u_y$, $u_z$, $p$) in three space dimensions plus
time. The system is a coupled nonlinear partial differential
equation system whose general well-posedness for arbitrary initial
data is one of the seven Clay Millennium Prize problems
\cite{text:white-fluid-mechanics}; the engineering problem is not
mathematical existence but tractable analytical and numerical
solutions in specific geometries.

The Navier-Stokes equation is the foundation of nearly all of fluid
mechanics from chapter 12 onward. The strategy of analytical fluid
mechanics is to find configurations in which the equation
simplifies enough to admit a closed-form solution (the exact
solutions of section 12.4), find configurations in which one term
is small and can be neglected (the boundary-layer approximation of
section 12.5), or solve the equation numerically on a discretised
grid (the computational-fluid-dynamics methods of section 12.7).

\engterm{Non-dimensionalisation}. The Navier-Stokes equation has a
characteristic non-dimensional form. Introduce a characteristic
length scale $L$, a characteristic velocity $U$, a characteristic
time scale $L/U$, and a characteristic pressure $\rho U^2$. Let
$\vec{u}^* = \vec{u}/U$, $\vec{x}^* = \vec{x}/L$, $t^* = tU/L$,
$p^* = (p - p_\infty)/(\rho U^2)$. The non-dimensional
Navier-Stokes equation becomes
\[
\frac{D\vec{u}^*}{Dt^*} = -\nabla^* p^* + \frac{1}{\text{Re}}
\nabla^{*2}\vec{u}^* + \frac{1}{\text{Fr}^2}\hat{g},
\]
where
\[
\text{Re} = \frac{\rho U L}{\mu} = \frac{U L}{\nu}, \quad
\text{Fr} = \frac{U}{\sqrt{gL}}.
\]
The \engterm{Reynolds number} Re is the ratio of inertial to
viscous forces; the \engterm{Froude number} Fr is the ratio of
inertial to gravitational forces. Two flows with the same
non-dimensional geometry and the same Re (and Fr, if gravity is
relevant) have the same non-dimensional velocity and pressure
fields. This is the principle of \engterm{dynamic similarity}: it
is why small wind-tunnel models tested at appropriate Reynolds
number predict the behaviour of full-scale aircraft, ship hulls,
and bridges.

\begin{principle}[The Reynolds number sets the flow regime]
The Reynolds number $\text{Re} = \rho U L/\mu = U L/\nu$ measures
the ratio of inertial to viscous forces in a flow. Low Re ($\ll 1$)
corresponds to creeping flow dominated by viscosity; intermediate
Re ($\sim 1$ to $\sim 10^3$) to laminar viscous flow; high Re
($\gtrsim 10^4$ to $10^5$, geometry dependent) to turbulent flow.
The Reynolds number is the single most important dimensionless
parameter in viscous fluid mechanics, and the working engineer
computes it before doing any other analysis.
\end{principle}

Reynolds's original 1883 experiment on the transition from laminar
to turbulent flow in a glass-walled pipe used a dye filament
injected on the pipe centreline. At low flow rates the filament
remained straight and parallel to the pipe axis, indicating
laminar flow; at high flow rates the filament dispersed across
the pipe, indicating turbulent mixing. Reynolds identified the
combination $\rho U D/\mu$ (with $D$ the pipe diameter) as the
parameter governing the transition, and reported a critical value
near $2000$ for the onset of intermittent turbulence and near
$13{,}000$ for fully developed turbulence under his conditions
\cite[pp.~935--982]{paper:reynolds1883}. The result is the
empirical basis of the laminar-turbulent transition criterion in
pipe flow.

\section{Pipe flow: laminar and turbulent}\pathtag{core}

A steady flow of incompressible Newtonian fluid through a long
circular pipe of diameter $D$ at low Reynolds number admits an
exact solution. With the axial coordinate $z$ along the pipe and
the radial coordinate $r$ from the centreline, the steady
Navier-Stokes equation in cylindrical coordinates gives, under
the assumptions of fully developed flow ($\partial u_z/\partial z
= 0$, $u_r = 0$, $u_\theta = 0$) and constant axial pressure
gradient $dp/dz = -G$,
\[
\mu\frac{1}{r}\frac{d}{dr}\left(r\frac{du_z}{dr}\right) = -G.
\]
Integrating twice with boundary conditions $u_z(r=R) = 0$ (no-slip
at the wall, with $R = D/2$) and $du_z/dr |_{r=0} = 0$ (symmetry
at the centreline),
\[
u_z(r) = \frac{G(R^2 - r^2)}{4\mu}.
\]
This is the \engterm{Hagen-Poiseuille velocity profile}, a
parabola with the maximum on the axis and zero at the wall
(figure~\ref{fig:vol03ch12:poiseuille-profile}). The
centreline velocity is $u_{\max} = GR^2/(4\mu)$, and the
cross-section-averaged velocity is half the maximum:
\[
\bar{u} = \frac{GR^2}{8\mu}.
\]
\input{volumes/03-force/ch12-viscous-flow-boundary-layers/figures/fig-poiseuille-profile}

The volume flow rate is $Q = \pi R^2 \bar{u} = \pi R^4 G/(8\mu)$,
giving the \engterm{Hagen-Poiseuille law}
\[
Q = \frac{\pi D^4 (-\Delta p)}{128 \mu L},
\]
where $L$ is the pipe length and $\Delta p = p_2 - p_1 = -GL$ is
the (negative) pressure change along the length. The flow rate
scales as $D^4$: doubling the diameter increases the flow rate by
sixteen for the same pressure drop. The same scaling explains why
arterial flow drops sharply when arteries narrow; a $50\,\%$
reduction in lumen diameter reduces the flow rate by a factor of
$16$.

The \engterm{Darcy-Weisbach equation} casts the pressure drop in
terms of a \engterm{friction factor} $f$:
\[
\Delta p = f\frac{L}{D}\,\frac{\rho \bar{u}^2}{2}.
\]
For laminar pipe flow, the friction factor is
\[
f = \frac{64}{\text{Re}_D}, \quad \text{Re}_D = \frac{\rho \bar{u} D}{\mu}.
\]
The relation is exact under the Hagen-Poiseuille assumptions and
holds for $\text{Re}_D \lesssim 2300$.

Above $\text{Re}_D \approx 2300$, the laminar pipe flow becomes
unstable to perturbations and transitions to turbulence. The
turbulent regime begins with intermittent slugs of disturbed flow
at $\text{Re}_D \sim 2300$ to $4000$ and reaches fully developed
turbulence above $\text{Re}_D \sim 4000$. In the turbulent regime
the velocity profile is much flatter than the laminar parabola
(with the velocity dropping rapidly only in a thin near-wall
region) and the friction factor depends on the relative pipe-wall
roughness $\epsilon/D$ in addition to $\text{Re}_D$. The contrast
between the two profile shapes is drawn in
figure~\ref{fig:vol03ch12:laminar-turbulent}: the turbulent
profile fills the core almost uniformly and falls steeply through
a thin near-wall region, so it carries a far larger wall velocity
gradient and a larger friction factor than the laminar parabola at
the same flow rate.

\input{volumes/03-force/ch12-viscous-flow-boundary-layers/figures/fig-laminar-turbulent-profiles}

The \engterm{Moody chart}
\cite{paper:moody-1944,text:white-fluid-mechanics}, shown
schematically in figure~\ref{fig:vol03ch12:moody},
plots $f$ versus $\text{Re}_D$ for various values of $\epsilon/D$,
condensing decades of empirical and analytical work into a single
working tool. For fully turbulent flow, the
implicit Colebrook-White equation \cite{paper:colebrook-1939}
\[
\frac{1}{\sqrt{f}} = -2\log_{10}\left(\frac{\epsilon/D}{3.7} +
\frac{2.51}{\text{Re}_D\sqrt{f}}\right)
\]
gives the friction factor; explicit approximations (Swamee-Jain,
Haaland) are used in practice for direct calculation. The Haaland
form
\[
\frac{1}{\sqrt{f}} = -1.8\log_{10}\!\left[
\left(\frac{\epsilon/D}{3.7}\right)^{1.11} +
\frac{6.9}{\text{Re}_D}\right]
\]
is explicit in $f$ and stays within a few percent of the
Colebrook-White root across the engineering range, which is why it
is the workhorse for direct calculation; the solver in
\texttt{code/moody.py} carries both forms and the friction-factor
table behind figure~\ref{fig:vol03ch12:moody} lives in
\texttt{data/moody\_friction.csv}. The chart
shows three regimes:

\input{volumes/03-force/ch12-viscous-flow-boundary-layers/figures/fig-moody-chart}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \engterm{Laminar regime} ($\text{Re}_D < 2300$): $f$
    depends only on $\text{Re}_D$, with $f = 64/\text{Re}_D$.
  \item \engterm{Transitional regime} ($2300 < \text{Re}_D <
    4000$): unstable, intermittent, no single-curve description.
  \item \engterm{Turbulent regime} ($\text{Re}_D > 4000$): $f$
    depends on $\text{Re}_D$ and $\epsilon/D$. At high enough
    Reynolds number (the \engterm{fully rough regime}), $f$
    depends only on $\epsilon/D$ and not on $\text{Re}_D$.
\end{itemize}

The Moody chart is the working tool for pipe-flow design in every
hydraulics, HVAC, and process-engineering application.

\section{Couette flow and other exact laminar solutions}\pathtag{standard}

\engterm{Couette flow}. A Newtonian fluid between two infinite
parallel plates, one at $y = 0$ and stationary, the other at
$y = h$ moving at velocity $U$ in the $x$-direction. In steady
state with no pressure gradient and gravity ignored, the
Navier-Stokes equation reduces to $d^2 u_x/dy^2 = 0$, with
solution
\[
u_x(y) = \frac{U y}{h}.
\]
The velocity profile is linear; the shear stress is constant
across the gap, $\tau = \mu U/h$. The force per unit area required
to move the upper plate is the same shear stress, which appears as
the viscous drag on the plate. This is the canonical configuration
for a rotating viscometer (concentric cylinders with a small gap),
which measures $\mu$ directly from the torque required to
maintain a known rotation rate.

\engterm{Combined Couette-Poiseuille flow}. The same geometry with
a pressure gradient added gives a velocity profile that is the
superposition of the linear Couette profile and a parabolic
Poiseuille component. For favourable pressure gradient
($dp/dx < 0$ with flow in the $+x$-direction), the profile is a
forward-leaning shape with maximum velocity above the moving
plate's level; for adverse pressure gradient, the profile has a
reverse-flow region near the stationary plate when the pressure
gradient is large enough. The three profiles are drawn together in
figure~\ref{fig:vol03ch12:couette-poiseuille}. The
adverse-pressure-gradient case is
the simplest analytical model of \engterm{flow reversal} that
underlies boundary-layer separation in section 12.6.

\input{volumes/03-force/ch12-viscous-flow-boundary-layers/figures/fig-couette-poiseuille-profiles}

\engterm{Stokes's first problem}. An infinite plate impulsively
accelerated to constant velocity $U$ at $t = 0$ in a fluid at
rest. The momentum diffuses out from the plate into the fluid; the
velocity profile at time $t$ is
\[
u_x(y, t) = U\,\text{erfc}\left(\frac{y}{2\sqrt{\nu t}}\right),
\]
where erfc is the complementary error function. The velocity
disturbance penetrates to a depth $\delta(t) \sim \sqrt{\nu t}$;
the relation $\delta \sim \sqrt{\nu t}$ is the canonical viscous
diffusion length, the spatial analogue of the heat-diffusion
relation of chapter 4 of Volume IV.

\engterm{Stokes flow past a sphere}. A sphere of radius $a$
moving at velocity $U$ through a viscous fluid at very low
Reynolds number (Re $\ll 1$, the \engterm{creeping flow} regime)
experiences a drag
\[
F_D = 6\pi \mu a U,
\]
the \engterm{Stokes drag law} \cite{paper:ch02t3b-stokes-1851}. The law underlies
the design of falling-ball viscometers, the settling velocity of
small particles in suspensions, and the propulsion of microorganisms.
The Stokes drag holds for Re $< 1$ on the sphere; for larger Re,
inertial corrections (Oseen, 1910) and eventual wake separation
modify the relation. The drag coefficient $C_D = F_D/(\tfrac{1}{2}
\rho U^2 \pi a^2) = 24/\text{Re}$ in the Stokes regime, $\sim 0.4$
at $\text{Re} = 10^5$ (the canonical drag-crisis transition), and
varies non-monotonically across the transition (the topic of
section 12.6). The full sphere-drag curve across nine decades of
Reynolds number is drawn in
figure~\ref{fig:vol03ch12:sphere-drag}; the tabulated standard
curve is in \texttt{data/sphere\_drag\_curve.csv} and the
iterative settling-velocity solver that uses it is in
\texttt{code/sphere\_terminal\_velocity.py}.

\input{volumes/03-force/ch12-viscous-flow-boundary-layers/figures/fig-sphere-drag-curve}

A settling or terminal-velocity calculation needs only the part of
this curve that brackets the answer, and the working device is to
know which of three regimes the body sits in. The Stokes line
$C_D = 24/\text{Re}$ holds below $\text{Re}\approx1$; the
Schiller-Naumann correlation $C_D = (24/\text{Re})(1 + 0.15\,
\text{Re}^{0.687})$ bridges the intermediate range; and above
$\text{Re}\approx10^3$ the coefficient settles onto the Newton
plateau $C_D\approx0.44$ until the drag crisis intervenes. The three
regimes and their boundaries are drawn in
figure~\ref{fig:vol03ch12:terminal-velocity-regimes}; the
regime check that worked examples~3 and~8 carry is a reading of which
segment of this curve applies.

\section{Boundary-layer theory}\pathtag{core}

Ludwig Prandtl's 1904 Heidelberg paper
\cite{paper:prandtl1904} resolved a paradox that had stood since
d'Alembert: an inviscid analysis of flow past a body gives zero
drag, but real bodies in real flow experience large drag. Prandtl
observed that viscosity, however small, cannot be neglected in a
thin region adjacent to the surface where the no-slip condition
$\vec{u} = 0$ at the wall forces a steep velocity gradient. He
named this region the \engterm{boundary layer} and showed that
inside it the Navier-Stokes equations simplify to a system that is
analytically tractable.

For a flat plate at zero angle of attack in a uniform freestream
$U_\infty$, the boundary-layer assumption is that the layer
thickness $\delta(x)$ is much smaller than the streamwise distance
$x$. Inside the layer, the streamwise momentum equation becomes
\[
u\frac{\partial u}{\partial x} + v\frac{\partial u}{\partial y} =
-\frac{1}{\rho}\frac{\partial p}{\partial x} + \nu\frac{\partial^2
u}{\partial y^2},
\]
with the wall-normal momentum equation reducing to $\partial
p/\partial y = 0$ (the pressure does not vary across the layer).
The pressure inside the boundary layer is therefore set by the
inviscid outer flow.

\input{volumes/03-force/ch12-viscous-flow-boundary-layers/figures/fig-boundary-layer-growth}

For a flat plate at zero pressure gradient ($\partial p/\partial x
= 0$), Heinrich Blasius (1908) solved the boundary-layer equations
by a similarity transformation
\cite{text:schlichting-boundary-layer}. The boundary-layer
thickness grows as
\[
\delta(x) \approx 5.0\,\sqrt{\frac{\nu x}{U_\infty}} =
\frac{5.0\,x}{\sqrt{\text{Re}_x}},
\]
where $\text{Re}_x = U_\infty x/\nu$ is the local Reynolds number
based on streamwise distance. The local skin-friction coefficient
is
\[
C_f(x) = \frac{\tau_w(x)}{\tfrac{1}{2}\rho U_\infty^2} =
\frac{0.664}{\sqrt{\text{Re}_x}},
\]
where $\tau_w$ is the wall shear stress. The total drag on a plate
of length $L$ is obtained by integrating $\tau_w(x)$:
\[
F_D = \tfrac{1}{2}\rho U_\infty^2 \cdot L \cdot \bar{C}_f, \quad
\bar{C}_f = \frac{1.328}{\sqrt{\text{Re}_L}}.
\]
These are the Blasius solutions; they form the analytical
foundation of boundary-layer engineering. The shape of the
underlying similarity profile is shown in
figure~\ref{fig:vol03ch12:blasius}: plotting $u/U_\infty$ against
the similarity variable $\eta = y\sqrt{U_\infty/(\nu x)}$ collapses
the profile at every streamwise station onto a single curve, with
the velocity reaching $99\%$ of the freestream at
$\eta_{99}\approx5.0$. The tabulated profile is in
\texttt{data/blasius\_profile.csv}, and the shooting-method solver
that reproduces it (and the wall-shear constant $f''(0)\approx
0.33206$ that fixes $C_f$) is in \texttt{code/blasius.py}.

\input{volumes/03-force/ch12-viscous-flow-boundary-layers/figures/fig-blasius-profile}

\begin{mastery}
\textbf{The Blasius similarity solution.} For a flat plate at zero
pressure gradient the boundary-layer equations admit a similarity
reduction: the streamwise velocity depends on $x$ and $y$ only
through the combination $\eta = y\sqrt{U_\infty/(\nu x)}$. Introduce
a stream function $\psi = \sqrt{\nu x\,U_\infty}\,f(\eta)$, so that
$u = \partial\psi/\partial y = U_\infty f'(\eta)$ and $v =
-\partial\psi/\partial x$. Substituting into the streamwise
momentum equation $u\,u_x + v\,u_y = \nu\,u_{yy}$ collapses the
partial differential equation to the ordinary differential equation
\[
2f''' + f f'' = 0,
\]
with boundary conditions $f(0) = 0$ (no penetration), $f'(0) = 0$
(no-slip), and $f'(\infty) = 1$ (match the freestream). The equation
is third order and nonlinear with a split boundary condition, so no
elementary closed form exists; it is integrated numerically. The
standard route is a shooting method: guess the wall curvature
$f''(0)$, integrate to large $\eta$, and adjust the guess until
$f'(\infty)\to 1$. The converged value $f''(0) = 0.33206$ fixes the
wall shear stress $\tau_w = \mu (\partial u/\partial y)_{y=0} =
0.332\,\rho U_\infty^2/\sqrt{\text{Re}_x}$, and hence the
skin-friction coefficient $C_f = 0.664/\sqrt{\text{Re}_x}$. The
$\sqrt{\nu x/U_\infty}$ scaling of the thickness, the
$1/\sqrt{\text{Re}_x}$ scaling of $C_f$, and the $1/\sqrt{
\text{Re}_L}$ scaling of the integrated drag all descend from this
one similarity reduction.
\end{mastery}

\engterm{Displacement thickness}. The boundary layer reduces the
mass flux past a surface compared to what an inviscid flow would
deliver. The \engterm{displacement thickness} $\delta^*$ is the
distance the outer streamline would have to be moved outward to
recover the inviscid mass flux:
\[
\delta^* = \int_0^\infty \left(1 - \frac{u}{U_\infty}\right) dy.
\]
For the Blasius profile, $\delta^*/\delta \approx 0.345$, so the
displacement thickness is about a third of the full
boundary-layer thickness. The displacement thickness is the
correction to the inviscid streamline geometry needed to account
for the boundary layer in coupled analyses.

\engterm{Momentum thickness}. The \engterm{momentum thickness}
$\theta$ is
\[
\theta = \int_0^\infty \frac{u}{U_\infty}\left(1 - \frac{u}{U_
\infty}\right) dy.
\]
The Kármán momentum integral relates $\theta$ to the wall shear
stress:
\[
\frac{d\theta}{dx} = \frac{C_f(x)}{2}
\]
(for zero pressure gradient). This integral form is the
working tool for approximate boundary-layer calculations when the
exact Blasius solution does not apply.

\engterm{Turbulent boundary layer}. Above a critical Reynolds
number (typically $\text{Re}_x \sim 5\times 10^5$ for a smooth
flat plate, lower with surface roughness or freestream disturbance),
the laminar boundary layer transitions to turbulence. The turbulent
boundary layer is thicker than the laminar layer at the same
$\text{Re}_x$:
\[
\delta(x) \approx 0.37\,x\,\text{Re}_x^{-1/5}.
\]
The skin-friction coefficient is also larger:
\[
C_f(x) \approx 0.0592\,\text{Re}_x^{-1/5}, \quad
\bar{C}_f \approx 0.074\,\text{Re}_L^{-1/5}.
\]
The turbulent boundary layer has a different internal structure
than the laminar layer: a thin \engterm{viscous sublayer} near the
wall where $u^+ = y^+$ (with $u^+ = u/u_\tau$ the velocity scaled
by the friction velocity $u_\tau = \sqrt{\tau_w/\rho}$ and $y^+ =
y u_\tau/\nu$ the wall-distance in viscous units), a \engterm{log
layer} where $u^+ = (1/\kappa)\ln y^+ + B$ (with $\kappa \approx
0.41$, $B \approx 5.0$), and an \engterm{outer wake region} where
the profile deviates from the log law. This \engterm{law of the
wall} structure is one of the few quantitative universals of
turbulent flow; figure~\ref{fig:vol03ch12:law-of-wall} shows it in
the customary semilog form, where the viscous sublayer and the log
layer appear as a straight rising segment and a straight log
segment bridged by a buffer region. The structure also dictates
near-wall meshing in turbulence-resolving CFD: a wall-resolved
calculation needs the first grid point inside the viscous sublayer
($y^+\lesssim1$), while a wall-function calculation places it in
the log layer.

\input{volumes/03-force/ch12-viscous-flow-boundary-layers/figures/fig-law-of-the-wall}

\engterm{Pressure gradient effects}. A favourable pressure
gradient (pressure decreasing in the flow direction) thins the
boundary layer and stabilises it against transition; an adverse
pressure gradient (pressure increasing) thickens the layer and
can produce \engterm{separation}: the wall-shear stress reverses
sign and the flow detaches from the surface, forming a separated
wake. Separation is the canonical failure mode of boundary-layer
attached flow, and it underlies airfoil stall, diffuser stall,
bluff-body wake formation, and many of the high-drag
configurations the next section enumerates. The mechanism is drawn
in figure~\ref{fig:vol03ch12:separation}: the near-wall fluid
decelerates under the rising pressure, the wall shear stress
vanishes at the separation point, and the flow reverses and
detaches downstream.

\section{Drag, lift, and the dimensionless numbers}\pathtag{standard}

The drag and lift on a body in a flow are conventionally
expressed in dimensionless form:
\[
C_D = \frac{F_D}{\tfrac{1}{2}\rho U^2 A}, \quad
C_L = \frac{F_L}{\tfrac{1}{2}\rho U^2 A},
\]
with $A$ a reference area (frontal area for drag, planform area
for lift). The coefficients depend on the body's shape and on the
dimensionless flow parameters. For incompressible viscous flow
without surface tension, the only dimensionless parameter is the
Reynolds number; for high-speed flow, the Mach number (chapter 13
of this volume) enters; for free-surface flow, the Froude number;
for surface-tension-dominated flow, the Weber number.

\engterm{Drag mechanisms}. The total drag on a body has two
components: \engterm{pressure drag} (form drag) from the integrated
pressure distribution over the body surface, and \engterm{skin-
friction drag} from the integrated viscous shear stress over the
body surface. The relative importance depends on the body shape
and on Reynolds number.

For a streamlined body (an airfoil at low angle of attack, a
fish-shaped fuselage), skin friction dominates: the pressure
recovers nearly fully over the rear of the body, and the drag
coefficient is small, $C_D \sim 0.01$ to $0.05$ for a well-designed
airfoil.

For a bluff body (a sphere, a cylinder, a cube), pressure drag
dominates: the flow separates from the rear of the body, leaving a
low-pressure wake that the surface pressure cannot recover. The
drag coefficient is large, $C_D \sim 0.4$ to $1.5$, and depends
weakly on Reynolds number above the transition regime.

For a flat plate at zero incidence (parallel to the flow), the
pressure drag vanishes and the drag coefficient equals the
skin-friction coefficient. For a flat plate normal to the flow,
the pressure drag dominates and $C_D \approx 2.0$ (the high end of
the bluff-body range).

\engterm{The drag crisis}. The drag coefficient of a sphere or
circular cylinder exhibits a characteristic drop near $\text{Re} =
3\times 10^5$, from $C_D \approx 0.5$ at slightly subcritical Re to
$C_D \approx 0.2$ at slightly supercritical Re. The drop is caused
by the laminar-turbulent transition in the boundary layer just
upstream of the separation point: a turbulent boundary layer
resists separation longer than a laminar one (because the turbulent
mixing transfers high-momentum freestream fluid to the near-wall
region), so the wake narrows and the pressure drag decreases
sharply. The drag crisis is the working basis of golf-ball dimple
design (the dimples trip the boundary layer to turbulence at lower
$\text{Re}$, lowering the drag in the operational range) and of
sandpaper strips on the front of competitive bicycle helmets
\cite{text:anderson-aerodynamics}. The drop is drawn for both a
sphere and a circular cylinder in
figure~\ref{fig:vol03ch12:drag-crisis}: each curve holds a
subcritical plateau, falls steeply through the crisis near
$\text{Re}=3\times10^5$, and recovers to a supercritical level
several times lower than the plateau.

\input{volumes/03-force/ch12-viscous-flow-boundary-layers/figures/fig-drag-crisis}

\engterm{The dimensionless numbers of fluid mechanics}. Beyond the
Reynolds number, several other dimensionless groups carry physical
meaning in specific regimes:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \engterm{Froude number} $\text{Fr} = U/\sqrt{gL}$: ratio of
    inertial to gravitational forces. Governs free-surface flow
    (ship waves, river flow, weir discharge).
  \item \engterm{Mach number} $M = U/a$, with $a$ the local speed
    of sound: ratio of inertial to elastic-compressive forces.
    Governs compressible flow (chapter 13 of this volume).
  \item \engterm{Weber number} $\text{We} = \rho U^2 L/\sigma$, with
    $\sigma$ the surface tension: ratio of inertial to
    surface-tension forces. Governs droplet breakup, splashing,
    spray formation.
  \item \engterm{Knudsen number} $\text{Kn} = \lambda/L$, with
    $\lambda$ the molecular mean free path: ratio of molecular to
    continuum length scales. Continuum mechanics applies for
    $\text{Kn} < 10^{-2}$; rarefied gas dynamics for higher Kn.
  \item \engterm{Strouhal number} $\text{St} = fL/U$, with $f$ a
    characteristic oscillation frequency: governs unsteady flow,
    vortex shedding, flutter.
\end{itemize}

The working engineer keeps these in a short table and computes
the relevant ones before applying any flow analysis.

\engterm{The Kármán vortex street}. A circular cylinder in cross
flow at $\text{Re}$ between about $40$ and $200$ sheds vortices
alternately from its top and bottom surfaces, forming a regular
pattern of staggered vortices in the wake. The shedding frequency
is set by the Strouhal number: $\text{St} = fD/U \approx 0.21$ over
a wide range of $\text{Re}$. The shedding produces an oscillating
lift on the cylinder, which can drive resonant vibration of
slender structures: chimneys, suspension-bridge cables, transmission
lines, marine risers. The standard design discipline against vortex-
induced vibration is to ensure that the shedding frequency at the
operating wind speed does not coincide with the structure's
natural frequencies; aerodynamic devices (strakes, helical
fairings) on a cylinder disrupt the shedding pattern and reduce
the response amplitude.

\section{Turbulence: the unfinished problem}\pathtag{mastery}

Above a critical Reynolds number that depends on the geometry, a
laminar flow becomes unstable to small perturbations and
transitions to \engterm{turbulence}: a flow regime characterised
by chaotic, three-dimensional, multi-scale velocity fluctuations.
Turbulent flows have been the central unsolved problem of fluid
mechanics since Reynolds's 1883 paper
\cite{paper:reynolds1883}; the field has developed extensive
empirical, analytical, and computational tools, but a complete
predictive theory of turbulence at arbitrary Reynolds number does
not yet exist.

\engterm{Statistical description}. A turbulent velocity field is
decomposed into a mean and a fluctuation:
\[
u_i(\vec{x}, t) = \bar{u}_i(\vec{x}) + u'_i(\vec{x}, t),
\]
where the overbar denotes a time average. The Reynolds-averaged
Navier-Stokes (RANS) equation governs the mean field:
\[
\bar{u}_j\frac{\partial \bar{u}_i}{\partial x_j} = -\frac{1}{\rho}
\frac{\partial \bar{p}}{\partial x_i} + \nu\nabla^2 \bar{u}_i
- \frac{\partial (\overline{u'_i u'_j})}{\partial x_j}.
\]
The new term $-\overline{u'_i u'_j}$ is the \engterm{Reynolds
stress tensor}, a correlation of velocity fluctuations that acts
as an additional stress on the mean flow. The Reynolds stress is
not a closed-form function of the mean field; the \engterm{closure
problem} of turbulence is to model the Reynolds stress in terms of
mean-field quantities. No universally accurate closure exists; all
working models (mixing length, $k$-$\epsilon$, $k$-$\omega$,
Reynolds-stress models, large-eddy simulations) make assumptions
that are accurate in some flow regimes and inaccurate in others.

\begin{mastery}
\textbf{Turbulence closures, from mixing length to RANS models.}
The closure problem is to express the Reynolds stress
$-\overline{u'_i u'_j}$ in terms of the mean field. The oldest
device is Boussinesq's \engterm{eddy-viscosity hypothesis}: model
the Reynolds stress as if it were a viscous stress with an enhanced,
flow-dependent viscosity $\nu_t$,
\[
-\overline{u'_i u'_j} = \nu_t\!\left(\frac{\partial\bar u_i}{
\partial x_j} + \frac{\partial\bar u_j}{\partial x_i}\right)
- \tfrac{2}{3}k\,\delta_{ij},
\]
with $k = \tfrac12\overline{u'_i u'_i}$ the turbulent kinetic
energy. Prandtl's \engterm{mixing-length model} sets $\nu_t = \ell_m^2
|d\bar u/dy|$ with $\ell_m$ a geometry-dependent length; near a
wall $\ell_m = \kappa y$, which recovers the log law. Two-equation
models carry transport equations for two turbulence scalars: the
$k$-$\epsilon$ model solves for $k$ and the dissipation rate
$\epsilon$ and sets $\nu_t = C_\mu k^2/\epsilon$; the $k$-$\omega$
model solves for $k$ and a specific dissipation rate $\omega$ and
behaves better in adverse pressure gradients and near walls. The
shear-stress-transport (SST) model blends $k$-$\omega$ near the wall
with $k$-$\epsilon$ in the freestream. All of these are RANS models:
they predict the time-averaged field and are affordable enough for
routine design, but their accuracy depends on whether the calibration
flows match the flow at hand, and every one of them mispredicts
separation onset under strong adverse pressure gradients to some
degree. Resolving the eddies directly (LES, DNS) removes the closure
assumptions at large cost; the cost ratio between RANS and a
wall-resolved LES at engineering Reynolds numbers runs to several
orders of magnitude.
\end{mastery}

\engterm{The energy cascade}. Kolmogorov's 1941 theory of locally
isotropic turbulence holds that at sufficiently high Reynolds
number, energy injected at large scales (by some forcing) cascades
through intermediate scales toward small scales by inertial
nonlinear interactions, until the smallest scales (the
\engterm{Kolmogorov scale} $\eta = (\nu^3/\epsilon)^{1/4}$, with
$\epsilon$ the dissipation rate per unit mass) become small
enough that viscous dissipation converts the kinetic energy to
heat. In the inertial subrange between the forcing scale and the
Kolmogorov scale, the energy spectrum follows Kolmogorov's
$k^{-5/3}$ law:
\[
E(k) = C\epsilon^{2/3}k^{-5/3}, \quad C \approx 1.5.
\]
The Kolmogorov $-5/3$ scaling has been confirmed in many
turbulent flows at very high Reynolds number. The corresponding
ratio of largest to smallest length scales is $L/\eta \sim
\text{Re}^{3/4}$; resolving all scales of turbulence in a direct
numerical simulation (DNS) is therefore prohibitively expensive
for engineering Reynolds numbers (a flow at $\text{Re} = 10^6$
has $L/\eta \sim 30{,}000$, requiring $\sim 30{,}000^3 \sim
3\times 10^{13}$ grid points in three dimensions). DNS remains
restricted to research-scale Reynolds numbers; engineering
analysis relies on RANS or large-eddy simulation (LES) with
modelled closures.

\engterm{Practical consequences}. Turbulent flow in engineering
practice is recognised by:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Higher skin-friction drag than laminar at the same Reynolds
    number (the turbulent boundary-layer scaling above).
  \item Higher heat transfer rate (the topic of Volume IV).
  \item Higher mass-transfer rate.
  \item Rapid mixing of dye, smoke, or other tracer scalars.
  \item Broadband acoustic radiation (the noise of a jet engine, a
    waterfall, a turbulent boundary layer on an aircraft wing).
  \item Resistance to separation under adverse pressure gradient
    (the basis of the drag crisis and of vortex generators on
    aircraft wings).
\end{itemize}

\section{Computational fluid dynamics introduced}\pathtag{mastery}

The Navier-Stokes equations admit no general analytical solution.
Numerical solution on a discretised grid is the working tool for
most engineering flow analysis; \engterm{computational fluid
dynamics} (CFD) is the body of techniques that does this.

The principal discretisation methods:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \engterm{Finite difference}: derivatives are approximated
    on a structured grid. Simple to implement, accurate, but
    awkward on complex geometries.
  \item \engterm{Finite volume}: integral conservation laws are
    enforced on each grid cell. The standard method for industrial
    CFD; handles complex geometries through unstructured grids.
  \item \engterm{Finite element}: weak formulation of the
    governing equations, suitable for complex geometries and
    higher-order schemes; the standard method in structural
    mechanics and some CFD codes.
  \item \engterm{Spectral / spectral-element}: expansion in
    smooth basis functions, used for high-accuracy DNS and LES at
    moderate Reynolds numbers.
\end{itemize}

The choice between RANS, LES, and DNS depends on the Reynolds
number and the accuracy requirement:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \engterm{RANS}: solves for the time-averaged flow with a
    turbulence closure model. Most affordable; appropriate for
    steady-state design calculations of bulk quantities (lift,
    drag, pressure distribution). Accuracy depends strongly on the
    closure model's match to the flow type.
  \item \engterm{LES}: resolves the large eddies and models the
    sub-grid scales. Computationally heavier than RANS by factors
    of $10^2$ to $10^4$; appropriate for unsteady flow physics
    where the large-scale dynamics dominates.
  \item \engterm{DNS}: resolves all scales. Computationally
    affordable only at modest Reynolds numbers ($\text{Re} \sim
    10^3$ to $10^5$ depending on geometry). Used for research and
    for validation of LES and RANS closures.
\end{itemize}

The discipline of CFD validation is to compare predictions
against experimental measurements at the same Reynolds number,
geometry, and boundary conditions. A CFD calculation that has
not been validated against experiment is a calculation, not a
prediction. The standard \engterm{verification and validation}
hierarchy distinguishes \engterm{verification} (the code solves
the equations correctly, checked by mesh refinement and
manufactured solutions) from \engterm{validation} (the equations
model the physics correctly, checked against experiment). Both
are required before a CFD prediction is treated as a design
basis.

\section{Worked examples}\pathtag{standard}

The relations of the preceding sections become a working method
only when they are run end to end on numbers. Six worked examples
follow, each one moving from a stated configuration to a quantity
an engineer would report.

\subsection*{Worked example 1: regime, friction factor, and pressure drop in a water main}

Water at $15\,\degC$ ($\rho = 999\,\si{\kilogram\per\meter\cubed}$,
$\mu = 1.14\times10^{-3}\,\si{\pascal\second}$) flows at
$\bar u = 1.5\,\si{\meter\per\second}$ through a commercial-steel
main of diameter $D = 100\,\si{\milli\meter}$, length $L = 200\,\si{
\meter}$, absolute roughness $\epsilon = 0.045\,\si{\milli\meter}$.
Find the pressure drop.

The Reynolds number is
\[
\text{Re}_D = \frac{\rho \bar u D}{\mu} = \frac{999\times1.5\times
0.1}{1.14\times10^{-3}} \approx 1.31\times10^{5},
\]
well into the turbulent regime. The relative roughness is
$\epsilon/D = 0.045/100 = 4.5\times10^{-4}$. The Haaland correlation
gives
\[
\frac{1}{\sqrt{f}} = -1.8\log_{10}\!\left[
\left(\frac{4.5\times10^{-4}}{3.7}\right)^{1.11} +
\frac{6.9}{1.31\times10^{5}}\right]
= -1.8\log_{10}(3.6\times10^{-5} + 5.3\times10^{-5}) \approx 7.27,
\]
so $f \approx 1/7.27^2 \approx 0.0189$. The Darcy-Weisbach pressure
drop is
\[
\Delta p = f\frac{L}{D}\frac{\rho \bar u^2}{2} = 0.0189\times
\frac{200}{0.1}\times\frac{999\times1.5^2}{2} \approx 4.25\times10^4
\,\si{\pascal},
\]
about $42\,\text{kPa}$, or roughly $4.3\,\si{\meter}$ of water
head. A laminar estimate ($f = 64/\text{Re}_D \approx 4.9\times
10^{-4}$) would have under-predicted the drop by a factor near
$39$; computing the regime first is what keeps the answer right.

\subsection*{Worked example 2: laminar boundary layer on a flat plate}

Air at $20\,\degC$ ($\nu = 1.5\times10^{-5}\,\si{\meter}^2/\si{
\second}$, $\rho = 1.2\,\si{\kilogram\per\meter\cubed}$) flows at
$U_\infty = 3\,\si{\meter\per\second}$ over a plate of length
$L = 0.5\,\si{\meter}$. Find the boundary-layer thickness and the
total skin-friction drag per unit span.

The trailing-edge Reynolds number is
\[
\text{Re}_L = \frac{U_\infty L}{\nu} = \frac{3\times0.5}{1.5\times
10^{-5}} = 1.0\times10^{5},
\]
below the smooth-plate transition value near $5\times10^5$, so the
layer is laminar over the whole plate. The Blasius thickness at the
trailing edge is
\[
\delta(L) = \frac{5.0\,L}{\sqrt{\text{Re}_L}} = \frac{5.0\times0.5}{
\sqrt{1.0\times10^{5}}} \approx 7.9\times10^{-3}\,\si{\meter}
\approx 7.9\,\si{\milli\meter}.
\]
The average skin-friction coefficient is $\bar C_f = 1.328/\sqrt{
\text{Re}_L} = 1.328/316 \approx 4.2\times10^{-3}$, so the
drag per unit span (counting one side) is
\[
\frac{F_D}{b} = \tfrac12\rho U_\infty^2 L\,\bar C_f =
0.5\times1.2\times3^2\times0.5\times4.2\times10^{-3} \approx
1.1\times10^{-2}\,\si{\newton\per\meter}.
\]
The layer is millimetres thick and the drag is small; both are
characteristic of low-speed laminar flow over a short plate.

\subsection*{Worked example 3: terminal velocity of a settling particle}

A sand grain of diameter $d = 0.2\,\si{\milli\meter}$ and density
$\rho_s = 2650\,\si{\kilogram\per\meter\cubed}$ settles in water at
$20\,\degC$ ($\rho_f = 998\,\si{\kilogram\per\meter\cubed}$,
$\mu = 1.0\times10^{-3}\,\si{\pascal\second}$). Find the terminal
velocity.

Assume the Stokes regime and check afterward. Balancing weight minus
buoyancy against Stokes drag, $\tfrac{\pi}{6}d^3(\rho_s-\rho_f)g =
3\pi\mu d U$, gives
\[
U = \frac{(\rho_s-\rho_f)g\,d^2}{18\mu} = \frac{(2650-998)\times
9.81\times(2\times10^{-4})^2}{18\times1.0\times10^{-3}} \approx
3.6\times10^{-2}\,\si{\meter\per\second}.
\]
The check on the Reynolds number is $\text{Re} = \rho_f U d/\mu =
998\times0.036\times2\times10^{-4}/10^{-3} \approx 7.2$, which is
not below unity. The Stokes assumption is therefore marginal; the
Schiller-Naumann correlation ($C_D = (24/\text{Re})(1+0.15\,\text{
Re}^{0.687})$) lowers the answer to roughly $2.4\,\si{\centi\meter
\per\second}$, and the iterative solver in
\texttt{code/sphere\_terminal\_velocity.py} returns that value.
The lesson is the same as in example~1: the regime check is part of
the calculation, not an afterthought.

\subsection*{Worked example 4: vortex shedding off a chimney}

A steel chimney of diameter $D = 2\,\si{\meter}$ stands in a steady
wind of $U = 12\,\si{\meter\per\second}$ (air, $\nu = 1.5\times
10^{-5}\,\si{\meter}^2/\si{\second}$). Its lowest cross-wind natural
frequency is $f_n = 0.6\,\si{\hertz}$. Is vortex-induced resonance a
risk?

The Reynolds number is $\text{Re} = UD/\nu = 12\times2/(1.5\times
10^{-5}) = 1.6\times10^{6}$, in the range where the Strouhal number
holds near $\text{St}\approx0.2$. The shedding frequency is
\[
f_s = \text{St}\,\frac{U}{D} = 0.2\times\frac{12}{2} = 1.2\,\si{
\hertz}.
\]
The lock-in wind speed, where $f_s = f_n$, is $U_{\text{lock}} =
f_n D/\text{St} = 0.6\times2/0.2 = 6\,\si{\meter\per\second}$. A
$6\,\si{\meter\per\second}$ wind is a common condition, so the
chimney will pass through lock-in routinely; helical strakes or a
stiffening that raises $f_n$ are warranted.

\subsection*{Worked example 5: drag power on a road vehicle}

A car of frontal area $A = 2.2\,\si{\meter}^2$ and drag coefficient
$C_D = 0.30$ cruises at $U = 100\,\text{km/h} = 27.8\,\si{\meter\per
\second}$ in air ($\rho = 1.2\,\si{\kilogram\per\meter\cubed}$).
Find the drag force and the power spent against drag.

The drag force is
\[
F_D = \tfrac12\rho U^2 A\,C_D = 0.5\times1.2\times27.8^2\times2.2
\times0.30 \approx 3.1\times10^{2}\,\si{\newton},
\]
and the power is $P = F_D U \approx 306\times27.8 \approx 8.5\,\text{
kW}$. The cubic dependence on speed is the headline: raising the
cruise speed to $120\,\text{km/h}$ multiplies the drag power by
$(120/100)^3 \approx 1.73$, to about $14.7\,\text{kW}$. This is why
highway fuel economy falls off steeply with speed.

\subsection*{Worked example 6: scale-model similarity}

A $1/10$-scale model of a submarine is to be tested in a water tunnel
to predict the drag on the full vessel, which cruises at $U_p =
5\,\si{\meter\per\second}$. At what tunnel speed must the model run
for dynamic similarity, and what does the measured model drag imply
for the prototype?

Dynamic similarity in a single-phase, fully submerged, incompressible
flow requires matching the Reynolds number. With the same fluid
($\nu_m = \nu_p$) and the model length $L_m = L_p/10$,
\[
\frac{U_m L_m}{\nu} = \frac{U_p L_p}{\nu} \implies U_m = U_p
\frac{L_p}{L_m} = 5\times10 = 50\,\si{\meter\per\second}.
\]
A $50\,\si{\meter\per\second}$ water-tunnel speed is demanding, which
is one practical reason marine testing often accepts a Reynolds-number
mismatch and corrects for it. If similarity is achieved, the drag
coefficients match, $C_{D,m} = C_{D,p}$, so the prototype drag is
\[
F_{D,p} = F_{D,m}\,\frac{\rho_p U_p^2 A_p}{\rho_m U_m^2 A_m}.
\]
With $A_p = 100\,A_m$ and $U_p^2/U_m^2 = (5/50)^2 = 1/100$, the two
factors cancel and $F_{D,p} = F_{D,m}$: at matched Reynolds number
with the same fluid, the model and prototype carry the same drag
force, a result that surprises until the cancellation is seen.

\subsection*{Worked example 7: flat-plate drag from the momentum integral}

The Blasius drag coefficient $\bar C_f = 1.328/\sqrt{\text{Re}_L}$
can be recovered without the full similarity solution by feeding an
assumed velocity profile through the Kármán momentum integral. The
exercise shows how a one-parameter profile guess delivers an answer
within a few percent of the exact constant, which is the working
method for boundary layers that the Blasius solution does not cover.

Assume the cubic profile $u/U_\infty = \tfrac32(y/\delta) -
\tfrac12(y/\delta)^3$ for $0\le y\le\delta$ and $u = U_\infty$
beyond. The profile satisfies no-slip ($u=0$ at $y=0$), the
freestream match ($u=U_\infty$ at $y=\delta$), and zero curvature at
the wall ($\partial^2 u/\partial y^2 = 0$ at $y=0$, the wall
condition that follows from the momentum equation at zero pressure
gradient). The momentum thickness of this profile is
\[
\theta = \int_0^\delta \frac{u}{U_\infty}\!\left(1 -
\frac{u}{U_\infty}\right)dy = \frac{39}{280}\,\delta \approx
0.139\,\delta,
\]
and the wall shear is
\[
\tau_w = \mu\left(\frac{\partial u}{\partial y}\right)_{y=0} =
\frac{3\mu U_\infty}{2\delta}.
\]
The Kármán relation $d\theta/dx = \tau_w/(\rho U_\infty^2)$ becomes
\[
\frac{39}{280}\frac{d\delta}{dx} = \frac{3\mu}{2\rho U_\infty\,
\delta} \implies \delta\,d\delta = \frac{280}{39}\frac{3\nu}{2
U_\infty}\,dx.
\]
Integrating from $\delta=0$ at $x=0$ gives
\[
\delta(x) = \sqrt{\frac{280}{13}\frac{\nu x}{U_\infty}} \approx
4.64\,\sqrt{\frac{\nu x}{U_\infty}} = \frac{4.64\,x}{\sqrt{\text{Re}_x}},
\]
against the Blasius $5.0$, an error of about $7\,\%$ in the thickness.
The skin-friction coefficient follows from $\tau_w$:
\[
C_f(x) = \frac{\tau_w}{\tfrac12\rho U_\infty^2} = \frac{3\nu}{
U_\infty\,\delta} = \frac{0.646}{\sqrt{\text{Re}_x}},
\]
against the Blasius $0.664$, an error of about $2.7\,\%$, and the
length-averaged value is $\bar C_f = 1.292/\sqrt{\text{Re}_L}$
against the exact $1.328$. The lesson is that the momentum integral
turns a plausible profile into a drag estimate accurate to a few
percent, and the same machinery extends to pressure gradients and to
turbulent profiles where no Blasius solution exists.

\subsection*{Worked example 8: terminal velocity of a hailstone across drag regimes}

A spherical hailstone of diameter $d = 10\,\si{\milli\meter}$ and
density $\rho_s = 900\,\si{\kilogram\per\meter\cubed}$ falls through
air at sea level ($\rho_a = 1.2\,\si{\kilogram\per\meter\cubed}$,
$\mu = 1.8\times10^{-5}\,\si{\pascal\second}$). Find the terminal
velocity, carrying the regime check at every step.

A Stokes estimate ($U = (\rho_s-\rho_a)g d^2/(18\mu)$) gives
$U \approx 900\times9.81\times(10^{-2})^2/(18\times1.8\times10^{-5})
\approx 2.7\times10^{3}\,\si{\meter\per\second}$, which is absurd; the
Stokes Reynolds number it implies, $\text{Re} = \rho_a U d/\mu \sim
1.8\times10^6$, is nine orders of magnitude past the Stokes ceiling
of unity, so the Stokes law does not apply. The hailstone is a bluff
body in the Newton-plateau regime
(figure~\ref{fig:vol03ch12:terminal-velocity-regimes}), where
$C_D\approx0.44$ is roughly constant. Balance weight minus buoyancy
against the bluff-body drag,
\[
\tfrac{\pi}{6}d^3(\rho_s-\rho_a)g = \tfrac12\rho_a U^2\,
\tfrac{\pi}{4}d^2\,C_D,
\]
and solve for the velocity:
\[
U = \sqrt{\frac{4(\rho_s-\rho_a)g\,d}{3\rho_a C_D}} = \sqrt{\frac{
4\times900\times9.81\times10^{-2}}{3\times1.2\times0.44}} \approx
15\,\si{\meter\per\second}.
\]
The regime check confirms the choice: $\text{Re} = \rho_a U d/\mu =
1.2\times15\times10^{-2}/(1.8\times10^{-5}) \approx 1.0\times10^4$,
comfortably inside the $10^3$ to $2\times10^5$ Newton plateau where
$C_D\approx0.44$ holds. A $15\,\si{\meter\per\second}$ impact speed
(about $54\,\text{km/h}$) is consistent with the damage a centimetre
hailstone does to a car roof. The arithmetic is the same as the
raindrop estimate in the exercises, with the regime check deciding
which drag law enters the force balance.

\subsection*{Worked example 9: laminar entrance length in a fuel line}

Flow entering a pipe from a reservoir does not have its fully
developed profile at the inlet; the boundary layers grow from the
walls and merge over an \engterm{entrance length} $L_e$ before the
parabolic Hagen-Poiseuille profile is established. A fuel line
carries diesel ($\rho = 840\,\si{\kilogram\per\meter\cubed}$, $\mu =
3.0\times10^{-3}\,\si{\pascal\second}$) at $\bar u = 0.4\,\si{\meter
\per\second}$ through a $D = 8\,\si{\milli\meter}$ tube. Find the
entrance length and state whether a $0.5\,\si{\meter}$ tube run is
mostly developing or mostly developed.

The Reynolds number is
\[
\text{Re}_D = \frac{\rho\bar u D}{\mu} = \frac{840\times0.4\times
0.008}{3.0\times10^{-3}} \approx 896,
\]
below $2300$, so the flow is laminar and the laminar entrance-length
correlation $L_e \approx 0.05\,\text{Re}_D\,D$ applies:
\[
L_e \approx 0.05\times896\times0.008 \approx 0.36\,\si{\meter}.
\]
The entrance length is $0.36\,\si{\meter}$ out of a $0.5\,\si{\meter}$
run, so most of the tube is in developing flow, where the wall shear
and the pressure gradient are larger than the fully developed values.
A pressure-drop calculation that applied Hagen-Poiseuille over the
whole length would under-predict the drop, because the developing
region carries an extra pressure loss (the profile is being
accelerated near the centre and decelerated near the walls). The
correction is the entrance-loss term: for a laminar developing flow
the additional drop over the developing region is of order
$\rho\bar u^2$, comparable here to the fully developed drop, so it
cannot be neglected. Had the flow been turbulent, the entrance length
would have been far shorter ($L_e\approx4.4\,\text{Re}_D^{1/6}D$,
typically ten to forty diameters), which is why entrance effects are
a laminar-flow concern more than a turbulent one.

\section{End-to-end case study: sizing a pump for a transfer line}\pathtag{standard}

The relations of this chapter become a design method when they are
run together on one system from the fluid and the geometry through to
a sized pump and a verdict. We size a centrifugal pump to move water
from a supply sump to an elevated process tank, working the chain
Reynolds number $\to$ friction factor $\to$ major loss $\to$ minor
losses $\to$ system curve $\to$ operating point $\to$ required power
$\to$ cavitation margin, carrying the numbers at every link. The
layout is the one drawn in figure~\ref{fig:vol03ch12:pump-layout}.

\paragraph{The duty and the fluid.} The plant requires a delivery of
$Q = 25\,\si{\liter\per\second} = 0.025\,\si{\meter\cubed\per\second}$
of water at $20\,\degC$ ($\rho = 998\,\si{\kilogram\per\meter\cubed}$,
$\mu = 1.0\times10^{-3}\,\si{\pascal\second}$, vapour pressure
$p_v = 2.34\,\si{\kilo\pascal}$ absolute). The supply sump free
surface sits $2\,\si{\meter}$ below the pump centreline (a suction
lift), and the delivery tank free surface sits $18\,\si{\meter}$
above the sump surface, so the static lift the pump must overcome is
$H_s = 18\,\si{\meter}$. The pipe is commercial steel of internal
diameter $D = 150\,\si{\milli\meter}$ and roughness $\epsilon =
0.045\,\si{\milli\meter}$; the suction run is $6\,\si{\meter}$ and the
discharge run is $120\,\si{\meter}$, for a total straight length
$L = 126\,\si{\meter}$.

\paragraph{Velocity and Reynolds number.} The bore area is
\[
A = \frac{\pi D^2}{4} = \frac{\pi (0.150)^2}{4} = 1.767\times10^{-2}
\,\si{\meter\squared},
\]
so the mean velocity is
\[
\bar u = \frac{Q}{A} = \frac{0.025}{1.767\times10^{-2}} \approx
1.41\,\si{\meter\per\second},
\]
a sensible duty velocity (the usual design band for water in steel is
$1$ to $3\,\si{\meter\per\second}$). The Reynolds number is
\[
\text{Re}_D = \frac{\rho\bar u D}{\mu} = \frac{998\times1.41\times
0.150}{1.0\times10^{-3}} \approx 2.1\times10^{5},
\]
fully turbulent.

\paragraph{Friction factor from Colebrook, checked against Haaland.}
The relative roughness is $\epsilon/D = 0.045/150 = 3.0\times10^{-4}$.
The implicit Colebrook-White equation
\[
\frac{1}{\sqrt f} = -2\log_{10}\!\left(\frac{\epsilon/D}{3.7} +
\frac{2.51}{\text{Re}_D\sqrt f}\right)
\]
is solved by iteration. Starting from a guess $f_0 = 0.02$
($\sqrt{f_0}=0.1414$): the right side is $-2\log_{10}(8.1\times10^{-5}
+ 2.51/(2.1\times10^5\times0.1414)) = -2\log_{10}(8.1\times10^{-5} +
8.45\times10^{-5}) = -2\log_{10}(1.66\times10^{-4}) = 7.56$, giving
$f_1 = 1/7.56^2 = 0.0175$. One more pass with $\sqrt{f_1}=0.1323$
gives $-2\log_{10}(8.1\times10^{-5} + 9.03\times10^{-5}) =
-2\log_{10}(1.71\times10^{-4}) = 7.53$, so $f = 1/7.53^2 \approx
0.0176$; the iteration has converged. The explicit Haaland form
returns $f\approx0.0177$, a $0.6\,\%$ difference, which confirms the
root. Take $f = 0.0176$. The solver behind
figure~\ref{fig:vol03ch12:moody} carries both forms in
\texttt{code/moody.py}.

\paragraph{Major loss along the pipe.} The Darcy-Weisbach head loss
over the straight run is
\[
h_{f,\text{major}} = f\frac{L}{D}\frac{\bar u^2}{2g} = 0.0176\times
\frac{126}{0.150}\times\frac{1.41^2}{2\times9.81} \approx 14.79\times
0.101 \approx 1.50\,\si{\meter}.
\]
(Working in head divides the Darcy-Weisbach pressure drop by $\rho g$;
the friction head is $1.50\,\si{\meter}$ of water, a pressure drop of
$\rho g h_f \approx 14.7\,\si{\kilo\pascal}$.)

\paragraph{Minor losses from fittings.} The line carries a
square-edged entrance ($K=0.5$), four $90^{\circ}$ long-radius elbows
($K=0.3$ each), one fully open gate valve ($K=0.15$), one swing check
valve ($K=2.0$), and an exit into the tank ($K=1.0$). The summed loss
coefficient is
\[
\sum K = 0.5 + 4\times0.3 + 0.15 + 2.0 + 1.0 = 4.85,
\]
and the minor-loss head is
\[
h_{f,\text{minor}} = \left(\sum K\right)\frac{\bar u^2}{2g} = 4.85
\times\frac{1.41^2}{2\times9.81} \approx 4.85\times0.101 \approx
0.49\,\si{\meter}.
\]
The fitting losses are about a third of the pipe-friction loss here,
which is typical for a run of this length; on a short, fitting-heavy
skid the minor losses can dominate, and on a long cross-country line
they are negligible. The total friction head at the duty flow is
$h_f = 1.50 + 0.49 \approx 1.99\,\si{\meter}$.

\paragraph{System curve and operating point.} The head the pump must
supply at flow $Q$ is the static lift plus the friction head, and the
friction head scales as $\bar u^2 \propto Q^2$:
\[
H_{\text{sys}}(Q) = H_s + \left(f\frac{L}{D} + \sum K\right)
\frac{\bar u^2}{2g} = 18 + k\,Q^2,
\]
where, with $\bar u = Q/A$, the coefficient is
\[
k = \left(f\frac{L}{D} + \sum K\right)\frac{1}{2gA^2} = (14.79 +
4.85)\frac{1}{2\times9.81\times(1.767\times10^{-2})^2} \approx 3204\,
\si{\meter\per(\meter\cubed\per\second)\squared}.
\]
At the duty flow $Q = 0.025\,\si{\meter\cubed\per\second}$ this gives
$H_{\text{sys}} = 18 + 3204\times0.025^2 = 18 + 2.00 = 20.0\,\si{
\meter}$, consistent with the static lift plus the $1.99\,\si{\meter}$
friction head computed above. A candidate pump has the falling
characteristic $H_{\text{pump}}(Q) = 55 - b\,Q^2$ with $b \approx
1.97\times10^4$ in the same units (a typical single-stage end-suction
curve; the manufacturer supplies it as a chart, here reduced to a
parabola for the algebra). The operating point is where supply meets
demand, $H_{\text{pump}} = H_{\text{sys}}$:
\[
55 - 1.97\times10^4\,Q^2 = 18 + 3204\,Q^2 \implies Q^2 = \frac{37}{
2.29\times10^4} \implies Q \approx 0.0402\,\si{\meter\cubed\per
\second}.
\]
The unthrottled pump would run at about $40\,\si{\liter\per\second}$,
above the $25\,\si{\liter\per\second}$ duty, so the operating point is
trimmed back to the duty flow by closing a control valve (raising
$\sum K$ until the system curve steepens to cross the pump curve at
$25\,\si{\liter\per\second}$) or, more efficiently, by reducing the
impeller speed. The crossing is drawn in
figure~\ref{fig:vol03ch12:pump-system-curve}. At the duty flow the
pump head is read from the curve as $H = 20\,\si{\meter}$ once it is
trimmed to the duty point.

\paragraph{Required power.} The hydraulic power delivered to the fluid
at the duty point is
\[
P_{\text{hyd}} = \rho g Q H = 998\times9.81\times0.025\times20.0
\approx 4.90\times10^{3}\,\si{\watt} \approx 4.9\,\si{\kilo\watt}.
\]
A single-stage water pump of this size runs at an efficiency near
$\eta\approx0.75$, so the shaft power is $P_{\text{shaft}} =
P_{\text{hyd}}/\eta \approx 4.9/0.75 \approx 6.5\,\si{\kilo\watt}$,
and a motor is selected one standard size above that ($7.5\,\si{\kilo
\watt}$) to carry the duty with margin. The cubic-in-speed lesson of
worked example~5 recurs here as a square-in-flow lesson: the friction
head, and with it the power spent against friction, grows as $Q^2$, so
oversizing the duty flow is expensive.

\paragraph{Cavitation margin (NPSH).} The suction side is the
hazard. The net positive suction head available is the absolute
pressure head at the pump inlet above the vapour-pressure head:
\[
\text{NPSH}_a = \frac{p_{\text{atm}} - p_v}{\rho g} - z_s -
h_{f,\text{suction}},
\]
where $z_s = 2\,\si{\meter}$ is the suction lift and
$h_{f,\text{suction}}$ is the friction head in the suction run. With
$p_{\text{atm}} = 101.3\,\si{\kilo\pascal}$ and $p_v = 2.34\,\si{
\kilo\pascal}$,
\[
\frac{p_{\text{atm}} - p_v}{\rho g} = \frac{(101.3 - 2.34)\times10^3}{
998\times9.81} \approx 10.1\,\si{\meter}.
\]
The suction-run friction head is the major plus minor loss over the
$6\,\si{\meter}$ suction pipe with its entrance and one elbow
($\sum K_s \approx 0.5 + 0.3 = 0.8$):
\[
h_{f,\text{suction}} = \left(f\frac{L_s}{D} + \sum K_s\right)\frac{
\bar u^2}{2g} = \left(0.0176\frac{6}{0.150} + 0.8\right)\times0.101
\approx (0.70 + 0.8)\times0.101 \approx 0.15\,\si{\meter}.
\]
So
\[
\text{NPSH}_a = 10.1 - 2.0 - 0.15 \approx 7.9\,\si{\meter}.
\]
The pump's required suction head $\text{NPSH}_r$ is read from the
manufacturer's curve at the duty flow; for a pump of this size and
speed it is typically $3$ to $4\,\si{\meter}$. Take $\text{NPSH}_r =
3.5\,\si{\meter}$. The margin is
\[
\text{NPSH}_a - \text{NPSH}_r \approx 7.9 - 3.5 = 4.4\,\si{\meter},
\]
comfortably positive. The standard hydraulic-design practice is a
margin of at least $0.5$ to $1\,\si{\meter}$, or a ratio
$\text{NPSH}_a/\text{NPSH}_r \gtrsim 1.3$, so this installation is
safe against cavitation \cite{text:ch02t3-karassik-pumps}. Were the
sump drawn down in service, or the suction line fouled, the margin
would shrink, which is why the suction side is kept short, large in
bore, and free of throttling fittings.

\paragraph{Verdict.} The transfer line carries $25\,\si{\liter\per
\second}$ of water at $\bar u = 1.41\,\si{\meter\per\second}$,
$\text{Re}_D = 2.1\times10^5$, with a friction factor $f = 0.0176$, a
total friction head of $2.0\,\si{\meter}$ on a static lift of
$18\,\si{\meter}$, met by a single-stage pump trimmed to a duty head
of $20\,\si{\meter}$, drawing $6.5\,\si{\kilo\watt}$ at the shaft
behind a $7.5\,\si{\kilo\watt}$ motor, with a cavitation margin of
$4.4\,\si{\meter}$. Every number in the verdict was carried from the
fluid property and the geometry through the chain of relations, and
each link is a relation from earlier in the chapter. A design that
skips the regime check picks the wrong friction law; a design that
skips the minor losses underestimates the head on a fitting-heavy
line; a design that skips the NPSH check installs a pump that
cavitates and erodes its impeller within a season. The end-to-end
discipline is to carry the chain unbroken.

\begin{mastery}
\textbf{The Falkner-Skan family: boundary layers with pressure
gradient.} The Blasius solution is the zero-pressure-gradient member
of a one-parameter family of similarity solutions discovered by
Falkner and Skan (1931). For an external flow whose edge velocity
varies as a power of distance, $U_e(x) = C x^m$, the boundary-layer
equations still admit a similarity reduction. With the similarity
variable $\eta = y\sqrt{(m+1)U_e/(2\nu x)}$ and stream function
$\psi = \sqrt{2\nu x\,U_e/(m+1)}\,f(\eta)$, the partial differential
equation collapses to the Falkner-Skan ordinary differential
equation
\[
f''' + f f'' + \beta\,[1 - (f')^2] = 0,
\]
with $\beta = 2m/(m+1)$ the dimensionless pressure-gradient parameter
and boundary conditions $f(0) = f'(0) = 0$, $f'(\infty) = 1$. The
edge velocity of an inviscid flow over a wedge of total opening angle
$\pi\beta$ has exactly this power-law form, so $\beta$ is the wedge
parameter; $\beta = 0$ ($m=0$) recovers the flat-plate Blasius case,
$\beta = 1$ is plane stagnation-point flow, and positive $\beta$ is a
favourable (accelerating) gradient that thins the layer and fills the
profile. Negative $\beta$ is an adverse (decelerating) gradient that
thins the wall shear; the wall shear $f''(0)$ falls with decreasing
$\beta$ and reaches zero at $\beta \approx -0.1988$, the point of
incipient separation. Below that value no attached similarity
solution exists, which is the analytical statement that a strong
enough adverse pressure gradient separates the boundary layer. The
single parameter $\beta$ thus carries the whole qualitative story of
section~12.5: favourable gradients stabilise and thin, adverse
gradients thicken and separate, and the separation threshold is a
definite number.
\end{mastery}

\section{Failure: separation, stall, and unexpected drag}\pathtag{core}

Every chapter in this work has a failure section. The viscous-flow
failure modes cluster into three families: boundary-layer
separation under adverse pressure gradient, transition-related
phenomena that change the drag coefficient discontinuously across
a Reynolds-number range, and turbulence-induced fluctuating loads
that drive resonant structural response.

\subsection{Boundary-layer separation and airfoil stall}

An airfoil at increasing angle of attack develops an increasing
adverse pressure gradient over the rear of its upper surface. The
boundary layer thickens, slows, and eventually reverses; the flow
separates from the surface and forms a large, low-pressure wake.
The lift, which had been growing linearly with angle of attack,
peaks and then drops sharply at the stall angle. The post-stall
drag is several times the pre-stall drag.

The stall angle for a typical conventional airfoil is $12^{\circ}$
to $20^{\circ}$, depending on Reynolds number, leading-edge
geometry, and surface roughness. High-lift devices on aircraft
wings (slats, slotted flaps, vortex generators) delay separation
and extend the usable angle-of-attack range; the working principle
is to inject high-energy flow into the boundary layer to overcome
the adverse pressure gradient near the trailing edge.

Stall is the canonical aerodynamic failure mode. Air France Flight
447, on 1 June 2009, entered a deep stall at $35{,}000\,\text{ft}$
after the autopilot disengaged on inconsistent airspeed data; the
crew did not recognise the stalled state, and the aircraft
descended in a stall until impact, with the loss of all $228$
people aboard \cite{acc:bea-af447}. The stall at altitude is
particularly hazardous: the recovery requires a substantial loss
of altitude to regain airspeed, the aerodynamic control
authority is degraded in the wake of the stalled wing, and the
cockpit instrumentation under unreliable airspeed conditions does
not give the crew an unambiguous indicator of the stalled state.
The AF447 lesson for boundary-layer analysis is that stall is a
flight-envelope boundary, not a post-hoc engineering observation.
The working aircraft certification discipline embeds stall-warning
systems, stick shakers, stall recovery procedures, and crew
training in unreliable-airspeed scenarios. The post-AF447
regulatory response included revised crew-training requirements
for unreliable airspeed and high-altitude upset, and changes to
autoflight handoff design \cite{acc:bea-af447}.

\subsection{Diffuser stall}

A diverging duct (diffuser) that tries to decelerate a flow and
recover its dynamic pressure can suffer separation when the
divergence angle is too large or the inlet velocity profile is too
non-uniform. The separated flow leaves the diffuser walls and
forms a free jet through the centre of the diffuser, with
recirculating regions in the corners. The pressure recovery drops
sharply; the diffuser loses its design function.

The threshold divergence angle is approximately $7^{\circ}$ on
each side for unaided flow (a $14^{\circ}$ total included angle).
Above this, separation is likely; below it, the boundary layer
typically stays attached. The discipline of diffuser design uses
this empirical limit, supplemented by computational and
experimental verification for specific geometries.

\subsection{Vortex-induced vibration}

A bluff body (a cylinder, a chimney, a suspension-bridge cable) in
cross flow sheds Kármán vortices at the Strouhal frequency. The
shedding produces an oscillating lift on the body, which excites
resonant vibration when the shedding frequency approaches a
natural frequency of the body. The amplitude can grow to levels
that produce fatigue failure or, in extreme cases, structural
collapse.

The original Tacoma Narrows suspension bridge collapse of
$7$ November $1940$ \cite{acc:tacoma-narrows-1941} is the
canonical large-scale case (also treated in chapter 6 of this
volume on vibrations and chapter 13 on bluff-body aerodynamics).
The deck's torsional self-excitation under steady wind was not
classical vortex shedding alone but a related aeroelastic
phenomenon (torsional flutter); the chapter's narrow point is
that the viscous-flow physics of vortex shedding and the
aeroelastic-flutter physics of bridge decks are both governed by
the same Reynolds-number-dependent separated-flow regimes.

Industrial cases of vortex-induced vibration: power-line galloping
in ice-coated conductors (where the asymmetric ice cross-section
produces an instability beyond simple Kármán shedding); marine
riser vibration in subsea oil and gas production; resonance of
exhaust stacks of power stations under moderate cross winds; and
acoustic resonance of vehicle door seals at highway speeds. The
working design discipline is to compute the Strouhal frequency at
the operating flow speed, check it against the structure's natural
frequencies, and either shift the structural frequency or modify
the cross-section to disrupt the vortex shedding.

\subsection{The drag crisis surprise}

A bluff body whose operating Reynolds number sits near the
drag-crisis transition is at risk of dramatic drag changes from
small perturbations. A circular cylinder at $\text{Re} = 2\times
10^5$ has $C_D \approx 0.5$; the same cylinder at $\text{Re} =
4\times 10^5$ has $C_D \approx 0.2$. A design that sits near the
transition can experience a factor-of-two change in drag from
small changes in surface roughness, freestream turbulence, or
operating speed.

The discipline is to know whether the design Reynolds number is
subcritical, supercritical, or near transition, and to design for
the bracket of possible $C_D$ values rather than for a single
point estimate. A wind-tunnel test in a low-turbulence facility
may under-predict the full-scale $C_D$ if the model sits in
subcritical conditions while the full-scale operates supercritical.

\subsection{The diagnostic principle for viscous-flow failures}

The viscous-flow diagnostic question is, at every body in any
flow:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item What is the Reynolds number? Laminar, transitional, or
    turbulent regime?
  \item What is the local pressure gradient on the body? Favourable
    (boundary layer thins), zero (Blasius), or adverse (separation
    risk)?
  \item Where is the separation point likely to be? Has it been
    measured or predicted by a validated tool?
  \item What is the post-separation wake structure? Steady, with
    vortex shedding, with reattachment?
  \item Is the Strouhal frequency at the design speed coincident
    with a structural natural frequency?
  \item Is the body near a drag-crisis transition?
\end{itemize}

A design that has answered all six is a viscous-flow-aware design.
A design that has answered none of the six is in the regime where
real-flow drag and lift can depart from the inviscid prediction by
factors of two or more, and the difference shows up as an
operating-cost surprise, a structural-fatigue surprise, or, in the
worst case, a stability or control surprise of the kind that
brings airplanes down.

\begin{principle}[Real-flow drag has two parts; both are Reynolds-number-dependent]
The drag on a body in viscous flow is the sum of skin-friction
drag (from the integrated viscous shear stress) and pressure drag
(from the integrated surface pressure). The relative importance
depends on the body's shape: streamlined bodies are
friction-dominated, bluff bodies are pressure-dominated. Both
components vary with Reynolds number, sometimes discontinuously
(the drag crisis). The discipline is to compute the Reynolds number
first, identify the regime, and use the regime-appropriate drag
model rather than an inviscid extrapolation.
\end{principle}

\begin{archetype}[Transport, scaling, failure: the boundary-layer triad]
The viscous-flow archetype puts three relations to work. The
Reynolds number determines the regime (laminar, transitional,
turbulent); the boundary-layer thickness scales as $\sqrt{\nu x/
U_\infty}$ (laminar) or $x \cdot \text{Re}_x^{-1/5}$ (turbulent);
the wall shear stress sets the skin-friction contribution to drag.
The archetype's failure mode is separation, triggered by adverse
pressure gradient; the failure's consequences are stall, drag
spikes, vortex shedding, and aeroelastic instability. Every
viscous-flow analysis works through these three: regime,
boundary-layer scaling, separation risk.
\end{archetype}

\begin{estimation}
\textbf{Estimate the skin-friction drag on a Boeing 737 fuselage at
cruise.}

\smallskip
\textit{Estimate, before reading on.}

\smallskip
A working estimate goes through the following moves. A Boeing
$737$ fuselage has a length of about $38\,\si{\meter}$ and a
diameter of about $3.8\,\si{\meter}$. Treat the fuselage as a
cylinder for the wetted-area estimate: the curved surface area is
approximately $\pi D L \approx 3.14 \times 3.8 \times 38
\approx 450\,\si{\meter}^2$. The full wetted surface including
nose, tail, wings, and tail surfaces is roughly twice the
fuselage value, so total wetted area $S_w \approx 900\,\si{\meter}
^2$.

At cruise (Mach $0.78$, altitude $35{,}000\,\text{ft}$), the
freestream is $U_\infty \approx 230\,\si{\meter\per\second}$,
the air density $\rho \approx 0.38\,\si{\kilogram\per\meter\cubed}$,
the air kinematic viscosity $\nu \approx 4\times 10^{-5}\,\si{\meter
}^2/\si{\second}$. The length-based Reynolds number is
\[
\text{Re}_L = \frac{U_\infty L}{\nu} = \frac{230 \times 38}{4
\times 10^{-5}} \approx 2.2\times 10^8.
\]
The boundary layer is fully turbulent over nearly the entire
fuselage; the average skin-friction coefficient is
\[
\bar{C}_f \approx \frac{0.074}{\text{Re}_L^{1/5}} \approx
\frac{0.074}{(2.2\times 10^8)^{0.2}} \approx \frac{0.074}{47}
\approx 0.0016.
\]
The skin-friction drag is then
\[
F_D = \tfrac{1}{2}\rho U_\infty^2 \cdot S_w \cdot \bar{C}_f
\approx 0.5 \times 0.38 \times 230^2 \times 900 \times 0.0016
\approx 14{,}500\,\si{\newton}.
\]
A working figure for total $737$ cruise drag is about $35\,\text{kN}$
to $45\,\text{kN}$; skin friction accounts for roughly half, with
the rest divided between induced (vortex-related) drag, pressure
drag at the rear, and miscellaneous excrescence drag.

The postmortem: the skin-friction estimate is within a factor of
two of the working figure, despite all the simplifications
(uniform $C_f$, cylinder approximation, neglect of wing thickness).
The factor-of-two-or-better accuracy is characteristic of
boundary-layer estimates done with the integrated turbulent
correlations; the same accuracy is what makes the relations useful
for first-pass design. A high-accuracy prediction requires
either wind-tunnel measurement or validated CFD with appropriate
turbulence model.
\end{estimation}

\section{Project}\pathtag{core}

\begin{project}[Measure pressure drop across a pipe; verify against the Moody chart]
The reader instruments a household pipe section, measures the
pressure drop as a function of flow rate, computes the friction
factor, and compares to the Moody chart for the pipe's
roughness. The deliverable is a twenty- to thirty-page engineering
report.

\textbf{Track}. Physical build plus measurement plus analysis.
\textbf{Hazard class}: low.

\textbf{Apparatus}. A length of household pipe (smooth copper,
PVC, or PEX) of known diameter ($15$ to $25\,\si{\milli\meter}$
typical), length of $1$ to $2\,\si{\meter}$, with pressure taps at
the inlet and outlet of the measured section. Pressure measurement
by U-tube manometer (water, oil, or mercury depending on the
pressure range), differential pressure transducer, or two
inexpensive digital pressure gauges. Flow rate measurement by
collecting the outflow over a measured time interval.

\textbf{Procedure}. The reader varies the flow rate over the
widest available range (from a low trickle to the maximum supply),
records the pressure drop and the volume flow rate at each
setting, and computes:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item The mean flow velocity $\bar{u} = Q/(\pi D^2/4)$.
  \item The Reynolds number $\text{Re}_D = \bar{u} D/\nu$, using
    water's kinematic viscosity at the measured temperature.
  \item The friction factor $f = (\Delta p \cdot D)/(L \cdot
    \tfrac{1}{2}\rho \bar{u}^2)$.
  \item A log-log plot of $f$ vs.\ $\text{Re}_D$, overlaid on the
    laminar curve $f = 64/\text{Re}_D$ and on the Colebrook-White
    or Haaland approximation for the pipe's estimated roughness.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Analysis}. The reader identifies:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item The Reynolds-number range in which the data fall (laminar,
    transitional, turbulent).
  \item The agreement (or disagreement) with the Moody-chart
    prediction at each $\text{Re}_D$.
  \item The estimated pipe roughness $\epsilon$ that best fits the
    data, compared to handbook values for the pipe material.
  \item Sources of error: pressure-tap geometry, entrance and exit
    effects (the data should be in the fully developed region,
    not in the developing flow near the pipe inlet), bend effects
    if any, flow-rate measurement uncertainty.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Reflection ($1{,}500$ to $3{,}000$ words)}. The reader
addresses:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item The accuracy with which the Moody chart predicts the
    measured friction factor across the operating range.
  \item The Reynolds-number boundary at which the data depart from
    the laminar prediction, compared to the textbook value of
    $\text{Re}_D = 2300$.
  \item One source of measurement uncertainty whose impact the
    reader was able to bound (or could not), with a brief
    discussion of why.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Deliverable}. The pipe-instrument setup (photographed and
dimensioned), the raw data, the analysis, and the reflection.

\textbf{Time}. Two to four hours of apparatus construction, three
to six hours of measurement, four to eight hours of analysis and
write-up.
\end{project}

\section{Exercises}

The exercises mix calculation, derivation, estimation, simulation,
design, diagnosis, failure analysis, and judgment.

\subsection*{Calculation}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
Water at $20\,\degC$ ($\rho = 998\,\si{\kilogram\per\meter\cubed}$,
$\mu = 1.0\times 10^{-3}\,\si{\pascal\second}$) flows through a
$25\,\si{\milli\meter}$ diameter pipe at $0.5\,\si{\meter\per
\second}$. Find the Reynolds number and identify the flow regime.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} $\text{Re}_D = \rho\bar u D/\mu =
998\times0.5\times0.025/(1.0\times10^{-3}) \approx 1.25\times10^{4}$.
This is well above $4000$, so the flow is turbulent. (The laminar
ceiling $\text{Re}_D\approx2300$ would correspond here to
$\bar u\approx0.09\,\si{\meter\per\second}$, so any ordinary tap
flow in this pipe is turbulent.)
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
For the same pipe and fluid as above, find the pressure drop per
metre of length in the fully developed laminar regime. Then
compute the pressure drop in turbulent flow at $\bar{u} = 3\,\si{
\meter\per\second}$ for a smooth pipe ($\epsilon \approx 0$), using
a friction factor from the explicit Haaland correlation
$f^{-1/2} = -1.8\log_{10}[(\epsilon/(3.7D))^{1.11} + 6.9/\text{Re}_D]$.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Laminar at $\bar u = 0.5\,\si{\meter
\per\second}$: $\text{Re}_D \approx 1.25\times10^4$ is actually
turbulent, so the laminar formula does not apply at this speed. Take
the laminar case at a speed inside the laminar range; the cleaner
reading of the prompt is to compute the Hagen-Poiseuille drop that
the laminar law \emph{would} give and the turbulent drop at
$3\,\si{\meter\per\second}$, then compare. Hagen-Poiseuille:
$\Delta p/L = 128\mu Q/(\pi D^4)$ with $Q = \bar u\,\pi D^2/4 =
0.5\times\pi\times0.025^2/4 = 2.45\times10^{-4}\,\text{m}^3/\text{s}$,
giving $\Delta p/L = 128\times10^{-3}\times2.45\times10^{-4}/(\pi
\times0.025^4) \approx 2.55\,\si{\pascal\per\meter}$ (equivalently
$f = 64/\text{Re}_D \approx 5.1\times10^{-3}$ with $\Delta p/L =
f\rho\bar u^2/(2D)$). Turbulent at $\bar u = 3\,\si{\meter\per
\second}$: $\text{Re}_D = 998\times3\times0.025/10^{-3} \approx
7.5\times10^{4}$; with $\epsilon\approx0$, Haaland gives
$f^{-1/2} = -1.8\log_{10}(6.9/7.5\times10^{4}) \approx 7.40$, so
$f \approx 0.0183$ and $\Delta p/L = f\rho\bar u^2/(2D) =
0.0183\times998\times9/(2\times0.025) \approx 3.3\times10^{3}\,\si{
\pascal\per\meter}$. The turbulent drop per metre is roughly three
orders of magnitude larger, partly because $\Delta p\propto\bar u^2$
turbulent versus $\bar u$ laminar, and partly the $36\times$ speed-
squared factor.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A flat plate of length $1\,\si{\meter}$ is in a freestream of air
($\rho = 1.225\,\si{\kilogram\per\meter\cubed}$, $\nu = 1.5\times
10^{-5}\,\si{\meter}^2/\si{\second}$) at $20\,\si{\meter\per
\second}$. Find $\text{Re}_L$, the Blasius boundary-layer thickness
$\delta$ at the trailing edge, and the average skin-friction
coefficient.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} $\text{Re}_L = U_\infty L/\nu =
20\times1/(1.5\times10^{-5}) = 1.33\times10^{6}$. This exceeds the
smooth-plate transition near $5\times10^5$, so most of the plate is
turbulent; the laminar Blasius formulas are an approximation valid
only over the front. Using the laminar relations as the prompt asks:
$\delta = 5.0\,L/\sqrt{\text{Re}_L} = 5.0/\sqrt{1.33\times10^6}
\approx 4.3\times10^{-3}\,\si{\meter}$ and $\bar C_f = 1.328/\sqrt{
\text{Re}_L} = 1.328/1153 \approx 1.15\times10^{-3}$. (For the
turbulent estimate one would use $\delta = 0.37\,L\,\text{Re}_L^{-1/5}
\approx 0.022\,\si{\meter}$ and $\bar C_f = 0.074\,\text{Re}_L^{-1/5}
\approx 4.4\times10^{-3}$, several times the laminar values; the
honest answer notes the plate is past transition.)
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A sphere of $5\,\si{\milli\meter}$ diameter falls through glycerin
($\rho = 1260\,\si{\kilogram\per\meter\cubed}$, $\mu = 1.5\,\si{
\pascal\second}$) under gravity. Find the terminal velocity using
the Stokes drag law. Check that the resulting Reynolds number is
in the Stokes regime ($\text{Re} < 1$).

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Need the sphere density; take a steel
sphere $\rho_s \approx 7800\,\si{\kilogram\per\meter\cubed}$.
Balance weight minus buoyancy against Stokes drag:
$U = (\rho_s - \rho_f)g\,d^2/(18\mu)$ with $d = 5\,\si{\milli\meter}$.
$U = (7800-1260)\times9.81\times(5\times10^{-3})^2/(18\times1.5)
= 6540\times9.81\times2.5\times10^{-5}/27 \approx 5.9\times10^{-2}
\,\si{\meter\per\second}$. Check: $\text{Re} = \rho_f U d/\mu =
1260\times0.059\times0.005/1.5 \approx 0.25 < 1$, so the Stokes
regime holds and the answer stands. (Glycerin's high viscosity is
what keeps such a fast-settling steel ball in the creeping regime.)
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Air ($\nu = 1.5\times 10^{-5}\,\si{\meter}^2/\si{\second}$) flows at
$10\,\si{\meter\per\second}$ past a circular cylinder of
$50\,\si{\milli\meter}$ diameter. Compute the Reynolds number, the
Strouhal number ($\approx 0.21$ in this range), and the vortex-
shedding frequency.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} $\text{Re} = UD/\nu = 10\times0.05/
(1.5\times10^{-5}) \approx 3.3\times10^{4}$, in the range where the
Strouhal number is roughly constant at $\text{St}\approx0.21$. The
shedding frequency is $f_s = \text{St}\,U/D = 0.21\times10/0.05 =
42\,\si{\hertz}$. (At $\text{Re}\approx3\times10^4$ the wake is a
fully turbulent vortex street; the $42\,\si{\hertz}$ tone is audible
as the aeolian whistle of a wire in wind.)
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A diffuser expands a $50\,\si{\milli\meter}$ diameter pipe to
$100\,\si{\milli\meter}$ diameter over a length of $300\,\si{\milli
\meter}$. Find the divergence half-angle and state whether the
diffuser is likely to suffer separation.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} The radius grows from $25\,\si{\milli
\meter}$ to $50\,\si{\milli\meter}$ over $300\,\si{\milli\meter}$,
so the half-angle is $\theta = \arctan[(50-25)/300] = \arctan(0.0833)
\approx 4.8^{\circ}$. This is below the rule-of-thumb threshold of
about $7^{\circ}$ per side, so the boundary layer is expected to stay
attached and the diffuser should recover pressure reasonably well.
The margin is modest, so a non-uniform or already-thickened inlet
profile could still trigger local separation.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A racing-car tyre rotates at $200\,\text{km/h}$ on
the road; the boundary layer on its outer surface is turbulent.
Estimate the boundary-layer thickness at a streamwise distance of
$0.3\,\si{\meter}$ on the tyre's leading face (assume air, sea-
level conditions).

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Surface speed $U = 200\,\text{km/h} =
55.6\,\si{\meter\per\second}$; air $\nu = 1.5\times10^{-5}\,\si{
\meter}^2/\si{\second}$. Local Reynolds number at $x = 0.3\,\si{
\meter}$: $\text{Re}_x = Ux/\nu = 55.6\times0.3/(1.5\times10^{-5})
\approx 1.1\times10^{6}$, turbulent (consistent with the prompt).
Turbulent thickness $\delta = 0.37\,x\,\text{Re}_x^{-1/5} = 0.37
\times0.3\times(1.1\times10^6)^{-0.2} \approx 0.111\times0.0615
\approx 6.8\times10^{-3}\,\si{\meter}$, about $7\,\si{\milli\meter}$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A car of frontal area $2.2\,\si{\meter}^2$ travels at $30\,\si{
\meter\per\second}$ in still air. Its drag coefficient is $0.32$.
Find the drag force and the power dissipated against drag.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} With $\rho = 1.2\,\si{\kilogram\per
\meter\cubed}$, $F_D = \tfrac12\rho U^2 A\,C_D = 0.5\times1.2\times
30^2\times2.2\times0.32 \approx 3.8\times10^{2}\,\si{\newton}$. Power
$P = F_D U = 380\times30 \approx 1.1\times10^{4}\,\si{\watt} =
11.4\,\text{kW}$. The cube-of-speed scaling means a $10\%$ speed
rise raises this power by about $33\%$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A long-span suspension-bridge cable of diameter $0.5\,\si{\meter}$
is in a $15\,\si{\meter\per\second}$ wind. Compute the Reynolds
number, the vortex-shedding frequency, and compare to a typical
cable natural frequency (state your assumption about the cable's
fundamental mode).

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Air $\nu = 1.5\times10^{-5}\,\si{\meter
}^2/\si{\second}$. $\text{Re} = UD/\nu = 15\times0.5/(1.5\times
10^{-5}) = 5\times10^{5}$, in the range where $\text{St}\approx0.2$.
Shedding frequency $f_s = \text{St}\,U/D = 0.2\times15/0.5 = 6\,\si{
\hertz}$. A long suspension-bridge main cable has a low fundamental,
often a fraction of a hertz to a few hertz; assume $f_n\approx
0.5\,\si{\hertz}$ for a $100\,\si{\meter}$ free length. Then $f_s =
6\,\si{\hertz}\gg f_n$, so this wind speed excites a higher cable
mode rather than the fundamental; lock-in would occur at a lower
wind speed for the fundamental ($U_{\text{lock}} = f_n D/\text{St} =
0.5\times0.5/0.2 = 1.25\,\si{\meter\per\second}$) and at successively
higher speeds for higher modes.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Derivation}\pathtag{standard}

\begin{exercise}
Derive the Hagen-Poiseuille velocity profile $u_z(r) = G(R^2 -
r^2)/(4\mu)$ from the steady cylindrical-coordinates Navier-Stokes
equation, stating the assumptions used.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Assume steady, incompressible,
axisymmetric, fully developed flow: $u_r = u_\theta = 0$,
$\partial u_z/\partial z = 0$, no swirl, constant properties, and a
constant axial pressure gradient $dp/dz = -G$. The $z$-momentum
Navier-Stokes equation in cylindrical coordinates collapses to
$\mu\,\tfrac1r\,\tfrac{d}{dr}(r\,du_z/dr) = -G$. Integrate once:
$r\,du_z/dr = -Gr^2/(2\mu) + C_1$. Symmetry at the axis,
$du_z/dr|_{r=0} = 0$, forces $C_1 = 0$. Integrate again:
$u_z = -Gr^2/(4\mu) + C_2$. No-slip at the wall, $u_z(R) = 0$,
gives $C_2 = GR^2/(4\mu)$, so $u_z(r) = G(R^2 - r^2)/(4\mu)$, the
parabola claimed.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Show that the average velocity in Hagen-Poiseuille flow is half
the centreline velocity, and that the laminar friction factor is
$f = 64/\text{Re}_D$.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Average over the area:
$\bar u = \tfrac{1}{\pi R^2}\int_0^R u_z(r)\,2\pi r\,dr =
\tfrac{2}{R^2}\int_0^R \tfrac{G}{4\mu}(R^2 - r^2)\,r\,dr =
\tfrac{G}{2\mu R^2}[R^2 r^2/2 - r^4/4]_0^R = \tfrac{G}{2\mu R^2}
\cdot R^4/4 = GR^2/(8\mu)$. Since $u_{\max} = GR^2/(4\mu)$, indeed
$\bar u = \tfrac12 u_{\max}$. For the friction factor, write the
Hagen-Poiseuille drop as $\Delta p = 32\mu L\bar u/D^2$ (from
$G = 8\mu\bar u/R^2 = 32\mu\bar u/D^2$). Set this equal to the
Darcy-Weisbach form $\Delta p = f\tfrac{L}{D}\tfrac{\rho\bar u^2}{2}$
and solve: $f = 64\mu/(\rho\bar u D) = 64/\text{Re}_D$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Derive the Blasius scaling $\delta(x) \sim \sqrt{\nu x/U_\infty}$
by setting the inertial and viscous terms equal in the boundary-
layer equation.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} In the boundary-layer momentum equation
the streamwise convective term scales as $u\,\partial u/\partial x
\sim U_\infty^2/x$ and the viscous term as $\nu\,\partial^2 u/
\partial y^2 \sim \nu U_\infty/\delta^2$. The boundary layer is the
region where these two balance, so $U_\infty^2/x \sim \nu U_\infty/
\delta^2$, giving $\delta^2 \sim \nu x/U_\infty$ and
$\delta \sim \sqrt{\nu x/U_\infty} = x/\sqrt{\text{Re}_x}$. The
order-one constant ($5.0$ for the $99\%$ thickness) comes from the
full Blasius solution, but the scaling is fixed by this balance.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Derive the Kármán momentum integral relation $d\theta/dx = C_f/2$
(for zero pressure gradient) by integrating the boundary-layer
momentum equation across the layer.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Integrate the steady boundary-layer
momentum equation $u\,u_x + v\,u_y = \nu\,u_{yy}$ across the layer
from $y=0$ to $y\to\infty$, using continuity $u_x + v_y = 0$ to
eliminate $v$ and integrating by parts. With zero pressure gradient
and the no-slip and freestream boundary conditions, the left side
reduces to $\tfrac{d}{dx}\int_0^\infty u(U_\infty - u)\,dy =
U_\infty^2\,d\theta/dx$, where $\theta$ is the momentum thickness;
the right side is the wall shear $\tau_w/\rho = \nu\,(u_y)_{y=0}$.
Dividing by $U_\infty^2$ and using $C_f = \tau_w/(\tfrac12\rho
U_\infty^2)$ gives $d\theta/dx = C_f/2$. (With a pressure gradient
extra terms in $\delta^*$ and $dU_\infty/dx$ appear; this is the
zero-gradient special case.)
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Show that the Reynolds number is the only dimensionless parameter
governing the steady incompressible Navier-Stokes equation in a
geometry of fixed shape, by non-dimensionalisation with a
characteristic velocity and length.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Scale $\vec u$ by $U$, lengths by $L$,
pressure by $\rho U^2$, time by $L/U$. Substituting into the steady
incompressible Navier-Stokes equation $\rho(\vec u\cdot\nabla)\vec u
= -\nabla p + \mu\nabla^2\vec u$ and dividing through by $\rho U^2/L$
gives $(\vec u^*\cdot\nabla^*)\vec u^* = -\nabla^* p^* + (1/\text{Re})
\nabla^{*2}\vec u^*$ with $\text{Re} = \rho U L/\mu$. The only
parameter surviving the scaling is $\text{Re}$ (gravity, if retained,
would add $1/\text{Fr}^2$). The boundary conditions on a fixed-shape
geometry are themselves dimensionless once positions are scaled by
$L$, so two flows with the same shape and the same $\text{Re}$ have
identical non-dimensional solutions, which is the similarity
principle.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Derive Stokes's drag law $F_D = 6\pi\mu a U$ from the creeping-
flow form of the Navier-Stokes equations around a sphere. Sketch
the derivation; the full algebra is in any standard reference.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} At $\text{Re}\ll1$ drop the inertial
term, leaving the Stokes equations $\nabla p = \mu\nabla^2\vec u$,
$\nabla\cdot\vec u = 0$. Taking the divergence shows the pressure is
harmonic, $\nabla^2 p = 0$. With the boundary conditions $\vec u =
\vec 0$ on the sphere and $\vec u\to\vec U$ at infinity, the solution
is a Stokeslet plus a potential dipole; the resulting surface stress
integrates to a force along $\vec U$. Two-thirds of the force is
viscous skin friction and one-third is pressure drag, summing to
$F_D = 6\pi\mu a U$. The drag coefficient is then $C_D = F_D/(\tfrac12
\rho U^2\pi a^2) = 24/\text{Re}$ with $\text{Re} = \rho U(2a)/\mu$.
The full angular dependence of $\vec u$ and $p$ is in
\cite{paper:ch02t3b-stokes-1851}.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Estimation}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the Reynolds number of human blood flow in the aorta.
State assumptions about blood density, viscosity, aortic diameter,
and mean flow velocity.

\paragraph{Reference estimate.} Take blood $\rho \approx 1060\,\si{
\kilogram\per\meter\cubed}$, $\mu \approx 3.5\times10^{-3}\,\si{
\pascal\second}$ (about $3.5\times$ water), aortic diameter $D
\approx 25\,\si{\milli\meter}$, mean velocity $\bar u\approx0.2\,\si{
\meter\per\second}$ (averaged over the cardiac cycle; peak systolic
is several times higher). Then $\text{Re}_D = \rho\bar u D/\mu =
1060\times0.2\times0.025/(3.5\times10^{-3}) \approx 1.5\times10^{3}$.
The mean-flow Reynolds number is just below the pipe-transition value,
so aortic flow is nominally laminar on average; peak-systolic
Reynolds numbers reach several thousand, producing transient,
disturbed flow near the valve, which is one reason aortic flow is
not cleanly laminar throughout the cycle.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the boundary-layer thickness on a sailing yacht hull at
its trailing edge while sailing at $5\,\si{\meter\per\second}$.
State assumptions about hull length and water properties.

\paragraph{Reference estimate.} Take a waterline length $L\approx
10\,\si{\meter}$ and water $\nu\approx1.0\times10^{-6}\,\si{\meter}^2/
\si{\second}$. $\text{Re}_L = U L/\nu = 5\times10/(1.0\times10^{-6}) =
5\times10^{7}$, far above the transition value, so the layer is
turbulent over nearly the whole hull. Turbulent thickness at the
stern: $\delta = 0.37\,L\,\text{Re}_L^{-1/5} = 0.37\times10\times
(5\times10^7)^{-0.2} \approx 3.7\times0.0182 \approx 6.7\times10^{-2}
\,\si{\meter}$, about $7\,\si{\centi\meter}$. A boundary layer of
several centimetres at the stern is consistent with the observation
that the rearmost hull region sits in a thick, slow-moving sheath of
water.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the drag force on a road cyclist at $10\,\si{\meter\per
\second}$ in still air. State assumptions about the cyclist's
frontal area, drag coefficient, and air density. Compare to a
typical cyclist's sustainable power output.

\paragraph{Reference estimate.} Take frontal area $A\approx0.4\,\si{
\meter}^2$, $C_D\approx0.9$ (upright rider plus bike), $\rho\approx
1.2\,\si{\kilogram\per\meter\cubed}$. Drag $F_D = \tfrac12\rho U^2 A
C_D = 0.5\times1.2\times10^2\times0.4\times0.9 \approx 21.6\,\si{
\newton}$. Drag power $P = F_D U \approx 216\,\si{\watt}$. A trained
amateur sustains roughly $200$ to $300\,\si{\watt}$, so $10\,\si{
\meter\per\second}$ ($36\,\text{km/h}$) is near the limit of what an
upright rider holds against air drag alone, which is why aerodynamic
position and clothing matter so much in time trials.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the terminal velocity of a raindrop of $2\,\si{\milli
\meter}$ diameter falling in still air. State whether the relevant
drag regime is Stokes or beyond, and choose the appropriate
correlation.

\paragraph{Reference estimate.} A Stokes estimate, $U = (\rho_w-
\rho_a)g d^2/(18\mu) = 1000\times9.81\times(2\times10^{-3})^2/(18
\times1.8\times10^{-5}) \approx 121\,\si{\meter\per\second}$, gives a
Reynolds number $\text{Re} = \rho_a U d/\mu = 1.2\times121\times
0.002/(1.8\times10^{-5}) \approx 1.6\times10^{4}$, far outside Stokes
($\text{Re}<1$). The drag is in the constant-$C_D$ bluff-body range,
so balance weight against $F_D = \tfrac12\rho_a U^2 (\tfrac\pi4 d^2)
C_D$ with $C_D\approx0.5$: $U = \sqrt{\tfrac{4\rho_w g d}{3\rho_a
C_D}} = \sqrt{4\times1000\times9.81\times0.002/(3\times1.2\times0.5)}
\approx 6.6\,\si{\meter\per\second}$. (Real $2\,\si{\milli\meter}$
drops deform and fall near $6.5\,\si{\meter\per\second}$, so the
bluff-body estimate is the right one; Stokes overshoots by twenty
times.)
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the friction-related power dissipation in a $50\,\si{
\meter}$ section of household water-supply pipe of $20\,\si{\milli
\meter}$ diameter carrying $0.2\,\text{L/s}$. State the relevant
Reynolds number and friction factor.

\paragraph{Reference estimate.} $Q = 0.2\times10^{-3}\,\text{m}^3/
\text{s}$, $D = 0.02\,\si{\meter}$, area $= \pi D^2/4 = 3.14\times
10^{-4}\,\si{\meter}^2$, so $\bar u = Q/A \approx 0.64\,\si{\meter
\per\second}$. Water $\rho=998$, $\mu=10^{-3}$: $\text{Re}_D =
998\times0.64\times0.02/10^{-3} \approx 1.27\times10^{4}$, turbulent.
Smooth-pipe Haaland gives $f\approx0.029$. Pressure drop over
$L=50\,\si{\meter}$: $\Delta p = f\tfrac{L}{D}\tfrac{\rho\bar u^2}{2}
= 0.029\times2500\times998\times0.41/2 \approx 1.5\times10^{4}\,\si{
\pascal}$. Dissipated power $P = \Delta p\,Q = 1.5\times10^4\times
2\times10^{-4} \approx 3\,\si{\watt}$. A few watts lost to pipe
friction in a long household run is small but not negligible over a
building's lifetime of pumping.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Simulation}\pathtag{mastery}

\begin{exercise}
Implement a finite-difference solver for the boundary-layer
equations on a flat plate with zero pressure gradient. Verify the
Blasius profile by comparing the computed wall shear and
displacement thickness to the analytical values.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} The cleanest route reuses the similarity
reduction: solve the Blasius ordinary differential equation $2f'''
+ ff'' = 0$ by shooting, as in \texttt{code/blasius.py}, and recover
$f''(0) = 0.33206$ (wall shear) and $\delta^*/\delta\approx0.345$
(displacement thickness). A direct marching finite-difference solver
of the boundary-layer partial differential equations discretises in
$y$, marches in $x$ with the freestream as the initial profile, and
should reproduce $C_f(x) = 0.664/\sqrt{\text{Re}_x}$ to within the
grid-refinement error; a wall-normal spacing fine near $y=0$ is
needed to resolve the steep wall gradient. Verification is a
convergence study: halve $\Delta y$ and confirm $f''(0)$ approaches
$0.332$ at the expected order.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Implement a panel method for two-dimensional incompressible viscous
flow past a circular cylinder, including a thin-boundary-layer
correction (Thwaites' method for laminar, Head's method for
turbulent). Verify against published $C_D$ vs.\ $\text{Re}$ data.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Build the inviscid potential flow from a
distribution of source or vortex panels on the cylinder, giving the
pressure distribution $C_p(\theta)$. Feed the resulting edge velocity
into a boundary-layer integral method (Thwaites for the laminar
portion, Head for the turbulent portion after a transition criterion)
to locate the separation point where the wall shear vanishes.
Pressure drag follows from integrating $C_p$ over the attached
surface up to separation; skin-friction drag follows from the
boundary-layer shear. Validate against
\texttt{data/sphere\_drag\_curve.csv} (or a cylinder equivalent): the
method should reproduce the subcritical plateau and, with a tripped
transition, the drag-crisis drop. A panel method without the
boundary-layer coupling returns the d'Alembert zero drag, which is
the negative control that shows why the viscous correction is the
whole point.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Run a simple LES of channel flow at $\text{Re}_\tau \approx 200$
using an existing open-source CFD code. Compare the mean velocity
profile in the log layer to the law-of-the-wall prediction
$u^+ = (1/\kappa)\ln y^+ + B$.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Set up a periodic channel at friction
Reynolds number $\text{Re}_\tau = u_\tau h/\nu\approx200$ with a
sub-grid-scale model (Smagorinsky or dynamic), driven by a constant
mean pressure gradient that fixes $u_\tau$. Run until the statistics
are stationary, then time-average the streamwise velocity, scale by
$u_\tau$ and $\nu$ to wall units, and overplot against
figure~\ref{fig:vol03ch12:law-of-wall}. The mean profile should show
$u^+ = y^+$ in the viscous sublayer and $u^+\approx(1/0.41)\ln y^+ +
5.0$ in the log region; the verification check is that the constants
$\kappa$ and $B$ come out near the standard values and that the
wall-normal resolution places several points inside $y^+<5$. A
coarse grid that misses the sublayer over-predicts the wall velocity
and is the diagnostic for insufficient near-wall resolution.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Design}\pathtag{standard}

\begin{exercise}
Design a piping system for a building's hot-water supply,
delivering $0.5\,\text{L/s}$ to each of $20$ fixtures across a
$30\,\si{\meter}$ run. Specify the pipe diameter at the trunk and
at the branches, and check the pressure drop against a maximum
allowable of $50\,\text{kPa}$.

\paragraph{Model-answer sketch.} Trunk flow if all fixtures draw at
once is $20\times0.5 = 10\,\text{L/s}$, but a diversity factor of
$0.3$ to $0.5$ is standard, so size the trunk for roughly $4\,\text{
L/s}$. Pick a target velocity near $1.5\,\si{\meter\per\second}$ to
limit noise and erosion: $A = Q/\bar u = 4\times10^{-3}/1.5 =
2.7\times10^{-3}\,\si{\meter}^2$, so $D\approx0.058\,\si{\meter}$;
choose a $54\,\si{\milli\meter}$ or $63\,\si{\milli\meter}$ standard
size. Branches carry $0.5\,\text{L/s}$ at the same velocity:
$D\approx0.02\,\si{\meter}$ ($20\,\si{\milli\meter}$). Check the
trunk drop with \texttt{code/pipe\_pressure\_drop.py}:
$\text{Re}_D = \rho\bar u D/\mu\approx
998\times1.5\times0.06/10^{-3}\approx9\times10^{4}$, turbulent,
$f\approx0.02$, $\Delta p = f(L/D)\tfrac{\rho\bar u^2}{2}\approx
0.02\times(30/0.06)\times998\times1.5^2/2\approx1.1\times10^{4}\,\si{
\pascal}$, well under $50\,\text{kPa}$. Add fitting and elevation
losses; if the margin is comfortable, a smaller trunk may be
acceptable. The deliverable states diameters, velocities, the
computed drop, and the margin to the limit.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Design a settling tank for separating sand ($\rho_s = 2650\,\si{
\kilogram\per\meter\cubed}$) from a water flow at $0.01\,\text{m}
^3\text{/s}$. Specify the tank's depth, length, and width, given
that all particles larger than $0.5\,\si{\milli\meter}$ must
settle out before exiting.

\paragraph{Model-answer sketch.} The settling velocity of a
$0.5\,\si{\milli\meter}$ sand grain sets the design. A Stokes
estimate gives $U_s = (\rho_s-\rho_f)g d^2/(18\mu) = 1650\times9.81
\times(5\times10^{-4})^2/(18\times10^{-3})\approx0.22\,\si{\meter\per
\second}$, but $\text{Re}=\rho_f U_s d/\mu\approx110$ is well past
Stokes, so use the intermediate-range drag and the iterative solver,
which lowers $U_s$ to roughly $0.07\,\si{\meter\per\second}$. The
ideal-settling criterion is that the overflow rate (flow divided by
plan area) not exceed $U_s$: $Q/(LW)\le U_s$, so $LW\ge0.01/0.07
\approx0.14\,\si{\meter}^2$. Pick depth so the horizontal residence
time exceeds the settling time from the surface: with depth $H$,
$t_{\text{settle}}=H/U_s$ and $t_{\text{residence}}=L W H/Q$, which
reduces to the same plan-area criterion. Choose, for example,
$L=2\,\si{\meter}$, $W=0.5\,\si{\meter}$ (plan area $1\,\si{\meter}
^2$, comfortably above the minimum), depth $H\approx0.5\,\si{\meter}$,
and keep the inlet velocity low to avoid re-suspension. The
deliverable states the settling velocity with its regime check, the
plan-area criterion, and the chosen dimensions with margin.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Design an exhaust stack of $50\,\si{\meter}$ height and $3\,\si{
\meter}$ diameter to survive a $25\,\si{\meter\per\second}$ wind
without vortex-induced resonance. Estimate the Strouhal frequency
and the stack's fundamental natural frequency; specify any
aerodynamic spoilers or structural changes needed.

\paragraph{Model-answer sketch.} Shedding frequency at the design
wind: $\text{Re} = UD/\nu = 25\times3/(1.5\times10^{-5}) =
5\times10^{6}$, so $\text{St}\approx0.2$ and $f_s = \text{St}\,U/D =
0.2\times25/3\approx1.7\,\si{\hertz}$. A $50\,\si{\meter}$ cantilever
stack has a low fundamental, on the order of $0.3$ to $1\,\si{\hertz}$
depending on wall thickness and base fixity; estimate it from
$f_n\approx(1.875)^2/(2\pi)\sqrt{EI/(\rho_L L^4)}$ for a uniform
cantilever. Lock-in occurs at $U_{\text{lock}} = f_n D/\text{St}$; if
the fundamental is near $0.5\,\si{\hertz}$, lock-in falls at $U
\approx7.5\,\si{\meter\per\second}$, a frequent wind, so vortex-
induced vibration is a real risk across the operating range. Remedies:
helical strakes over the top third of the stack (the standard fix,
disrupting coherent shedding), a tuned mass damper, or stiffening to
move $f_n$ out of the common-wind lock-in band. The deliverable
states $f_s$, the estimated $f_n$, the lock-in speed, and the chosen
countermeasure with its rationale.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Design the underbody and rear-end shape of a road vehicle to
reduce its drag coefficient from a baseline $C_D = 0.35$ to a
target $0.28$. State the principal mechanisms (rear-spoiler shape,
underbody flatness, wheel housing, trailing-edge taper) and
estimate the contribution of each.

\paragraph{Model-answer sketch.} The target is a $20\%$ reduction in
$C_D$, of which the largest single share usually comes from managing
rear separation: a tapered rear (boat-tail) or a properly sized
spoiler delays or organises separation and narrows the wake, worth
perhaps $0.03$ to $0.04$ in $C_D$. A flat, sealed underbody with a
rear diffuser reduces underbody turbulence and pressure drag, worth
another $0.01$ to $0.02$. Wheel-housing treatment and partial wheel
shrouding cut the disturbed flow around the wheels, worth around
$0.01$. Smoothing the front (rounded leading edges, managed cooling-
air path) trims a further $0.005$ to $0.01$. Summing the central
estimates gives roughly $0.06$ to $0.08$, enough to move $0.35$ to
$0.28$ if the measures combine without too much interaction. The
deliverable lists each mechanism, its estimated contribution, and the
caveat that the contributions are not strictly additive and need
wind-tunnel or CFD confirmation.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Diagnosis and reverse engineering}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
A pump is delivering only $60\,\%$ of its rated flow at the rated
discharge pressure. Identify three viscous-flow mechanisms (pipe
fouling, partial valve closure, cavitation in the pump) that could
produce this and the diagnostic that would distinguish among them.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Fouling raises the effective roughness
and lowers the diameter, shifting the system curve up; the diagnostic
is to inspect or borescope the line and to compare the measured
pressure drop at known flow with the new-pipe Moody prediction (a
drop larger than predicted points to fouling). A partially closed
valve adds a localised loss; the diagnostic is to check valve
position and to look for a large, concentrated pressure drop across
the valve rather than distributed along the run. Cavitation in the
pump shows as a flow-and-head deficit accompanied by noise and
vibration, worsening as suction pressure falls; the diagnostic is to
raise the net positive suction head (lower the pump, raise the supply
level, or throttle the discharge rather than the suction) and see
whether the deficit clears. The three are distinguished by where the
loss is concentrated (distributed, at the valve, or at the pump) and
by the acoustic and suction-pressure signatures.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
An exhaust stack on a power station has begun to oscillate
visibly under moderate winds. Identify the most probable
mechanism, the test that would confirm it, and one immediate and
one permanent remedial measure.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} The most probable mechanism is vortex-
induced vibration: at the prevailing wind speed the Strouhal shedding
frequency has come into lock-in with one of the stack's natural
frequencies. The confirming test is to measure the oscillation
frequency and the wind speed, compute $f_s = \text{St}\,U/D$, and
check that the observed motion frequency matches both $f_s$ and a
modal frequency of the stack (an instrumented modal survey settles
it). Immediate measure: restrict operation or temporarily attach
guy cables or a perimeter rope-and-chain spoiler to break the
shedding coherence. Permanent measure: fit helical strakes over the
upper third of the stack, or install a tuned mass damper, or stiffen
the structure to move $f_n$ out of the common-wind lock-in band.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A wind-tunnel test of a passenger-car model at $1/5$ scale gives
$C_D = 0.42$, but full-scale road testing gives $C_D = 0.32$.
Identify the most probable cause of the discrepancy, given that
the wind tunnel was operated at a freestream velocity matching
the full-scale highway speed.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} The probable cause is a Reynolds-number
mismatch. At $1/5$ scale with the same speed, the model Reynolds
number is one-fifth of the full-scale value, $\text{Re}_m =
\text{Re}_p/5$. If that lower Reynolds number leaves the model in a
subcritical regime (laminar separation, wider wake) while the full-
scale vehicle runs supercritical (turbulent boundary layer, delayed
separation, narrower wake), the model reads a higher $C_D$. The fix
for similarity is to match Reynolds number, which would require a
tunnel speed of five times the road speed (often impractical) or a
pressurised or cryogenic tunnel; the practical fix is to trip the
model boundary layer to turbulence with roughness strips so it
separates like the full-scale flow. The discrepancy is the everyday
reason model drag must be Reynolds-corrected before it is trusted.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A water-supply pipe section has measured pressure drop $20\,\%$
larger than the Moody-chart prediction for new pipe of its
material. Identify what physical change in the pipe could account
for this and the inspection that would confirm or refute it.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} A $20\%$ excess pressure drop at fixed
flow points to increased roughness or reduced bore: scale or
corrosion deposits raise $\epsilon/D$ and shrink $D$, both of which
raise the friction factor (and the $D^{-5}$ sensitivity of
Darcy-Weisbach makes even a small bore reduction strong). Back out the
implied roughness: solve Colebrook for the $\epsilon/D$ that
reproduces the measured $f$ at the operating Reynolds number, and
compare to the new-pipe handbook value. The confirming inspection is
a borescope or an ultrasonic wall-thickness and deposit survey of the
inner wall; a cut sample settles it. If the bore and wall are clean,
revisit the flow-rate measurement and the temperature (hence
viscosity) before blaming the pipe.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Failure analysis}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
The BEA report on Air France Flight 447 \cite{acc:bea-af447}
identifies a sequence beginning with pitot-tube icing and ending
with deep stall and impact. Argue, in $200$ to $300$ words, how
the boundary-layer physics of high-altitude stall constrains the
recovery procedure, and what design discipline (cockpit
indication, autoflight handoff, crew training) the case
illuminates.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} A strong answer ties the recovery
constraint to the separated-flow physics: in a deep stall the wing
boundary layer is massively separated, lift has collapsed and drag is
several times its attached value, so the only way to reattach the
flow is to lower the angle of attack, which at altitude demands a
nose-down attitude and a large loss of altitude to rebuild airspeed.
The thin air at $35{,}000\,\text{ft}$ leaves little margin between
stall and overspeed, and the separated wake degrades control
authority. The case illuminates three disciplines: unambiguous
cockpit indication of the stalled state (angle-of-attack display,
stall warning that stays valid under unreliable airspeed), clean
autoflight handoff that does not drop the crew into manual control at
a high workload moment, and crew training in high-altitude upset and
unreliable-airspeed recovery \cite{acc:bea-af447}. The
boundary-layer point is that stall is a flight-envelope boundary set
by separation, not a recoverable nuisance.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A diffuser at the inlet of a centrifugal compressor stage stalls
intermittently at off-design flow rates, producing surge cycles.
Identify the boundary-layer mechanism, the parameter (typically
diffuser flow angle or diffuser area ratio) that controls it, and
the design countermeasure.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} The mechanism is boundary-layer
separation in the diffuser (vaned or vaneless) under the adverse
pressure gradient that the diffuser imposes to recover dynamic
pressure. At off-design flow the incidence onto the diffuser changes,
the local pressure gradient steepens, the boundary layer separates,
and the stage cannot sustain the pressure rise, which couples into the
cyclic charge-and-discharge of surge. The controlling parameter is the
diffuser inlet flow angle (equivalently the area ratio and divergence),
which sets whether the gradient stays within the attached limit.
Countermeasures: variable-geometry diffuser vanes that re-aim with
flow, bleed or recirculation to widen the stable range, and a surge
or stall margin built into the operating line so the machine does not
run near the separation boundary.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A modern long-span suspension bridge has been instrumented with
accelerometers and has shown low-amplitude oscillation at
specific moderate wind speeds. Argue, in $200$ to $300$ words,
the boundary-layer / vortex-shedding mechanism most likely
responsible, distinguishing it from the larger-amplitude
aeroelastic flutter that destroyed the original Tacoma Narrows
bridge \cite{acc:tacoma-narrows-1941}.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} The low-amplitude, narrow-wind-speed-band
oscillation is the signature of vortex-induced vibration: the Kármán
shedding frequency $f_s = \text{St}\,U/D$ has come into lock-in with a
deck or cable mode, and the response self-limits because the shedding
forcing is bounded and the lock-in range is narrow. The mechanism is
forced resonance by a bounded aerodynamic load. Torsional flutter, the
larger-amplitude phenomenon that destroyed the original Tacoma Narrows
deck, is different in kind: it is a self-excited aeroelastic
instability in which the deck motion feeds energy into itself, so the
amplitude grows without bound above a critical wind speed rather than
locking to a shedding frequency. The distinguishing observations are
amplitude (bounded versus divergent), the wind-speed dependence (a
narrow lock-in band versus a critical-speed threshold above which it
grows), and whether the motion frequency tracks $\text{St}\,U/D$
(vortex shedding) or sits at a structural torsional mode independent
of $U$ (flutter).
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Judgment}\pathtag{standard}

\begin{exercise}
A senior engineer argues that pipe-flow design can be done with
the Hagen-Poiseuille relation across the full operating range,
because the typical design Reynolds number is well below $2300$.
Write a one-paragraph reply that takes the argument seriously,
identifies the operating-range conditions under which it is true,
and identifies the conditions under which it fails.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} A fair reply grants the case where it
holds: for genuinely viscous-dominated flows (very low velocity, small
bore, or high-viscosity fluid) the Reynolds number stays below $2300$
across the operating range, and Hagen-Poiseuille is then exact, not
approximate. The argument fails wherever the operating range reaches
the transition: most water and air distribution systems run at
$\text{Re}_D$ of $10^4$ to $10^6$, where the flow is turbulent and the
friction factor follows the Moody chart, not $64/\text{Re}_D$. The
test is to compute $\text{Re}_D$ at the highest design flow, not the
nominal one; a system that is laminar at average flow can be turbulent
at peak draw. The honest position is that Hagen-Poiseuille is the right
tool only after the regime check confirms laminar flow at the worst-
case operating point.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A junior engineer asks why CFD results are reported with three
significant figures when the underlying turbulence closure
introduces uncertainties of $10$ to $30\,\%$. Write a one-paragraph
reply that distinguishes verification from validation and the
status of CFD predictions absent experimental anchoring.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} The reply separates two things. The three
significant figures report the precision with which the code solved its
discretised equations, which is a verification question answered by
mesh refinement and order-of-accuracy checks; a converged solver can
report many digits. Those digits say nothing about whether the
equations and the turbulence closure model the real flow, which is the
validation question, answered only by comparison with experiment. A
closure carrying $10$ to $30\%$ model uncertainty means the prediction
is accurate to that band regardless of how many digits the solver
prints. The honest report states the converged value with its
discretisation error and, separately, the model-form uncertainty from
the closure; an unvalidated CFD result is a precise solution of an
approximate model, not a prediction of the flow.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A designer proposes to omit boundary-layer-transition analysis on
a wind-turbine blade because the operating Reynolds number is in
the fully turbulent range. Write a one-paragraph response
addressing whether transition still matters and, if so, on what
parts of the blade.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Transition still matters. The chord-based
Reynolds number on a wind-turbine blade falls sharply toward the root,
where the chord and local speed are small, so the inboard sections can
sit in a transitional or even laminar regime even when the tip is
fully turbulent. The blade also carries a laminar run near the leading
edge before transition, and the transition location moves with angle
of attack and with surface contamination (insect debris, erosion,
rain), which shifts the drag and the separation behaviour. The
practical consequences are a measurable drag and annual-energy penalty
from leading-edge roughness and a sensitivity of stall behaviour to
transition. The response is that transition analysis is warranted on
the inboard sections and on the leading-edge region, and that surface
cleanliness and erosion protection are part of keeping the assumed
transition behaviour valid.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Identify a viscous-flow situation in your immediate environment
where the boundary-layer regime controls observable behaviour.
State, in $200$ to $300$ words, the Reynolds number you estimate,
the regime it corresponds to, the dominant viscous mechanism, and
the consequence you observe.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Open-ended; a complete answer names a
concrete situation, estimates its Reynolds number with stated length
and velocity scales, places it in a regime, names the mechanism, and
ties it to an observation. One worked example: honey dripping from a
spoon, length scale $\sim10\,\si{\milli\meter}$, velocity $\sim
0.1\,\si{\meter\per\second}$, $\nu\sim10^{-2}\,\si{\meter}^2/\si{
\second}$, so $\text{Re}\sim0.1\ll1$, the creeping-flow regime
dominated by viscosity, which is why the thread is smooth and slow and
does not break into droplets the way water does. Another: cigarette
smoke rising in still air, $\text{Re}$ of a few hundred near the
source, where the laminar plume is visibly smooth before it transitions
to a turbulent, chaotically mixing column a short distance up, a
direct sighting of the laminar-to-turbulent transition. The grading
point is that the estimate, the regime, the mechanism, and the
observation hang together.
\end{exercise}
