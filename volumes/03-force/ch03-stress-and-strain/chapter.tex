\noindent
Chapter 2 closed with the observation that the global statics of a
structure does not catch a joint failure; the joint's free-body
diagram does. This chapter takes the next step inward: the joint's
free-body diagram does not catch the material's failure, either; the
material's stress state does. We pass from the resultant force on a
cross-section to the distribution of force inside the section, and
from the deformation of a member to the deformation of an
infinitesimal element of material at any point inside the member. The
moves give the engineer the working vocabulary of mechanics of
materials: stress, strain, the elastic constants, the principal
stresses, the yield criterion, and the stress concentration that
turns an apparently safe member into a part that breaks.

\section{Internal forces become stresses}\pathtag{core}

A beam with an axial tensile force $N$ at a cross-section transmits
that force across the section by a distribution of force per unit
area. The local force per unit area is the \engterm{stress}. For a
straight prismatic member loaded uniaxially and at sufficient distance
from the load application points, the distribution is uniform across
the section, and the stress is simply
\[
\sigma = \frac{N}{A},
\]
the axial force divided by the cross-sectional area. The dimension is
$[\text{force}]/[\text{length}]^2$, with SI unit the pascal,
$1\,\si{\pascal} = 1\,\si{\newton\per\meter\squared}$.

The uniformity assumption requires distance from the load application
points. Saint-Venant's principle, established by Adh\'emar Jean
Claude Barr\'e de Saint-Venant in 1855 and now a standard result in
elasticity, states that the difference between two statically
equivalent loading distributions on a small region of a body's
boundary decays rapidly with distance from the loaded region. Far
from the load, the only thing the cross-section knows about the load
is its resultant. The standard engineering rule is that the
uniformity assumption holds at distances exceeding the largest cross-
section dimension from any concentrated load. Within that boundary
layer, the stress distribution is non-uniform, and the local geometry
matters; we return to that case in section 3.8.

For a beam under transverse load, the internal force at a cross-section
has three components (axial $N$, shear $V$, bending moment $M$ in two
dimensions; six components in three dimensions). Each component
produces its own stress distribution across the section:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Axial $N$ produces uniform normal stress $\sigma = N/A$.
  \item Bending moment $M$ produces a linear normal stress
    distribution $\sigma = -M y / I$ for elastic bending of a slender
    beam, with $y$ measured from the neutral axis and $I$ the second
    moment of area about that axis (chapter 8 of this volume).
  \item Shear $V$ produces a shear stress distribution. For a
    rectangular section, the shear stress is parabolic with maximum
    at the neutral axis and zero at the top and bottom surfaces; the
    formula $\tau = VQ / (Ib)$ gives the local shear stress for any
    section (chapter 8).
\end{itemize}

The stress at a point under combined loading is the sum of the
contributions, by superposition. The summation is valid because
elasticity is linear in the regime this chapter treats; non-linear
material behaviour breaks superposition and is the subject of section
3.7.

The engineer's first move on a stressed body is therefore the same as
the statician's first move on a loaded structure: identify the
internal forces at each cross-section, then translate those into the
stress field at every point. The first translation is global to
cross-section (statics, chapter 2); the second is cross-section to
point (this chapter).

\section{Normal and shear stress}\pathtag{core}

At a point inside a loaded body, the state of stress is a tensor
quantity, not a vector. The reason is that the force per unit area at
a point depends on the orientation of the surface the area is taken
on. Imagine an infinitesimal cube of material at the point, aligned
with a chosen coordinate system. Each face of the cube has a force
per unit area associated with it; the components of that force per
unit area on each face are what we call the components of the stress
tensor.

We adopt the convention that the stress component $\sigma_{ij}$ is
the force per unit area in direction $j$ acting on the face whose
outward normal is in direction $i$. The diagonal components
($\sigma_{xx}$, $\sigma_{yy}$, $\sigma_{zz}$) are \engterm{normal
stresses}: they act along the outward normal of their face. The
off-diagonal components ($\sigma_{xy}$, $\sigma_{xz}$, $\sigma_{yz}$,
and so on) are \engterm{shear stresses}: they act parallel to their
face.

A second convention: tension is positive, compression negative for
normal stresses. For shear, the sign is set by the direction
convention on the coordinate axes; a positive $\sigma_{xy}$ on the
$+x$ face acts in the $+y$ direction.

The stress tensor in three dimensions has nine components arranged in
a $3 \times 3$ matrix:
\[
[\sigma] = \begin{pmatrix}
\sigma_{xx} & \sigma_{xy} & \sigma_{xz} \\
\sigma_{yx} & \sigma_{yy} & \sigma_{yz} \\
\sigma_{zx} & \sigma_{zy} & \sigma_{zz}
\end{pmatrix}.
\]
The tensor is symmetric: $\sigma_{ij} = \sigma_{ji}$. The symmetry
follows from moment equilibrium of the infinitesimal cube. If it
were not symmetric, the cube would carry a net moment and would not
be in equilibrium. The symmetry leaves six independent components in
three dimensions, three in two dimensions ($\sigma_{xx}$,
$\sigma_{yy}$, $\sigma_{xy}$).

The stress tensor is sometimes written as the \engterm{Cauchy stress
tensor}, after Augustin-Louis Cauchy, who established its tensor
nature in 1822. Cauchy's contribution was the recognition that the
force per unit area at a point on any surface through the point can
be obtained from the stress tensor by a linear operation: if $\hat{n}$
is the unit outward normal to the surface, the traction vector $\vec
{t}$ on that surface is
\[
\vec{t} = [\sigma] \hat{n},
\]
or in index notation, $t_i = \sigma_{ij} n_j$. This is \engterm{Cauchy's
formula}, and it is the bridge from the global concept of stress on a
particular section to the local concept of stress at a point. The
formula's compactness hides the work: stress is a tensor because the
question ``what is the force per unit area at this point'' is
ill-posed without specifying the surface orientation.

\begin{principle}[The stress at a point is a tensor]
At any point inside a loaded body, the local state of stress is a
symmetric second-rank tensor with six independent components in three
dimensions. The traction vector on any surface through the point is
obtained from the tensor by Cauchy's formula, $\vec{t} = [\sigma]
\hat{n}$. The tensor character of stress is what makes stress
transformation under change of coordinate axes a non-trivial
operation; the Mohr's-circle construction of section 3.5 is the
graphical version of the transformation rule.
\end{principle}

\section{Strain: small deformation theory}\pathtag{core}

A loaded body deforms. The relative motion of nearby material points
is the \engterm{strain}. We restrict attention to small deformations,
in which the displacement of any point is small compared to the
body's dimensions and the strains are small compared to unity (a
strain of $1$ would mean a body had stretched to twice its length;
in elastic engineering practice strains are typically $10^{-4}$ to
$10^{-3}$, well within the small-strain regime).

Consider a one-dimensional bar of length $L$ that is stretched to
length $L + \Delta L$ by axial loading. The \engterm{engineering
normal strain} is
\[
\epsilon = \frac{\Delta L}{L},
\]
dimensionless. Engineering strain is the standard small-strain
measure for elastic problems; it is sometimes called the
\engterm{nominal} strain to distinguish from the \engterm{true} (or
logarithmic) strain $\epsilon_{\text{true}} = \ln(L'/L)$ used in
large-deformation plasticity. For elastic strains the two coincide
to leading order.

In two and three dimensions, strain is defined locally as a tensor.
For a displacement field $\vec{u}(x, y, z)$, the small-strain tensor
is the symmetric part of the displacement gradient:
\[
\epsilon_{ij} = \frac{1}{2}\left(\frac{\partial u_i}{\partial x_j}
+ \frac{\partial u_j}{\partial x_i}\right).
\]
The diagonal components are \engterm{normal strains}: $\epsilon_{xx}
= \partial u_x / \partial x$ is the fractional change in length of an
element originally aligned with the $x$-axis. The off-diagonal
components are \engterm{shear strains}: $\epsilon_{xy} = \frac{1}{2}
(\partial u_x/\partial y + \partial u_y/\partial x)$ is the
infinitesimal rotation-symmetric part of the angle change between
two material lines originally along the $x$- and $y$-axes.

A common notational hazard: engineering shear strain $\gamma_{xy} = 2
\epsilon_{xy}$. The factor of two arises because engineering shear
strain measures the total angle change between two perpendicular
lines, while the tensor strain $\epsilon_{xy}$ measures half of
that. Hooke's law for shear stress is conventionally written $\tau =
G \gamma$, using engineering shear strain $\gamma$, not $2G
\epsilon_{xy}$. The reader will encounter both conventions and must
track which is in use.

The strain tensor is symmetric (by definition of the symmetric part
of the gradient) and has six independent components in three
dimensions, three in two dimensions. The antisymmetric part of the
displacement gradient is the local rigid-body rotation, which does
not produce strain; the strain tensor captures only the
deformation.

\section{Hooke's law and elastic constants}\pathtag{core}

For an isotropic linear elastic material, the stress tensor and the
strain tensor are proportional by a linear relation involving two
independent elastic constants. The generalised Hooke's law, in tensor
form, is
\[
\sigma_{ij} = \lambda \,\epsilon_{kk} \,\delta_{ij} + 2\mu
\,\epsilon_{ij},
\]
where $\lambda$ and $\mu$ are the Lam\'e constants, $\delta_{ij}$ is
the Kronecker delta, and the repeated index $k$ is summed (so
$\epsilon_{kk} = \epsilon_{xx} + \epsilon_{yy} + \epsilon_{zz}$ is
the trace of the strain tensor, also called the volumetric strain).

In engineering practice the Lam\'e constants are usually replaced by
two more directly measurable constants: the \engterm{Young's modulus}
$E$ and the \engterm{Poisson's ratio} $\nu$.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Young's modulus $E$ is the proportionality constant in
    uniaxial loading: $\sigma = E \epsilon$ for a bar stretched
    axially. Units are pascals. Steel has $E \approx 200\,\text{GPa}$,
    aluminum alloys $\approx 70\,\text{GPa}$, structural wood (along
    grain) $\approx 10\,\text{GPa}$, concrete $\approx 30\,\text{GPa}$.
  \item Poisson's ratio $\nu$ is the negative of the ratio of
    transverse strain to axial strain in uniaxial loading: a bar
    stretched axially contracts laterally by $\nu \epsilon$. For most
    engineering metals $\nu \approx 0.3$; for cork, $\nu \approx 0$
    (cork shows essentially no lateral contraction, which is why it
    inserts cleanly into bottles); for rubber, $\nu \approx 0.5$
    (rubber is nearly incompressible).
\end{itemize}

The \engterm{shear modulus} $G$ relates shear stress to engineering
shear strain, $\tau = G \gamma$. The \engterm{bulk modulus} $K$
relates mean pressure to volumetric strain, $p = -K \epsilon_{kk}$.
For isotropic materials these are not independent:
\[
G = \frac{E}{2(1+\nu)}, \quad K = \frac{E}{3(1-2\nu)}.
\]
Two constants suffice for isotropic linear elasticity; any pair from
$(E, \nu, G, K, \lambda, \mu)$ may be used, and the others are
algebraically determined.

For an isotropic linear elastic material in three dimensions, Hooke's
law in the engineering constants is
\[
\epsilon_{xx} = \frac{1}{E}\left[\sigma_{xx} - \nu(\sigma_{yy} +
\sigma_{zz})\right],
\]
with analogous equations for $\epsilon_{yy}$ and $\epsilon_{zz}$,
and the shear relations $\gamma_{ij} = \tau_{ij} / G$ for $i \neq j$.
The form makes evident that a uniaxial stress in one direction
produces strain in all three coordinate directions: axial extension,
and transverse contraction by Poisson's ratio.

Hooke's 1678 statement of the law was scalar (extension proportional
to force in a spring); Cauchy's 1822 generalisation made it tensorial.
The conceptual unity is intact: stress is a linear function of strain
for small deformations of elastic materials. The chapter's discipline
is the precise statement and use of that linear function.

\section{Stress transformation, Mohr's circle}\pathtag{standard}

The stress tensor at a point is the same physical quantity regardless
of the coordinate system used to express its components, but the
numerical values of the components change under rotation of the
coordinate axes. The transformation rule is the standard tensor
rotation rule. In two dimensions, with axes rotated by angle $\theta$
counterclockwise, the new components are
\begin{align*}
\sigma_{x'x'} &= \frac{\sigma_{xx} + \sigma_{yy}}{2} +
\frac{\sigma_{xx} - \sigma_{yy}}{2}\cos 2\theta + \sigma_{xy}\sin
2\theta, \\
\sigma_{y'y'} &= \frac{\sigma_{xx} + \sigma_{yy}}{2} -
\frac{\sigma_{xx} - \sigma_{yy}}{2}\cos 2\theta - \sigma_{xy}\sin
2\theta, \\
\sigma_{x'y'} &= -\frac{\sigma_{xx} - \sigma_{yy}}{2}\sin 2\theta +
\sigma_{xy}\cos 2\theta.
\end{align*}
The factor of $2$ inside the trigonometric arguments is the source of
much undergraduate confusion; the physical meaning is that the
stress tensor is invariant under rotation by $\pi$, not by $2\pi$, so
the transformation period is $\pi$ rather than $2\pi$.

There exists a particular orientation of axes at which the shear
stresses vanish and the normal stresses take their extreme values.
The corresponding axes are the \engterm{principal axes}; the normal
stresses on those axes are the \engterm{principal stresses}. In two
dimensions, the principal stresses are
\[
\sigma_{1,2} = \frac{\sigma_{xx} + \sigma_{yy}}{2} \pm \sqrt{\left(
\frac{\sigma_{xx} - \sigma_{yy}}{2}\right)^2 + \sigma_{xy}^2},
\]
with $\sigma_1 \geq \sigma_2$ by convention. The orientation of the
principal axes is given by
\[
\tan 2\theta_p = \frac{2 \sigma_{xy}}{\sigma_{xx} - \sigma_{yy}}.
\]
The two principal stresses and the angle $\theta_p$ together replace
the three components $(\sigma_{xx}, \sigma_{yy}, \sigma_{xy})$. The
principal-axis representation strips away coordinate-system
arbitrariness and presents the stress state in its intrinsic form.

The maximum shear stress at the point is also computable directly:
\[
\tau_{\max} = \frac{\sigma_1 - \sigma_2}{2} = \sqrt{\left(
\frac{\sigma_{xx} - \sigma_{yy}}{2}\right)^2 + \sigma_{xy}^2},
\]
attained on planes oriented at $45^{\circ}$ to the principal axes.
The maximum shear is the basis of the Tresca yield criterion (section
3.6).

The \engterm{Mohr's circle} is a graphical construction that
encapsulates the transformation. Plot points on the $(\sigma, \tau)$
plane representing the stress on the $x$-face ($\sigma_{xx},
\sigma_{xy}$) and on the $y$-face ($\sigma_{yy}, -\sigma_{xy}$). The
two points are diametrically opposite on a circle whose centre is at
$((\sigma_{xx} + \sigma_{yy})/2, 0)$ and whose radius is $\tau_{
\max}$. Rotating the physical axes by $\theta$ corresponds to
rotating the diametrically-opposite points by $2\theta$ on the
circle, in the opposite sense (clockwise on the circle for
counterclockwise on the body). The intersections with the
horizontal axis give the principal stresses; the highest point on
the circle gives $\tau_{\max}$.

Mohr's circle is the engineer's mental machinery for stress
transformation. The numerical formulas above are the analytical
back-up; in practice a working stress analyst sketches the circle
in seconds and reads off the principal stresses and the maximum
shear without computation.

In three dimensions there are three principal stresses $\sigma_1
\geq \sigma_2 \geq \sigma_3$ obtained as the eigenvalues of the
stress tensor. The Mohr's circle generalises to three circles in
the same plane; the maximum shear is $(\sigma_1 - \sigma_3)/2$,
attained on planes oriented at $45^{\circ}$ to the first and third
principal axes.

\section{Yield and failure criteria}\pathtag{core}

A ductile material under stress remains elastic up to a threshold
stress called the \engterm{yield stress} $\sigma_y$. Above the
threshold, the material begins to deform plastically: irreversible
deformation accumulates with continued loading. The question that
yield criteria answer is, for a general state of stress (with
several non-zero components of the stress tensor), at what
combination of stresses does the material yield?

The yield stress is not itself a tensor: it is a scalar property of
the material, determined by a uniaxial tension test. The yield
criterion is a function of the stress tensor that, at the yield
threshold, equals $\sigma_y$. Two criteria are in standard
engineering use.

\subsection{Tresca: maximum shear stress}

The \engterm{Tresca criterion}, due to Henri Tresca's nineteenth-
century experiments on extrusion of metals, predicts yield when the
maximum shear stress reaches half the uniaxial yield stress:
\[
\tau_{\max} = \frac{\sigma_1 - \sigma_3}{2} = \frac{\sigma_y}{2},
\quad \text{or equivalently} \quad \sigma_1 - \sigma_3 = \sigma_y.
\]
Tresca is conservative (it predicts yield at a lower combined-stress
state than experiments show for most ductile metals) and simple. It
is the default yield criterion in many design codes, including the
ASME Boiler and Pressure Vessel Code.

\subsection{Von Mises: octahedral shear / distortion energy}

The \engterm{von Mises criterion}, proposed by Richard von Mises in
1913 and given a physical interpretation by Heinrich Hencky in 1924
as a distortion-energy criterion, predicts yield when the von Mises
equivalent stress reaches the uniaxial yield stress:
\[
\sigma_{\text{vM}} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{2}\left[(\sigma_1 - \sigma_2)^2
+ (\sigma_2 - \sigma_3)^2 + (\sigma_3 - \sigma_1)^2\right]} =
\sigma_y.
\]
For a general stress state with off-diagonal components,
\[
\sigma_{\text{vM}} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{2}\left[(\sigma_{xx} -
\sigma_{yy})^2 + (\sigma_{yy} - \sigma_{zz})^2 + (\sigma_{zz} -
\sigma_{xx})^2 + 6(\sigma_{xy}^2 + \sigma_{yz}^2 + \sigma_{zx}^2)
\right]}.
\]
Von Mises is less conservative than Tresca and matches experimental
data on ductile metals more closely. It is the default in most
structural-analysis software and most fatigue and finite-element
work. The two criteria agree exactly for uniaxial stress and for
pure shear scaled appropriately; they differ most in biaxial states.

The choice between Tresca and von Mises depends on the application.
For pressure vessels and code-governed structural design, Tresca's
conservatism is a feature. For component design where weight is at
a premium (aerospace, transportation), von Mises's accuracy is
preferable. Most working engineers know both and choose by context.

\begin{principle}[Yield is a tensor question]
A scalar yield stress $\sigma_y$ is a material property obtained from
a uniaxial tension test. The yield criterion (Tresca, von Mises, or
another) is the function of the full stress tensor that, at the
yield threshold, equals $\sigma_y$. The principal stresses are the
intermediate computation. A design check against yield requires (1)
the stress tensor at every point of interest, (2) the principal
stresses at that point, (3) the chosen yield criterion evaluated at
those principal stresses, and (4) comparison to the material's
yield stress with the appropriate factor of safety.
\end{principle}

\section{Material non-linearity, plasticity preview}\pathtag{mastery}

The linear elasticity of the previous sections assumes a stress-strain
relation that is linear and reversible. Real materials depart from
that idealisation in several directions.

\engterm{Plasticity}: above the yield stress, ductile materials
deform irreversibly. The stress-strain curve in uniaxial tension
typically shows a linear elastic region up to $\sigma_y$, a brief
yield plateau (in some materials), and a strain-hardening region in
which the stress required to continue plastic deformation grows with
accumulated strain. The strain-hardening behaviour is captured by
hardening laws (Ramberg-Osgood, power-law, bilinear) fit to
experimental data. After the maximum (ultimate) stress, the
specimen necks and fractures; the strain at fracture is the
material's ductility.

\engterm{Strain-rate sensitivity}: the yield stress and the
hardening law depend on the rate of straining. At low strain rates
(quasi-static, typical of structural service), strain-rate effects
are small. At high strain rates (impact, blast, shock), the yield
stress can be twice or more the static value, and the hardening law
changes character.

\engterm{Temperature dependence}: the elastic moduli, the yield
stress, and the hardening law all depend on temperature. Steel near
its melting point is essentially viscous; aluminum at $400^{\circ}\
\mathrm{C}$ has lost a third of its room-temperature yield strength;
polymers transition from rubbery to glassy at temperatures specific
to the polymer.

\engterm{Anisotropy}: many engineering materials (wood along and
across grain, rolled metals, composites, single crystals) are not
isotropic. The elastic constants depend on direction; the yield
stress depends on direction; the failure mode depends on direction.
The isotropic constitutive law of section 3.4 is replaced by a
tensorial elastic-stiffness matrix with up to twenty-one independent
components.

\engterm{Time-dependent behaviour}: at elevated temperatures, metals
creep (strain accumulates under sustained load); under cyclic
loading, they fatigue (cracks initiate and grow); in
chemically-aggressive environments, they corrode and stress-corrosion-
crack. Chapter 2 of Volume X treats fatigue and fracture mechanics
in depth; Chapter 3 of Volume X treats corrosion, wear, and
degradation.

The linear-elastic, isotropic, time-independent, isothermal,
small-strain model of sections 3.1-3.5 is the engineer's working
default. It is exact for many real materials in many real situations,
and a good first approximation for the rest. The corrections listed
above are added as needed; the discipline is knowing which correction
the situation requires.

\section{Stress concentration}\pathtag{core}

Saint-Venant's principle, cited in section 3.1, says that far from a
loaded region the only thing the body knows is the load's resultant.
The complement of the principle is that \emph{near} a discontinuity,
the local geometry matters very much. A hole in a plate, a fillet at
a step in shaft diameter, a keyway in a rotating shaft, a square
inside corner in a notched bar, a thread root on a fastener: each
of these produces a local stress field much larger than the nominal
stress on the gross section. The factor by which the local stress
exceeds the nominal is the \engterm{stress concentration factor}
$K_t$.

For a circular hole of radius $a$ in an infinite plate under remote
uniaxial tension $\sigma_0$, the classical Kirsch solution (1898)
gives the stress at the hole's edge as
\[
\sigma_{\max} = 3 \sigma_0,
\]
so $K_t = 3$. The factor of three is independent of the hole
diameter (so long as the hole is small compared to the plate's other
dimensions). The maximum occurs at the two points on the hole's edge
that lie on the diameter perpendicular to the load direction; the
edges parallel to the load actually experience a compressive stress
of magnitude $\sigma_0$.

Other canonical concentration factors, from elasticity-theory
solutions and from experimental measurement
\cite[appendices]{text:crandall-mech-of-solids}:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Elliptical hole with semi-axes $a$ (perpendicular to load)
    and $b$ (along load): $K_t = 1 + 2 a / b$. A circular hole has
    $a = b$ and $K_t = 3$; a long crack-like ellipse ($b \to 0$) has
    $K_t \to \infty$.
  \item Filleted shaft step (large to small diameter): $K_t$ ranges
    from about $1.5$ to $3$ depending on the ratio of fillet radius
    to small-shaft diameter; a sharp $90^{\circ}$ corner with zero
    radius has $K_t \to \infty$ in the elastic solution.
  \item Standard machine-screw thread root: $K_t \approx 2.5$ to
    $3.5$ in fatigue. The thread root is the canonical fatigue
    initiation site in a bolt.
  \item Square notch in a tension member, sharp internal corner:
    $K_t \approx 5$ or higher, depending on the depth ratio.
\end{itemize}

The elasticity factor $K_t$ is the linear elastic answer. Real
materials, especially ductile ones, redistribute stress locally by
small plastic deformation at the concentration site, and the
effective stress concentration in service is usually less than
$K_t$. For ductile static loading the redistribution is sufficient
that $K_t$ is often neglected and the gross-section stress used
instead. For brittle materials, for fatigue, and for dynamic
loading, $K_t$ is critical and the redistribution does not save
the part.

The engineering practice is to design fillet radii, fillets, and
transitions to keep $K_t$ low (typically below $2$) at every
geometric discontinuity. Sharp internal corners are the most
expensive things in mechanical design.

\section{Failure: where parts actually break and why}\pathtag{core}

A material loaded above the threshold its constitutive law allows
will fail. The chapter's failure section names the canonical sites
and mechanisms.

\subsection{Failure modes by material class}

\engterm{Ductile metals} (mild steel, aluminum alloys, copper) fail
by yielding followed by plastic deformation followed by ductile
fracture. The fracture surface is rough, often shows a cup-and-cone
geometry, and is preceded by visible necking. The yield criterion
governs the onset; the strain-hardening exponent governs the
ductility before fracture.

\engterm{Brittle materials} (cast iron, ceramics, glass, concrete in
tension, hardened steels) fail by sudden fracture with little or no
preceding plastic deformation. The fracture is initiated at a
microscopic flaw and propagates rapidly under tensile loading. The
governing model is fracture mechanics rather than yield: a stress
concentration that a ductile metal would redistribute, a brittle
material amplifies into crack propagation. Brittle materials are
strong in compression, weak in tension; their tensile strength is
typically $5$ to $20$ times less than their compressive strength.

\engterm{Composites} fail by a mixture of matrix yielding, matrix
cracking, fibre fracture, fibre-matrix debonding, and delamination,
in proportions that depend on the laminate stacking sequence and
the loading direction. The failure criterion is multi-component
(Tsai-Wu, Hashin, etc.) and the failure mode is often non-catastrophic
(damage accumulates gradually before the laminate's load capacity is
exceeded).

\engterm{Polymers} can be ductile (polycarbonate, polyethylene at
room temperature) or brittle (PMMA, polystyrene) depending on
temperature and strain rate; the same polymer can be ductile at low
strain rate and brittle at impact rates. Polymer failure is also
strongly time-dependent through creep at sustained stress and
through stress-relaxation at sustained strain.

\subsection{The stress concentration as failure initiator}

Real parts almost always fail at stress concentrations. The
canonical sites are bolt holes, fillet radii, thread roots, weld
toes, keyways, sharp internal corners, surface scratches, and
fabrication defects. Each of these is a place where the local
stress field is significantly larger than the nominal stress on
the gross section.

The mechanism is most acutely visible in two cases:

\engterm{Brittle fracture under static load}: a brittle material at
a sharp stress concentration sees a local stress that exceeds its
fracture strength even when the nominal stress is well below it.
The crack initiates at the concentration and propagates rapidly
across the section. Historical case: the early commercial jet
airliners of the mid-twentieth century experienced sudden in-flight
fuselage breakup after a small number of flight cycles. The accident
investigations identified fatigue cracks initiating at the square
corners of cabin window cutouts; the square corners had elastic
stress-concentration factors of approximately $K_t \approx 4$, and
the cyclic pressurisation produced fatigue at the concentration
even though the gross-section stresses were well below the
material's static strength. Subsequent airliner designs used
rounded (low-$K_t$) cabin windows; the lesson generalised across
all pressurised aerospace structures.

\engterm{Block shear at a bolted joint}: the modified upper
box-beam connection of the Hyatt Regency walkway, introduced in
chapter 1 of this volume, failed by block shear, the bolted-joint
failure mode of chapter 2. The mechanism is a stress concentration
at the bolt-to-plate interface combined with tension on the
plate's net section and shear along the planes parallel to the
load between the bolt and the plate edge. The NBS report
identified the doubled load on the modified connection and the
geometry of the bolt-to-flange spacing as the proximate causes;
the deeper cause is the stress concentration that any bolted
connection carries by construction, and the engineer's discipline
of designing the bolt pattern, edge distance, and plate thickness
to keep the concentration's amplified stress below the material's
capacity \cite{acc:nbs-hyatt-1982}.

\subsection{The diagnostic principle}

When a part has failed in service, the first diagnostic question is
where the fracture initiated. The initiation site is almost always
a stress concentration. The second question is whether the
concentration was anticipated in the design (and a finite, conservative
$K_t$ applied) or unanticipated (a defect, a fabrication scratch,
a service-induced notch, an unintended geometric feature). The
third question is whether the loading exceeded the design envelope.
The diagnostic discipline is the subject of Chapter 12 of Volume X,
forensic engineering; the chapter-3 lesson is that mechanics of
materials is the engineering of the answer to question one.

\begin{principle}[Parts break at stress concentrations]
Static or fatigue failure of a mechanical part initiates, in nearly
all cases, at a stress concentration: a hole, a fillet, a thread
root, a weld toe, a keyway, a surface scratch, or an internal defect.
Design against failure requires both (a) the stress field on the
gross section, computed from the loads and the section geometry, and
(b) the stress concentration factor at every geometric discontinuity,
applied as a multiplier on the nominal stress. The product of
nominal stress and $K_t$ is the local stress to be compared against
the material's allowable.
\end{principle}

\section{Project}\pathtag{core}

\begin{project}[Instrument a cantilever beam; measure strain at
three locations; compare to theory]
The reader instruments a small wooden or aluminum cantilever beam
with strain gauges at three locations, loads the beam in known
increments, and compares the measured strains to the predictions of
linear elastic beam theory. The deliverable is a thirty- to
fifty-page engineering report.

\textbf{Track}. Physical instrumentation plus analysis.
\textbf{Hazard class}: low. Strain gauges and conditioning
electronics carry no significant hazard at the loads used. Bench
testing only.

\textbf{Apparatus}. A cantilever beam of $25\,\si{\milli\meter} \times
3\,\si{\milli\meter}$ aluminum or $30\,\si{\milli\meter} \times 5\,
\si{\milli\meter}$ wood (oak, maple, or similar), $300\,\si{\milli
\meter}$ free length, clamped at one end in a bench vice. Three foil
strain gauges of $120\,\si{\ohm}$ nominal resistance bonded to the
top surface at distances $50\,\si{\milli\meter}$, $150\,\si{
\milli\meter}$, and $250\,\si{\milli\meter}$ from the clamped end. A
quarter-bridge strain-gauge conditioner (a commercial unit or a
Wheatstone bridge built from a power supply and an instrumentation
amplifier). A multimeter or oscilloscope for the conditioner's
output. A small set of calibration weights ($50\,\si{\gram}$ to
$500\,\si{\gram}$).

\textbf{Procedure}. The reader bonds the strain gauges, builds or
calibrates the conditioner, applies the calibration weights at the
free end of the cantilever in $50\,\si{\gram}$ increments, and
records the conditioner's output at each load and each gauge
location. The reader also records the beam's tip deflection (with
a ruler or a dial indicator) at each load.

\textbf{Analysis}. The reader computes, from linear elastic beam
theory:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item The bending moment at each gauge location as a function of
    applied tip load.
  \item The predicted top-surface bending stress at each gauge
    location, $\sigma = M y_{\max} / I$, with $y_{\max} = h/2$ and
    $I = bh^3/12$.
  \item The predicted strain at each gauge location, $\epsilon =
    \sigma / E$.
  \item The predicted tip deflection $\delta = PL^3 / (3EI)$.
\end{itemize}

The reader plots measured strain versus predicted strain at each
gauge location and computes the linear-regression slope (should be
$1.00 \pm 0.05$ if the bonding, the gauge factor, the conditioner
gain, and the beam properties are all correct). The reader plots
measured tip deflection versus predicted tip deflection and computes
the slope similarly.

\textbf{Reflection ($1{,}500$ to $3{,}000$ words)}. The reader
addresses:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Where the measured strain agrees with theory and where it
    departs; what caused the departure.
  \item Whether the strain at the gauge nearest the clamp is
    influenced by the boundary condition (Saint-Venant principle).
  \item The uncertainty of the elastic modulus $E$ used in the
    prediction; how the uncertainty propagates to the predicted
    strain.
  \item One quantity in the experiment whose measurement was the
    limiting source of error; how a second iteration would address
    it.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Deliverable}. The bonded beam (photographed), the
conditioner schematic, the strain data, the deflection data, the
calculations, the plots, and the reflection.

\textbf{Time}. Six to twelve hours of setup (gauge bonding is the
slowest step), one to two hours of measurement, four to eight hours
of analysis and write-up.
\end{project}

\section{Exercises}

The exercises mix calculation, derivation, estimation, simulation,
design, diagnosis, failure analysis, and judgment. Solutions are
provided for calculation and derivation exercises; estimation
exercises receive published reference estimates with the editor's
working; design, diagnosis, failure-analysis, and judgment exercises
receive rubrics rather than single answers. Exercises are tagged
with their reader-path tier.

\subsection*{Calculation}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
A steel rod of diameter $20\,\si{\milli\meter}$ carries an axial
tensile load of $50\,\text{kN}$. Find the normal stress in the rod.
State the result in MPa and as a fraction of the typical structural
steel yield stress $\sigma_y = 250\,\text{MPa}$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A bar of mild steel ($E = 200\,\text{GPa}$, $\nu = 0.3$) has a
uniaxial stress $\sigma_{xx} = 100\,\text{MPa}$, $\sigma_{yy} =
\sigma_{zz} = 0$. Find the three engineering normal strains.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A point in a body has stress state $\sigma_{xx} = 80\,\text{MPa}$,
$\sigma_{yy} = 40\,\text{MPa}$, $\sigma_{xy} = 30\,\text{MPa}$. Find
the two principal stresses, the orientation of the principal axes,
and the maximum shear stress.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
For the stress state in the previous exercise, evaluate the von
Mises equivalent stress and the Tresca equivalent stress (using
$\sigma_3 = 0$ for the out-of-plane principal stress). Compare to a
yield stress of $\sigma_y = 100\,\text{MPa}$ and state whether the
material has yielded by either criterion.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A thin-walled cylindrical pressure vessel of inner radius $r =
0.5\,\si{\meter}$ and wall thickness $t = 8\,\si{\milli\meter}$
contains gas at $p = 2\,\text{MPa}$. Find the hoop stress, the
longitudinal stress, and the maximum shear stress in the wall.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A circular shaft of diameter $40\,\si{\milli\meter}$ transmits a
torque of $500\,\text{N}\cdot\text{m}$. The maximum shear stress in
the shaft is $\tau_{\max} = 16T / (\pi d^3)$. Compute it. Compare
to a yield stress in shear of $\tau_y = \sigma_y/2$ for steel with
$\sigma_y = 350\,\text{MPa}$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A steel cable of cross-sectional area $200\,\si{\milli\meter}^2$
carries a tension of $50\,\text{kN}$. Find the normal stress and the
elongation of a $20\,\si{\meter}$ length of the cable. Take $E =
200\,\text{GPa}$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A circular hole of diameter $10\,\si{\milli\meter}$ pierces a wide
plate carrying remote uniaxial tension of $50\,\text{MPa}$. Find the
maximum stress at the hole's edge using the Kirsch solution.
Identify the two points on the hole's circumference where the
maximum occurs.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A standard $M16$ machine bolt of root diameter $13.5\,\si{\milli
\meter}$ carries an axial tensile load of $40\,\text{kN}$. Find the
nominal stress on the root. Apply a fatigue stress concentration of
$K_f = 3.0$ and state the effective fatigue stress.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A point in a body has principal stresses $\sigma_1 = 200\,\text{MPa}$,
$\sigma_2 = 100\,\text{MPa}$, $\sigma_3 = -50\,\text{MPa}$. Evaluate
the von Mises and Tresca equivalent stresses. State which criterion
is more conservative.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Derivation}\pathtag{standard}

\begin{exercise}
Derive the relations $G = E/[2(1+\nu)]$ and $K = E/[3(1-2\nu)]$ for
an isotropic linear elastic material. State the limiting values of
$\nu$ for which each result is finite and positive.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Show that, for a uniaxial stress state $\sigma_{xx} = \sigma$,
$\sigma_{yy} = \sigma_{xy} = 0$, the principal stresses are
$\sigma_1 = \sigma$, $\sigma_2 = 0$, and the maximum shear stress is
$\sigma/2$ at $45^{\circ}$ to the loading direction.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Derive Mohr's circle from the two-dimensional stress transformation
formulas in section 3.5. State the geometric construction explicitly.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
For pure shear ($\sigma_{xx} = \sigma_{yy} = 0$, $\sigma_{xy} =
\tau$), show that the principal stresses are $\sigma_1 = \tau$,
$\sigma_2 = -\tau$, and that the von Mises equivalent stress is
$\sqrt{3} \tau$. Compare to the Tresca equivalent stress for the
same state.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Derive the thin-walled-cylinder hoop and axial stress formulas
$\sigma_{\text{hoop}} = pr/t$ and $\sigma_{\text{axial}} = pr/(2t)$
from equilibrium considerations on appropriate free-body diagrams of
half-cylinders. State the assumptions that make the formulas
accurate.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[\textit{deferred to Chapter 8}]
Derive the bending stress formula $\sigma = -My/I$ for a slender
elastic beam under pure bending. State the assumptions used. Chapter
8 of this volume develops the full beam-theory machinery.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}

\subsection*{Simulation}\pathtag{mastery}

\begin{exercise}
Implement a Mohr's circle calculator that accepts $(\sigma_{xx},
\sigma_{yy}, \sigma_{xy})$ and returns $(\sigma_1, \sigma_2, \theta_p,

\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Implement a yield-criterion comparison: given a stress state
$(\sigma_{xx}, \sigma_{yy}, \sigma_{zz}, \sigma_{xy}, \sigma_{yz},

Test on five stress states chosen to highlight the differences
between the criteria.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Simulate the elastic stress field around a circular hole in a plate
under uniaxial tension using a finite-difference or finite-element
method. Compare the maximum stress at the hole's edge to the Kirsch

\end{exercise}

Design a steel hanger rod to suspend a $1{,}000\,\si{\kilogram}$ load

\begin{exercise}
Design a cylindrical thin-walled pressure vessel to contain gas at

\begin{exercise}
Redesign a $20\,\si{\milli\meter}$-diameter shaft that steps down to
$15\,\si{\milli\meter}$ diameter without a fillet (sharp shoulder),

\text{MPa}$ at the small section. Choose a fillet radius to keep the

\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Design a bolted lap joint to carry a tensile force of $60\,\text{kN}$
between two $10\,\si{\milli\meter}$-thick steel plates, using two

section tension, and block shear.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Design a thread for a fastener carrying $100\,\text{kN}$ axial tensile
load, with a factor of safety of $3$ against yield at the thread
root. Specify the nominal diameter, the thread pitch, and the
material grade. State the $K_t$ assumed at the thread root.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Diagnosis and reverse engineering}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
A steel bracket has fractured cleanly through its narrowest section

\end{exercise}

A bolted joint shows visible elongation of the bolt holes in the

\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A welded I-beam connection has cracked along the weld toe at the

\end{exercise}

A cantilever beam loaded at its free end has visibly bent at a

\end{exercise}
