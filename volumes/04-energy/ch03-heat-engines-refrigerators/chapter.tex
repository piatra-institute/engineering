\noindent
A heat engine takes thermal energy from a hot reservoir, converts part
of it to work, and rejects the remainder to a cold reservoir
(figure \ref{fig:vol04ch03:engine-refrigerator-flow}a). A
refrigerator runs the same machinery in reverse: input work moves
thermal energy from a cold reservoir to a hot one
(figure \ref{fig:vol04ch03:engine-refrigerator-flow}b). The Carnot bound
of chapter~1 fixes the maximum efficiency for given reservoir
temperatures; the working cycles of this chapter approximate the bound
by different geometries optimised for different applications. Rankine
for power plants, Brayton for jet engines and gas turbines, Otto for
spark-ignition cars, Diesel for compression-ignition engines and
ships, vapour-compression for refrigeration and air conditioning, and
combined cycles for the highest practical efficiencies.

\section{Carnot cycle as the reference}\pathtag{core}

The \engterm{Carnot cycle} consists of four reversible processes
operating between two thermal reservoirs at $T_h$ and $T_c$
\cite{hist:carnot1824}:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Isothermal expansion at $T_h$: the working fluid absorbs heat
    $Q_h$ from the hot reservoir while delivering work.
  \item Adiabatic expansion from $T_h$ to $T_c$: the fluid cools as
    it continues to deliver work, with no heat transfer.
  \item Isothermal compression at $T_c$: work is done on the fluid as
    it rejects heat $Q_c$ to the cold reservoir.
  \item Adiabatic compression from $T_c$ to $T_h$: work is done on
    the fluid as it heats back to the hot-reservoir temperature.
\end{enumerate}

On the $T$-$s$ diagram the Carnot cycle is a rectangle: two horizontal
isotherms and two vertical adiabats (figure
\ref{fig:vol04ch03:carnot-ts}). The area enclosed by the
rectangle equals the net work per cycle; the area below the upper
isotherm equals the heat absorbed; the area below the lower isotherm
equals the heat rejected. The thermal efficiency is

\input{volumes/04-energy/ch03-heat-engines-refrigerators/figures/fig-carnot-ts.tex}
\[
\eta_{\text{Carnot}} = 1 - \frac{T_c}{T_h},
\]
the famous Carnot bound. The bound is universal: no cyclic device
operating between the same two reservoirs can exceed it, regardless
of the working fluid, the cycle geometry, or the engineering of the
components. The proof is in chapter~1 of this volume; the bound is
the engineering content of the second law applied to power
production.

The Carnot cycle is not used in practice because the four processes
require infinitely slow operation to remain reversible, and the
required working-fluid behaviour (isothermal heat transfer with the
reservoirs) places impractical demands on the heat exchangers. The
cycle is the reference against which practical cycles are measured.
A real engine operating between $T_h$ and $T_c$ achieves some
fraction of the Carnot efficiency:
\[
\eta_{\text{actual}} = \eta_{\text{Carnot}} \times \eta_{\text{second-law}},
\]
with the second-law efficiency typically $30\,\%$ to $60\,\%$ for
working engines. The gap between Carnot and actual is the working
field of cycle optimisation.

\engterm{The reversed Carnot cycle for refrigeration}. Running the
Carnot cycle in reverse (counter-clockwise on the $T$-$s$ diagram)
gives a reversible refrigerator. The coefficient of performance for
cooling is
\[
\text{COP}_{\text{Carnot},R} = \frac{T_c}{T_h - T_c},
\]
and for heating (heat pump),
\[
\text{COP}_{\text{Carnot},HP} = \frac{T_h}{T_h - T_c} =
\text{COP}_{\text{Carnot},R} + 1.
\]
Both diverge as $T_h - T_c \to 0$ and approach unity from above as
$T_h - T_c$ grows (figure \ref{fig:vol04ch03:cop-vs-temperature-lift}).
The Carnot COP is the working bound for the
performance of any refrigerator or heat pump between the same two
reservoir temperatures.

\input{volumes/04-energy/ch03-heat-engines-refrigerators/figures/fig-cop-vs-temperature-lift.tex}

\begin{mastery}
The $T$-$s$ diagram is not only a picture of the cycle; it is an exact
accounting of work and of work destroyed, and reading it that way turns
the second law into a measuring tool. For any reversible process the heat
transferred is the area under the path on the $T$-$s$ plane, $q = \int T
\, ds$, so the area enclosed by a reversible cycle is the net heat, which
by the first law over a cycle equals the net work. The Carnot rectangle
makes this literal: its height is the temperature lift, its width is the
entropy span $\Delta s = q_h/T_h$ of the isothermal heat intake, and its
area $(T_h - T_c)\Delta s$ is exactly $w_{\text{net}}$. The heat rejected
is the strip below the lower isotherm, $T_c \Delta s$, and the ratio of
that strip to the full column under the upper isotherm is $T_c/T_h$, the
Carnot result read straight off the geometry.

The same diagram measures irreversibility. When heat $q$ crosses a finite
temperature gap from a source at $T_1$ to a sink at $T_2 < T_1$, the
source loses entropy $q/T_1$ and the sink gains $q/T_2$, so the universe
gains $s_{\text{gen}} = q(1/T_2 - 1/T_1) > 0$. The work that transfer
forfeited, the area on the $T$-$s$ plane between where the heat could have
been delivered and where it actually was, is the lost work
\[
w_{\text{lost}} = T_0 \, s_{\text{gen}},
\]
the Gouy-Stodola relation, with $T_0$ the dead-state temperature. Every
irreversible step in a real cycle, the finite-temperature heat transfer
in a boiler, the throttle in a refrigerator, the non-isentropic
expansion in a turbine, spreads the cycle's path out in entropy and so
opens an area of lost work that a $T$-$s$ plot displays directly. The
practical reading: to find where a cycle is bleeding work, look for the
processes that move it horizontally on the $T$-$s$ plane at a temperature
far from where the heat is wanted, and attack those, because their area
is the work the second law says was thrown away.
\end{mastery}

\begin{archetype}[Optimisation under second-law constraint]
A cycle's design problem is to maximise the work output per unit heat
input, subject to the constraint that the entropy generated per cycle
be non-negative. The Carnot cycle saturates the constraint at zero;
real cycles generate entropy through finite-temperature heat
transfer, throttling, friction, and combustion at finite affinity.
The engineering art is to identify which irreversibility costs the
most, redesign the component that produces it, and reapproach the
Carnot bound. Across the chapter, the optimisation archetype recurs:
each cycle of sections 3.2 to 3.6 is a different geometric
compromise between Carnot ideality and practical heat-exchanger,
turbine, and compressor design.
\end{archetype}

\begin{mastery}
The Carnot efficiency is the bound for a reversible engine, which
requires infinitely slow operation and so produces zero power. An
engine that produces useful power must transfer heat across a finite
temperature difference, and a more useful bound asks for the efficiency
at maximum power output rather than the efficiency at zero power. For an
engine with finite-conductance heat exchangers on both reservoirs, the
efficiency at maximum power is the Curzon-Ahlborn value
\[
\eta_{\text{CA}} = 1 - \sqrt{\frac{T_c}{T_h}},
\]
which lies below the Carnot value $1 - T_c/T_h$. For a $560\,\degC$
boiler and a $30\,\degC$ condenser, the Carnot bound is $64\,\%$ while
the Curzon-Ahlborn value is $1 - \sqrt{303/833} = 40\,\%$, close to the
efficiency that real plants achieve. The agreement is partly
coincidental (real plants are not optimised for maximum power, and their
losses are not the single finite-conductance loss the model assumes),
but the lesson holds: the reversible bound overstates what a
power-producing machine can reach, and a finite-rate bound is the right
yardstick for a machine that must deliver power, not just work.
\end{mastery}

\section{Rankine cycle}\pathtag{core}

The \engterm{Rankine cycle} is the working power-plant cycle: it
generates more than half of the world's electricity through coal,
natural-gas combined cycles (where the Rankine cycle is the bottom
half), nuclear, geothermal, and concentrated-solar plants. The
working fluid is water; the cycle has four components and four
states.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Pump (1 to 2): liquid water is pressurised from condenser
    pressure $p_c$ to boiler pressure $p_b$. Specific work input
    $w_p = v_f(p_b - p_c)$, small compared to the turbine work.
  \item Boiler (2 to 3): the high-pressure water is heated to
    saturated or superheated vapour at $p_b$. Specific heat input
    $q_h = h_3 - h_2$.
  \item Turbine (3 to 4): the vapour expands to condenser pressure
    $p_c$, delivering work $w_t = h_3 - h_4$. The expansion is
    nearly isentropic in a well-designed turbine.
  \item Condenser (4 to 1): the low-pressure two-phase mixture
    condenses to saturated liquid at $p_c$, rejecting heat $q_c =
    h_4 - h_1$.
\end{enumerate}

The thermal efficiency is
\[
\eta_R = \frac{w_t - w_p}{q_h} = 1 - \frac{q_c}{q_h}.
\]

\input{volumes/04-energy/ch03-heat-engines-refrigerators/figures/fig-rankine-ts.tex}

On a $T$-$s$ diagram (figure \ref{fig:vol04ch03:rankine-ts}), the cycle
is bounded by two isobars (the boiler
and condenser pressures) and the turbine and pump processes. The
boiler isobar dips into the two-phase region and rises into the
superheated regime; the condenser isobar lies in the two-phase region
at low temperature. The cycle's efficiency depends strongly on the
boiler and condenser conditions:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Raising the boiler pressure raises the average temperature
    of heat addition and increases efficiency. Modern supercritical
    plants operate at boiler pressures of $25$ to $30\,\text{MPa}$.
  \item Raising the boiler temperature (superheat, reheat) raises the
    average temperature of heat addition further and increases
    efficiency.
  \item Lowering the condenser pressure (and hence the condenser
    saturation temperature) lowers the average temperature of heat
    rejection and increases efficiency. Practical plants run with
    condensers at $4$ to $10\,\text{kPa}$, with the saturation
    temperature limited by the cooling-water supply.
\end{itemize}

\engterm{Reheat Rankine cycle}. After partial expansion through the
high-pressure turbine, the steam returns to the boiler for reheating
back to a high temperature, then completes its expansion in the low-
pressure turbine. Reheat raises the average heat-addition temperature
and (more importantly) raises the steam quality at the low-pressure
turbine exit, limiting blade erosion. Most large power plants use
single or double reheat.

\engterm{Regenerative Rankine cycle}. Some steam is bled from the
turbine at intermediate pressures and used to heat the feedwater
before the boiler. The bled-steam regenerative feedwater heating
raises the average temperature of heat addition and reduces the
heat that must be supplied at the boiler from the high-temperature
flame. Practical large plants use six to ten regenerative
feedwater heaters.

Modern coal-fired Rankine plants achieve net thermal efficiencies of
$40$ to $45\,\%$; ultra-supercritical plants reach $47\,\%$.
Nuclear Rankine plants are limited to lower turbine inlet
temperatures (typically $280\,\degC$ saturated steam) for fuel-cladding
reasons and achieve efficiencies near $33\,\%$. The Carnot bound
between a $300\,\degC$ boiler and a $30\,\degC$ condenser is
$1 - 303/573 = 47\,\%$; the Carnot bound between a $560\,\degC$
boiler and a $30\,\degC$ condenser is $1 - 303/833 = 64\,\%$. Working
Rankine plants reach the Carnot bound to within a factor of $0.6$ to
$0.7$ \cite{text:moran-shapiro-thermo}.

\section{Brayton cycle}\pathtag{core}

The \engterm{Brayton cycle} is the working cycle of jet engines and
land-based gas turbines. The working fluid is air (drawn from
atmosphere, mixed with fuel and combusted, and exhausted back to
atmosphere) or, in a closed Brayton cycle, helium or carbon dioxide.
The four processes:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Compressor (1 to 2): air is compressed adiabatically from
    atmospheric to combustor inlet pressure. Pressure ratio $r_p =
    p_2/p_1$.
  \item Combustor (2 to 3): fuel is burned in the compressed air at
    approximately constant pressure, raising the temperature to the
    turbine inlet temperature $T_3$.
  \item Turbine (3 to 4): the high-pressure hot gas expands
    adiabatically back to atmospheric pressure, delivering work.
  \item Exhaust (4 to 1): the gas is rejected to atmosphere (open
    cycle) or cooled in a heat exchanger and returned to the
    compressor (closed cycle).
\end{enumerate}

The ideal-gas air-standard Brayton cycle's thermal efficiency depends
only on the pressure ratio:
\[
\eta_B = 1 - \frac{1}{r_p^{(\gamma-1)/\gamma}}.
\]
For air with $\gamma = 1.4$, the efficiency rises from $37\,\%$ at
$r_p = 5$ to $54\,\%$ at $r_p = 15$ to $62\,\%$ at $r_p = 30$ to $67\,\%$
at $r_p = 50$. Modern aero-derivative gas turbines run at pressure
ratios above $40$; large industrial gas turbines run at $15$ to $20$.
The companion \texttt{cycle\_efficiencies.py} reproduces these figures and
the Otto and Diesel curves, and \texttt{brayton\_cycle.py} returns the four
state temperatures and the back-work ratio for any inlet condition.

\input{volumes/04-energy/ch03-heat-engines-refrigerators/figures/fig-brayton-pv-ts.tex}

\input{volumes/04-energy/ch03-heat-engines-refrigerators/figures/fig-efficiency-vs-ratio.tex}

Figure \ref{fig:vol04ch03:brayton-pv-ts} traces the cycle on both the
$p$-$v$ and $T$-$s$ planes, and figure \ref{fig:vol04ch03:efficiency-vs-ratio}
plots the Otto and Brayton efficiency curves against ratio so the
diminishing return on each added unit of ratio is visible at a glance.

The efficiency formula understates the practical bound. The Brayton
cycle is limited by the maximum allowable turbine inlet temperature,
set by the metallurgy of the turbine blades and the effectiveness of
blade cooling. Modern aero-engines run with $T_3$ above $1700\,
\si{\kelvin}$, well above the un-cooled blade material's melting
point; the blades survive through internal air-channel cooling, film
cooling, and ceramic thermal-barrier coatings. The cycle's actual
efficiency depends on $r_p$ and $T_3$ together; for each $T_3$ there
is an optimum $r_p$ that maximises specific work, and a higher $r_p$
that maximises thermal efficiency.

The split between those two optima is the recurring tension of
gas-turbine design. Specific work, the net work per kilogram of air, sets
how large and heavy a machine must be to make a given power, and it peaks
at a moderate pressure ratio because at low $r_p$ the cycle does little
work while at high $r_p$ the compressor consumes most of what the turbine
makes. Thermal efficiency, by the air-standard formula, climbs
monotonically with $r_p$, so it wants a higher ratio than specific work
does. An aero-engine, where weight is the master constraint, leans toward
the specific-work optimum and accepts a lower efficiency; a stationary
power turbine, where fuel cost dominates over a long life, leans toward
the efficiency optimum and accepts a heavier machine. Raising $T_3$ shifts
both optima to higher $r_p$ and raises the specific work at every ratio,
which is why the relentless climb in turbine inlet temperature, sustained
by blade cooling and thermal-barrier coatings, has driven gas-turbine
performance for decades. Each $100\,\si{\kelvin}$ of additional inlet
temperature buys both more work per kilogram and headroom to raise the
pressure ratio, and the metallurgy of the first turbine stage is the
binding constraint on how far the trade can be pushed.

\engterm{Regenerative Brayton cycle}. Exhaust heat from the turbine
exit (still at several hundred Celsius) is recovered to preheat the
combustor inlet air. The regenerator (a counter-flow heat exchanger)
recovers some of the rejected heat and reduces the heat required in
the combustor. Ground-based gas turbines for cogeneration or
combined-cycle use frequently include regeneration; aero-engines do
not, because the regenerator's weight is prohibitive.

\engterm{Intercooled and reheated Brayton}. Compression in two stages
with cooling between stages reduces compressor work; expansion in
two stages with reheating between stages increases turbine work. The
combined intercooled-regenerative-reheat Brayton cycle approaches a
Carnot cycle in the limit of many intercooler and reheat stages.
Practical implementations use one or two of each.

The Brayton cycle's advantage over Rankine is its compactness and
high specific power: a Brayton turbine can produce $10\,\text{MW}/
\text{tonne}$, against $\sim 1\,\text{MW}/\text{tonne}$ for Rankine
machinery. Its disadvantage is its lower efficiency at single-cycle
operation; the combined cycle (Brayton on top, Rankine on bottom)
solves this and is the modern standard for large new power
generation.

\section{Otto, Diesel, Atkinson cycles}\pathtag{standard}

The reciprocating internal combustion engines of cars, trucks, ships,
and stationary generators use variants of three cycles, all derived
from the same piston-cylinder geometry but differing in how fuel is
introduced and combusted.

\engterm{The Otto cycle} models the spark-ignition gasoline engine.
The four strokes (intake, compression, power, exhaust) map onto a
four-process cycle with the working fluid treated as air:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Isentropic compression (1 to 2) from bottom dead centre to
    top dead centre, compression ratio $r = V_1/V_2$.
  \item Constant-volume heat addition (2 to 3): combustion at top
    dead centre, treated as instantaneous, raising the gas
    temperature to $T_3$.
  \item Isentropic expansion (3 to 4) back to bottom dead centre.
  \item Constant-volume heat rejection (4 to 1): exhaust and intake,
    treated as instantaneous, returning the gas to its initial
    temperature.
\end{enumerate}

\input{volumes/04-energy/ch03-heat-engines-refrigerators/figures/fig-otto-diesel-pv.tex}

The Otto cycle's air-standard thermal efficiency is
\[
\eta_O = 1 - \frac{1}{r^{\gamma-1}},
\]
depending only on the compression ratio. For $\gamma = 1.4$ and $r =
8$ to $10$ (typical for modern gasoline engines), $\eta_O$ ranges
from $56\,\%$ to $60\,\%$. The real engine's brake thermal efficiency
is roughly $25\,\%$ to $35\,\%$ at the design operating point, with
the gap to the Otto ideal due to: combustion at non-constant volume,
heat loss to cylinder walls during combustion and expansion, friction
in the engine, pumping losses, incomplete combustion, finite valve-
opening dynamics, and exhaust gas residual.

The Otto compression ratio is limited by the fuel's auto-ignition
behaviour. A gasoline-air mixture compressed to too high a ratio will
self-ignite during the compression stroke, producing knock, which
damages the engine. The fuel's octane number measures its resistance
to auto-ignition; higher-octane fuels permit higher compression
ratios and higher efficiency. Variable-compression-ratio engines
adjust the geometry in response to load to operate at the highest
allowable compression at each load.

\engterm{The Diesel cycle} models the compression-ignition engine
(figure \ref{fig:vol04ch03:otto-diesel-pv}b, against the Otto cycle in
panel a). The differences from the Otto cycle:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Compression is of air alone, not air-fuel mixture; the
    compression ratio is therefore not limited by fuel auto-ignition.
    Typical compression ratios are $14$ to $22$ for Diesel engines,
    against $8$ to $10$ for gasoline.
  \item Fuel is injected at top dead centre and burns as it is
    injected; the combustion is modelled as constant-pressure
    (process 2 to 3) rather than constant-volume.
\end{itemize}

The Diesel cycle's air-standard thermal efficiency is
\[
\eta_D = 1 - \frac{1}{r^{\gamma-1}} \cdot \frac{r_c^\gamma - 1}{
\gamma(r_c - 1)},
\]
where $r_c = V_3/V_2$ is the cut-off ratio (the volume ratio at the
end of combustion to that at top dead centre). For comparable
compression ratios, the Diesel efficiency is slightly less than the
Otto, because the constant-pressure heat addition occurs at a lower
average temperature than constant-volume. The Diesel advantage comes
from the higher compression ratio it can tolerate. Working Diesel
engines achieve brake thermal efficiencies of $40\,\%$ to $50\,\%$
(for marine engines and the most efficient automotive Diesels) and
in many applications outperform comparable gasoline engines.

\engterm{The Atkinson cycle} differs from Otto by separating the
expansion ratio from the compression ratio: the effective compression
is reduced (by late-closing or early-closing the intake valve), while
the geometric expansion stroke is the full piston travel. The
expansion ratio exceeds the effective compression ratio, recovering
more work from the combustion gas before exhaust. The thermal
efficiency rises (typically $5\,\%$ to $10\,\%$ relative to Otto) at
the cost of reduced specific power. The Atkinson cycle is the working
cycle of most hybrid-electric vehicles (Toyota Prius, Honda Insight,
many others), where the electric drive compensates for the reduced
specific power.

The mechanism is worth stating plainly, because it shows where the extra
work comes from. In an Otto cycle the gas at the end of expansion is still
well above atmospheric pressure when the exhaust valve opens; the energy
in that residual pressure is dumped to the exhaust, having done no work.
The Atkinson arrangement lets the gas expand further, against the longer
expansion stroke, so it leaves the cylinder closer to atmospheric
pressure and surrenders less unused pressure energy. The price is that
the same long stroke is not filled on the intake, so each cycle processes
less air and makes less power for a given engine size. The
\engterm{Miller cycle} reaches the same expansion-greater-than-compression
geometry through valve timing combined with forced induction, so that a
supercharger or turbocharger restores the lost charge mass; it is the
Atkinson idea adapted to engines that cannot afford the specific-power
penalty. Both cycles trade peak power for efficiency, and both make sense
exactly where a second power source, an electric motor or a boost system,
covers the demand peaks the downsized air path can no longer meet.

\section{Worked examples across the cycles}\pathtag{standard}

Three worked examples fix the cycle arithmetic that the exercises will
exercise at scale. Each carries its numbers to the point where the
answer is interpretable; the table values follow the standard
thermodynamics references \cite{text:cengel-boles,
text:moran-shapiro-thermo}.

\paragraph{Otto cycle, compression ratio nine.} Take $r = 9$, inlet
$T_1 = 300\,\si{\kelvin}$ and $p_1 = 100\,\text{kPa}$, and a heat input
$q_{\text{in}} = 2000\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}$, with cold-air-standard
constants $\gamma = 1.4$, $c_v = 0.718\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}\cdot\text{K}$.
The isentropic compression gives $T_2 = T_1 r^{\gamma-1} =
300 \times 9^{0.4} = 722\,\si{\kelvin}$ and $p_2 = p_1 r^\gamma =
100 \times 9^{1.4} = 2167\,\text{kPa}$. Constant-volume combustion adds
$q_{\text{in}}/c_v = 2000/0.718 = 2786\,\si{\kelvin}$, so $T_3 = 722 +
2786 = 3508\,\si{\kelvin}$ and $p_3 = p_2 T_3/T_2 = 10{,}500\,\text{kPa}$.
The efficiency is $\eta_O = 1 - 9^{-0.4} = 0.585$, so the net work is
$0.585 \times 2000 = 1170\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}$. The peak temperature
$3508\,\si{\kelvin}$ is far above any real flame temperature, which is
the first sign that the air-standard model overstates the real cycle: a
real charge dissociates and loses heat to the walls long before reaching
$3500\,\si{\kelvin}$. The solver \texttt{otto\_cycle.py} reproduces every
number here, including the mean effective pressure of $1528\,\text{kPa}$.

\paragraph{Brayton cycle, pressure ratio twelve.} Take $r_p = 12$,
$T_1 = 300\,\si{\kelvin}$, $T_3 = 1400\,\si{\kelvin}$, $\gamma = 1.4$,
$c_p = 1.005\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}\cdot\text{K}$. The isentropic
exponent is $(\gamma-1)/\gamma = 0.286$, so $T_2 = 300 \times 12^{0.286}
= 610\,\si{\kelvin}$ and $T_4 = 1400 \times 12^{-0.286} = 688\,\si{
\kelvin}$. The turbine work is $c_p(T_3 - T_4) = 1.005 \times 712 =
715\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}$; the compressor work is $c_p(T_2 - T_1) =
1.005 \times 310 = 312\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}$; the net work is
$715 - 312 = 404\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}$. The heat input is $c_p(T_3 -
T_2) = 1.005 \times 790 = 794\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}$, so $\eta_B =
404/794 = 0.508$, matching $1 - 12^{-0.286}$. The back-work ratio
$w_c/w_t = 312/715 = 0.44$ is the Brayton cycle's defining burden:
nearly half the turbine output is consumed driving the compressor,
which is why a small drop in compressor or turbine efficiency erases a
large fraction of the net work.

\paragraph{Vapour-compression refrigerator, R-134a.} Take an
evaporator at $-10\,\degC$ and a condenser at $40\,\degC$, with an ideal
(isentropic) compressor, saturated vapour at the evaporator exit, and
saturated liquid at the condenser exit. From the saturation table in
\texttt{r134a\_saturation.csv}, the evaporator-exit vapour has $h_1 =
392.4\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}$ and the condenser-exit liquid has $h_3 =
256.4\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}$. Following the constant-entropy line from
state 1 up to the condenser pressure gives $h_2 \approx
425.6\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}$. The throttle is isenthalpic, so $h_4 = h_3
= 256.4\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}$. The cooling effect is $q_c = h_1 - h_4 =
136\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}$; the compressor work is $w_c = h_2 - h_1 =
33.2\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}$; the cooling COP is $136/33.2 = 4.1$. The
Carnot COP between $263\,\si{\kelvin}$ and $313\,\si{\kelvin}$ is
$263/50 = 5.3$, so the ideal cycle reaches $77\,\%$ of Carnot, and the
throttling loss (an irreversible pressure drop replacing a reversible
expander) accounts for most of the gap.

\paragraph{Reheat Rankine, where the second expansion pays off.} Take a
boiler at $15\,\text{MPa}$ and $550\,\degC$, a reheat back to $550\,\degC$
at an intermediate pressure of $2\,\text{MPa}$, and a condenser at
$10\,\text{kPa}$, with isentropic turbine and pump (figure
\ref{fig:vol04ch03:reheat-rankine-ts} traces the path). From the
superheated tables, the high-pressure turbine inlet has $h_3 = 3448\,
\text{kJ}/\text{kg}$ and $s_3 = 6.52\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}\cdot\text{K}$.
Expanding isentropically to $2\,\text{MPa}$ lands in the superheated
region at $h_4 = 2902\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}$, so the high-pressure stage
delivers $h_3 - h_4 = 546\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}$. Reheating at
$2\,\text{MPa}$ back to $550\,\degC$ gives $h_5 = 3578\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}$
and $s_5 = 7.57\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}\cdot\text{K}$. The low-pressure
expansion to $10\,\text{kPa}$ reaches a quality $x_6 = (7.57 - 0.649)/
7.50 = 0.923$ and $h_6 = 191.8 + 0.923 \times 2392.8 = 2401\,\text{kJ}/
\text{kg}$, so the low-pressure stage delivers $h_5 - h_6 = 1177\,\text{kJ}
/\text{kg}$. The total turbine work is $546 + 1177 = 1723\,\text{kJ}/
\text{kg}$. The pump work, with $v_f = 0.00101$ and a $15\,\text{MPa}$
rise, is $w_p = 0.00101 \times (15000 - 10) = 15.1\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}$,
so $h_2 = 191.8 + 15.1 = 207\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}$. The reheat adds heat
twice: $q_h = (h_3 - h_2) + (h_5 - h_4) = (3448 - 207) + (3578 - 2902) =
3241 + 676 = 3917\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}$. The thermal efficiency is
$\eta = (1723 - 15.1)/3917 = 0.436$, about $44\,\%$. Two results carry the
lesson. The exit quality $0.923$ comfortably clears the wet-steam erosion
limit, where the single-expansion cycle of the second worked example sat
at $0.82$; that protection of the last turbine stages is the first reason
to reheat. And the efficiency rose past the simple cycle's $37\,\%$
because the second heat addition lifts the average temperature at which
heat enters, the second reason. The reheat pressure is itself an
optimisation: too high and the second expansion is short, too low and the
reheat adds heat at a temperature barely above the condenser, so plants
place it near a quarter of the boiler pressure, as here.

\input{volumes/04-energy/ch03-heat-engines-refrigerators/figures/fig-reheat-rankine-ts.tex}

\paragraph{Brayton with real component efficiencies.} Return to the
$r_p = 12$, $T_3 = 1400\,\si{\kelvin}$ Brayton case, but now let the
compressor and turbine fall short of isentropic, $\eta_c = 0.84$ and
$\eta_t = 0.88$, the values a working gas turbine reaches. The isentropic
endpoints are unchanged: $T_{2s} = 610\,\si{\kelvin}$ and $T_{4s} = 688\,
\si{\kelvin}$. The real compressor needs more work, so its exit is hotter,
$T_2 = T_1 + (T_{2s} - T_1)/\eta_c = 300 + 310/0.84 = 669\,\si{\kelvin}$,
and the real turbine delivers less, so its exit is hotter too, $T_4 = T_3
- \eta_t(T_3 - T_{4s}) = 1400 - 0.88 \times 712 = 773\,\si{\kelvin}$. The
turbine work falls to $c_p(T_3 - T_4) = 1.005 \times 627 = 630\,\text{kJ}
/\text{kg}$ and the compressor work rises to $c_p(T_2 - T_1) = 1.005
\times 369 = 371\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}$, leaving a net work of only
$259\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}$, down from the ideal $404$. The heat input
drops a little because the compressor delivers air already hotter,
$q_h = c_p(T_3 - T_2) = 1.005 \times 731 = 735\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}$, so
$\eta = 259/735 = 0.352$. Two component efficiencies near $0.85$ pulled
the cycle efficiency from $51\,\%$ to $35\,\%$ and the net work down by a
third. The mechanism is the back-work ratio: with nearly half the turbine
output already committed to the compressor, a small fractional loss in
each machine bites twice, once as turbine output forgone and once as
compressor input demanded. This sensitivity is why gas-turbine makers
spend so heavily on compressor and turbine aerodynamics, and why the
back-work ratio, not the pressure ratio alone, is the number that governs
how forgiving the cycle is.

\paragraph{Two-stage refrigeration with intercooling.} A single-stage
vapour-compression cycle pulling a low evaporator temperature against a
warm condenser pays twice: the compressor works across a large pressure
ratio at low efficiency, and its discharge runs hot enough to stress the
oil and the valves. Splitting the compression into two stages with a
flash intercooler between them addresses both. Take R-134a with an
evaporator at $-30\,\degC$ and a condenser at $40\,\degC$, an intermediate
pressure chosen near the geometric mean of the evaporator and condenser
pressures (the rule that equalises the two stages' pressure ratios). The
evaporator pressure is $0.085\,\text{MPa}$ and the condenser pressure is
$1.017\,\text{MPa}$, so the geometric-mean intermediate is $\sqrt{0.085
\times 1.017} = 0.294\,\text{MPa}$, a saturation temperature near
$0\,\degC$. The low-stage compressor lifts vapour from $-30\,\degC$ to
$0\,\degC$; the flash chamber at $0\,\degC$ separates the liquid from the
flash vapour, the liquid feeds the evaporator through a second throttle,
and the flash vapour mixes with the low-stage discharge to de-superheat
it before the high-stage compressor lifts the combined flow to the
condenser. The mixing drops the high-stage inlet temperature, so the
high-stage discharge is cooler than a single stage's would be, and each
stage works across a pressure ratio of about $3.5$ rather than the single
stage's $12$. Carrying the enthalpies through the two-stage flow gives a
COP near $2.4$ against the single stage's $1.9$ for the same duty, a gain
of roughly a quarter, with the larger benefit the cooler discharge that
lets the machine run the low evaporator temperature at all. The
arithmetic rewards a careful mass balance at the flash chamber, since the
two stages carry different mass flows; that balance is the content of one
of the chapter's exercises.

\section{An end-to-end case study: a $400\,\text{MW}$ combined-cycle plant}\pathtag{standard}

The chapter's separate cycles meet in one machine when an engineer must
size and rate a combined-cycle power plant. We carry a single fuel input
through a Brayton topping cycle, a heat-recovery steam generator, and a
Rankine bottoming cycle, to a net plant efficiency and an electrical
rating, then read the result against the second law. Every section of the
chapter appears once: the Brayton pressure ratio and turbine inlet
temperature, the back-work ratio, the heat-recovery temperature window,
the Rankine boiler and condenser conditions, and the combined-cycle
efficiency formula of section 3.6. The plant is the one whose energy flow
figure \ref{fig:vol04ch03:combined-cycle-flow} sketched; here we put the
numbers on it.

\subsection{The topping cycle}

The gas turbine draws air at $T_1 = 288\,\si{\kelvin}$ ($15\,\degC$, the
standard rating condition) and $101\,\text{kPa}$, compresses it at a
pressure ratio $r_p = 18$ through a compressor of isentropic efficiency
$\eta_c = 0.86$, burns natural gas to a turbine inlet temperature $T_3 =
1600\,\si{\kelvin}$, and expands through a turbine of isentropic
efficiency $\eta_t = 0.89$ back to atmospheric pressure. With $\gamma =
1.4$ and exponent $0.286$, the isentropic compressor exit is $T_{2s} =
288 \times 18^{0.286} = 657\,\si{\kelvin}$, so the real exit is $T_2 =
288 + (657 - 288)/0.86 = 717\,\si{\kelvin}$. The isentropic turbine exit
is $T_{4s} = 1600 \times 18^{-0.286} = 701\,\si{\kelvin}$, so the real
exit is $T_4 = 1600 - 0.89 \times (1600 - 701) = 800\,\si{\kelvin}$, that
is $527\,\degC$, the temperature that sets the heat available to the
bottoming cycle.

Per kilogram of air, with $c_p = 1.005\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}\cdot\text{K}$:
the turbine work is $c_p(T_3 - T_4) = 1.005 \times 800 = 804\,\text{kJ}/
\text{kg}$, the compressor work is $c_p(T_2 - T_1) = 1.005 \times 429 =
431\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}$, and the net gas-turbine work is $373\,\text{kJ}
/\text{kg}$. The back-work ratio is $431/804 = 0.54$, on the high side
because the pressure ratio is high; over half the turbine output runs the
compressor. The heat added in the combustor is $c_p(T_3 - T_2) = 1.005
\times 883 = 887\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}$, so the gas-turbine thermal
efficiency standing alone is $\eta_{\text{GT}} = 373/887 = 0.421$, about
$42\,\%$. The exhaust carries the balance, $887 - 373 = 514\,\text{kJ}/
\text{kg}$, away at $527\,\degC$, and that hot stream is the resource the
rest of the plant exists to harvest.

\subsection{The heat-recovery steam generator}

The exhaust at $800\,\si{\kelvin}$ cannot be cooled all the way to ambient
in the steam generator: the stack must stay above the water dew point to
avoid acid condensation, and the steam it raises cannot be hotter than
the gas that heats it. We cool the exhaust to a stack temperature of
$390\,\si{\kelvin}$ ($117\,\degC$), recovering $c_p(800 - 390) = 1.005
\times 410 = 412\,\text{kJ}$ per kilogram of air, which is $412/514 =
0.80$ of the exhaust heat above ambient. That recovered $412\,\text{kJ}/
\text{kg-air}$ is the heat input to the Rankine cycle. The temperature
window the generator works across, from the $527\,\degC$ gas inlet down
to the $117\,\degC$ stack, is the same window figure
\ref{fig:vol04ch03:combined-cycle-ts} marks between its dashed lines; the
steam the generator raises must thread that window, which fixes the steam
pressure and the achievable superheat.

\input{volumes/04-energy/ch03-heat-engines-refrigerators/figures/fig-combined-cycle-ts.tex}

\subsection{The bottoming cycle}

The steam cycle takes that heat at a boiler pressure of $10\,\text{MPa}$,
superheated to $500\,\degC$ (held below the $527\,\degC$ gas inlet by the
pinch the heat exchanger needs), and rejects to a condenser at
$8\,\text{kPa}$, the saturation pressure for the available cooling water.
From the worked Rankine arithmetic of this chapter, a $10\,\text{MPa}/
500\,\degC$ boiler against an $8\,\text{kPa}$ condenser, with a turbine
isentropic efficiency of $0.88$, gives a steam-cycle thermal efficiency
near $\eta_{\text{ST}} = 0.36$. The bottoming cycle therefore converts
$0.36 \times 412 = 148\,\text{kJ}$ of the recovered heat to work, per
kilogram of the topping cycle's air.

\subsection{The plant rating}

The two cycles together deliver $373 + 148 = 521\,\text{kJ}$ of work per
kilogram of air, against the $887\,\text{kJ}$ of fuel heat the combustor
supplied. The combined-cycle thermal efficiency is
\[
\eta_{cc} = \frac{521}{887} = 0.587,
\]
about $59\,\%$, which the section 3.6 formula reproduces:
$\eta_{cc} = \eta_{\text{GT}} + \eta_{\text{HRSG}}(1 - \eta_{\text{GT}})
\eta_{\text{ST}} = 0.421 + 0.80 \times 0.579 \times 0.36 = 0.421 + 0.167
= 0.588$. The gas turbine alone reached $42\,\%$; reusing its exhaust
lifted the plant to $59\,\%$, the difference being the $148\,\text{kJ}$
the bottoming cycle squeezed from heat the single cycle threw away.

To reach a $400\,\text{MW}$ net electrical rating, allow a generator and
mechanical efficiency of $0.985$ on each shaft and a plant auxiliary load
(pumps, fans, controls) of about $2\,\%$ of gross. The net specific work
is then roughly $0.965 \times 521 = 503\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg-air}$, so the
air mass flow is
\[
\dot m_{\text{air}} = \frac{400 \times 10^3\,\si{\kilo\watt}}
{503\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}} = 795\,\text{kg}/\text{s},
\]
a flow a single large heavy-duty gas turbine plus its steam train can
supply. The fuel power follows from the efficiency: $\dot Q_{\text{fuel}}
= 400/0.587 \times (503/521) = 658\,\text{MW}$ of natural gas, about
$13\,\text{kg}/\text{s}$ at a heating value of $50\,\text{MJ}/\text{kg}$.
A reader checking these against a published rating plate will find them in
the right neighbourhood; the published machines reach a point or two
higher through multi-pressure heat recovery and reheat in the steam
train, refinements the next subsection reads off the second law.

\subsection{Where the work potential went}

\input{volumes/04-energy/ch03-heat-engines-refrigerators/figures/fig-exergy-destruction.tex}

The first law says $41\,\%$ of the fuel energy left unconverted, split
between the stack and the condenser. That accounting is silent on which
loss was avoidable. The exergy accounting of figure
\ref{fig:vol04ch03:exergy-destruction} answers the question the plant
manager actually has. Of the fuel's work potential, the combustion step
destroys the largest single share, near $28\,\%$, by releasing chemical
energy as heat across an enormous temperature gap between the flame and
the working gas; no turbine in the plant ever sees that lost potential.
The compressor and turbine irreversibilities, the ones the component
efficiencies $\eta_c$ and $\eta_t$ measured, account for a few percent
each, and the steam train a few more. The stack and the condenser, which
carry most of the first-law energy loss, carry little exergy, because
they reject heat at low temperature where each joule holds little work
value. The design moral is that the visibly hot stack is not where the
plant loses its capacity to do work; the combustor and the heat-addition
path are. That is the reasoning behind every refinement that raises the
turbine inlet temperature or recovers exhaust heat at multiple pressures:
each one narrows a temperature gap across which work potential was being
spent for nothing.

\section{Refrigeration cycles}\pathtag{standard}

A refrigerator moves heat from a cold reservoir to a hot one. The
working cycle is the inverse of a heat-engine cycle; the working
configurations are vapour-compression (the dominant industrial and
domestic refrigerator), absorption (used where waste heat is
available cheaper than electricity), and gas-cycle (used in
specialised applications including aerospace and cryogenics).

\engterm{Vapour-compression cycle}. The cycle consists of four
processes (matching the worked example in chapter~2):

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Compressor (1 to 2): the refrigerant vapour at evaporator
    pressure is compressed to condenser pressure. Specific work
    input $w_c = h_2 - h_1$.
  \item Condenser (2 to 3): the high-pressure vapour rejects heat to
    the high-temperature reservoir and condenses to saturated liquid.
    Specific heat rejected $q_h = h_2 - h_3$.
  \item Expansion valve (3 to 4): the saturated liquid is throttled
    to evaporator pressure, partly flashing to vapour. The throttle
    is isenthalpic: $h_4 = h_3$.
  \item Evaporator (4 to 1): the two-phase mixture absorbs heat from
    the low-temperature reservoir and boils to saturated vapour.
    Specific heat absorbed $q_c = h_1 - h_4$.
\end{enumerate}

The COP for cooling is $\text{COP}_R = q_c/w_c$; for heating (heat
pump), $\text{COP}_{HP} = q_h/w_c$.

\input{volumes/04-energy/ch03-heat-engines-refrigerators/figures/fig-vapor-compression-schematic.tex}

\input{volumes/04-energy/ch03-heat-engines-refrigerators/figures/fig-vapor-compression-ph.tex}

Figure \ref{fig:vol04ch03:vapor-compression-schematic} shows the four
components as a physical loop, and figure
\ref{fig:vol04ch03:vapor-compression-ph} plots the cycle on the
pressure-enthalpy plane, where the throttle reads as a vertical line and
the evaporator and condenser read as horizontal segments. The companion
\texttt{vapor\_compression.py} takes the four state enthalpies from the
\texttt{r134a\_saturation.csv} table and returns the cooling and heating
COPs.

The choice of refrigerant is set by the temperature range and the
environmental and safety constraints. Historical refrigerants
include ammonia (NH$_3$, high COP, toxic, used in industrial
refrigeration), CFCs and HCFCs (R-12, R-22, banned due to ozone
depletion), HFCs (R-134a, R-410A, high global-warming potential,
being phased out in many jurisdictions), HFOs (R-1234yf, low GWP,
the current automotive air-conditioning standard), natural
refrigerants (CO$_2$, isobutane, propane, ammonia, water for very-
high-temperature applications). The Kigali Amendment
\cite{law:kigali-amendment-2016} to the Montreal Protocol
\cite{law:montreal-protocol-1987} sets a global schedule for phasing
down HFCs over the coming decades. The \texttt{refrigerants.csv} table
collects the global-warming potential, ozone-depletion potential, and
ASHRAE safety class of the common working fluids, current as of 2024;
the World Health Organization performance specification for
vaccine-storage refrigerators \cite{web:who-pqs-refrigerators-2024}
illustrates how a single application fixes the refrigerant and the
operating envelope together.

\engterm{Absorption refrigeration} uses a thermal driving force in
place of mechanical compressor work. The basic ammonia-water
absorption cycle: ammonia vapour evaporates at low pressure (the
cooling effect), is absorbed into water at low temperature, the
ammonia-water solution is pumped to high pressure, the ammonia is
boiled out of the solution by heating, the ammonia vapour condenses
at high pressure (the heat rejection), and the cycle closes through
a throttle. The work input is the small pumping work plus the
thermal energy that drives the ammonia out of solution. Absorption
chillers driven by waste heat or solar heat are economical where the
heat is available at low cost; their COPs are lower than vapour-
compression but their primary-energy efficiency can be higher when
the heat input would otherwise be discarded.

\engterm{Gas-cycle refrigeration} (reversed Brayton, Stirling, Joule-
Thomson) operates with a permanent gas (helium, hydrogen, air) and
is the working choice for cryogenic applications below $-100\,\degC$
where conventional refrigerants are unavailable. Aerospace cabin air
conditioning, helium liquefaction (for superconducting magnets and
fusion experiments), and natural-gas liquefaction all use gas-cycle
machinery.

\engterm{The heat pump and its seasonal balance}. A heat pump is the
vapour-compression cycle run for the heat it rejects rather than the cold
it produces, and its design problem is governed by a fact the steady
COP hides: both the building's demand and the machine's ability to meet
it move with the outdoor temperature, and they move in opposite
directions. As it gets colder the building loses heat faster, so the
demand rises; at the same time the compressor must pump heat across a
wider temperature lift, so the delivered capacity and the COP both fall.
Figure \ref{fig:vol04ch03:heat-pump-balance} plots the two trends. They
cross at the \engterm{balance point}, the outdoor temperature below which
the heat pump can no longer carry the load alone and a supplemental
source, usually electric resistance heat at $\text{COP}=1$, must make up
the shortfall. Every hour spent below the balance point dilutes the
seasonal average performance with resistance heat, which is why the
single steady COP overstates what a real installation delivers over a
winter.

The number that captures the whole season is the \engterm{seasonal
coefficient of performance}, the total heat delivered over a heating
season divided by the total electricity drawn, supplemental heat
included. A unit with a steady COP of $4$ at mild conditions may post a
seasonal COP near $2.5$ once cold hours, defrost cycles, and resistance
backup are summed, the gap depending on the climate and on where the
balance point was set. Setting the balance point is the central sizing
decision: a larger heat pump pushes the balance point colder and leans
less on resistance heat, at higher capital cost and a penalty in mild
weather where it cycles on and off; a smaller unit is cheaper and runs
efficiently in the shoulder seasons but surrenders more cold hours to
expensive backup. Cold-climate heat pumps with variable-speed
compressors and improved low-temperature capacity push the balance point
toward $-15\,\degC$ or below, which is what makes the technology viable
in heating-dominated climates that resistance-backed single-speed units
could not serve economically. The field-log sample in
\texttt{heat\_pump\_field\_log.csv} records the capacity and COP fade with
outdoor temperature that the figure idealises.

\section{Combined cycles, cogeneration, trigeneration}\pathtag{mastery}

A \engterm{combined cycle} uses two cycles in series: the heat
rejected by the top cycle drives the bottom cycle. The standard
configuration in modern power generation is a Brayton cycle on top
(natural-gas-fired gas turbine) and a Rankine cycle on bottom (steam
turbine driven by a heat-recovery steam generator). The gas turbine's
exhaust at $\sim 600\,\degC$ contains enough thermal energy to
generate steam at $500\,\degC$ and $15\,\text{MPa}$ for the Rankine
bottoming cycle. The combined-cycle efficiency, which is the sum of
the two cycles' efficiencies minus their product (in a careful
formulation that accounts for the recovered heat), reaches $60\,\%$
to $64\,\%$ in modern installations
(figure \ref{fig:vol04ch03:combined-cycle-flow}). Combined-cycle plants
are the most efficient large fossil-fuel power producers.

\input{volumes/04-energy/ch03-heat-engines-refrigerators/figures/fig-combined-cycle-flow.tex}

\engterm{Cogeneration} (also called combined heat and power, CHP)
recovers useful heat from the rejection of a power cycle. A gas
turbine driving an electric generator also produces steam from its
exhaust; the steam is used for district heating, industrial process
heat, or absorption cooling. The combined first-law efficiency of a
well-engineered CHP plant exceeds $80\,\%$ (versus $33\,\%$ to $60\,\%$
for the same gas turbine running electricity-only). The second-law
analysis is more subtle: the recovered heat is delivered at a lower
temperature than the original combustion, so its exergy content is
proportionally smaller. CHP is the right choice where the heat load
matches the temperature and timing of the cycle's rejection;
electricity-only operation is the right choice where it does not.

\begin{mastery}
The first-law efficiency of cogeneration counts a joule of $90\,\degC$
hot water as equal to a joule of electricity, which a second-law
accounting refuses to do. The \engterm{exergy} of a heat flow $Q$
delivered at temperature $T$, relative to a dead-state environment at
$T_0$, is $Q(1 - T_0/T)$, the maximum work that flow could in principle
produce. A joule of electricity is pure exergy; a joule of $90\,\degC$
($363\,\si{\kelvin}$) heat against a $15\,\degC$ ($288\,\si{\kelvin}$)
environment carries only $1 - 288/363 = 0.21$ joule of exergy. A CHP
plant delivering $40\,\%$ of its fuel as electricity and $45\,\%$ as
$90\,\degC$ heat has a first-law efficiency of $85\,\%$ but a
second-law (exergy) efficiency of roughly $40\,\% + 0.21 \times 45\,\% =
50\,\%$. The first-law figure is the right one when the question is fuel
burned per unit of delivered energy; the exergy figure is the right one
when the question is how much of the fuel's work potential survived.
Quoting the $85\,\%$ against an electricity-only plant's $40\,\%$
compares unlike quantities and flatters the CHP plant.
\end{mastery}

\engterm{Trigeneration} adds refrigeration (typically through an
absorption chiller driven by part of the recovered heat) to the
cogeneration mix, supplying electricity, heating, and cooling from a
single fuel input. Large commercial buildings, hospitals,
universities, and data centres operate trigeneration plants where the
three loads coincide.

\engterm{District heating and cooling networks} extend cogeneration
and trigeneration to neighbourhood and city scale. A central plant
supplies hot water (or steam) and chilled water to a network of
consumer buildings through insulated pipes. The economic case
depends on the heat-load density, the climate, and the available fuel
prices; district heating dominates in Scandinavia, eastern Europe,
and parts of East Asia. Chapter~6 of this volume develops the heat-
exchanger and pumping discipline that the network's design depends
on.

\section{Failure: efficiency claims that violated the second law}\pathtag{core}

The history of energy engineering is filled with proposals, products,
and patents that claim cycle efficiencies exceeding the second-law
bound. The proposals are sometimes naive (an inventor who has not
internalised the Carnot bound), sometimes deliberate (a marketing
team selling a low-quality refrigerator with inflated COP claims),
and sometimes the output of a long measurement campaign whose
systematic errors have not been controlled. The chapter's failure
section catalogues the recurring patterns and the diagnostic that
detects each. The companion \texttt{efficiency\_claim\_check.py}
implements the two-step diagnosis for engine, heat-pump, and
combustion-appliance claims, and \texttt{cycle\_efficiency\_benchmarks.csv}
collects the realised efficiencies of the major cycle classes against
their Carnot bounds for the plausibility check of step two.

\subsection{The over-unity heat pump}

A vendor claims a heat pump with COP greater than the Carnot value
for its operating temperatures. The first diagnostic: compute the
Carnot COP for the claimed reservoir temperatures and confirm the
claimed COP exceeds it.

Working numbers: a domestic air-source heat pump operates at $T_h =
35\,\degC$ (condenser interior) and $T_c = -5\,\degC$ (outdoor coil
under typical winter conditions). The Carnot heating COP is $T_h/
(T_h - T_c) = 308/40 = 7.7$. A claimed COP of $4$ to $5$ is plausible
($52$ to $65\,\%$ of Carnot). A claimed COP of $8$ violates the
Carnot bound and is either a measurement error (most often
misidentified reservoir temperatures, or steady-state efficiency
confused with cyclic efficiency including defrost penalties), a
mistake in the COP definition (cooling COP for a heat pump quoted as
heating COP, or the inverse), or an unaccounted input (gas-fired
defrost system, electric supplemental heater).

The diagnostic for a heat-pump claim: identify $T_h$ and $T_c$ from
the actual operating boundary, compute the Carnot COP, and require
the claimed COP to lie below it.

\subsection{The 100\% efficient combustion device}

A vendor sells a heating appliance (a residential furnace, a
commercial boiler) and claims a first-law efficiency exceeding
$100\,\%$. The claim is often supported by the appliance's measured
output divided by the lower heating value (LHV) of the fuel; a
\engterm{condensing} appliance recovers the latent heat of the water
vapour in the combustion products and can deliver more heat than the
LHV figure indicates. The ratio of higher heating value (HHV) to LHV
is approximately $1.11$ for natural gas, so an apparent efficiency of
$108\,\%$ (LHV basis) is $97\,\%$ on an HHV basis and entirely
plausible.

The diagnostic: identify whether the efficiency is quoted on HHV or
LHV basis. An LHV-basis efficiency above the HHV/LHV ratio (a
condensing-furnace efficiency above $111\,\%$, a condensing-boiler
above $110\,\%$) violates the first law; below that ratio, the
appliance is condensing and the claim is consistent. Working
condensing furnaces achieve $90\,\%$ to $97\,\%$ HHV efficiency
($98\,\%$ to $106\,\%$ LHV).

\subsection{The water-fuelled car}

A class of proposals claims to drive an internal-combustion engine on
water by electrolysing the water in situ and burning the resulting
hydrogen-oxygen mixture. The first-law balance: the work required to
electrolyse water is the same as (or, accounting for irreversibilities,
greater than) the heat released by combusting the resulting gases. The
proposed device is a closed loop with no net energy input that
produces work; it is a first-kind perpetual motion machine.

The diagnostic: draw the boundary around the proposed device and ask
where the energy comes from. If the answer is the on-board battery,
the device is a low-efficiency battery-electric vehicle; the
electrolysis-and-recombustion loop is an energy-losing detour. If
the answer is "the water itself," the proposal violates the first
law.

The proposal recurs because hydrogen-oxygen combustion is genuinely
exothermic (relative to the gases), and the electrolysis is
genuinely energy-storing (relative to the gases). The trick is the
direction: combustion releases the energy that electrolysis put in.
A net round-trip cannot produce energy.

\subsection{The Carnot-violating refrigerator}

A consumer product is advertised with a cooling capacity and a power
input that imply a COP higher than the Carnot bound for its
operating temperatures. Working example: a small ``thermoelectric''
refrigerator advertised as cooling $5\,\text{L}$ to $20\,\degC$ below
ambient (so $T_c \approx 0\,\degC$, $T_h \approx 20\,\degC$) at
$3\,\text{W}$ of input. The Carnot COP is $273/20 = 13.6$, so a
$3\,\text{W}$ input could in principle provide $40\,\text{W}$ of
cooling; the advertised capacity might be $30\,\text{W}$. The
advertised numbers are internally consistent with the Carnot bound.
The actual product, of course, achieves perhaps $20\,\%$ of Carnot
(thermoelectrics have poor exergy efficiency), so the real cooling
capacity is closer to $8\,\text{W}$. The error in the marketing is
not a Carnot violation but an unrealistic figure-of-merit assumption.

The diagnostic: separate the Carnot-physics check from the realised-
efficiency check. A claim that violates Carnot is a second-law
violation; a claim that satisfies Carnot but overstates the realised
fraction is a marketing exaggeration or a measurement error.

\begin{principle}[Two-step diagnosis of an efficiency claim]
For any cyclic energy device, the efficiency or COP claim is checked
in two steps. Step one: identify the operating reservoir temperatures
and compute the Carnot bound (efficiency, $\text{COP}_R$, or
$\text{COP}_{HP}$ as appropriate). A claim above the bound violates
the second law; the diagnostic is the bound itself. Step two: if the
claim is below the Carnot bound, identify the realised second-law
efficiency $\eta_{II} = \eta_{\text{claimed}}/\eta_{\text{Carnot}}$
and compare to known performance for the device class. Working
Rankine plants achieve $\eta_{II} \approx 0.6$ to $0.7$; vapour-
compression refrigerators $\approx 0.5$ to $0.7$; internal-combustion
engines $\approx 0.4$ to $0.6$. A claim with implausibly high
$\eta_{II}$ may not violate physics, but it is the diagnostic for a
careful look at the measurement protocol.
\end{principle}

\begin{estimation}
\textbf{Estimate the thermal efficiency of a typical compact-car
spark-ignition engine at its rated operating point.}

\smallskip
\textit{Estimate, before reading on.}

\smallskip
A compact car gets approximately $7\,\text{L}/100\,\text{km}$ on
gasoline in mixed driving. The energy content of gasoline is roughly
$32\,\text{MJ}/\text{L}$ (lower heating value). The energy delivered
to the fuel-burning engine per kilometre is $7 \times 32/100 = 2.24
\,\text{MJ}/\text{km} = 2240\,\text{kJ}/\text{km}$.

The road power demand at a moderate cruising speed of $100\,\text{km}/
\text{h}$ for a $1500\,\text{kg}$ compact car: rolling resistance at
$C_r \approx 0.01$ gives $F_{\text{roll}} = 0.01 \times 1500 \times
9.81 = 147\,\text{N}$. Aerodynamic drag at $A C_d \approx 0.7\,
\text{m}^2$ and air density $1.2\,\text{kg}/\text{m}^3$ at
$28\,\text{m}/\text{s}$ gives $F_{\text{drag}} = 0.5 \times 1.2 \times
0.7 \times 28^2 = 329\,\text{N}$. Total road force $\sim 480\,\text{N}$;
road power $\sim 480 \times 28 = 13.5\,\text{kW}$.

Per kilometre at $100\,\text{km}/\text{h}$: $13.5\,\text{kW} \times
36\,\text{s} = 487\,\text{kJ}$. The thermal efficiency at the
cruising point is roughly $487/2240 \approx 22\,\%$.

The estimate is a tank-to-wheels number that includes drivetrain
losses ($\sim 15\,\%$ of engine output), so the engine's brake thermal
efficiency at this operating point is closer to $26\,\%$. Including
operation at non-optimal points (city traffic, cold starts, idling),
the lifecycle average drops further.

Modern compact-car gasoline engines have peak brake thermal
efficiencies near $35\,\%$ at the design operating point and average
$25\,\%$ to $30\,\%$ over typical driving cycles. Manufacturer claims
for ``up to $40\,\%$ thermal efficiency'' refer to the design point,
not the average.

The postmortem: the estimate is correctly in the $20\,\%$ to $30\,\%$
range. The largest fragile inputs are the assumed fuel economy
(varies with vehicle and driving) and the aerodynamic drag (depends
on vehicle shape). The thermal efficiency is bounded above by the
Otto-cycle ideal (around $60\,\%$ for $r = 10$), and the real engine
achieves $30\,\%$ to $50\,\%$ of that.
\end{estimation}

\section{Project}\pathtag{core}

\begin{project}[Estimate the thermal efficiency of a household car
engine three ways]
The reader estimates the thermal efficiency of a familiar
spark-ignition or compression-ignition engine through three
independent calculations and compares the three to one another and to
the manufacturer's stated efficiency. The deliverable is a fifteen-
to twenty-five-page report.

\textbf{Track}. Calculation plus data-collection (driving log).
\textbf{Hazard class}: low.

\textbf{Method 1: tank-to-wheels from a driving log}. Over four weeks,
record every refuelling: odometer reading, litres of fuel, fuel
type, route categorisation (city, highway, mixed). Compute average
fuel consumption $\text{L}/100\,\text{km}$. Use the road-load
formula (rolling resistance plus aerodynamic drag) with stated
vehicle parameters to estimate the work delivered to the wheels per
kilometre. Compute the tank-to-wheels efficiency $\eta_1 =
W_{\text{wheels}}/Q_{\text{fuel}}$, and back out the engine's brake
thermal efficiency by accounting for drivetrain and accessory
losses (state the assumed loss fraction).

\textbf{Method 2: rated power and manufacturer fuel-economy
specification}. Read the manufacturer's rated power (kW) and rated
fuel-economy figures from the vehicle handbook. Use the rated test
cycle to back out the engine's brake thermal efficiency at the rated
point.

\textbf{Method 3: air-standard cycle analysis}. From the engine's
compression ratio (printed in the handbook) and the appropriate
air-standard cycle (Otto for spark-ignition, Diesel for compression-
ignition), compute the ideal-cycle thermal efficiency. Apply a
typical brake-to-ideal ratio (state the assumption) to estimate the
actual efficiency.

\textbf{Comparison}. Tabulate the three efficiencies, the
manufacturer's stated efficiency (if available), and discuss the
sources of discrepancy. State which method is most sensitive to
which uncertainty.

\textbf{Reflection ($1{,}500$ to $2{,}500$ words)}. The reader
addresses:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Which method gave the highest estimate; which gave the
    lowest? Why?
  \item Where does the gap from the Carnot bound originate? Identify
    the largest single irreversibility.
  \item One design change that would improve the engine's
    efficiency, and the cost of that change.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Deliverable}. The driving log, the three calculations, the
comparison table, and the reflection.

\textbf{Time}. Four weeks of passive logging, six to ten hours of
calculation, four to six hours of write-up.

\textbf{Optional extension}. A reader with a household heat pump can
repeat the three-way comparison for the heat pump, logging delivered
heat against electricity and outdoor temperature, in the spirit of the
\texttt{heat\_pump\_field\_log.csv} sample, and compare the field COP to
the Carnot bound at each outdoor temperature.
\end{project}

\section{Exercises}

\subsection*{Calculation}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
A Carnot engine operates between $T_h = 600\,\si{\kelvin}$ and $T_c =
300\,\si{\kelvin}$. Find the thermal efficiency. If the heat input is
$1\,\text{MJ}$, find the work output and the heat rejected.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} The efficiency is $\eta = 1 - T_c/T_h =
1 - 300/600 = 0.5$. The work output is $W = \eta Q_h = 0.5 \times
1\,\text{MJ} = 0.5\,\text{MJ}$. The heat rejected is $Q_c = Q_h - W =
1 - 0.5 = 0.5\,\text{MJ}$. As a check, $Q_c/Q_h = T_c/T_h = 0.5$, the
defining property of the reversible cycle.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A steam Rankine cycle has a boiler at $5\,\text{MPa}/450\,\degC$ and
a condenser at $10\,\text{kPa}$. Assuming ideal (isentropic) turbine
and pump, find the thermal efficiency. Use the steam tables.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} From the steam tables: at the boiler exit
($5\,\text{MPa}$, $450\,\degC$) the superheated vapour has $h_3 \approx
3317\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}$ and $s_3 \approx 6.82\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}
\cdot\text{K}$. The turbine is isentropic to $10\,\text{kPa}$, where
$s_f = 0.649$ and $s_{fg} = 7.50$, so the exit quality is $x_4 =
(6.82 - 0.649)/7.50 = 0.82$ and $h_4 = 191.8 + 0.82 \times 2392.8 =
2154\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}$. The condenser exit is saturated liquid,
$h_1 = 191.8\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}$, $v_f = 0.00101\,\text{m}^3/
\text{kg}$. The pump work is $w_p = v_f(p_b - p_c) = 0.00101 \times
(5000 - 10) = 5.0\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}$, so $h_2 = 196.8$. Then $q_h =
h_3 - h_2 = 3120$, $w_t = h_3 - h_4 = 1163$, and $\eta_R = (w_t -
w_p)/q_h = (1163 - 5)/3120 = 0.371$, about $37\,\%$. The exit quality
$0.82$ sits at the low end of acceptable turbine-exit wetness, which is
the practical reason reheat is added.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
An air-standard Brayton cycle has a pressure ratio of $12$ and a
turbine inlet temperature of $1400\,\si{\kelvin}$. Compressor inlet
is at $300\,\si{\kelvin}$ and $100\,\text{kPa}$. Assuming
$\gamma = 1.4$ and isentropic compression and expansion, find the
thermal efficiency, the net work per unit mass, and the back-work
ratio.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} With $c_p = 1.005\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}
\cdot\text{K}$ and exponent $(\gamma-1)/\gamma = 0.286$: $T_2 = 300
\times 12^{0.286} = 610\,\si{\kelvin}$ and $T_4 = 1400 \times
12^{-0.286} = 688\,\si{\kelvin}$. Turbine work $w_t = c_p(T_3 - T_4) =
1.005 \times 712 = 715\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}$; compressor work $w_c =
c_p(T_2 - T_1) = 1.005 \times 310 = 312\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}$; net work
$w_{\text{net}} = 403\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}$. Heat input $q_h = c_p(T_3 -
T_2) = 1.005 \times 790 = 794\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}$, so $\eta_B =
403/794 = 0.508$, matching $1 - 12^{-0.286}$. The back-work ratio is
$w_c/w_t = 312/715 = 0.44$. The high back-work ratio is the Brayton
cycle's signature. The solver \texttt{brayton\_cycle.py} returns the
same values.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
An Otto cycle has a compression ratio of $9$, a heat input of
$2000\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}$, and initial conditions $T_1 = 300\,\si{
\kelvin}$, $p_1 = 100\,\text{kPa}$. With $\gamma = 1.4$, find the
maximum temperature, the maximum pressure, the thermal efficiency,
and the mean effective pressure.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Compression: $T_2 = 300 \times 9^{0.4} =
722\,\si{\kelvin}$, $p_2 = 100 \times 9^{1.4} = 2167\,\text{kPa}$.
Constant-volume heat addition with $c_v = 0.718$: $T_3 = T_2 +
q_{\text{in}}/c_v = 722 + 2000/0.718 = 3508\,\si{\kelvin}$ (the maximum
temperature), and $p_3 = p_2 (T_3/T_2) = 2167 \times 3508/722 =
10{,}530\,\text{kPa}$ (the maximum pressure). Efficiency $\eta_O = 1 -
9^{-0.4} = 0.585$, so $w_{\text{net}} = 0.585 \times 2000 = 1170\,
\text{kJ}/\text{kg}$. For the MEP, $v_1 = RT_1/p_1 = 0.287 \times
300/100 = 0.861\,\text{m}^3/\text{kg}$ and $v_2 = v_1/9 = 0.0957$, so
$\text{MEP} = w_{\text{net}}/(v_1 - v_2) = 1170/0.765 = 1529\,\text{kPa}$.
The peak temperature is unphysically high because the air-standard model
ignores dissociation and wall heat loss; \texttt{otto\_cycle.py} returns
these values.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A Diesel cycle has a compression ratio of $18$ and a cut-off ratio of
$2.5$. With $\gamma = 1.4$, find the thermal efficiency. Compare to
the Otto-cycle efficiency at the same compression ratio.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} The Diesel efficiency is
\[
\eta_D = 1 - \frac{1}{r^{\gamma-1}}\cdot\frac{r_c^\gamma - 1}{
\gamma(r_c - 1)}
= 1 - \frac{1}{18^{0.4}}\cdot\frac{2.5^{1.4} - 1}{1.4(2.5 - 1)}.
\]
Here $18^{0.4} = 3.18$, $2.5^{1.4} = 3.61$, so the cut-off bracket is
$(3.61 - 1)/(1.4 \times 1.5) = 2.61/2.1 = 1.24$. Then $\eta_D = 1 -
(1/3.18)(1.24) = 1 - 0.391 = 0.609$, about $61\,\%$. The Otto cycle at
$r = 18$ would give $\eta_O = 1 - 18^{-0.4} = 1 - 0.314 = 0.686$, about
$69\,\%$. The Otto value is higher at equal compression ratio because
its constant-volume heat addition occurs at a higher average
temperature. The Diesel's real advantage is that it can run at $r = 18$
at all, where a gasoline charge would knock.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A heat pump operates between $T_c = 270\,\si{\kelvin}$ (outdoor air)
and $T_h = 295\,\si{\kelvin}$ (indoor air). It delivers $10\,\text{kW}$
of heating with an actual COP $50\,\%$ of the Carnot value. Find the
electrical input power and the heat absorbed from the outdoor air.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} The Carnot heating COP is $T_h/(T_h - T_c)
= 295/25 = 11.8$. The actual COP is half that, $5.9$. The electrical
input is $\dot W = \dot Q_h/\text{COP} = 10\,\text{kW}/5.9 =
1.69\,\text{kW}$. The heat absorbed from the outdoor air is the first
law on the device: $\dot Q_c = \dot Q_h - \dot W = 10 - 1.69 =
8.3\,\text{kW}$. The point of the heat pump is that it delivers
$10\,\text{kW}$ of heating from $1.7\,\text{kW}$ of electricity by
pulling the balance out of cold outdoor air, where a resistive heater
would draw the full $10\,\text{kW}$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A vapour-compression refrigeration cycle uses R-134a with evaporator
at $-10\,\degC$, condenser at $40\,\degC$, isentropic compression,
saturated liquid at condenser exit, saturated vapour at evaporator
exit. Find the COP, the specific cooling capacity, and the
compressor work per kilogram.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} From \texttt{r134a\_saturation.csv}:
saturated vapour at $-10\,\degC$ has $h_1 = 392.4\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}$;
the isentropic compression to the $40\,\degC$ condenser pressure reaches
$h_2 \approx 425.6\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}$; saturated liquid at $40\,\degC$
has $h_3 = 256.4\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}$, and the throttle gives $h_4 =
h_3 = 256.4$. The specific cooling capacity is $q_c = h_1 - h_4 =
392.4 - 256.4 = 136\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}$. The compressor work is $w_c =
h_2 - h_1 = 33.2\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}$. The cooling COP is $q_c/w_c =
136/33.2 = 4.1$. Against the Carnot COP $263/50 = 5.3$, the ideal cycle
reaches $77\,\%$ of Carnot, the loss almost all in the throttle. The
solver \texttt{vapor\_compression.py} returns the same figures.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A combined-cycle plant has a gas-turbine top cycle with $\eta_{\text{GT}}
= 38\,\%$ and a steam-turbine bottom cycle that converts $60\,\%$ of
the gas-turbine exhaust heat to additional work, with the bottom
cycle's own thermal efficiency of $35\,\%$. Compute the overall
combined-cycle efficiency.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Per unit fuel energy, the gas turbine
produces work $0.38$ and rejects $1 - 0.38 = 0.62$ as exhaust. The
heat-recovery steam generator captures $60\,\%$ of that exhaust,
$0.60 \times 0.62 = 0.372$, as heat into the bottom cycle. The bottom
cycle converts $35\,\%$ of that to work, $0.35 \times 0.372 = 0.130$.
The total work per unit fuel is $0.38 + 0.130 = 0.51$, so the combined
efficiency is $51\,\%$. The equivalent compact form is $\eta_{cc} =
\eta_{\text{GT}} + \eta_{\text{HRSG}}(1 - \eta_{\text{GT}})
\eta_{\text{ST}}$. The result exceeds either cycle alone, which is the
case for the combined cycle.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Derivation}\pathtag{standard}

\begin{exercise}
Derive the Carnot efficiency $\eta = 1 - T_c/T_h$ for a reversible
heat engine between two reservoirs at $T_h$ and $T_c$, using the
entropy balance over the cycle.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Over one cycle the working fluid returns to
its initial state, so its net entropy change is zero. For a reversible
engine the only entropy transfers are with the two reservoirs: the fluid
gains $Q_h/T_h$ from the hot reservoir during isothermal heat intake and
loses $Q_c/T_c$ to the cold reservoir during isothermal heat rejection,
with no entropy generated. The cyclic entropy balance is therefore
$Q_h/T_h - Q_c/T_c = 0$, giving $Q_c/Q_h = T_c/T_h$. The first law over
the cycle gives $W = Q_h - Q_c$, so $\eta = W/Q_h = 1 - Q_c/Q_h =
1 - T_c/T_h$. The substitution of the entropy ratio for the heat ratio
is the whole content of the result.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Derive the air-standard Brayton-cycle thermal efficiency $\eta = 1 -
r_p^{-(\gamma-1)/\gamma}$ in terms of the pressure ratio $r_p$ and
$\gamma$, treating the compressor and turbine as isentropic.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Heat is added at constant pressure $q_h =
c_p(T_3 - T_2)$ and rejected at constant pressure $q_c = c_p(T_4 -
T_1)$, so $\eta = 1 - q_c/q_h = 1 - (T_4 - T_1)/(T_3 - T_2)$. The two
isentropic legs share the same pressure ratio, so $T_2/T_1 = T_3/T_4 =
r_p^{(\gamma-1)/\gamma}$. Write $T_2 = T_1 r_p^{(\gamma-1)/\gamma}$ and
$T_3 = T_4 r_p^{(\gamma-1)/\gamma}$; then $T_3 - T_2 = (T_4 -
T_1)r_p^{(\gamma-1)/\gamma}$. Substituting,
\[
\eta = 1 - \frac{T_4 - T_1}{(T_4 - T_1)r_p^{(\gamma-1)/\gamma}}
= 1 - r_p^{-(\gamma-1)/\gamma}.
\]
The temperatures cancel, leaving the efficiency a function of the
pressure ratio alone.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Derive the air-standard Otto-cycle thermal efficiency $\eta = 1 -
r^{-(\gamma-1)}$ in terms of the compression ratio $r$ and $\gamma$.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Heat is added and rejected at constant
volume, so $q_h = c_v(T_3 - T_2)$ and $q_c = c_v(T_4 - T_1)$, giving
$\eta = 1 - (T_4 - T_1)/(T_3 - T_2)$. The two isentropic legs share the
same volume ratio $r$, so $T_2/T_1 = T_3/T_4 = r^{\gamma-1}$. Then $T_2 =
T_1 r^{\gamma-1}$ and $T_3 = T_4 r^{\gamma-1}$, so $T_3 - T_2 = (T_4 -
T_1)r^{\gamma-1}$. Substituting,
\[
\eta = 1 - \frac{T_4 - T_1}{(T_4 - T_1)r^{\gamma-1}}
= 1 - r^{-(\gamma-1)}.
\]
The structure is identical to the Brayton derivation, with the volume
ratio replacing the pressure ratio and the exponent $\gamma - 1$
replacing $(\gamma-1)/\gamma$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Show that the Carnot COP for a refrigerator, $\text{COP}_R = T_c/(T_h
- T_c)$, and for a heat pump, $\text{COP}_{HP} = T_h/(T_h - T_c)$,
satisfy $\text{COP}_{HP} = \text{COP}_R + 1$.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Subtract directly:
\[
\text{COP}_{HP} - \text{COP}_R = \frac{T_h}{T_h - T_c} -
\frac{T_c}{T_h - T_c} = \frac{T_h - T_c}{T_h - T_c} = 1.
\]
The result also follows from the energy balance: the heat delivered to
the hot side is the heat pulled from the cold side plus the work input,
$Q_h = Q_c + W$. Dividing by $W$ gives $\text{COP}_{HP} = \text{COP}_R +
1$ for any device, reversible or not, so the identity is not special to
the Carnot case.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Estimation}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the maximum thermal efficiency of a coal-fired power plant
with boiler at $560\,\degC$ and cooling-water condenser at $30\,\degC$,
and compare to the typical achieved efficiency ($43\,\%$ for modern
ultra-supercritical plants).

\paragraph{Reference estimate.} Convert to kelvin: $T_h = 833\,\si{
\kelvin}$, $T_c = 303\,\si{\kelvin}$. The Carnot bound is $\eta = 1 -
303/833 = 1 - 0.364 = 0.636$, about $64\,\%$. The achieved $43\,\%$ is
$43/64 = 0.67$ of the Carnot bound, a second-law efficiency near $0.7$,
which is typical for a well-engineered Rankine plant. The gap to Carnot
comes from finite-temperature heat transfer in the boiler and
condenser, irreversibilities in the turbine and pump, and the fact that
heat is added over a range of temperatures rather than isothermally at
$T_h$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the COP of a typical residential air-source heat pump
operating in a $-10\,\degC$ outdoor environment to heat a $20\,\degC$
indoor space. State the second-law efficiency assumed.

\paragraph{Reference estimate.} In kelvin, $T_h = 293$, $T_c = 263$,
lift $30\,\si{\kelvin}$. The Carnot heating COP is $293/30 = 9.8$. Real
air-source heat pumps run at $40$ to $50\,\%$ of Carnot at this lift
once defrost cycling and the temperature pinch in the heat exchangers
are counted. Taking $\eta_{II} = 0.45$ gives a field COP of $0.45 \times
9.8 = 4.4$. The field-log data in \texttt{heat\_pump\_field\_log.csv}
shows a field COP near $2.2$ at a $-8\,\degC$ outdoor temperature, lower
than this clean estimate because the logged days include defrost
penalties and supplemental resistive heating, which the simple
second-law fraction does not capture. A defensible answer is COP $4$ to
$4.5$ for the steady operating point, falling toward $2$ to $3$ over a
real cold-weather duty cycle.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the thermal efficiency of a household electric oven
considering only the heating of the food (not the oven cavity or
losses to the kitchen).

\paragraph{Reference estimate.} Heating a $1\,\text{kg}$ dish from
$20\,\degC$ to $180\,\degC$, with a food specific heat near $3\,\text{kJ}
/\text{kg}\cdot\text{K}$, needs $3 \times 1 \times 160 = 480\,\text{kJ}$
delivered to the food. A typical oven draws $2\,\text{kW}$ and takes
perhaps $40\,\text{min} = 2400\,\text{s}$ for the bake including
preheat, so it consumes $2 \times 2400 = 4800\,\text{kJ}$. The
food-only efficiency is $480/4800 \approx 10\,\%$. The figure is low
because most of the electricity heats the oven cavity, the door, and
the kitchen, and is lost on every air exchange. This is a first-law
efficiency; there is no Carnot bound to bump against, since resistive
heating converts electricity to heat at $100\,\%$ and the loss is
entirely in where the heat goes, not in the conversion.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the cooling capacity required to maintain a $300\,\text{m}^2$
office space in a $35\,\degC$ climate at indoor $22\,\degC$, and the
corresponding electrical input for an air-conditioner with
$\text{COP} = 3$.
\paragraph{Reference estimate.} A common rule of thumb for an office in
a hot climate is a cooling load near $100$ to $150\,\text{W}/\text{m}^2$
once envelope gain, occupants, lighting, and equipment are summed. Take
$120\,\text{W}/\text{m}^2$: the cooling load is $300 \times 120 =
36\,\text{kW}$ (about $10$ tons of refrigeration). At a seasonal COP of
$3$, the electrical input is $\dot Q_c/\text{COP} = 36/3 = 12\,\text{kW}$.
A sanity check from first principles: the dominant gains are solar
through glazing and internal loads, both of order tens of watts per
square metre, so $36\,\text{kW}$ for a $300\,\text{m}^2$ floor is the
right order. Raising the COP to $4$ with a better unit would cut the
electrical draw to $9\,\text{kW}$.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Simulation}\pathtag{mastery}

\begin{exercise}
Implement a Rankine-cycle solver: given boiler pressure, boiler
temperature, condenser pressure, turbine and pump isentropic
efficiencies, return the cycle's thermal efficiency, the specific
work, and the cycle state points. Verify against textbook values.

\paragraph{Model-answer sketch.} Structure the solver as four steps
matching the four components. Pump: with feedwater enthalpy and specific
volume from the saturated-liquid line at $p_c$, compute $h_2 = h_1 +
v_f(p_b - p_c)/\eta_p$. Boiler: read $h_3$, $s_3$ from the superheated
table at $(p_b, T_b)$. Turbine: find the isentropic exit state at $s_4 =
s_3$ and $p_c$ (compute the quality if inside the dome), get $h_{4s}$,
then apply $h_4 = h_3 - \eta_t(h_3 - h_{4s})$. Condenser: $h_1$ from the
saturated-liquid line at $p_c$. Then $\eta = (h_3 - h_4 - (h_2 -
h_1))/(h_3 - h_2)$. Verify on exercise 2: the ideal case
($\eta_t = \eta_p = 1$) should return $\eta \approx 0.37$ and exit
quality $\approx 0.82$. A non-isentropic turbine at $\eta_t = 0.85$
drops the efficiency to roughly $0.32$ and raises the exit quality,
because the irreversible expansion ends further inside the superheated
or higher-quality region.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Implement an Otto-cycle simulator using a finite-rate combustion
model (Wiebe function) instead of instantaneous heat addition.
Compare the simulated thermal efficiency to the air-standard ideal
for the same compression ratio and heat input.

\paragraph{Model-answer sketch.} Replace the instantaneous
constant-volume heat addition with a heat-release schedule following the
Wiebe function, the cumulative burned-mass fraction
\[
x_b(\theta) = 1 - \exp\!\left[-a\left(\frac{\theta -
\theta_0}{\Delta\theta}\right)^{m+1}\right],
\]
with typical $a = 5$, $m = 2$, burn duration $\Delta\theta \approx
40$ to $60$ crank-angle degrees. Integrate the first law over crank
angle with the cylinder volume from the slider-crank kinematics, adding
$dQ = q_{\text{in}} \, dx_b$ at each step and updating temperature and
pressure for the instantaneous volume. The finite burn duration spreads
heat release across the early expansion rather than concentrating it at
top dead centre, so the simulated thermal efficiency falls a few points
below the air-standard $1 - r^{-(\gamma-1)}$, the gap widening as the
burn duration lengthens. Adding a wall-heat-loss term widens it further.
The exercise's lesson is that the air-standard ideal is an upper bound
that real combustion timing erodes.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Implement a vapour-compression cycle simulator using a refrigerant
property-table look-up (or CoolProp). Given $T_{\text{evap}}$,
$T_{\text{cond}}$, isentropic compressor efficiency, sub-cooling at
condenser exit, and superheat at evaporator exit, return the cycle
COP and the four state points. Verify on the worked R-134a example.

\paragraph{Model-answer sketch.} State 1 is at the evaporator pressure
with the specified superheat above $T_{\text{evap}}$; read $h_1$, $s_1$.
For the compressor, find the isentropic discharge $h_{2s}$ at $s_1$ and
the condenser pressure, then $h_2 = h_1 + (h_{2s} - h_1)/\eta_c$. State 3
is at the condenser pressure with the specified sub-cooling below
$T_{\text{cond}}$; read $h_3$. The throttle gives $h_4 = h_3$. Then
$\text{COP}_R = (h_1 - h_4)/(h_2 - h_1)$. With zero superheat, zero
sub-cooling, and $\eta_c = 1$ on the $-10\,\degC$ / $40\,\degC$ R-134a
case, the solver should return COP $\approx 4.1$, matching
\texttt{vapor\_compression.py}. Adding sub-cooling raises $q_c$ and the
COP; a compressor efficiency below one raises $h_2$ and lowers the COP.
The library \texttt{CoolProp} supplies the property look-ups for an
arbitrary refrigerant.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Design}\pathtag{standard}

\begin{exercise}
Specify the operating conditions (boiler pressure and temperature,
condenser pressure) for a $50\,\text{MW}$ Rankine plant that uses
cooling water at $20\,\degC$ as its heat sink. Target a thermal
efficiency of at least $35\,\%$.

\paragraph{Model-answer sketch.} Set the condenser first: with
$20\,\degC$ cooling water, a condenser saturation temperature near
$35\,\degC$ to $40\,\degC$ gives a workable approach; that fixes the
condenser pressure at roughly $6$ to $7\,\text{kPa}$ (the saturation
pressure of water at $\sim 38\,\degC$). To reach $35\,\%$ thermal
efficiency, the average temperature of heat addition must be high, so
choose a boiler at $\sim 10\,\text{MPa}$ and superheat to $\sim
500\,\degC$, a standard subcritical condition. The Carnot bound between
$500\,\degC$ ($773\,\si{\kelvin}$) and $38\,\degC$ ($311\,\si{\kelvin}$)
is $1 - 311/773 = 0.60$; a real Rankine plant reaching $\sim 0.65$ of
that lands near $39\,\%$, comfortably above the target. The
$50\,\text{MW}$ output then fixes the steam mass flow from $\dot W =
\dot m \, w_{\text{net}}$. State the assumed turbine efficiency
($\sim 0.85$) and note that reheat would be the next step to keep the
turbine-exit quality acceptable.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Specify the pressure ratio and turbine inlet temperature for a
$10\,\text{MW}$ industrial Brayton-cycle gas turbine targeting a
specific work output of $300\,\text{kJ}/\text{kg}$ and a thermal
efficiency of at least $35\,\%$.

\paragraph{Model-answer sketch.} Two targets, two knobs ($r_p$ and
$T_3$). Efficiency depends mainly on $r_p$: $\eta_B = 1 -
r_p^{-0.286} \ge 0.35$ needs $r_p \ge 5$, but specific work for a given
$T_3$ peaks at an intermediate $r_p$, so pick a moderate $r_p \approx
12$ to $16$, which gives $\eta_B \approx 0.51$ to $0.54$ ideal (and
$\sim 0.35$ once compressor and turbine inefficiencies are applied).
With $T_1 = 300\,\si{\kelvin}$ and $r_p = 14$, $T_2 \approx 638\,\si{
\kelvin}$. The specific-work target $w_{\text{net}} = c_p[(T_3 - T_4) -
(T_2 - T_1)] = 300$ then sets $T_3$: solving with $T_4 = T_3
r_p^{-0.286}$ gives $T_3 \approx 1400$ to $1500\,\si{\kelvin}$, within
reach of cooled blades. The $10\,\text{MW}$ rating fixes the air mass
flow from $\dot m = \dot W/w_{\text{net}} = 10{,}000/300 \approx
33\,\text{kg}/\text{s}$. State the assumed component efficiencies
($\eta_c \approx 0.85$, $\eta_t \approx 0.88$).
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Specify the refrigerant, the evaporator temperature, and the condenser
temperature for a household refrigerator that maintains $4\,\degC$
inside in a $22\,\degC$ kitchen. State the COP target and the design
margin against the Carnot bound.

\paragraph{Model-answer sketch.} The evaporator must run below the
cold-space temperature and the condenser above the kitchen, so size the
heat exchangers for a pinch of about $10\,\si{\kelvin}$ on each side:
evaporator at $-6\,\degC$ ($267\,\si{\kelvin}$), condenser at
$32\,\degC$ ($305\,\si{\kelvin}$). The Carnot cooling COP for the
reservoir pair ($4\,\degC$ box, $22\,\degC$ kitchen) is $277/18 = 15.4$,
but the device works across the wider evaporator-to-condenser span, with
Carnot COP $267/38 = 7.0$. A realised COP near $0.5$ of that, so $\sim
3$ to $3.5$, is a sound target, leaving a healthy margin below the
bound. Choose a low-GWP natural refrigerant such as isobutane (R-600a)
or propane (R-290), which the \texttt{refrigerants.csv} table lists
among the low-GWP hydrocarbons used in domestic appliances, in a small
hermetic compressor. State the
$10\,\si{\kelvin}$ pinch assumptions explicitly, since they set the gap
between the reservoir Carnot COP and the device Carnot COP.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Diagnosis and reverse engineering}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
A combined-cycle plant's measured electrical output divided by the
fuel input gives a thermal efficiency of $40\,\%$, against a design
specification of $58\,\%$. Identify the most probable cause from
among (a) compressor degradation, (b) turbine blade damage, (c)
heat-recovery steam generator fouling, (d) condenser cooling-water
flow restriction, and state the diagnostic measurement that would
distinguish them.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} An $18$-point efficiency shortfall is large
enough to point at a major component, and each candidate leaves a
distinct signature. (a) Compressor degradation shows as a fallen
pressure ratio and raised compressor-exit temperature for the same speed;
measure the compressor discharge pressure and temperature. (b) Turbine
blade damage shows as low turbine output for the measured inlet
conditions and possibly raised exhaust temperature; compare turbine
shaft power to the gas-path energy drop. (c) HRSG fouling shows as a
high gas-side exit temperature (heat not transferred to the steam) and
reduced steam production; measure the HRSG stack temperature and steam
flow. (d) Condenser flow restriction shows as a raised condenser
pressure and saturation temperature; measure the condenser vacuum. The
single most diagnostic reading is the gas-turbine exhaust and HRSG stack
temperatures: a normal gas turbine with a hot stack indicts the HRSG or
bottom cycle, while a low gas-turbine output with a normal stack indicts
the gas turbine itself.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A residential air-conditioner uses noticeably more electricity in
the second half of the summer than the first half, while delivering
the same cooling. Identify the most probable cause from among (a)
refrigerant leak, (b) condenser fouling, (c) evaporator coil dirt,
(d) thermostat calibration drift, and the diagnostic measurement that
would identify each.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Same cooling for more electricity means a
fallen COP, so the suspect is anything that widens the
evaporator-to-condenser pressure span or starves the cycle. (a) A
refrigerant leak lowers the evaporator pressure and the suction
superheat rises; measure the low-side pressure and superheat. (b)
Condenser fouling (dirt on the outdoor coil, accumulated over the
summer, which fits the seasonal timing) raises the condenser pressure
and the compressor lift; measure the high-side pressure and the
condenser approach temperature. (c) Evaporator coil dirt or a clogged
filter restricts airflow, lowers the evaporator temperature, and can
ice the coil; measure the air-side temperature drop and pressure across
the indoor coil. (d) Thermostat drift would change the delivered
cooling, not the electricity per unit cooling, so it does not fit the
symptom. The seasonal onset most strongly indicts (b): an outdoor coil
collects dust and debris through a summer of operation, and a hose-down
is the first corrective step.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A diesel engine's exhaust temperature is observed to be $50\,\degC$
higher than its commissioning value at the same load. Identify the
operational meaning and the corrective action.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} A higher exhaust temperature at equal load
means combustion is finishing later in the cycle or more fuel is being
burned for the same output, both signs of degraded efficiency: heat that
should have been turned into work during expansion is leaving in the
exhaust. Probable causes are retarded injection timing, worn or fouled
injectors giving poor atomisation and late burn, reduced compression
(worn rings or leaking valves dropping the compression temperature), or
a fouled turbocharger or charge-air cooler raising the intake
temperature. Corrective actions, in order of intrusiveness: check and
clean or replace the air filter and charge-air cooler, verify and reset
injection timing, test and service the injectors, and (if those do not
recover the temperature) inspect compression and the valve train.
Rising exhaust temperature is a standard early-warning indicator on
marine and stationary diesels precisely because it integrates several
efficiency-eroding faults into one cheap measurement.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Failure analysis}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
A 2023 patent application claims a heat pump that delivers a COP of
$10$ between a $20\,\degC$ indoor space and a $0\,\degC$ outdoor air
source. Identify the Carnot bound and write a single sentence
rejecting the claim.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} The Carnot heating COP for these reservoirs
is $T_h/(T_h - T_c) = 293/(293 - 273) = 293/20 = 14.65$. A claimed COP
of $10$ is below this bound, so the claim does not violate the second
law outright; the careful rejection turns on the realised fraction.
``A COP of $10$ between these reservoirs would require a second-law
efficiency of $10/14.65 = 0.68$, far above the $0.4$ to $0.5$ that
production air-source heat pumps achieve across real heat exchangers and
a defrost duty, so the claim is implausible and the burden falls on the
applicant to show the operating boundary, the measurement protocol, and
any unaccounted input.'' This is the lesson of the two-step diagnosis:
when a claim clears the Carnot bound, the rejection must rest on the
realised-efficiency check, not on a bound it does not in fact exceed.
The companion \texttt{efficiency\_claim\_check.py} reports both the bound
and the implied fraction.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A start-up advertises an internal-combustion engine that runs on
``water plus a catalyst,'' achieving $80\,\%$ thermal efficiency.
Identify the first-law violation and the diagnostic experiment that
would detect the hidden energy input.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Water is the product of hydrogen
combustion, not a fuel: it sits at the bottom of the energy ladder, with
no chemical energy left to release. A ``catalyst'' lowers a reaction
barrier but cannot supply energy, so a device that consumes only water
and a catalyst and produces net work is a first-kind perpetual motion
machine, forbidden by the first law. If the water is first electrolysed
to hydrogen and oxygen and the gases are then burned, the energy
recovered cannot exceed the electrical energy spent on electrolysis, so
the loop is energy-losing, not energy-producing. The diagnostic
experiment: draw a control boundary around the whole device and meter
every crossing, electrical input, any battery state of charge, any
consumable beyond water, over a run long enough to exhaust a hidden
store. The energy out will track an input the advertisement omitted (a
battery, a pressurised-gas cylinder, or a fuel additive); if the only
genuine input is water, the claimed output simply will not appear.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A consumer-grade ``infrared sauna'' is sold as $99\,\%$ efficient. The
product converts $1\,\text{kW}$ of electricity into infrared radiation
that heats the user's body. Discuss what ``efficient'' means in this
context and whether the figure is consistent with thermodynamics.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Resistive heating converts electricity to
heat at essentially $100\,\%$, so a $99\,\%$ figure for ``electricity to
heat'' is unremarkable and not the relevant quantity. ``Efficient'' for
a sauna must mean the fraction of the emitted heat that reaches the
intended target rather than warming the cabinet, the air, and the room.
That figure depends on geometry, emitter placement, and how much of the
radiation the body intercepts, and it is far below $99\,\%$ in practice
because much of the radiated and convected heat misses the user. The
$99\,\%$ claim is consistent with thermodynamics (it does not violate
any law) but it answers the wrong question: there is no Carnot bound on
a resistive heater, and quoting the conversion efficiency tells the
buyer nothing about delivered warmth. The honest figure is the
target-heating fraction, which the advertisement does not state.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Judgment}\pathtag{standard}

\begin{exercise}
A junior engineer proposes to evaluate a power plant by ``how much
of the fuel ends up as useful electricity.'' Write a one-paragraph
reply that distinguishes first-law (energy) and second-law (exergy)
analyses and identifies when each is appropriate.

\paragraph{Model-answer sketch.} The proposed metric is the first-law
(energy) efficiency, electricity out over fuel energy in, and it is the
right number when the question is fuel burned per unit of delivered
work, for instance comparing two plants that both make only
electricity. It is silent, though, on the quality of the energy: a plant
that dumps half its fuel as $40\,\degC$ waste heat and one that dumps it
as $400\,\degC$ heat score the same on the first law, yet the second
plant has wasted far more work potential. The second-law (exergy)
analysis weights each energy stream by its capacity to do work, $1 -
T_0/T$ for heat at temperature $T$, and so reveals where the work
potential was destroyed (combustion, heat transfer across large
temperature gaps) rather than merely where energy left. Use the
first-law figure for fuel accounting and the exergy figure for finding
which component to redesign and for comparing plants that deliver heat
and power together.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A facility manager argues that, since cogeneration uses ``waste
heat,'' it has zero marginal cost for the heating it provides. Write
a one-paragraph reply identifying the partial truth and the
qualifications.

\paragraph{Model-answer sketch.} The partial truth is real: when a power
cycle is already running to make electricity and would otherwise reject
its heat to a cooling tower or stack, capturing that heat does add
useful output for little extra fuel, so the marginal fuel cost of the
heat can indeed be small. The qualifications are several. First, raising
steam at a useful temperature for the heat load often forces the cycle
to run at a lower electrical efficiency (back-pressure operation), so
the heat is partly paid for in lost electricity, not in nothing.
Second, the recovered heat is delivered at a much lower temperature than
the combustion, so its exergy, its work value, is a fraction of its
energy; calling it free flatters it against electricity. Third, the
plant carries capital, maintenance, and distribution costs for the heat
network that are not zero. The right statement is that cogeneration has
a low marginal fuel cost for heat when the heat load matches the cycle's
rejection in temperature and timing, not that the heat is free.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A potential customer asks why a heat pump for home heating is
preferable to a high-efficiency natural-gas furnace. Write a one-
paragraph reply that uses the Carnot bound and the typical realised
efficiencies to frame the comparison.

\paragraph{Model-answer sketch.} A condensing gas furnace converts close
to all of the fuel's heating value into delivered heat, but it cannot
exceed $100\,\%$: each joule of gas yields at most a joule of heat. A
heat pump is not bound by $100\,\%$ because it moves heat rather than
making it; its Carnot heating COP between, say, a $20\,\degC$ indoor and
a $0\,\degC$ outdoor is $293/20 = 14.7$, and a real unit delivers $3$ to
$4$ units of heat per unit of electricity. So one unit of electricity
through a heat pump delivers three to four units of heat, while one unit
of gas through a furnace delivers at most one. The comparison that
matters to the customer then turns on the local prices of electricity
and gas and on the carbon intensity of the grid: the heat pump wins on
delivered heat per unit of purchased energy by a wide margin, and the
gap narrows only where electricity is far costlier than gas or where the
outdoor temperature is so low that the COP collapses toward one.
\end{exercise}
