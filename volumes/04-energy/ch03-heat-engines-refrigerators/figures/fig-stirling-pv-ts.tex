\caption[The ideal Stirling cycle]{The ideal Stirling cycle on the $p$-$V$
plane (a) and the $T$-$s$ plane (b). Two isotherms at $T_h$ and $T_c$ carry the
heat exchange with the reservoirs; two constant-volume legs (dashed, right
panel) connect them. The constant-volume legs exchange heat with an internal
regenerator rather than with the reservoirs: the gas cooling from $T_h$ to
$T_c$ along leg 2 to 3 deposits its heat in the regenerator matrix, and the gas
heating from $T_c$ to $T_h$ along leg 4 to 1 draws that same heat back. With a
perfect regenerator the only external heat transfers are the two isothermal
legs, so the cycle's efficiency equals the Carnot value $1 - T_c/T_h$, which
the $T$-$s$ rectangle shows directly: it is the same rectangle the Carnot cycle
draws, with isochoric sides replacing adiabatic ones because the regenerator
makes the side legs internally reversible. The regenerator is the component
that makes the Stirling cycle a practical Carnot-efficient engine on paper; in
hardware its finite effectiveness and the dead volume it adds are where the
real losses live.}
\label{fig:vol04ch03:stirling-pv-ts}
\end{figure}
