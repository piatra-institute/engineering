% from the oscillating pressure source through the regenerator to the cold heat
% exchanger, then the pulse tube itself, the hot heat exchanger, and the orifice
% and reservoir that phase-shift the gas motion. The absence of a cold moving
% part is the whole point: the piston sits at the warm end.

\caption[Pulse-tube cryocooler schematic]{A pulse-tube cryocooler laid out as a
train of components. The pressure oscillator at the warm end drives the working
gas, usually helium, back and forth through the regenerator, which stores and
returns the gas's heat as it shuttles. The cold heat exchanger absorbs the load
$Q_c$; the pulse tube is an open column of gas whose oscillation, phase-shifted by
the orifice and reservoir at the far end, carries heat from the cold exchanger up
to the hot exchanger, which rejects $Q_h$ to ambient. The machine's defining
feature is that no piston or displacer sits at the cold end: the only moving part
is the warm pressure oscillator, and the pulse tube replaces the cold displacer of
a Stirling cooler with a column of gas. That absence of a cold moving part is why
the pulse tube reaches long unattended lifetimes and low vibration, the properties
that carried it into space-telescope detector cooling and superconducting-magnet
service where a mechanism that runs for years without maintenance outranks the
modest coefficient of performance.}
\label{fig:vol04ch03:pulse-tube-schematic}
\end{figure}
