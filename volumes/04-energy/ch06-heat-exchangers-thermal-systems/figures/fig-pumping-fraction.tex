\caption[Pumping power as a fraction of the heat duty]{The pumping power a stream
costs, expressed as a fraction of the heat the exchanger transfers. Raising the
velocity lifts the film coefficient roughly as $u^{0.8}$ and the transferred
heat with it, while the pumping power climbs as $u^3$, so the ratio of pumping
cost to heat delivered grows as about $u^{2.2}$. At the customary liquid design
band of one to three metres per second the fraction sits well under one percent,
which is why pumping is a minor charge against a well-placed exchanger. Push the
velocity high enough and the fraction climbs into the percent range, the region
where the fluid costs more to move than the marginal heat is worth, and the
design has become pumping-limited rather than area-limited. The curve is the same
$u^3$-against-$u^{0.8}$ competition the cost trade-off drew, now read as a
dimensionless efficiency rather than a money cost.}
\label{fig:vol04ch06:pumping-fraction}
\end{figure}
