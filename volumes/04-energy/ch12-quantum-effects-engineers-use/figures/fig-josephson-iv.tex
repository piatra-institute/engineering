% effect); above it the junction switches to a resistive branch and develops a

\caption[Josephson-junction current-voltage curve]{Current against voltage for a
Josephson junction, two superconductors separated by a thin barrier. Below a
critical current $I_c$ the junction carries a supercurrent of Cooper pairs at
exactly zero voltage, the vertical branch, a macroscopic quantum effect with no
classical analogue. Driving the current past $I_c$ breaks the pair transport and
the junction switches to a resistive branch, developing a finite voltage set by
the normal-state resistance. The critical current is tunable by an applied
magnetic field, the property a superconducting quantum interference device reads
out to measure fields far below a nanotesla; the same junction under an applied
voltage oscillates at the Josephson frequency $2eV/h$, the relation that fixes
the volt in the modern SI.}
\label{fig:vol04ch12:josephson-iv}
\end{figure}
