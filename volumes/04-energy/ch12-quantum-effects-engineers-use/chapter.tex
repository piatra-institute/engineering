\noindent
Quantum mechanics enters engineering at the scale where the action of
the relevant process approaches Planck's constant. The chapter treats
the working quantum content the practising engineer carries:
energy-band semiconductors, photon-electron interactions in
LED and laser diodes and photovoltaic cells, the MOSFET transistor as
a quantum-mechanical device, MRI, electron microscopy, X-ray, and the
shot-noise and Johnson-noise floors that set the limits of classical
electronics. The full quantum-mechanical apparatus is treated in
Volume V (where it serves as the substrate for chemistry); here we
extract the engineering content.

\section{Why classical physics is not enough at the device scale}\pathtag{core}

A device with characteristic length $L$, characteristic energy $E$,
and characteristic time $\tau$ has \engterm{characteristic action}
$S \sim E\tau$. When $S$ is much larger than $\hbar = 1.055\times
10^{-34}\,\si{\joule\second}$, classical physics is the correct
description. When $S$ approaches $\hbar$, quantum effects become
operationally important; when $S$ is on the order of $\hbar$, the
device's behaviour is dominated by quantum mechanics.

For an electron in a copper wire at room temperature, the thermal
energy is $k_{B}T \approx 4\times 10^{-21}\,\si{\joule}$; the
relaxation time is $\tau \approx 25\,\si{\femto\second}$; the
action is $S \sim 10^{-34}\,\si{\joule\second} \sim \hbar$. The
electron is quantum-mechanical at the atomic scale. A wire of
macroscopic length contains $\sim 10^{22}$ electrons; the
ensemble-averaged behaviour (Ohm's law $V = IR$) is classical even
though each carrier is quantum. The classical-quantum boundary is at
the device's smallest feature size, not at its macroscopic dimension.

Three operational regimes follow:
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \engterm{Classical regime}: feature size $\gg$ de~Broglie
    wavelength. Mechanical engineering, structural engineering, most
    fluid mechanics, most circuit design above the nanometre level.
  \item \engterm{Mesoscopic regime}: feature size comparable to the
    de~Broglie wavelength or the coherence length. Modern transistors
    (channel length below $10\,\si{\nano\meter}$), quantum dots,
    single-photon devices, low-temperature superconducting circuits.
  \item \engterm{Quantum regime}: feature size $\ll$ de~Broglie
    wavelength, action $\sim \hbar$. Individual atomic and molecular
    processes; MRI; electron microscopy; X-ray sources.
\end{itemize}

The \engterm{de~Broglie wavelength} of a particle of momentum $p$ is
\[
\lambda_{\text{dB}} = \frac{h}{p},
\]
where $h = 2\pi\hbar$. For a thermal electron at room temperature
($E = (3/2)k_{B}T \approx 0.04\,\si{\electronvolt}$, $p =
\sqrt{2m_{e}E} \approx 1.1\times 10^{-25}\,\si{\kilogram\meter\per
\second}$), $\lambda_{\text{dB}} \approx 6\,\si{\nano\meter}$. Modern
transistors with channel lengths below this scale are unambiguously
in the mesoscopic regime; their design depends on quantum-mechanical
calculation rather than on classical drift-diffusion.

Two experimental results forced quantisation on physics before any
device used it, and both remain the working starting points for the
engineer. The photoelectric effect shows that light delivers energy in
quanta $h\nu$: below a threshold frequency no electrons are emitted at
any intensity, and above it the maximum electron kinetic energy rises
linearly with frequency at a slope fixed by Planck's constant
(Figure~\ref{fig:vol04ch12:photoelectric-stopping}). The blackbody
spectrum shows that the electromagnetic field's modes are quantised:
the measured radiance peaks and then falls toward short wavelength
(Figure~\ref{fig:vol04ch12:blackbody-curves}), where the classical
equipartition result diverges. The code in
\texttt{code/photon\_energy.py} carries the photon arithmetic and the
$1.24\,\text{eV}\cdot\si{\micro\meter}$ rule; the data in
\texttt{data/photoelectric\_stopping.csv} and
\texttt{data/blackbody\_spectrum.csv} reproduce the two figures.

\section{Photon, electron, semiconductor}\pathtag{core}

A \engterm{photon} is the quantum of the electromagnetic field. Its
energy is $E = h\nu = \hbar\omega$, where $\nu$ is the frequency. At
$\lambda = 633\,\si{\nano\meter}$ (red HeNe laser), $E = 1.96\,\si{
\electronvolt}$. At $\lambda = 1\,\si{\micro\meter}$ (near-infrared),
$E = 1.24\,\si{\electronvolt}$. The simple rule $E[\si{\electronvolt}]
= 1.24/\lambda[\si{\micro\meter}]$ is the working conversion the
engineer carries to bench experiments.

An \engterm{electron} in vacuum has rest mass $m_{e} = 9.109\times
10^{-31}\,\si{\kilogram}$ and charge $-e$. In a solid, the electron
moves through a periodic potential created by the atomic lattice; its
behaviour is described by Bloch's theorem, which gives the
wavefunction as a plane-wave envelope times a lattice-periodic
function. The effective mass $m^{*}$ describes the electron's
response to slowly-varying external forces and differs from the
free-electron mass: in silicon's conduction band, $m^{*}_{e} \approx
0.26\,m_{e}$; in GaAs, $m^{*}_{e} \approx 0.067\,m_{e}$. Effective
mass is the working content of every band-structure calculation in
engineering.

\begin{mastery}
The square-root density of states $g(E) \propto \sqrt{E - E_{c}}$ of
Figure~\ref{fig:vol04ch12:density-of-states} is the three-dimensional
result. Confining the carriers to a thin layer changes it. In a
quantum well (one dimension confined) the density of states becomes a
staircase of constant steps; in a quantum wire (two dimensions
confined) it acquires inverse-square-root spikes; in a quantum dot
(all three confined) it collapses to a set of delta functions, an
artificial atom. The design payoff is sharper gain spectra: the
quantum-well laser concentrates the available states near the band
edge, lowering threshold current and narrowing the emission line
relative to a bulk device. Quantum-dot films exploit the discrete
levels for narrow, size-tunable emission in displays. The same
confinement that raises the ground-state energy of a particle in a box
(treated in the exercises) sets the emission wavelength here.
\end{mastery}

A \engterm{semiconductor} is a material with a small energy gap
between its valence and conduction bands. At $T = 0$ the valence band
is fully occupied and the conduction band is empty; the material is
an insulator. At finite temperature, thermal excitation lifts some
electrons across the gap, leaving holes (effective positive charges)
in the valence band. The intrinsic carrier density is
\[
n_{i} = \sqrt{N_{c}N_{v}}\exp(-E_{g}/2k_{B}T),
\]
where $N_{c}$, $N_{v}$ are effective density of states in the
conduction and valence bands, and $E_{g}$ is the band gap. For
silicon at $300\,\si{\kelvin}$, $E_{g} = 1.12\,\si{\electronvolt}$
and $n_{i} \approx 10^{10}\,\si{\per\centi\meter\cubed}$; for GaAs,
$n_{i} \approx 2\times 10^{6}\,\si{\per\centi\meter\cubed}$; for
germanium, $n_{i} \approx 2\times 10^{13}\,\si{\per\centi\meter
\cubed}$. The band gap is the property that places a material in one
of three electrical classes
(Figure~\ref{fig:vol04ch12:energy-bands}); the band gaps, gap types,
effective masses, and intrinsic carrier densities used through this
chapter are collected in \texttt{data/semiconductor\_properties.csv}.
The reference work for these numbers and for the device physics that
follows is Sze and Ng \cite{text:sze-semiconductor}.

\input{volumes/04-energy/ch12-quantum-effects-engineers-use/figures/fig-energy-bands.tex}

\input{volumes/04-energy/ch12-quantum-effects-engineers-use/figures/fig-density-of-states.tex}

\engterm{Doping} replaces a fraction of the host atoms with impurities
of higher (n-type, e.g., phosphorus in silicon) or lower (p-type,
boron in silicon) valence. A doping density of $10^{18}\,\si{\per
\centi\meter\cubed}$ in silicon produces a free-electron density of
the same value, eight orders of magnitude above the intrinsic
density. The free-electron concentration is now set by the dopant
rather than by the thermal excitation; the carrier density is
\engterm{extrinsic}.

A \engterm{pn~junction} forms at the boundary between an n-doped
region and a p-doped region. The chemical-potential difference drives
electron diffusion from n to p and hole diffusion from p to n; the
charged regions left behind (positive ionised donors on the n side,
negative ionised acceptors on the p side) build up an electric field
opposing further diffusion. At equilibrium the diffusion and drift
currents cancel and a depletion region of width $\sim 100\,\si{\nano
\meter}$ at typical doping forms. The band edges bend across the
junction by the built-in potential while the Fermi level stays flat
(Figure~\ref{fig:vol04ch12:pn-junction-bands}); this single bent-band
picture, driven electrically or illuminated, is the object behind the
diode, the LED, the laser, and the solar cell.

\input{volumes/04-energy/ch12-quantum-effects-engineers-use/figures/fig-pn-junction-bands.tex}

Applying a voltage $V$ across the junction with the p side at higher
potential than the n side (\engterm{forward bias}) reduces the
barrier and exponentially increases the current; reversing the bias
slightly enlarges the barrier and the current saturates at a small
reverse-saturation value. The Shockley diode equation is
\[
I(V) = I_{s}\left[\exp(eV/k_{B}T) - 1\right],
\]
with $I_{s}$ the reverse-saturation current (typically $10^{-12}\,\si{
\ampere}$ for a small-signal silicon diode at room temperature). The
$26\,\si{\milli\volt}$ thermal voltage $k_{B}T/e$ at $300\,\si{\kelvin}$
is the working number for diode-circuit analysis; a $60\,\si{\milli
\volt}$-per-decade increase in current is the standard diode-curve
slope.

\section{LED, laser, photovoltaic cell}\pathtag{core}

The pn junction emits and absorbs photons at the band-gap energy
when electrons and holes recombine or are generated.

A \engterm{light-emitting diode} (LED) is a pn junction in a direct-
band-gap semiconductor, forward-biased. Electrons and holes injected
across the junction recombine radiatively, emitting photons at energy
$\hbar\omega \approx E_{g}$. Red LEDs use AlGaAs or AlGaInP
($E_{g} \approx 1.9\,\si{\electronvolt}$); green LEDs use GaP or
InGaN at modest indium content; blue and white LEDs use InGaN at
high indium content. Silicon and germanium are indirect-band-gap
semiconductors and do not emit light efficiently as LEDs; they are
not used for emission. The internal quantum efficiency of modern
LEDs exceeds $80\,\%$; the external efficiency (limited by light
extraction from the high-index semiconductor) is in the $40$ to
$70\,\%$ range with good encapsulation.

\input{volumes/04-energy/ch12-quantum-effects-engineers-use/figures/fig-led-laser-levels.tex}

A \engterm{laser diode} differs from an LED in that an optical cavity
is formed by cleaved end facets, which provide the feedback for
stimulated emission
(Figure~\ref{fig:vol04ch12:led-laser-levels}). The standard reference
for the photonics in this section is Saleh and Teich
\cite{text:saleh-teich-photonics}. Above the threshold current, stimulated emission
dominates spontaneous emission, producing coherent monochromatic
output. Modern telecom laser diodes at $1.55\,\si{\micro\meter}$ have
threshold currents of $\sim 20\,\si{\milli\ampere}$, output powers of
$10\,\si{\milli\watt}$ continuous, and linewidths below $10\,\si{
\mega\hertz}$ with distributed-feedback structures.

A \engterm{photovoltaic cell} (solar cell) is a pn junction in a
semiconductor, reverse-biased or in open circuit, illuminated. Photons
of energy above $E_{g}$ create electron-hole pairs in the depletion
region; the built-in field separates them, sending electrons to the
n side and holes to the p side, producing a photocurrent. The
short-circuit current scales with photon flux; the open-circuit
voltage approaches $E_{g}/e$ at low temperature and high illumination.

The Shockley-Queisser limit for a single-junction solar cell at
standard $1\,\text{sun}$ illumination (AM1.5G, $1000\,\si{\watt\per
\meter\squared}$) is $\sim 33\,\%$ at the optimum band gap of
$1.34\,\si{\electronvolt}$ (close to GaAs). Modern silicon cells at
$E_{g} = 1.12\,\si{\electronvolt}$ achieve $\sim 26\,\%$ in
laboratory record cells (current as of 2024); commercial silicon
modules reach $20$ to $23\,\%$. Multi-junction cells stacking
materials of different band gaps reach $\sim 47\,\%$ at concentrated
illumination; the cost is the additional materials and the
mechanical complexity of the concentrator system. The single-junction
ceiling and the location of its optimum band gap are shown in
Figure~\ref{fig:vol04ch12:shockley-queisser}.

\input{volumes/04-energy/ch12-quantum-effects-engineers-use/figures/fig-shockley-queisser.tex}

\begin{archetype}[Scaling and interface]
The LED, the laser diode, and the photovoltaic cell are the same pn
junction operated in three different regimes. Each is the interface
archetype made explicit: at the junction, electrons and holes
recombine to produce light or are separated by the built-in field to
produce current. The chapter's working content is that the device's
behaviour is set by the physics of the junction, scaled by the area
of the device and the temperature of operation.
\end{archetype}

\section{The transistor as a quantum device}\pathtag{standard}

The \engterm{metal-oxide-semiconductor field-effect transistor}
(MOSFET) is the standard device of digital electronics. A thin oxide
insulates a metal (or polysilicon) gate from a semiconductor channel
between two doped source and drain regions. Voltage on the gate
modulates the carrier density in the channel by capacitive coupling;
the source-to-drain current is the working output.

In the long-channel regime (channel length $\gg$ de~Broglie
wavelength), the MOSFET is well-described by the drift-diffusion
equations of classical semiconductor physics, with thresholds and
saturation behaviour captured by the standard Pao-Sah or Sah-Shockley
equations. In the short-channel regime, ballistic transport (the
electron crosses the channel without scattering), quantum tunnelling
through the oxide barrier, and quantum confinement of the channel
carriers become important. Modern $5\,\si{\nano\meter}$-process
nodes (TSMC N5, Samsung 5LPP, Intel 4) have channel lengths of $\sim
6$-$10\,\si{\nano\meter}$ and rely on quantum-mechanical simulations
during device design.

The \engterm{quantum tunnelling} through the gate oxide sets the
leakage current of every modern transistor. The tunnelling current
density through a barrier of height $\phi$ and thickness $d$ scales
as
\[
J_{\text{tunnel}} \sim \exp(-2d\sqrt{2m^{*}\phi}/\hbar).
\]
For SiO$_{2}$ with $\phi \approx 3.1\,\si{\electronvolt}$, doubling
$d$ reduces leakage by a factor of $\sim 10^{4}$; halving $d$
increases it by the same factor
(Figure~\ref{fig:vol04ch12:tunneling-well}). The script
\texttt{code/tunneling\_leakage.py} tabulates this exponential
sensitivity against oxide thickness.

\input{volumes/04-energy/ch12-quantum-effects-engineers-use/figures/fig-tunneling-well.tex} The scaling motivated the industry
transition from SiO$_{2}$ to high-$k$ dielectrics (HfO$_{2}$) around
2007, allowing the same effective capacitance with a thicker
physical layer and lower tunnelling.

A \engterm{flash memory} cell stores information in a floating gate
isolated from the channel by a thin oxide. Writing the cell tunnels
electrons through the oxide onto the floating gate; reading senses
the threshold shift the trapped charge produces; erasing reverses
the tunnelling. The cell's retention (the time over which the
trapped charge persists) is set by the same exponential tunnelling
formula and is $\sim 10$ years at room temperature with a typical
oxide thickness of $\sim 8\,\si{\nano\meter}$. The retention
degrades at elevated temperature and after many write-erase cycles
(the wear of the oxide); this is the working specification of every
solid-state storage device.

\section{MRI, electron microscopy, X-ray}\pathtag{standard}

Three imaging modalities at the engineering quantum scale are MRI
(magnetic resonance imaging), electron microscopy, and X-ray
imaging.

\engterm{MRI} uses the nuclear magnetic resonance of hydrogen nuclei
(protons) in tissue. In a static magnetic field $B_{0}$, the proton's
two spin states are split in energy by $\Delta E = \gamma\hbar B_{0}$,
where $\gamma = 2.675\times 10^{8}\,\si{\radian\per\second\per\tesla}$
is the gyromagnetic ratio of the proton. At $B_{0} = 1.5\,\si{\tesla}$,
$\Delta E$ corresponds to a Larmor frequency of $63.9\,\si{\mega
\hertz}$; at $3\,\si{\tesla}$, $127.7\,\si{\mega\hertz}$. The standard
MRI sequence applies a $90^{\circ}$ RF pulse at the Larmor frequency
to flip the equilibrium magnetisation into the transverse plane,
measures the free-induction decay or echo signal, and reconstructs
the image from spatial-frequency information encoded by gradient
fields. The spatial resolution is $\sim 1\,\si{\milli\meter}$ at
clinical field strengths and below $100\,\si{\micro\meter}$ at high
field for small-bore imaging.

\engterm{Electron microscopy} uses an electron beam at $10$ to
$300\,\si{\kilo\volt}$ accelerating voltage as the imaging probe.
The de~Broglie wavelength of a $100\,\si{\kilo\volt}$ electron is
$\sim 3.7\,\si{\pico\meter}$, far smaller than visible-light
wavelengths and capable in principle of resolving individual atoms.
Practical resolution is limited by lens aberrations (electron
optics suffers from spherical aberration that is much harder to
correct than in light optics) to $\sim 0.1\,\si{\nano\meter}$ with
aberration-corrected instruments. The transmission electron
microscope (TEM) images electrons that traverse a thin sample; the
scanning electron microscope (SEM) rasters a focused beam and detects
secondary electrons, giving surface-topology images of bulk samples.

\engterm{X-ray imaging} uses photons of energy $1$ to $200\,\si{
\kilo\electronvolt}$, with $\lambda$ from $0.01$ to $1\,\si{\nano
\meter}$, generated by accelerated electrons hitting a metal target
(bremsstrahlung and characteristic emission) or by synchrotron
radiation from relativistic electrons in a storage ring. Medical
X-ray imaging exploits differential absorption between bone and soft
tissue (bone's higher electron density absorbs more); X-ray
diffraction maps the crystal structure of materials; X-ray
photoelectron spectroscopy maps surface composition. The quantum
content is that the X-ray is a photon whose interaction probability
is set by the photoelectric absorption cross section, scaling roughly
as $Z^{4}/E^{3}$ in the diagnostic range.

\section{Worked examples}\pathtag{standard}

Three worked instances fix the chapter's daily arithmetic: a photon
energy, a diode operating point, and a noise budget.

\paragraph{Photon energy and the working rule.}
A fibre-optic transmitter runs at $\lambda = 1.55\,\si{\micro\meter}$.
The photon energy is $E = hc/\lambda$. Rather than carry constants, we
use the working rule $E[\si{\electronvolt}] = 1.24 / \lambda[\si{
\micro\meter}] = 1.24/1.55 = 0.80\,\si{\electronvolt}$. The momentum is
$p = h/\lambda = (6.63\times 10^{-34})/(1.55\times 10^{-6}) = 4.28\times
10^{-28}\,\si{\kilogram\meter\per\second}$, and the frequency is
$\nu = c/\lambda = 1.93\times 10^{14}\,\si{\hertz} = 193\,\si{\tera
\hertz}$. The $0.80\,\si{\electronvolt}$ photon energy is below
silicon's $1.12\,\si{\electronvolt}$ gap, which is why silicon is
transparent at telecom wavelengths and why InGaAs, not silicon, is the
photodiode material for fibre links. The arithmetic matches the table
produced by \texttt{code/photon\_energy.py}.

\paragraph{Diode operating point.}
A silicon diode with $I_{s} = 10^{-12}\,\si{\ampere}$ carries a forward
current $I = 1\,\si{\milli\ampere}$. The forward voltage follows from
inverting the Shockley equation: $V = V_{T}\ln(I/I_{s} + 1) \approx
(0.0259)\ln(10^{-3}/10^{-12}) = 0.0259 \times \ln(10^{9}) = 0.0259
\times 20.7 = 0.536\,\si{\volt}$. Raising the current by a factor of
ten to $10\,\si{\milli\ampere}$ adds $V_{T}\ln 10 = 0.0259 \times 2.303
= 60\,\si{\milli\volt}$, the standard decade slope. The forward drop of
a silicon diode is therefore near $0.6$ to $0.7\,\si{\volt}$ across the
ordinary range of operating currents, the working number for every
diode-circuit estimate.

\paragraph{Receiver noise budget.}
A photodiode receiving $P = 1\,\si{\micro\watt}$ at $\lambda = 850\,
\si{\nano\meter}$ with quantum efficiency $\eta = 0.8$ produces a
photocurrent $I = \eta e P \lambda / (hc)$. With $\lambda/(hc) = 1/(1.46
\,\si{\electronvolt}\cdot e) $, the responsivity $\eta e\lambda/(hc) =
0.8 \times 850/1240 = 0.55\,\si{\ampere\per\watt}$, so $I = 0.55\,\si{
\micro\ampere}$. The shot-noise current in a bandwidth $\Delta f = 100
\,\si{\mega\hertz}$ is $\sqrt{2eI\Delta f} = \sqrt{2 \times 1.6\times
10^{-19}\times 5.5\times 10^{-7}\times 10^{8}} = \sqrt{1.76\times
10^{-17}} = 4.2\,\si{\nano\ampere}$. The shot-noise-limited
signal-to-noise ratio is $0.55\,\si{\micro\ampere}/4.2\,\si{\nano
\ampere} \approx 130$, or $42\,\si{\decibel}$, before any Johnson noise
of the load resistor is added. The budget shows the design rule: at
this power the link is comfortably above the shot-noise floor, and the
amplifier's thermal noise, not the photon statistics, will set the
sensitivity.

\section{Failure: quantum-noise floors and the limits of classical design}\pathtag{core}

Quantum mechanics sets fundamental noise floors that no amount of
classical engineering can suppress. Three are operationally important
in modern engineering.

\engterm{Shot noise} arises from the discreteness of charge: a current
$I$ flowing through a junction shows fluctuations of variance
$\sigma_{I}^{2} = 2eI\Delta f$ over a measurement bandwidth $\Delta
f$, where $e$ is the electron charge. A photodiode receiving an
average optical power $P$ at wavelength $\lambda$ has photocurrent
$I = \eta eP\lambda/(hc)$ (with $\eta$ the quantum efficiency) and
shot noise of variance $2eI\Delta f$. The signal-to-noise ratio is
$I/\sqrt{2eI\Delta f} = \sqrt{I/(2e\Delta f)}$, scaling with the
square root of the photon flux. Shot noise dominates the noise budget
of every optical communications receiver, every astronomical
photometer, and every photon-counting experiment. The Monte Carlo
check in \texttt{code/shot\_noise\_mc.py} confirms that the photon
count is Poisson, so its standard deviation is the square root of the
mean.

\engterm{Johnson-Nyquist noise} (thermal noise) is the voltage
fluctuation across a resistor at temperature $T$: the open-circuit
RMS voltage is
\[
V_{n} = \sqrt{4k_{B}TR\Delta f},
\]
where $R$ is the resistance and $\Delta f$ is the bandwidth, the result
Johnson measured and Nyquist explained in 1928
\cite{paper:ch05-johnson-1928, paper:ch05-nyquist-1928}. At room
temperature ($T = 290\,\si{\kelvin}$) and $1\,\si{\kilo\hertz}$
bandwidth, a $50\,\si{\ohm}$ resistor produces $V_{n} = 28\,\si{\nano
\volt}$ RMS; a $1\,\si{\mega\ohm}$ resistor produces $4\,\si{\micro
\volt}$ RMS. Johnson noise sets the noise floor of every passive
electrical measurement; suppressing it requires either cooling the
resistor (cryogenic low-noise amplifiers in radio astronomy) or
reducing $R$ (low-impedance source matching), or both. The reciprocal
of the Boltzmann constant $k_{B} = 1.380\,649\times 10^{-23}\,\si{
\joule\per\kelvin}$ is now fixed exactly in the 2019 SI, and Johnson
noise is the working metrological link between voltage and
temperature.

\engterm{Quantum-limited amplifier noise} is the unavoidable noise an
amplifier adds to a quantum signal. The standard quantum limit (SQL)
for a phase-preserving linear amplifier is half a photon per mode at
each input; this contributes a noise temperature of $T_{q} =
\hbar\omega/(2k_{B})$. At a microwave frequency of $10\,\si{\giga
\hertz}$, $T_{q} \approx 0.24\,\si{\kelvin}$; below this, modern
quantum-limited parametric amplifiers operate at $T \sim T_{q}$. At
optical frequencies, $T_{q}$ is much larger than any practical
ambient temperature, so optical amplifiers are typically already
quantum-limited.

\begin{mastery}
The half-photon noise floor of a phase-preserving amplifier is not a
hardware limit; it follows from the commutation relations of the field
operators. A linear amplifier that multiplies a coherent input by gain
$G$ must add at least $(G-1)/2$ noise photons per mode at the output to
keep the amplified state physical, which at large gain is half a photon
referred to the input. The escape is to give up phase information: a
phase-sensitive amplifier, which amplifies one quadrature and
de-amplifies the other, can in principle add no noise to the amplified
quadrature. Squeezed-light interferometry uses exactly this trade to
push gravitational-wave detectors below the standard quantum limit in a
chosen quadrature, at the cost of added noise in the conjugate one. The
engineering content is that the quantum floor can be moved between
observables but not removed.
\end{mastery}

The quantum-noise floors are not the result of imperfect engineering;
they are the result of nature's quantum-mechanical structure. An
engineering design that requires signal-to-noise below the quantum
floor is impossible; the design must either reduce the noise
bandwidth, increase the signal power, or accept the quantum limit.

\begin{principle}[The quantum floor is the engineering floor]
Below the quantum-noise floor of the relevant signal (shot noise for
classical light, Johnson noise for thermal-electric measurements,
the standard quantum limit for amplification of coherent signals), no
classical engineering can recover the signal. The discipline is to
identify which noise floor governs the application and design the
system to operate within a factor of a few of that floor; designing
for less is over-engineering, designing for more is impossible.
\end{principle}

\begin{estimation}
\textbf{Question.} A consumer-grade CMOS image sensor with $1\,\si{
\micro\meter}$ pixel pitch and $2000\,e^{-}$ full-well capacity
images a scene at $1\,\si{\lux}$ illuminance with a $30\,\si{\milli
\second}$ exposure. Estimate the per-pixel photon shot noise in
electrons, and the achievable signal-to-noise ratio at full well.

\textbf{Estimate, before reading on.} State a factor-of-three-
confidence answer before reading the working.

\textbf{Working.} At $1\,\si{\lux}$, the photometric illuminance
corresponds to about $1.4\times 10^{-3}\,\si{\watt\per\meter
\squared}$ at the peak photopic wavelength. The pixel area is $10^{-12}\,
\si{\meter\squared}$. Photons per pixel per second: $1.4\times 10^{-3}
\times 10^{-12} / (h\nu) \approx 4\times 10^{6}\,\si{\per\second}$
at $\lambda = 550\,\si{\nano\meter}$ ($h\nu = 3.6\times 10^{-19}\,\si{
\joule}$). In a $30\,\si{\milli\second}$ exposure, $\sim 10^{5}$
photons fall on each pixel; with $\sim 50\,\%$ quantum efficiency and
$\sim 10\,\%$ lens transmission and obstruction at $f/2$, the pixel
collects $\sim 5000$ photoelectrons. This exceeds the $2000\,e^{-}$
full well: the pixel saturates. At full well, shot noise is $\sqrt{
2000}\approx 45$ electrons RMS; SNR is $2000/45 \approx 44$, or
$33\,\si{\decibel}$.

\textbf{Postmortem.} The order-of-magnitude estimate captures the
real working trade-off: in bright light a small-pixel sensor
saturates and SNR is set by shot noise at the full-well level. In
dim light (sub-lux) the pixel collects only tens or hundreds of
photoelectrons, SNR is at best $\sim 10$, and read-noise of
$\sim 1\,e^{-}$ becomes a meaningful contributor. The numerical
specifications are representative of smartphone cameras current as
of 2025.
\end{estimation}

\section{Project}\pathtag{core}

\begin{project}[Characterise the I-V curve of a diode]
Measure the current-voltage characteristic of a small-signal silicon
diode and identify the regions where the Shockley diode equation
holds and where it deviates.

\textbf{Materials.}
A small-signal silicon diode (1N4148 is the standard inexpensive
choice); a bench DC supply with current limit ($0$ to $5\,\si{\volt}$,
$10\,\si{\milli\ampere}$ limit); a multimeter capable of resolving
$0.1\,\si{\micro\ampere}$ current; a series resistor of $100\,\si{
\ohm}$; alligator clips; a logarithmic-scale plotting program.

\textbf{Procedure.}
\begin{enumerate}
  \item Wire the diode in series with the $100\,\si{\ohm}$ resistor
    and the bench supply; place the multimeter in series for current
    measurement.
  \item Sweep the supply voltage from $0$ to $1.0\,\si{\volt}$ in
    $25$-$\si{\milli\volt}$ steps; record the current at each step
    after waiting for thermal equilibrium ($\sim 5\,\si{\second}$).
  \item In the regime $V < 0.4\,\si{\volt}$, the current is below the
    multimeter's noise floor; flag these points as upper bounds.
  \item In the regime $0.4 < V < 0.7\,\si{\volt}$, the current
    increases exponentially; record carefully.
  \item In the regime $V > 0.7\,\si{\volt}$, series resistance of
    the diode (and the bench resistor) begins to dominate; the
    current departs from the exponential.
  \item Plot $\ln I$ versus $V$. The exponential regime appears as a
    straight line with slope $1/V_{T} = 1/(0.026\,\si{\volt})$ at
    room temperature.
  \item Extract the saturation current $I_{s}$ from the intercept.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Report.} A one-page report with the schematic, the
log-current-versus-voltage plot, the fitted slope and intercept, and
identification of the exponential, ohmic-series-resistance, and
saturation regions. Expected: the slope corresponds to a thermal
voltage within $\pm 10\,\%$ of $26\,\si{\milli\volt}$ at room
temperature; the saturation current is in the range $10^{-9}$ to
$10^{-13}\,\si{\ampere}$ for a small-signal diode.
Figure~\ref{fig:vol04ch12:diode-iv-semilog} shows the three regions a
clean sweep reveals on a semilog plot. A representative sweep is in
\texttt{data/diode\_iv\_sweep.csv}, and \texttt{code/diode\_iv\_fit.py}
performs the straight-line fit over the exponential region and recovers
$I_{s}$ and the thermal voltage; run against the sample data it returns
$26\,\si{\milli\volt}$ and $I_{s} \approx 1\,\si{\pico\ampere}$.

\end{project}

\subsection*{Calculation}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
A photon has wavelength $\lambda = 405\,\si{\nano\meter}$ (blue
laser). Compute its energy in electronvolts and joules, its momentum,
and its frequency.
\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Working rule: $E = 1.24/0.405 = 3.06\,\si{
\electronvolt}$, equal to $3.06 \times 1.602\times 10^{-19} = 4.90\times
10^{-19}\,\si{\joule}$. Momentum $p = h/\lambda = 6.63\times 10^{-34}/
4.05\times 10^{-7} = 1.64\times 10^{-27}\,\si{\kilogram\meter\per
\second}$. Frequency $\nu = c/\lambda = 3\times 10^{8}/4.05\times 10^{-7}
= 7.40\times 10^{14}\,\si{\hertz} = 740\,\si{\tera\hertz}$. These match
the first row of \texttt{code/photon\_energy.py}.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A silicon photodiode receives $1\,\si{\milli\watt}$ of light at
$\lambda = 850\,\si{\nano\meter}$. Compute the maximum possible
photocurrent (at $100\,\%$ quantum efficiency) and the shot noise of
the photocurrent in a $1\,\si{\mega\hertz}$ bandwidth.
\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Responsivity at unit efficiency is
$e\lambda/(hc) = \lambda[\si{\micro\meter}]/1.24 = 0.85/1.24 = 0.69\,\si{
\ampere\per\watt}$, so $I = 0.69\,\si{\ampere\per\watt}\times 10^{-3}\,
\si{\watt} = 0.69\,\si{\milli\ampere}$. Shot noise current $\sqrt{2eI
\Delta f} = \sqrt{2\times 1.6\times 10^{-19}\times 6.9\times 10^{-4}
\times 10^{6}} = \sqrt{2.2\times 10^{-16}} = 1.5\times 10^{-8}\,\si{
\ampere} = 15\,\si{\nano\ampere}$. The shot-noise-limited SNR is
$0.69\,\si{\milli\ampere}/15\,\si{\nano\ampere} \approx 4.6\times 10^{4}$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A silicon pn junction at $T = 300\,\si{\kelvin}$ has reverse-
saturation current $I_{s} = 5\times 10^{-12}\,\si{\ampere}$. Compute
the current at $V = 0.5\,\si{\volt}$, $V = 0.6\,\si{\volt}$, and
$V = 0.7\,\si{\volt}$ forward bias.
\paragraph{Solution sketch.} With $V_{T} = 0.0259\,\si{\volt}$,
$I = I_{s}\exp(V/V_{T})$. At $0.5\,\si{\volt}$: $\exp(0.5/0.0259) =
\exp(19.3) = 2.4\times 10^{8}$, so $I = 1.2\,\si{\milli\ampere}$. At
$0.6\,\si{\volt}$: $\exp(23.2) = 1.2\times 10^{10}$, $I = 58\,\si{\milli
\ampere}$. At $0.7\,\si{\volt}$: $\exp(27.0) = 5.6\times 10^{11}$,
$I = 2.8\,\si{\ampere}$. Each $0.1\,\si{\volt}$ multiplies the current
by $\exp(0.1/0.0259) \approx 47$, close to two decades; in a real diode
series resistance caps the $0.7\,\si{\volt}$ value well below
$2.8\,\si{\ampere}$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Compute the Larmor frequency of a proton in a $1.5\,\si{\tesla}$ MRI
magnet, given $\gamma_{p}/(2\pi) = 42.58\,\si{\mega\hertz\per\tesla}$.
\paragraph{Solution sketch.} $f = (\gamma_{p}/2\pi)B_{0} = 42.58\,\si{
\mega\hertz\per\tesla}\times 1.5\,\si{\tesla} = 63.9\,\si{\mega\hertz}$.
At $3\,\si{\tesla}$ the same proportionality gives $127.7\,\si{\mega
\hertz}$. The RF coil must be tuned to this frequency, which is why a
$1.5\,\si{\tesla}$ and a $3\,\si{\tesla}$ scanner use different coils.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Compute the Johnson-Nyquist noise voltage of a $10\,\si{\kilo\ohm}$
resistor at room temperature in a $1\,\si{\kilo\hertz}$ bandwidth, and
the equivalent noise current in a $1\,\si{\mega\ohm}$ resistor.
\paragraph{Solution sketch.} $V_{n} = \sqrt{4k_{B}TR\Delta f}$. With
$4k_{B}T = 1.6\times 10^{-20}\,\si{\joule}$ at $290\,\si{\kelvin}$:
$V_{n} = \sqrt{1.6\times 10^{-20}\times 10^{4}\times 10^{3}} = \sqrt{1.6
\times 10^{-13}} = 4.0\times 10^{-7}\,\si{\volt} = 0.40\,\si{\micro
\volt}$. The noise current of a resistor is $I_{n} = V_{n}/R = \sqrt{4
k_{B}T\Delta f/R}$; for $1\,\si{\mega\ohm}$, $V_{n} = \sqrt{1.6\times
10^{-20}\times 10^{6}\times 10^{3}} = 4.0\,\si{\micro\volt}$ and
$I_{n} = 4.0\,\si{\micro\volt}/10^{6}\,\si{\ohm} = 4.0\,\si{\femto
\ampere}$. Larger resistance gives more noise voltage but less noise
current.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Compute the de~Broglie wavelength of an electron accelerated through
$100\,\si{\kilo\volt}$, neglecting relativistic correction.
Recompute with the relativistic correction and report the error of
the non-relativistic calculation.
\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Non-relativistic: $\lambda = h/\sqrt{2m_{e}
eV}$. With $eV = 1.6\times 10^{-14}\,\si{\joule}$, $\sqrt{2m_{e}eV} =
\sqrt{2\times 9.11\times 10^{-31}\times 1.6\times 10^{-14}} = 1.71\times
10^{-22}\,\si{\kilogram\meter\per\second}$, so $\lambda = 6.63\times
10^{-34}/1.71\times 10^{-22} = 3.88\,\si{\pico\meter}$. Relativistic:
$p = \sqrt{2m_{e}eV(1 + eV/2m_{e}c^{2})}$ with $m_{e}c^{2} = 511\,\si{
\kilo\electronvolt}$, so the correction factor is $\sqrt{1 + 100/1022} =
\sqrt{1.098} = 1.048$, giving $\lambda = 3.88/1.048 = 3.70\,\si{\pico
\meter}$. The non-relativistic value is high by about $4.8\,\%$, which
matters at electron-microscope accuracy.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Derivation}\pathtag{standard}

\begin{exercise}
Derive the Shockley diode equation by setting up the drift-diffusion
current under forward bias, assuming low injection and quasi-neutral
regions. State the assumptions explicitly.
\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Assume: abrupt junction, complete
ionisation, low-level injection, no recombination in the depletion
region, and Boltzmann statistics. The applied bias $V$ raises the
minority-carrier concentration at the depletion edge by the factor
$\exp(eV/k_{B}T)$ over equilibrium (the law of the junction): $n_{p}(0)
= n_{p0}\exp(eV/k_{B}T)$. In the quasi-neutral region minority carriers
obey the steady diffusion equation $D\,d^{2}\Delta n/dx^{2} = \Delta n/
\tau$, whose solution decays exponentially with diffusion length
$L = \sqrt{D\tau}$. The diffusion current at the edge is $J = eD\,
d\Delta n/dx = (eD/L)n_{p0}[\exp(eV/k_{B}T) - 1]$. Summing the electron
and hole contributions and identifying $I_{s} = eA(D_{n}n_{p0}/L_{n} +
D_{p}p_{n0}/L_{p})$ gives $I = I_{s}[\exp(eV/k_{B}T) - 1]$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Derive the Johnson-Nyquist formula $V_{n} = \sqrt{4k_{B}TR\Delta f}$
by considering a resistor in thermal equilibrium connected to a
transmission line, applying equipartition to each mode of the line.
\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Connect two equal resistors $R$ by a
lossless transmission line of length $\ell$ and characteristic
impedance $R$ (matched, no reflections). The line supports standing-wave
modes spaced $\Delta\nu = c/2\ell$, so the number of modes in a band
$\Delta f$ is $2\ell\Delta f/c$. In thermal equilibrium each mode
carries energy $k_{B}T$ by equipartition (the classical Rayleigh-Jeans
limit, valid for $h\nu \ll k_{B}T$). Power flowing one way along the
line is the mode energy times mode density times speed: $P = k_{B}T
\Delta f$. In equilibrium each resistor delivers this power to the
other. Modelling the noise source as an EMF $V_{n}$ behind $R$ driving
the matched load $R$, the delivered power is $V_{n}^{2}/4R$. Setting
$V_{n}^{2}/4R = k_{B}T\Delta f$ gives $V_{n}^{2} = 4k_{B}TR\Delta f$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Derive the shot-noise spectral density $S_{I} = 2eI$ for a
unidirectional current of mean $I$ comprised of independent
elementary-charge transits, by treating the current as a Poisson
sum of impulses.
\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Model the current as $i(t) = \sum_{k}
e\,h(t - t_{k})$, with transit times $t_{k}$ a Poisson process of rate
$\lambda$ and $h$ a normalised pulse shape, $\int h\,dt = 1$. The mean
current is $I = e\lambda$. Carson's theorem (or the Wiener-Khinchin
result for a shot-noise process) gives the one-sided power spectral
density $S_{I}(f) = 2e^{2}\lambda|H(f)|^{2}$, where $H$ is the Fourier
transform of $h$. For pulses short compared with the measurement
bandwidth, $|H(f)|^{2} \to 1$, so $S_{I} = 2e^{2}\lambda = 2eI$. The
variance in a bandwidth $\Delta f$ is $\sigma_{I}^{2} = S_{I}\Delta f =
2eI\Delta f$. The independence of transits, the Poisson assumption, is
what makes the variance equal $e$ times the mean count, the result the
Monte Carlo in \texttt{code/shot\_noise\_mc.py} confirms.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Derive the Shockley-Queisser limit for a single-junction solar cell
by combining the detailed-balance condition (radiative recombination
balanced by absorption) with the blackbody spectrum at the
operating temperature.
\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Treat the cell as a body that absorbs
every photon above the gap $E_{g}$ from the $5800\,\si{\kelvin}$ solar
spectrum and re-emits as a $300\,\si{\kelvin}$ blackbody whose chemical
potential equals $eV$. The net current is $J(V) = e[\,\Phi_{\text{sun}}(
E_{g}) - \Phi_{\text{emit}}(E_{g}, V)\,]$, where $\Phi$ are the photon
fluxes above the gap. The short-circuit current is the absorbed solar
photon flux above $E_{g}$; the open-circuit voltage is set when emission
balances absorption. Maximising $J(V)\cdot V$ over the band gap balances
two losses: photons below $E_{g}$ are lost (favours small gap) and
photon energy above $E_{g}$ thermalises to $E_{g}$ (favours large gap).
The maximum of the product, evaluated against the AM1.5G spectrum,
peaks near $33\,\%$ at $E_{g} \approx 1.34\,\si{\electronvolt}$, the
curve of Figure~\ref{fig:vol04ch12:shockley-queisser}.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Estimation}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the minimum number of photons per second that the dark-
adapted human eye can detect, given typical psychophysical
measurements. State the consequent quantum efficiency of the visual
system.
\paragraph{Reference estimate.} The classic Hecht-Shlaer-Pirenne
threshold experiment found that a flash containing roughly $50$ to
$150$ photons at the cornea is seen about half the time. Allowing for
reflection at the cornea ($\sim 4\,\%$ lost), absorption in the ocular
media ($\sim 50\,\%$ lost), and the fraction missing the rod outer
segments, only about $5$ to $14$ photons are actually absorbed by rods.
The rods themselves are near single-photon detectors, so the
inefficiency is mostly delivery, not transduction: the photoreceptor
quantum efficiency is of order tens of percent while the whole-eye
efficiency from corneal photons is only $\sim 10\,\%$. The operational
consequence is that vision at threshold is shot-noise limited; the
flicker a dark-adapted observer sees is the Poisson statistics of a
handful of photons.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the temperature at which the intrinsic carrier concentration
of silicon equals the doping density of $10^{15}\,\si{\per\centi
\meter\cubed}$, the conventional moderate-doping level. State the
operational consequence for high-temperature semiconductor electronics.
\paragraph{Reference estimate.} Set $n_{i}(T) = \sqrt{N_{c}N_{v}}\exp(
-E_{g}/2k_{B}T) = 10^{15}\,\si{\per\centi\meter\cubed}$, with the
density of states scaling as $T^{3/2}$. Solving numerically (the
bisection in \texttt{code/intrinsic\_carrier.py}) gives $T \approx
600\,\si{\kelvin}$, about $330\,{}^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$. Above this
temperature the thermally generated intrinsic carriers exceed the
dopants, the junction loses its designed asymmetry, and diodes and
transistors stop working: leakage rises sharply and the device can run
away thermally. This is why ordinary silicon is rated to junction
temperatures around $150\,{}^{\circ}\mathrm{C}$ with margin, and why
wide-gap materials such as SiC and GaN are chosen for hot or
high-voltage power electronics, where their larger $E_{g}$ pushes the
crossover hundreds of degrees higher.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the tunnelling-current density through a $2\,\si{\nano
\meter}$-thick SiO$_{2}$ gate oxide at $V = 1\,\si{\volt}$ bias.
State the consequent leakage current density of a $50\,\si{\nano
\meter}\times 50\,\si{\nano\meter}$ MOSFET gate.
\paragraph{Reference estimate.} Direct-tunnelling models and measured
data for a $2\,\si{\nano\meter}$ SiO$_{2}$ at $1\,\si{\volt}$ put the
current density at roughly $1$ to $10\,\si{\ampere\per\centi\meter
\squared}$ (order of magnitude; the exact value is exponentially
sensitive to thickness, as \texttt{code/tunneling\_leakage.py} shows).
Take $J \approx 1\,\si{\ampere\per\centi\meter\squared}$. The gate area
is $50\times 50 = 2500\,\si{\nano\meter\squared} = 2.5\times 10^{-11}
\,\si{\centi\meter\squared}$, so the per-gate leakage is $J\times A
\approx 2.5\times 10^{-11}\,\si{\ampere} = 25\,\si{\pico\ampere}$.
Across $10^{9}$ gates on a chip this sums to tens of milliamperes of
static leakage, the standby-power problem that drove the move to
high-$k$ dielectrics.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the dose (in milliSieverts) absorbed by a patient during a
chest X-ray. State assumptions about beam energy, time, and tissue
penetration.
\paragraph{Reference estimate.} A posterior-anterior chest film uses a
beam of roughly $120\,\si{\kilo\volt}$ peak, a few milliampere-seconds
of tube current, and a source-to-image distance near $1.8\,\si{\meter}$.
The effective dose tabulated for this examination is about $0.02\,\si{
\milli\sievert}$, with the lateral view adding a comparable amount.
Sanity check: $0.02\,\si{\milli\sievert}$ is a few days of natural
background radiation (background is $\sim 2$ to $3\,\si{\milli\sievert}$
per year), which matches the clinical rule of thumb that a single chest
radiograph carries negligible stochastic risk. A chest CT, by contrast,
delivers $\sim 5$ to $7\,\si{\milli\sievert}$, two-plus years of
background, the comparison that governs the order against the
information gained.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Simulation}\pathtag{mastery}

\begin{exercise}
Implement a one-dimensional Poisson-Schr\"odinger solver for the
electron density in a pn junction with arbitrary doping profile.
Verify against the depletion-approximation analytical solution for
a uniform-doping abrupt junction.
\paragraph{Model-answer sketch.} Discretise position on a grid. Iterate
two coupled equations to self-consistency: Poisson, $d^{2}\phi/dx^{2} =
-\rho(\phi)/\varepsilon$ with $\rho = e(p - n + N_{D}^{+} - N_{A}^{-})$
and Boltzmann (or Fermi-Dirac) carrier densities $n,p$ as functions of
the local potential; and, in the quantum-corrected version,
Schr\"odinger for the confined states feeding back into $n$. Use a
Gummel or Newton outer loop with damping to converge $\phi$. Verify by
turning off the quantum term and comparing against the
depletion-approximation result for an abrupt junction: depletion width
$W = \sqrt{2\varepsilon V_{bi}(N_{A}+N_{D})/(eN_{A}N_{D})}$ and the
parabolic potential drop across it. Agreement to a few percent away
from the junction edges confirms the solver; the quantum correction
then shows the carrier density pushed back from the oxide interface, the
effect that reduces gate capacitance in scaled devices.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Implement a Monte Carlo simulation of shot noise on a photodiode at
mean photon-arrival rate $\lambda$ photons per second over a $1\,\si{
\milli\second}$ measurement interval. Verify that the variance of
the photon count equals the mean (Poisson statistic) and identify
the regime in which the Gaussian approximation begins to be useful.
\paragraph{Model-answer sketch.} The reference implementation is
\texttt{code/shot\_noise\_mc.py}. Draw many trials of the count in the
window, each a Poisson deviate of mean $N = \lambda\,t$, and report the
sample mean and variance. The simulation returns variance/mean within a
percent of $1$ for every mean tested, confirming the Poisson statistic
and hence shot-noise standard deviation $\sqrt{N}$. Overlaying a normal
density of the same mean and variance, the agreement is poor for
$N \lesssim 5$ (the distribution is visibly skewed and discrete) and
good for $N \gtrsim 10$ to $20$, the practical onset of the Gaussian
approximation. The takeaway for the bench: at high photon counts shot
noise is Gaussian and adds in quadrature with other noise; at low counts
the Poisson skew matters and photon-counting statistics must be used.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Design}\pathtag{standard}

\begin{exercise}
Design a silicon-photodiode-based receiver for a $100\,\si{\mega
\bit\per\second}$ optical link at $\lambda = 850\,\si{\nano\meter}$.
Specify the photodiode area, quantum efficiency, transimpedance-
amplifier gain, and the achievable shot-noise-limited sensitivity.
\paragraph{Model-answer sketch.} Bandwidth requirement $\sim 70\,\si{
\mega\hertz}$ sets a small junction (capacitance below a few picofarad),
so a $\sim 100\,\si{\micro\meter}$-diameter device. Pick $\eta \approx
0.8$ at $850\,\si{\nano\meter}$ (responsivity $0.55\,\si{\ampere\per
\watt}$). A transimpedance gain of $\sim 10\,\si{\kilo\ohm}$ converts a
$\si{\micro\ampere}$ signal to tens of millivolts while keeping the
amplifier noise below the photocurrent shot noise. For a target
bit-error rate of $10^{-9}$ ($Q \approx 6$), the required received
power is set by demanding signal current $\geq Q$ times the total noise.
At $\Delta f = 70\,\si{\mega\hertz}$ the shot-noise floor at $1\,\si{
\micro\watt}$ received is a few tens of nanoamperes, giving a
shot-noise-limited sensitivity in the $-30$ to $-35\,\text{dBm}$
range; in practice the transimpedance resistor's Johnson noise,
not shot noise, sets the floor, which is why the gain is chosen as high
as the bandwidth allows.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Design a small-signal silicon diode for use as a temperature sensor.
Specify the forward bias current and the temperature-dependence
sensitivity, and identify the calibration procedure that converts
forward voltage to absolute temperature.
\paragraph{Model-answer sketch.} Drive the diode at a constant forward
current (a few tens of microampere to a milliampere) from a current
source, so the measured forward voltage tracks temperature. At fixed
current the forward voltage falls by about $2\,\si{\milli\volt\per
\kelvin}$ over the ordinary range, the dominant term coming from the
temperature dependence of $I_{s}$ through the band gap, not from the
explicit $V_{T}$ factor. The relation is close to linear over a
hundred-degree span, so a two-point calibration (ice point and a known
elevated temperature) fixes the slope and intercept. For higher
accuracy, biasing at two currents and taking the difference
$\Delta V = V_{T}\ln(I_{1}/I_{2})$ gives a voltage proportional to
absolute temperature that depends only on the current ratio and
fundamental constants, the principle of the silicon bandgap temperature
reference.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Design a single-junction solar cell architecture (substrate, doping,
junction depth, top contact, anti-reflection coating) for $\eta \ge
20\,\%$ at $1\,\text{sun}$ AM1.5G illumination. State the
manufacturing constraints and the failure modes that limit the cell
lifetime.
\paragraph{Model-answer sketch.} A monocrystalline silicon wafer
($E_{g} = 1.12\,\si{\electronvolt}$, just below the Shockley-Queisser
optimum of Figure~\ref{fig:vol04ch12:shockley-queisser}) with a
lightly doped p-type base ($\sim 10^{16}\,\si{\per\centi\meter\cubed}$)
and a shallow heavily doped n$^{+}$ emitter ($\sim 0.3\,\si{\micro
\meter}$ deep) reaches $20\,\%$ readily; high-efficiency designs add a
back surface field and passivated contacts. The junction must be shallow
so blue photons absorbed near the surface reach the field before
recombining. A textured surface plus a $\sim 75\,\si{\nano\meter}$
silicon-nitride anti-reflection and passivation layer cuts reflection
and surface recombination. The top contact is a narrow metal grid
trading shading loss against series resistance. Lifetime is limited by
encapsulant yellowing, moisture ingress corroding the metallisation,
thermal-cycling fatigue of the solder joints, and potential-induced
degradation; the cell semiconductor itself outlasts the module
packaging.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Diagnosis and reverse engineering}\pathtag{standard}

\begin{exercise}
A photodiode measurement shows a dark current that exceeds the
specified shot-noise floor by an order of magnitude. Identify three
candidate causes (excessive bias voltage causing impact ionisation,
surface contamination at the depletion-region edge, thermal
generation at junction defects) and the supplementary measurement
that distinguishes them.
\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Sweep the reverse bias: impact ionisation
shows a sharp super-linear rise of dark current as the breakdown voltage
is approached, the other two do not. Sweep temperature: thermal
generation at defects follows the $n_{i}(T)$ or $\sqrt{n_{i}}$
activation with a near-half-gap activation energy on an Arrhenius plot;
surface leakage has a weaker, often near-ohmic temperature dependence
and tracks humidity and surface treatment. So a current-versus-bias
curve isolates impact ionisation, and a current-versus-temperature
Arrhenius plot separates bulk thermal generation (half-gap slope) from
surface contamination (shallow slope).
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A MOSFET in a digital circuit has a gate-leakage current $100\times$
the specified value at room temperature, even though the gate-oxide
thickness was measured correctly during fabrication. Identify two
candidate causes (gate-oxide defects creating localised tunnelling
paths, ionic contamination of the oxide producing space-charge
modulation) and the failure-analysis technique that distinguishes
them.
\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Localised defect paths give leakage that is
spotty across nominally identical devices, often shows abrupt
soft-breakdown steps in time (stress-induced leakage current), and
appears as discrete hot spots under emission microscopy or conductive
atomic-force microscopy. Ionic contamination (mobile $\mathrm{Na^{+}}$,
$\mathrm{K^{+}}$) shifts the threshold voltage with bias-temperature
stress and shows hysteresis and drift in capacitance-voltage curves; a
triangular-voltage-sweep or bias-temperature-stress measurement reveals
the mobile-charge density. So spatial emission microscopy points to
discrete defects, while bias-temperature-stress C-V hysteresis points to
ionic contamination.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Failure analysis}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
A laser diode used in a fibre-optic transmitter fails after $10\,000$
hours of operation, less than $10\,\%$ of its rated lifetime. Identify
the candidate failure modes (catastrophic optical damage at the front
facet, dark-line defects in the active region, ohmic-contact
degradation) and the diagnostic test for each.
\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Catastrophic optical damage is sudden and
irreversible: the light output collapses in one step, and facet
inspection (optical or electron microscopy) shows a melted or cratered
facet. Dark-line defects degrade gradually: the threshold current
creeps up over hours of operation, electroluminescence imaging shows
growing dark lines or spots in the active stripe, and the failure
tracks operating current density. Ohmic-contact degradation raises the
series resistance: the forward voltage at fixed current rises over
time while the slope efficiency above threshold changes little, and a
four-point I-V measurement isolates the contact resistance. So a
light-output trace (step versus creep), facet imaging, and a forward
I-V over life separate the three.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A photodiode's responsivity drops by $40\,\%$ after exposure to
$100\,\si{\kilo\gray}$ of ionising radiation. Identify the
mechanism (radiation-induced defects in the depletion region) and
the design strategy that would improve radiation tolerance.
\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Displacement damage and ionising dose
create deep-level traps in and near the depletion region; these act as
recombination centres that capture photogenerated carriers before they
are collected, lowering the collection efficiency and hence the
responsivity, while also raising dark current. The design responses are
to shrink the carrier path so collection beats recombination (a
thinner active region or a stronger drift field), to choose a more
radiation-tolerant material or doping, to add shielding for the expected
dose, and to anneal the device (mild heating) where the application
allows, since some defects are removed thermally. The trade is collection
volume and quantum efficiency against radiation hardness.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A high-resolution MRI scan shows a uniform $5\,\%$ signal-intensity
gradient across the field of view, undegraded after annual
calibration. Identify the candidate causes (gradient-coil
miscalibration, B$_{0}$-field inhomogeneity, RF-coil non-uniformity)
and the supplementary scan that distinguishes them.
\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Image a uniform phantom. RF-coil
non-uniformity (the $B_{1}$ field) produces a smooth intensity shading
that changes when the coil is swapped or when a transmit-field map
($B_{1}$ map) is acquired, and it depends on flip angle; this is the
most common cause of a smooth gradient. $B_{0}$ inhomogeneity distorts
geometry and shifts frequency: a field map or a shift of the readout
gradient polarity moves the artefact, and it worsens at higher field and
near tissue interfaces. Gradient-coil miscalibration scales spatial
position: a phantom of known dimensions images with the wrong size or a
position-dependent intensity that tracks the gradient axis and reverses
with gradient polarity. So a uniform-phantom scan plus a $B_{1}$ map, a
$B_{0}$ field map, and a geometry check on a ruler phantom separate the
three.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Judgment}\pathtag{standard}

\begin{exercise}
A research group must choose between a silicon photodiode array and
a silicon avalanche photodiode for low-light fluorescence detection.
Identify the trade-off in terms of quantum efficiency, dark noise,
gain stability, and operating-voltage requirements.
\paragraph{Solution sketch.} The plain photodiode array has high
quantum efficiency, unity gain, low and stable dark current, and runs at
a few volts; its limit is that at very low light the readout-amplifier
noise dominates the small signal. The avalanche photodiode supplies
internal gain (tens to hundreds), lifting the signal above amplifier
noise, but the avalanche multiplication adds excess noise (a noise
factor that grows with gain), the gain is steeply temperature- and
bias-dependent so it needs a stabilised high-voltage supply (tens to
hundreds of volts), and its dark current is amplified too. So choose the
avalanche device when the light is faint enough that amplifier noise
would otherwise dominate and the system can hold the bias stable;
choose the plain array when the light is adequate and stability,
simplicity, and low operating voltage matter more.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A consulting client must decide between a Si and a GaAs photovoltaic
cell for a satellite installation. Identify the trade-off in terms of
efficiency, radiation hardness, cost, and weight.
\paragraph{Solution sketch.} GaAs (and III-V multi-junction cells built
on it) sits at the Shockley-Queisser optimum, so beginning-of-life
efficiency is higher (single-junction GaAs near $28\,\%$, triple-junction
above $30\,\%$, against silicon's $\sim 20$ to $26\,\%$), and the direct
gap lets a thin absorber tolerate displacement damage better, so the
end-of-life efficiency after years of proton and electron dose is
markedly higher; the cells are also light per watt. The cost is high:
III-V epitaxy on germanium substrates is far more expensive than
silicon. On the ground that price wins and silicon dominates; in orbit,
where launch mass and end-of-life power per area dominate the cost
function, the higher efficiency and radiation hardness of GaAs justify
the premium, which is why most spacecraft fly III-V cells. The decision
turns on whether dollars per watt or watts per kilogram after
irradiation governs the system.
\end{exercise}
