\caption[Optical coherence tomography]{Optical coherence tomography is a
low-coherence Michelson interferometer read for depth. A broadband source, chosen
for a short coherence length of a few micrometres, is split into a reference arm
ending at a scanning mirror and a sample arm that reaches into tissue. Light
returning from a depth inside the tissue interferes with the reference light only
when the two path lengths match to within the coherence length, so the short
coherence that ruins a length-measuring interferometer here selects a thin depth
slice. Scanning the reference arm sweeps the matched depth through the sample and
maps reflectivity against depth, the axial A-scan; scanning the beam laterally
stacks A-scans into a cross-sectional image. The depth resolution is set by the
source bandwidth through the coherence length, not by any lens, which is why OCT
resolves micrometre-scale retinal layers that no confocal microscope reaches at
that working distance.}
\label{fig:vol04ch11:oct-system}
\end{figure}
