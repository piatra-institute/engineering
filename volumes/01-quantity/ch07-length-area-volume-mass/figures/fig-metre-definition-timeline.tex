\caption[Three definitions of the metre, 1799 to present]{The three
definitions of the metre over its history. The platinum-iridium
prototype, deposited at the Archives nationales in 1799 and
recognised by the 1st CGPM in 1889, served for 161 years; the
krypton-86 wavelength replaced it in 1960 (11th CGPM), defining
the metre as $1{,}650{,}763.73$ wavelengths of the orange-red
transition; the 17th CGPM (Resolution 1, 1983) fixed the speed of
light at $c = 299{,}792{,}458\,\si{\meter\per\second}$ exactly,
making the metre the distance light travels in $1/299{,}792{,}458$
of a second. The horizontal bars at the top show the approximate
relative standard uncertainty achievable for a one-metre
realisation under each definition: the prototype was limited to
about $10^{-4}$ in the early period and about $10^{-7}$ once
bath-comparison protocols were in routine use; the krypton
wavelength reached about $10^{-8}$; the speed-of-light definition
reaches about $10^{-11}$ with a modern iodine-stabilised laser.
Each redefinition was triggered when a better physical realisation
existed than the artefact or transition in force. Source: editors'
synthesis of BIPM SI Brochure \cite{std:bipm-si2019} and CGPM
resolution archives.}
