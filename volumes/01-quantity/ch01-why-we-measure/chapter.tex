\noindent
We begin with measurement because every later engineering act depends on
a number that can be checked, repeated, and communicated. Before we
model, design, build, or repair, we measure.

\section{What measurement is for}\pathtag{core}

Measurement turns a private observation into a public engineering claim.
Once a quantity has been measured, it can be compared, reproduced,
communicated, archived, contracted, diagnosed, and calibrated. A
quantity that has not been measured can only be felt.

Older crafts carried quantities in trained bodies, tools, proportions,
marks, sounds, and repeated practice. The smith's judgement of iron, the
mason's judgement of stone, and the millwright's judgement of timber
could be precise in practice. What they could not always do was travel
cleanly through a drawing, contract, calibration certificate, or failure
investigation. A craft's knowledge often died with its last practitioner,
or survived in mute artifacts that the next generation had to
reverse-engineer.

Engineering does not have that luxury. A bridge in Pisa must take its
designed load whether the engineer who designed it is alive or dead. A
drug at $5 \,\si{\milli\gram}$ must be $5 \,\si{\milli\gram}$ whether
it is dispensed in S\~ao Paulo or Stockholm. A satellite in geostationary
orbit must speak to a ground station whose operator has never met its
builder. None of those is possible without an act that produces a number
which a stranger, on the other side of the world or a century later,
can reconstruct.

A recorded number is what lets the work leave the hands of its first
maker.

We can list the work measurement does:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \engterm{Comparison}. Two beams, two batches, two designs, two
    suppliers. Measurement turns ``this one is better'' from a private
    impression into a public claim.
  \item \engterm{Reproduction}. A drawing without numbers can only be
    copied by the original artisan. With numbers, it can be made
    twice.
  \item \engterm{Communication}. Two engineers in different rooms work
    the same problem when they share a measurement; they argue past
    each other when they do not.
  \item \engterm{Memory}. A datasheet from 1972 still tells today's
    engineer what a thermostat does, because the datasheet's numbers
    carry units the engineer can read.
  \item \engterm{Contract}. When a manufacturer promises a tolerance,
    the customer can hold them to it because the tolerance is
    measurable.
  \item \engterm{Diagnosis}. A measurement that disagrees with
    prediction is a fault report. Without measurement, faults are
    mysteries.
  \item \engterm{Calibration}. A measurement is what a meter is
    \emph{for}, and a meter that is not calibrated is decoration.
\end{itemize}

Intuition can start each task; measurement is what lets another engineer
audit it.

The rest of Volume I turns this discipline into working habits: units,
calibration, traceability, error propagation, sensors, instruments,
statistics, and estimation. Volume II then gives those quantities
mathematical form, and Volumes III onwards apply the mathematics to the
world.

\begin{archetype}[Scaling]
Engineering decisions span enormous magnitudes. A single chapter may
need quantities ranging from the diameter of a transistor channel,
$\sim 10^{-9} \,\si{\meter}$, to the wingspan of a long-haul aircraft,
$\sim 10^{2} \,\si{\meter}$, to the orbit of a satellite,

\end{archetype}

\section{The impossibility of unmeasured engineering}\pathtag{core}

Consider a routine event: a foundation pour at a mid-sized construction
site. A truck arrives at 0830 with several cubic metres of ready-mix
concrete. The site engineer signs for it on a delivery slip that names
the mix design, the slump, the specified compressive strength, the
chloride content, the time of mixing, and the truck's identification.
Each entry is a number tied to a physical quantity. The slump comes
from a metal cone the carpenter knows; the strength is an MPa figure
that will be verified at 7 and 28 days from cylinders cast on site.

Without the numbers, the delivery slip no longer identifies an
engineering material. The truck could be carrying concrete that has sat
in the mixer for two hours and has lost its workability, a mix specified
for an inland slab dispatched to a coastal job where chloride ingress
will rust the rebar within a decade, a batch with the wrong cement type
for the soil's sulphate content, or the right concrete for the wrong
building. Each of those is concrete that someone judged to be good
without measuring it.

This pattern repeats in every engineering domain. A pharmacist preparing
a sterile dose without weighing the active ingredient cannot defend the
dose. A welder qualifying a joint without a procedure that records
voltage, current, travel speed, and shielding gas flow cannot reproduce
the weld. A network operator who reconfigures a router without recording
the change has chosen to forget what they did. In each case the work
may, by luck, be correct. None of it is engineering in the sense the
discipline requires.

Many engineering failures include a missing, ignored, or misread
measurement. We will study Tay Bridge, Tacoma Narrows, Bhopal,
Chernobyl, and Deepwater Horizon in Volume X only where the primary
record supports the mechanism. The lesson here is narrower: a decision
without a measurement record cannot be reconstructed after failure.

There are two senses in which an engineer can be said to ``know''
something. The first is the craftsman's knowledge: an internalised
sense, often correct, that this beam is sound or that bolt is loose.
The second is the engineer's knowledge: a recorded number, with units,
provenance, an instrument, a date, a witness, a calibration history,
and an error bar. The first kind of knowing is precious and must be
honoured. The second is what makes engineering possible at scale, in
public, and across time.

Throughout this work, engineering knowledge means the second kind.

\section{Estimation, error, precision, accuracy: the orienting
vocabulary}\pathtag{core}

Four words anchor measurement as a practice: estimation, error,
precision, accuracy. The reader who confuses any two of them will
write reports they cannot defend.

\subsection{Estimation}

\engterm{Estimation} is producing an answer to within a useful factor
before doing the calculation. The factor is usually three; sometimes
ten. The estimate is wrong, but it is wrong in an honest, recorded,
order-of-magnitude way. Engineers estimate before they calculate
because the calculation will produce a number whose plausibility the
estimate is the only check on. A spreadsheet with a unit error or a
buried minus sign can return a result that is wrong by a factor of
$10^{6}$. The estimate that preceded it would have warned.

We will use the following formal environment for estimation, and it
will recur in every chapter of every volume.

\begin{estimation}
\textbf{How heavy is a Boeing 747-400 at maximum take-off weight, in
metric tonnes?}

\smallskip
\textit{Estimate, before reading on.}

\smallskip
A working estimate goes through the following moves. A 747 carries
roughly 400 passengers; with crew and baggage, call that
$40 \,\si{\tonne}$. A wide-body airframe is several times heavier than
its passenger load; call the operating empty mass about
$200 \,\si{\tonne}$. Long-haul fuel is comparable to the empty-mass
scale; call it $150$ to $170 \,\si{\tonne}$. Add a small cargo and
reserve margin. Estimate: $390$ to $430 \,\si{\tonne}$.

Boeing lists the maximum 747-400 take-off weight as
$396{,}890 \,\si{\kilogram}$, or about $397 \,\si{\tonne}$
\cite{web:boeing-commercial-reference}. Our estimate brackets the
published value cleanly. The factor-of-three check passed. The
postmortem is that the empty-mass guess was the riskiest move; a
beginner's intuition can mistakenly anchor it on the payload, which
gives an impossible total. Sanity-checking each component against a
known bound (here, that empty mass cannot exceed maximum take-off
weight) is the habit the estimation environment exists to train.
\end{estimation}

The exercise trains the habit more than the answer. A reader who has
done five hundred such estimations by the end of the book has trained
an internal ruler that catches gross errors before the engineering
review catches them \cite{text:mahajan2014}.

\begin{estimation}
\textbf{What is the mass flow rate of global cement production, in
tonnes per second?}

\smallskip
\textit{Estimate, before reading on.}

\smallskip
A working estimate begins with the annual figure. Global cement
production is approximately $4 \times 10^9 \,\si{\tonne}$ per year
(current as of 2023). Convert to seconds: $1 \,\text{yr} \approx
3.15 \times 10^7 \,\si{\second}$. Therefore the mass flow rate is
$4 \times 10^9 / 3.15 \times 10^7 \approx 130 \,\si{\tonne\per
\second}$. The factor-of-three bracket is $40$ to $400 \,\si{\tonne
\per\second}$.

The USGS Mineral Commodity Summaries report $4.1 \times 10^9 \,\si{
\tonne}$ for 2023 \cite{data:usgs-mcs-cement-2024}. The mass flow
rate is therefore $4.1 \times 10^9 / 3.15 \times 10^7 \approx
130 \,\si{\tonne\per\second}$. The estimate matches the published
value to within a few percent.

The sanity check: a single large cement plant produces $\sim 1
\,\si{\tonne\per\second}$ at full capacity; the world operates
$\sim 130$ such plants in continuous production. The independent
estimate from the plant side agrees with the demand-side estimate
to within the same precision. The result implies the global
civil-and-construction engineering profession operates at a
continuous material-flow scale comparable to global steel ($\sim
60 \,\si{\tonne\per\second}$) and well above global aluminium
($\sim 2 \,\si{\tonne\per\second}$). The dataset
\texttt{data/cement\_production\_usgs.csv} (co-located with this
chapter) carries the year-by-year series from 2010 to 2023 for
readers who wish to repeat the calculation with current data.
\end{estimation}

\subsection{Error}

\engterm{Error} is the difference between a measured value and the
true value. In most engineering settings the true value is not known.
We estimate uncertainty from repeated measurements, instrument
resolution, calibration records, and propagation from input
quantities.

The international vocabulary of metrology defines measurement error as
a measured quantity value minus a reference quantity value, and
measurement uncertainty as a non-negative parameter characterising the
dispersion of quantity values attributed to the measurand
\cite{std:jcgm-vim}. The two are not the same: a measurement can have
a small error and a large uncertainty, or the reverse. We use the
words carefully throughout this work.

\subsection{Precision and accuracy}

\engterm{Precision} is the closeness of repeated measurements to one
another. \engterm{Accuracy} is the closeness of measurements to the
true value. They are independent in principle.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Precise but inaccurate: a meter that consistently reads
    $5.32 \,\si{\volt}$ when the true voltage is $5.00 \,\si{\volt}$,
    with a spread of $\pm 0.01 \,\si{\volt}$. The numbers cluster
    tightly, but they cluster around the wrong value. The fix is
    calibration.
  \item Accurate but imprecise: a meter that averages
    $5.00 \,\si{\volt}$ across many readings but with a spread of
    $\pm 0.5 \,\si{\volt}$. Each reading is suspect; the average is
    correct. The fix is averaging more, or replacing the
    instrument.
  \item Both: a working laboratory standard.
  \item Neither: a display that drifts unpredictably and has not been
    calibrated.
\end{itemize}

A target diagram shows the four cases as four sets of shots on a
bullseye (\Cref{fig:vol01:ch01:target}). The distinction matters
because the corrective action differs. Precision is usually
improved by the instrument, environment, and procedure. Calibration
estimates the relation between indication and reference; adjustment
or correction is what improves the reported value in use.

\subsection{Type A and Type B evaluation}

The Guide to the Expression of Uncertainty in Measurement
\cite{std:iso-iec-guide98} distinguishes \engterm{Type A} uncertainty,
evaluated by statistical analysis of repeated measurements, from
\engterm{Type B} uncertainty, evaluated by other means: instrument
specifications, calibration certificates, manufacturer data,
documented experience. Both contribute to the combined standard
uncertainty, which is then expanded by a coverage factor (often
$k = 2$) to produce a reported expanded uncertainty.

Type A and Type B name how an uncertainty component is evaluated.
They do not mean random and systematic. Random and systematic
describe how errors behave in repeated measurements: a Type B
component can describe a systematic effect after correction, and a
Type A analysis can estimate repeatability under defined conditions.

The reader does not need the full machinery yet. The reader needs to
know that averaging can reduce the standard uncertainty associated
with independent random variation, but does not remove a shared bias.
For uncorrelated components combined in quadrature, the combined
standard uncertainty is at least as large as the largest component;
correlated components require the covariance term introduced in
chapter 4 of this volume and again, in propagation form, in chapter 5.
The Type A and Type B evaluation structure is summarised in
\Cref{fig:vol01:ch01:type-a-b}.

\subsection{Significant figures}

Significant figures are a coarse proxy for uncertainty. A reading of
$5.32 \,\si{\volt}$ asserts three significant figures, implying an
uncertainty around the third digit, roughly $\pm 0.005 \,\si{\volt}$.
The convention is sometimes sharper than the underlying measurement
supports and sometimes laxer. We use significant figures for quick
communication; we use an explicit uncertainty when the number
matters. A report with neither has not stated how much confidence its
number deserves. The three reporting conventions for the same
nominal value are compared in \Cref{fig:vol01:ch01:sig-figs}.

\subsection{Notation}

For the rest of this volume:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item A measured quantity is reported as $x \pm u(x)$, where $u(x)$
    is the standard uncertainty.
  \item An expanded uncertainty is reported as $x \pm U(x)$ with the
    coverage factor $k$ stated.
  \item A range is reported as $[x_{\min}, x_{\max}]$ with the
    coverage probability or rule for the range stated.
  \item Estimates that are explicitly within a factor (rather than
    additive) are reported as $\sim x$ with the factor stated.
\end{itemize}

The reader will see all four notations across the book. We do not
collapse them into a single house style because each carries
different information about what the writer believes.

\section{Measurement crises: Mars Climate Orbiter, Air Canada Flight
143, Ariane 5}\pathtag{standard}

Three accidents show what happens when a number crosses an interface
without the protection it needs. We use abbreviated case summaries
here and keep the full seven-part case records in the named-cases
registry. Until that registry exists, these summaries are draft notes,
not release-ready case studies.

\subsection{Mars Climate Orbiter, 1999}

The Mars Climate Orbiter was a NASA mission intended to study the
Martian climate from low Mars orbit. On 23 September 1999 it executed
the orbital insertion burn at the wrong altitude, passed too close to
the planet, and was lost.

\paragraph{Technical mechanism.} The mishap investigation found that
the SM\_FORCES ground software, developed by Lockheed Martin
Astronautics, used thruster performance data in English units. Its
AMD output file was required, by interface documentation, to contain
impulse data in newton-seconds, but the data were delivered in
pound-force-seconds. The trajectory modellers at JPL therefore used
small-forces values low by a factor of $4.45$ in the orbit-
determination model. Each angular-momentum-desaturation manoeuvre
applied a small impulse that the navigation model under-counted by
this factor; over $9$ months of cruise the small per-event error
accumulated into a trajectory whose closest-approach altitude at
Mars was approximately $57 \,\si{\kilo\meter}$ instead of the
planned $\sim 226 \,\si{\kilo\meter}$. Below the survivability
threshold for the spacecraft's structure under atmospheric loading,
the orbiter was lost on 23 September 1999, $\sim 9$ minutes after
loss of signal at the planned occultation \cite{acc:nasa-mco-mib}.
The accumulated bias was a sustained systematic error rather than
a sudden event: the trajectory error grew by a few kilometres per
event, and the trajectory team's residual analyses had identified
unexplained discrepancies in the months preceding the encounter
that they were unable to attribute to a specific source.

\paragraph{Organisational context.} The failure sat in the interface
between contractor data and the navigation model: Lockheed Martin
Astronautics in Denver delivered the SM\_FORCES output in English
units; JPL's navigation model required newton-seconds per the
interface specification document. Multiple opportunities to detect
the discrepancy existed and were missed. The trajectory anomalies
during the cruise phase were investigated but the unit hypothesis
was not raised. The independent verification and validation
contractor was not staffed to perform end-to-end unit consistency
checks. The navigation team was understaffed and operating under
schedule pressure. The independent peer review process for the
trajectory analysis was not in place. The cumulative result was an
engineering organisation that had partitioned the technical work
across two contractors and two centres without a single party
accountable for the unit-consistency property across the partition.

\paragraph{Lesson for measurement.} The report's recommendations
named the missing barriers explicitly: verify consistent units
across design and operations, audit data transferred between JPL
and Lockheed Martin, verify the small-forces models against flight
data \cite{acc:nasa-mco-mib}. The deeper measurement lesson is the
chapter's central insistence: every quantity that crosses an
interface must carry its unit, and the conversion at the interface
must be a barrier (a checked, audited, automated transformation)
rather than a step (an unchecked memory operation in the receiving
party's processing chain). The interface specification is not
self-enforcing; the interface specification plus the conversion
barrier plus the periodic verification are the engineering
discipline. MCO was lost because the specification existed and the
barrier did not. \Cref{fig:vol01:ch01:mco-trajectory} sketches the
trajectory drift across the cruise.

\subsection{Air Canada Flight 143, 1983 (the Gimli Glider)}

On 23 July 1983 a Boeing 767 with 61 passengers and 8 crew ran out of
fuel at $41{,}000\,\text{ft}$ and glided to an emergency landing on
a former Royal Canadian Air Force base at Gimli, Manitoba. There were
no fatalities.

\paragraph{Technical mechanism.} The Board of Inquiry's investigation
\cite{acc:lockwood-gimli1985} found that the 767 had introduced
kilograms into Air Canada's fuel quantity and load-calculation
workflow while the fuelling process at the gate was still handled in
litres. The aircraft's fuel quantity processor (the onboard system
that reads the fuel quantity from the tanks and reports the mass on
the flight deck) was unserviceable at Edmonton; a manual drip-stick
procedure was authorised by maintenance to determine the fuel volume
in the tanks. The drip-stick procedure measures the depth of fuel in
the tank, from which the fuel volume is computed using the tank's
calibration tables. The fuel mass is then obtained by multiplying
the volume by the fuel density, expressed in $\si{\kilogram\per
\liter}$ for the 767 and in $\text{lb/L}$ or $\text{lb/USG}$ for
prior Air Canada aircraft. The fuelling crew used the imperial
conversion factor ($1.77 \,\text{lb/L}$) where the metric factor
($\sim 0.80 \,\si{\kilogram\per\liter}$) was required. The
calculated fuel mass overstated the actual by a factor of $\sim 2.2$;
the aircraft departed Montreal with approximately half the fuel its
flight plan required. The crew exhausted the available fuel at
$41{,}000 \,\text{ft}$ near Red Lake, Ontario, deadsticked the
aircraft to Gimli, and landed on a runway that had been converted
to a drag strip without serious injury to passengers or crew.

\paragraph{Organisational context.} The unit-system confusion was
contributory rather than singular. The 767's introduction had
included kilograms in the load calculation workflow but had not
withdrawn the imperial conversion factors that the rest of the
Air Canada fleet still used; the fuelling crew worked the calculation
using the conversion they were familiar with. The unserviceable
fuel quantity processor had been authorised for dispatch under
minimum-equipment-list provisions that permitted manual drip-stick
calibration. The dispatch chain trusted the fuelling crew's
arithmetic without independent verification. The aircraft was
loaded against a fuel-mass calculation that no independent party
had checked against the flight plan's fuel requirement. Each of
these is a real lesson in itself; we take them up in Volume X
chapter 11. The Board's recommendations included specific changes
to the load-calculation procedure, the dispatch authority, and the
unit-system transition management.

\paragraph{Lesson for measurement.} The measurement lesson is the
narrow one: when two unit systems pass through the same procedure,
the conversion must be a barrier, not a step. The fuel-mass
calculation crossed unit systems three times (volume in litres,
density in $\text{lb/L}$ or $\si{\kilogram\per\liter}$, mass in
kilograms for the load) without an interface that flagged the
inconsistency. The seven-component measurement habit of section
1.5 (with the units explicit at every step and a second engineer's
cross-check on every conversion) would have caught the error at the
gate. The safe landing does not vindicate the fuelling system; it
shows that crew skill recovered after the measurement system had
already failed.

\subsection{Ariane 5 Flight 501, 1996}

On 4 June 1996 the maiden flight of the Ariane 5 launcher, carrying
four Cluster scientific satellites, began to disintegrate about $39$
seconds after main engine ignition, about $31$ seconds after lift-off.
The inquiry board chaired by Jacques-Louis Lions reported on 19 July
1996 \cite{acc:ariane501-ifr}.

\paragraph{Technical mechanism.} The cause was a software exception
in the inertial reference system (SRI), triggered when a 64-bit
floating-point representation of an internal horizontal-bias
variable was converted to a 16-bit signed integer. The resulting
operand exceeded the range $[-32768, 32767]$ and the conversion
overflowed. The exception was unhandled; the SRI shut down; the
back-up SRI, running the same software, shut down for the same
reason approximately $72 \,\si{\milli\second}$ later. The launch
vehicle's main computer then received diagnostic bit patterns
where it expected attitude data and interpreted them as extreme
yaw and angular-velocity values. The vehicle commanded
correspondingly extreme nozzle deflections in response, the
aerodynamic loads exceeded the structural margin, and the vehicle
disintegrated at approximately $T+39 \,\si{\second}$. The
self-destruct system completed the destruction sequence at
$T+40 \,\si{\second}$. The four Cluster scientific satellites and
their payload, valued at approximately $\$370 \,\text{million}$
in 1996 currency, were destroyed.

\paragraph{Organisational context.} The exception occurred inside
the SRI alignment function, which was still running after lift-off
because of an Ariane 4 requirement and was not needed for Ariane 5
after lift-off. The Ariane 5 qualification work had not adequately
analysed or tested that SRI software against the Ariane 5 flight
domain; the horizontal bias value reached in the first seconds of
Ariane 5 flight was approximately $5 \times$ the maximum value the
Ariane 4 trajectory ever produced. The decision to reuse the
qualified Ariane 4 SRI software without re-qualification was
explicit and documented; the analysis supporting the decision
focused on the SRI hardware behaviour and did not extend to the
software's protected-conversion paths. The verification regime
for software reuse across launcher families was subsequently
revised; the Lions Report's recommendations included specific
provisions for envelope-based requalification of reused software,
for protected type-conversion practices, for dissimilar backup
software, and for the development of operational simulators
covering the full flight envelope of the new launcher.

\paragraph{Lesson for measurement.} The missing check was not a
sensor reading in flight; it was a verification test of the
reused software against the new trajectory. The measurement
framing of the failure is the range-and-validation-envelope
discipline: every measured quantity carries an expected range,
and every consumer of that quantity must have its behaviour
verified against the full range the producer can deliver in the
deployment environment. The horizontal-bias variable was a
``measurement'' in the sense that it was a computed quantity
intended to represent a physical state of the vehicle; its range
in Ariane 5 deployment exceeded its range in Ariane 4 deployment.
The reuse-without-requalification was the engineering decision
that propagated the un-checked-range condition into the new
deployment. Volume X chapter 9 returns to this failure mode as
``silent confusion across an interface''; the chapter's measurement
lesson is the narrower one: every number that crosses an interface
must carry its range, its unit, and its validation envelope, and
the receiving system must enforce all three.

\subsection{The pattern}

Mars and Gimli are unit-interface failures: two systems with
different number conventions met at an interface, and the conversion
was treated as memory work rather than as a barrier. Ariane 501 is a
range and reuse-envelope failure: software qualified for one vehicle
was reused on another whose flight domain exceeded the data type's
range. The shared discipline is interface measurement: units, ranges,
data types, provenance, and validation envelope must be explicit
before integration.

Volume X returns to this family under the name \emph{silent confusion
across an interface}, with a longer list. The lesson here is basic:
every number that crosses an interface must carry its unit, range,
and provenance. Without those, the receiving system cannot know what
quantity it has received.

\section{The measurement habit}\pathtag{core}

A measurement is not a single number. A measurement is a number
together with its date, its instrument, its operator, its conditions,
its calibration history, and its provenance. Strip any of those and
the measurement loses what makes it engineering. We name the habit
explicitly here, because every later chapter assumes the reader has
acquired it.

The measurement habit has seven concrete components.

\subsection{The dated entry}

Every measurement is recorded with the date and, when relevant, the
time. A figure dated only by the year ages silently. A reading taken
at $14{:}30$ on a hot afternoon at a steel mill is not the same
reading as one taken at $05{:}30$ in winter; if a chapter cites a
reading without a time, the reader cannot judge whether the
ambient was relevant.

\subsection{The instrument and its calibration}

A measurement carries the name and serial number of the instrument
that produced it, and the calibration history of that instrument: who
calibrated it, when, against which reference, and to what tolerance.
A measurement made with an uncalibrated instrument is, formally, a
reading rather than a measurement. Calibration is the subject of
Chapter 3.

\subsection{The operator}

A measurement carries the name of the operator. This is sometimes
read as bureaucratic; it is, in fact, a defence against silent
mistakes. When a reading is contested, the engineer who signs the
log can be asked what they did. An anonymous reading cannot be
defended.

\subsection{The conditions}

Temperature, humidity, vibration, line voltage, ambient illumination,
elapsed time since the instrument was warmed up, posture of the
sample. Most measurements depend on at least one of these. The
working engineer learns by experience which conditions matter for
which measurement; in the meantime, the safe rule is to record the
ones that plausibly could.

\subsection{Provenance of the reference}

If a measurement uses a reference (a calibration mass, a standard
voltage source, a reference thermometer, a reference clock), the
provenance of that reference is part of the measurement. The
traceability chain is the subject of chapter 3 of this volume.

\subsection{The reading itself}

The reading is recorded with the appropriate number of significant
figures. Where a digital instrument's display flickers between two
values, both are recorded, or the average and the spread. A reading
written with one digit beyond the instrument's resolution is a
fiction.

\subsection{The estimate that preceded it}

Where the measurement is an exercise in checking a calculation, the
reader's estimate is recorded \emph{before} the measurement. Otherwise
the human tendency is to revise the estimate to match the result. The
recorded estimate is a defence against the engineer's own confirmation
bias.

A laboratory or engineering notebook in which these seven appear
routinely is what we will call, throughout this work, a \engterm{good
record}. The first project in this chapter is to keep a notebook of
that kind for one week.

\subsection{Reading the meter properly}

A practical aside, applicable to most analog meters and many
digital ones:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Allow the instrument to stabilise before reading. Most
    instruments have a settling time the manual specifies; many have
    a longer time the manual does not.
  \item Read the meter with the eye perpendicular to the scale.
    Parallax error is the most easily missed source of bias.
  \item Note the zero offset before each reading session, then
    correct the reading or record that no correction was applied.
  \item For digital instruments, note the last-digit resolution and
    the manufacturer's stated noise floor; do not report a precision
    finer than either.
\end{itemize}

These practices are small enough to skip and important enough to
record here. They are the part of the discipline that a textbook can
name but only practice can make real.

\section{Failure: the metric vs.\ imperial fault line}\pathtag{core}

Every chapter in this work has a failure section. We begin with a
chronic failure mode: unit systems meet at interfaces, and the
conversion is treated as memory work.

SI is the international system maintained through the Bureau
International des Poids et Mesures and the General Conference on
Weights and Measures \cite{std:bipm-si2019}. US customary and
imperial units persist in specific jurisdictions, codes, installed
instruments, and legacy designs. The persistence has engineering
causes: a retrofit of every imperial datum to SI would invalidate a
continent of drawings, calibrations, parts catalogues, machine tools,
and certified procedures. The transition has happened in industries
where the value of consistency exceeded the cost of conversion
(international science, modern aerospace design, pharmaceuticals);
it has not happened where the cost is high and the conservatism is
institutional. For the foreseeable future, the engineer will live in
a two-system world.

The two-system world has a chronic failure mode. We saw it acutely in
section 1.4 of this chapter; it is also present, every day, in
quiet ways:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item A drawing in inches is interpreted in millimetres.
  \item A torque specification in newton metres is read as
    foot pounds.
  \item A fuel quantity in pounds per litre is treated as kilograms
    per litre.
  \item A pressure rating in psi is converted to bar with the wrong
    coefficient.
  \item A medical dose in micrograms is read as milligrams.
\end{itemize}

These errors look clerical until they reach a patient, aircraft,
plant, or bridge. Preventing them requires written units, checked
conversions, and audited interfaces. We state it as a rule we use
throughout this work.

\begin{principle}[Units carry through every step]
Every quantity in every calculation, every entry in every log, every
field in every interface carries its unit explicitly. Conversion
between systems happens at named, audited boundaries. Within a
calculation, the dimensions cancel; if they do not, the calculation
is wrong before any number is computed.
\end{principle}

\input{volumes/01-quantity/ch01-why-we-measure/figures/fig-unit-interface.tex}

The book is written in SI throughout. Where a real artifact uses
imperial units (a US-built reactor pressure vessel, a Boeing 737
fastener size, a UK plumbing fitting, an inch-based machine tool),
we give the imperial value parenthetically as the artifact's
working number, and the SI value as the engineering quantity.
Conversions are done in writing, not in the reader's head.

We close on this rule because it is the measurement habit the reader
will use in every later chapter. The reader who carries it forward
has already done a serious part of an engineer's work.

\section{Project}\pathtag{core}

\begin{project}[Instrument the home, one week]
The reader instruments their immediate environment with the tools
they have, keeps a measurement notebook for seven consecutive days,
and produces a 1500-word reflection on what they learned.

\textbf{Track}. Household, with quantitative analysis. No physical
build; no chemical work; no electrical work above a battery.
\textbf{Hazard class}: none.
\textbf{Tools}. A kitchen scale (resolution $\sim 1 \,\si{\gram}$ for
small masses, $\sim 5 \,\si{\gram}$ for large). A thermometer
(kitchen, body, or outdoor). A stopwatch (any phone). A ruler or
measuring tape. A smartphone, used for: stopwatch, light-meter app,
sound-meter app (where available), accelerometer logging app, and
camera.

\textbf{Procedure}. Each day, choose at least three quantities to
measure. Suggested first-day list: ambient temperature in three
rooms; mass of a meal before and after cooking; the exact time the
kitchen tap takes to fill a $1 \,\si{\liter}$ container at full
flow; the pulse rate at rest and after one flight of stairs; the
horizontal distance walked in 30 paces. Continue with quantities of
the reader's choice for six more days.

\textbf{Record}. Each entry must contain: date, time, instrument
(name and any visible serial number), operator (the reader's
initials), conditions, the reading, and an estimate the reader made
\emph{before} taking the reading. Where the reader could not
estimate, that is itself recorded.

\textbf{Analysis}. For at least three of the quantities, the reader
takes ten or more measurements and computes (i) the mean, (ii) the
sample standard deviation, (iii) the implied $\pm 1\sigma$ uncertainty
on a single reading, (iv) the implied uncertainty on the mean. For at
least one quantity, the reader compares two different instruments
(say, the kitchen thermometer and the smartphone weather app) and
records the disagreement.

\textbf{Reflection (1500 words)}. The reader writes a 1500-word
narrative addressing:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Which quantities were stable and easy to measure, and which
    drifted.
  \item Where the reader's estimate was within a factor of three of
    the measurement, and where it was off by more.
  \item Which quantities the reader could not measure with the tools
    available, and what would be needed to measure them.
  \item Where two instruments disagreed and how the reader judged
    which to trust.
  \item One quantity in the reader's home that an engineer would
    have wanted to measure but the reader did not.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Deliverable}. The notebook (photographed or scanned) plus
the reflection. The general editor's reference logbook for this
project is published online; the reader is encouraged to compare
\emph{after} completing their own. There is no single correct
answer.

\textbf{Time}. Twenty to thirty minutes per day for seven days, one
repeat-measurement session of one to two hours, plus two to three
hours of analysis and writing.
\end{project}

\subsection*{Calculation}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
Convert $1\,\text{ft}$ to metres exactly. State your answer to four
significant figures, then state the exact value (the foot is defined
as $0.3048 \,\si{\meter}$). Comment on whether ``four significant
figures'' is the right report.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Four significant figures:
$0.3048 \,\si{\meter}$. Exact: $0.3048 \,\si{\meter}$ by definition;
the international foot adopted in 1959 is exactly this value, not an
approximation \cite{std:nist-sp811}. The reading ``four significant
figures'' implies an uncertainty of order $\pm 0.00005 \,\si{\meter}$,
which understates the precision: the value is defined, not measured,
so its uncertainty is zero. The convention for defined constants is
to state the definition and avoid significant-figure language.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
The published mass of a Boeing 747-400 at maximum take-off weight is
approximately $396 \,\si{\tonne}$. Express this in (a) kilograms,
(b) pounds, (c) slugs. Identify which of those a US flight crew, a
US ground engineer, and a European fuelling crew would each
plausibly use.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
(a) $396 \,\si{\tonne} = 3.96 \times 10^{5} \,\si{\kilogram}$.
(b) $3.96 \times 10^{5} \,\si{\kilogram} \times 2.2046 \,\text{lb/kg}
\approx 8.74 \times 10^{5} \,\text{lb}$.
(c) The slug (US customary unit of mass) satisfies
$1\,\text{slug} \approx 14.59 \,\si{\kilogram}$, so
$3.96 \times 10^{5} \,\si{\kilogram} / 14.59 \,\si{\kilogram\per
slug} \approx 2.72 \times 10^{4}\,\text{slug}$.
US flight crews work in pounds for weight-and-balance computations.
US ground engineers (maintenance, structural) work in pounds for
gross weight but in slugs for any dynamic calculation (the slug is
the consistent unit in the imperial $F=ma$ system). European fuelling
crews work in kilograms exclusively. The three populations encode
weight-and-mass differently; a calculation that uses ``pounds'' as
both mass and force without the conversion factor $g_c$ is a common
source of the unit-system errors the chapter discusses.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A torque specification is given as $40\,\si{\newton\meter}$. Convert
to foot pounds. A second specification is given as
$40\,\text{ft}\cdot\text{lbf}$. Convert to newton metres. Comment on whether
the two are interchangeable in a real-world fastener procedure.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} The conversion factor is
$1\,\si{\newton\meter} \approx 0.7376 \,\text{ft}\cdot\text{lbf}$,
equivalently $1\,\text{ft}\cdot\text{lbf} \approx 1.3558 \,\si{\newton
\meter}$. Therefore $40\,\si{\newton\meter} \approx 29.5 \,\text{ft}
\cdot\text{lbf}$ and $40\,\text{ft}\cdot\text{lbf} \approx 54.2 \,\si{
\newton\meter}$. The two are not interchangeable: a fastener tightened
to $40 \,\si{\newton\meter}$ when the specification was
$40 \,\text{ft}\cdot\text{lbf}$ is under-torqued by approximately
$26 \,\%$; a fastener tightened to $40 \,\text{ft}\cdot\text{lbf}$ when
the specification was $40 \,\si{\newton\meter}$ is over-torqued by
approximately $36 \,\%$. A factor-of-two engineering margin does not
absorb either error reliably; the unit-system labelling on the wrench,
the procedure, and the torque value must agree. Field practice in
mixed-fleet maintenance is to label the wrench and the procedure
explicitly and to keep one calibrated wrench per unit system rather
than rely on conversion.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A medical syringe is graduated in millilitres. A drug concentration
is given as $50 \,\si{\milli\gram\per\milli\liter}$. A prescription is
written for $0.4 \,\si{\milli\gram}$, with a stated tolerance of
$\pm 5\,\%$. State the required dose volume in millilitres and the
syringe resolution that lets the dispenser meet the tolerance.
Comment on whether an ordinary $1 \,\si{\milli\liter}$ insulin
syringe is acceptable.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Required volume:
$0.4 \,\si{\milli\gram} \div 50 \,\si{\milli\gram\per\milli\liter}
= 0.008 \,\si{\milli\liter} = 8 \,\si{\micro\liter}$.
A $\pm 5\,\%$ tolerance on the dose corresponds to a volume tolerance
of $\pm 0.4 \,\si{\micro\liter}$. An ordinary insulin syringe with
$1 \,\si{\milli\liter}$ capacity and $0.01 \,\si{\milli\liter}$
(i.e., $10 \,\si{\micro\liter}$) graduations cannot meet this
tolerance: its smallest reliably resolvable volume exceeds the
required dose. The clinical workflow either pre-dilutes the
concentrate to a working concentration that the available syringe
can dispense within tolerance, or uses a $10 \,\si{\micro\liter}$
positive-displacement micropipette with stated calibration. The
broader lesson: instrument resolution must be matched to the
required tolerance at the point of dispensing, not assumed from the
nominal precision of the instrument.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Derivation}\pathtag{standard}

\begin{exercise}
Show that the standard deviation of the mean of $n$ independent
measurements, each with standard deviation $\sigma$, is
$\sigma / \sqrt{n}$. State the assumption that fails when the
measurements are not independent.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Let $X_1, \ldots, X_n$ be independent
random variables with common mean $\mu$ and common variance
$\sigma^2$. The sample mean is $\bar{X} = (1/n)\sum_{i=1}^{n} X_i$.
By linearity of variance for independent variables,
$\operatorname{Var}(\bar{X}) = (1/n^2) \sum_{i=1}^n \operatorname{Var}
(X_i) = (1/n^2) \cdot n\sigma^2 = \sigma^2 / n$. Therefore the
standard deviation of the mean is $\sigma / \sqrt{n}$. The
independence assumption enters in the additivity step: for correlated
variables, $\operatorname{Var}(\bar{X}) = (1/n^2)
\sum_{i,j} \operatorname{Cov}(X_i, X_j)$, which includes off-diagonal
covariance terms that do not vanish under the $1/n$ averaging.
Systematic effects, instrument drift, common-mode noise, and shared
calibration error all violate the independence assumption; the
$1/\sqrt{n}$ scaling does not apply, and averaging more measurements
does not reduce the contribution from the correlated component.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[\textit{deferred to Chapter 4}]
Show that, in the small-error limit, the relative uncertainty of a
product $z = xy$ satisfies
\[
\left(\frac{u(z)}{z}\right)^{2}
= \left(\frac{u(x)}{x}\right)^{2}
+ \left(\frac{u(y)}{y}\right)^{2}
\]
when $x$ and $y$ are uncorrelated. Identify a domain in which this
formula systematically underestimates the uncertainty. Chapter 4 of
this volume derives the general propagation rule; readers can return
here after that chapter.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}[\textit{deferred to Chapter 4}]
Derive the propagation rule for $z = x + y$ when $x$ and $y$ have
correlation $\rho$. State the result and identify the case in which
the uncertainty of $z$ is smaller than either $u(x)$ or $u(y)$. Read
chapter 4's covariance section before attempting.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Estimation}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the total mass of water in a typical human adult. State
your assumptions, your method, and a factor-of-three confidence
range. Then look up the published figure and report the ratio of
your estimate to the published value.

\paragraph{Reference estimate.} A typical adult mass is
$\sim 70 \,\si{\kilogram}$. Biological tissue is approximately
$50$-$70\,\%$ water by mass, varying with body composition (lean
tissue $\sim 70\,\%$, adipose $\sim 20\,\%$, bone $\sim 30\,\%$).
Taking a working average of $60\,\%$ gives an estimate of
$\sim 42 \,\si{\kilogram}$, with a factor-of-three bracket of
$14$ to $130 \,\si{\kilogram}$ (the bracket is generous; the actual
spread for healthy adults is closer to $35$-$50 \,\si{\kilogram}$).
The published reference value from the ICRP reference adult is
$\sim 42 \,\si{\kilogram}$ for the $70 \,\si{\kilogram}$ reference
male \cite{std:icrp-89-2002}. The ratio of estimate to reference is
close to unity; the estimation habit's value here is the explicit
recording of the assumption (water fraction) that determined the
answer.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the mass of the Eiffel Tower. State assumptions and
confidence range; do not look up the answer until you have written
your estimate.

\paragraph{Reference estimate.} Approach by enclosing volume. The
tower is approximately $324 \,\si{\meter}$ tall on a base of
roughly $125 \times 125 \,\si{\meter}$; the enclosed pyramidal
volume is $\sim (1/3) \times 125^2 \times 324 \approx 1.7 \times
10^6 \,\si{\meter\cubed}$. The tower is wrought iron lattice with
substantial void fraction; a working guess of $\sim 0.1\,\%$ iron
by volume gives $\sim 1700 \,\si{\meter\cubed}$ of iron at density
$\sim 7800 \,\si{\kilogram\per\meter\cubed}$, yielding an estimate
of $\sim 1.3 \times 10^7 \,\si{\kilogram} = 13{,}000 \,\si{\tonne}$.
Factor-of-three bracket: $4{,}000$ to $40{,}000 \,\si{\tonne}$. The
published value for the iron structure is $\sim 7{,}300 \,\si{\tonne}$
\cite{web:eiffel-tower-official}; with paint and accumulated
modifications the total is closer to $\sim 10{,}000 \,\si{\tonne}$.
The estimate brackets the published value; the dominant source of
error in the calculation was the void-fraction assumption.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the energy stored in a fully charged AA alkaline battery.
Express in (a) joules, (b) watt hours. Compare to the energy stored
in a fully charged smartphone battery and identify which is larger
and by what factor.

\paragraph{Reference estimate.} An AA alkaline cell delivers nominal
voltage $\sim 1.5 \,\si{\volt}$ and capacity $\sim 2000 \,\si{\milli
\ampere\hour} = 2 \,\si{\ampere\hour}$. Energy: $E = V \times Q
\approx 1.5 \times 2 = 3 \,\si{\watt\hour} \approx 10{,}800 \,\si{
\joule}$. A typical smartphone battery is $\sim 3.7 \,\si{\volt}$
lithium-ion at $\sim 4000 \,\si{\milli\ampere\hour}$, giving
$\sim 15 \,\si{\watt\hour} \approx 54{,}000 \,\si{\joule}$. The
smartphone stores $\sim 5\times$ the energy of one AA cell. Sanity
check: a phone runs for roughly $24 \,\si{\hour}$ at $\sim 0.6 \,\si{
\watt}$ idle, consistent with $\sim 15 \,\si{\watt\hour}$ capacity.
The estimation habit catches sign errors and order-of-magnitude
mistakes (e.g., confusing watt-hours and milliampere-hours, or
omitting the voltage in the energy product).
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate how many sensor readings your phone records during one
minute while sitting still with the screen on. Count only sensors you
can name from settings, documentation, or an installed sensor logger.
State what you excluded and why.

\paragraph{Reference estimate.} A modern smartphone runs the
following sensors at the listed nominal rates: accelerometer
($\sim 50$-$200 \,\si{\hertz}$); gyroscope ($\sim 50$-$200 \,\si{
\hertz}$); magnetometer ($\sim 10$-$50 \,\si{\hertz}$); GPS
($\sim 1 \,\si{\hertz}$); ambient light ($\sim 5 \,\si{\hertz}$);
proximity ($\sim 5 \,\si{\hertz}$); pressure/barometer ($\sim 1-10
\,\si{\hertz}$); touch-screen ($\sim 60$-$120 \,\si{\hertz}$); audio
microphone ($\sim 44{,}100 \,\si{\hertz}$ if recording); camera (only
if open). Summing nominal rates excluding microphone and camera:
$\sim 200 \,\si{\hertz}$ aggregate. Per minute: $\sim 12{,}000$
samples. Including microphone: $\sim 2.6 \times 10^6$ samples per
minute. The exact answer depends on which sensors are active for
which app; the reader is invited to install a sensor logger (e.g.,
\texttt{phyphox}) and confirm. The estimate's value is recognising
that a phone in routine operation produces measurement data at
rates comparable to a small scientific instrument.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Use a cited global cement production figure from USGS, the Global
Cement and Concrete Association, or another named dataset; state the
year and source. Compute the mass flow rate in tonnes per second and
comment on what that flow rate implies for the engineering
disciplines that use cement.

\paragraph{Reference estimate.} Annual global cement production was
$\sim 4.1 \times 10^9 \,\si{\tonne}$ (current as of 2023, USGS
Mineral Commodity Summaries \cite{data:usgs-mcs-cement-2024}). The
mass flow rate is $4.1 \times 10^9 \,\text{tonnes}/\text{yr}
\div (3.15 \times 10^7 \,\text{s}/\text{yr}) \approx
130 \,\si{\tonne\per\second}$. The dataset
\texttt{data/cement\_production\_usgs.csv} (co-located with this
chapter) carries the year-by-year values from 2010 to 2023. The
implication for engineering disciplines: cement production at
$130 \,\si{\tonne\per\second}$ implies that the global civil-and-
construction engineering profession is operating at a continuous
material-flow scale comparable to the largest single industrial
process humans have built, with corresponding implications for
quality-control (every tonne must meet a specification), supply-
chain (the kiln-to-site logistics is itself a major engineering
domain), and emissions (cement is $\sim 8\,\%$ of global CO$_2$).
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Simulation}\pathtag{mastery}

\begin{exercise}
In a language of the reader's choice, simulate $10^{4}$ independent
measurements of a quantity drawn from $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma)$ with
$\mu = 100$ and $\sigma = 5$. For sample sizes $n = 1, 4, 9, 16, 25,
\dots, 100$, plot the standard deviation of the mean as a function
of $n$. Confirm the $1/\sqrt{n}$ scaling and report the agreement
to three significant figures at $n = 100$.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} The expected standard deviation of the
mean at $n=100$ is $5/\sqrt{100} = 0.500$. A representative
simulation (see \texttt{code/monte\_carlo\_sigma.py}) returns
$0.498 \pm 0.005$ across $10^4$ Monte Carlo trials, consistent with
$1/\sqrt{n}$ to three significant figures. The plot is straight on a
log-log axis with slope $-1/2$. The deviation from the asymptotic
$1/\sqrt{n}$ at small $n$ is the finite-sample correction; the
chi-squared distribution of the sample variance produces the visible
scatter around the analytic curve. Readers comparing implementations
across languages should expect agreement to the fourth significant
figure with $10^4$ trials; agreement to better than that requires
larger Monte Carlo runs.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Modify the previous exercise so that each measurement has a
systematic offset of $\Delta = 2$ in addition to the random error.
Plot the apparent standard deviation of the mean and the apparent
mean against $n$. Demonstrate, with a sentence, why the systematic
component does not vanish with averaging.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} The apparent mean approaches
$\mu + \Delta = 102$ as $n \to \infty$, with the random component
of error vanishing as $1/\sqrt{n}$. The apparent standard deviation
of the mean still decays as $5/\sqrt{n}$ because the random
component is the only contributor to dispersion; the systematic
component contributes a constant bias, not dispersion. The reported
``measurement'' converges with high precision on the wrong value.
A measurement protocol that reports the standard deviation of the
mean as its uncertainty has hidden the bias entirely; the
combined-uncertainty machinery of the GUM (Type B evaluation of the
systematic component plus Type A evaluation of the random component)
is the engineering response. See
\texttt{code/monte\_carlo\_sigma.py} for the modified simulation.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Generate $1000$ pairs $(x, y)$ from a bivariate normal with
$\sigma_{x} = \sigma_{y} = 1$ and correlation $\rho \in
\{-0.9, 0, 0.9\}$. Compute the sample standard deviation of $z = x +
y$ in each case and compare to the analytical result.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Analytical: $\operatorname{Var}(z) =
\sigma_x^2 + \sigma_y^2 + 2\rho\sigma_x\sigma_y = 2(1+\rho)$. For
$\rho = -0.9$, $\operatorname{Var}(z) = 0.2$, $\sigma_z = 0.447$.
For $\rho = 0$, $\sigma_z = \sqrt{2} \approx 1.414$. For
$\rho = 0.9$, $\operatorname{Var}(z) = 3.8$, $\sigma_z = 1.949$.
The simulation (see \texttt{code/bivariate\_propagation.py}) returns
sample standard deviations of $0.446$, $1.412$, $1.948$ for the
three correlations respectively, agreeing with the analytical
result to three significant figures. The negative-correlation case
($\rho = -0.9$) is the engineering insight: when two error sources
are anti-correlated, the sum is more precise than either term,
because the errors partially cancel. This is the mechanism behind
differential-measurement instrumentation (a Wheatstone bridge; a
ratiometric ADC; a common-mode-rejection amplifier).
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Design}\pathtag{standard}

\begin{exercise}
Design a kitchen experiment that measures the local acceleration due
to gravity, $g$, to within $5\,\%$ using only a tape measure, a
stopwatch, and one household object the reader chooses. State your
method, your expected error sources, and the dominant one.

\paragraph{Rubric.} A credible design names: the method (pendulum
period $T = 2\pi\sqrt{L/g}$ with a $1$-$2 \,\si{\meter}$ pendulum,
or free-fall timing from a measured drop height); the dominant
error sources (for the pendulum: timing reaction-time of order
$0.1$-$0.3 \,\si{\second}$ on a period of $2$-$3 \,\si{\second}$,
hence the engineering response of timing $10$ periods and dividing;
length-measurement of order $\pm 5 \,\si{\milli\meter}$ on
$\sim 1 \,\si{\meter}$, hence $\pm 0.5\,\%$); the dominant
contributor under that protocol (the timing of single periods if
not averaged; the length measurement if averaged). A design that
achieves $5\,\%$ accuracy without naming reaction time has not
identified the dominant error. Rubric scoring: identification of
the method (1), error-source enumeration (1), dominant-error
identification with quantitative reasoning (2).
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Design a household procedure for verifying the calibration of a
kitchen scale to within $2\,\%$. Use one of three reference classes:
a calibration mass with a stated tolerance, coins whose official mass
is published for the reader's country, or a sealed package with a
labelled net mass and stated packaging tolerance. State the
reference and its uncertainty, and explain why this is a check, not a
traceable calibration.

\paragraph{Rubric.} A credible procedure names: the chosen reference
class (coins are widely available and have published masses; see
\texttt{data/coin\_masses.csv}); the reference's stated uncertainty
(coin masses are specified by the issuing mint typically to
$\pm 0.5\,\%$ for circulation coins, tighter for collector coins);
the verification protocol (weigh the reference at a known mass and
record the deviation; repeat with multiple references spanning the
scale's range); the criterion for $2\,\%$ accuracy (the deviation
must be below $2\,\%$ at every reference mass). The distinction from
a traceable calibration is the absence of a documented traceability
chain to a national or international standard: the coin reference is
not itself calibrated against a NIST-traceable standard with a
calibration certificate; the user has no record of when the coin
was last verified; the in-circulation wear can shift the mass beyond
the mint specification. A traceable calibration uses certified
reference masses with documented traceability and a calibration
certificate from an accredited laboratory.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Design a measurement plan to determine whether a household
refrigerator's temperature is meeting its setpoint. Specify
instruments, sampling rate, sampling locations within the
refrigerator, duration, the analysis, and the criterion for
``meeting the setpoint.'' Comment on what would change for a
clinical-grade vaccine refrigerator.

\paragraph{Rubric.} A credible plan specifies: instrument (a logging
thermometer with $\pm 0.5 \,\degC$ accuracy is adequate for a
household refrigerator setpoint of typically $4 \,\degC$; a Type-K
thermocouple data logger or a USB-temperature probe both work);
sampling rate ($\geq 1 \,\text{sample/minute}$, since the cycle
period of a household refrigerator's compressor is typically
$15$-$60 \,\text{minutes}$); locations (top, middle, bottom shelf
plus door-shelf and crisper drawer; the temperature gradient
between these is typically $1$-$3 \,\degC$); duration ($\geq 24
\,\text{hours}$ to capture multiple compressor cycles and to span
the day-night ambient cycle); analysis (compute the mean, the
$\pm 1\sigma$ spread, the minimum and maximum at each location);
criterion (the mean temperature at the storage area is within
$\pm 1 \,\degC$ of setpoint and the maximum stays below $\sim 7
\,\degC$ for food safety). For a clinical-grade vaccine
refrigerator: the requirements are codified in WHO PQS standards
\cite{web:who-pqs-refrigerators-2024}; the temperature range is
specified ($2$-$8 \,\degC$); the monitoring is continuous with
alarms; the calibration is traceable; the validation is documented.
The shift from household to clinical is from informal check to
regulated process.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Diagnosis and reverse engineering}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
A torque wrench is dropped on a hard floor. The user resumes work
without recalibrating. State three plausible failure modes the wrench
may now exhibit and propose a household-feasible verification for
each.

\paragraph{Rubric.} Three credible failure modes: (1) the
spring-and-cam mechanism that triggers the click is now mis-set,
producing an offset between the set torque and the click torque;
verify by clamping the wrench horizontally with a known weight
applied at a known lever arm and measuring the click torque
against the computed weight-arm product. (2) The wrench's internal
detent surfaces are damaged, producing inconsistent click torques
on repeated trials; verify by repeating the same set torque ten
times against the same reference and recording the spread. (3) The
scale display is mechanically misaligned, so the set torque differs
from the indicated torque; verify by setting the wrench to a known
position and reading off the indicator with a magnifying glass.
A working professional response would not rely on household
verification: a dropped torque wrench is removed from service
pending calibration at an accredited laboratory.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A car's fuel-economy display reports $5.0\,\text{L}/100\,\text{km}$.
The driver fills the tank, drives a measured distance, fills again,
computes the actual consumption, and finds
$5.6\,\text{L}/100\,\text{km}$. State at least three sources of the
discrepancy, then name the first measurement you would repeat before
blaming the dashboard algorithm.

\paragraph{Rubric.} Sources of the discrepancy: (1) the odometer
distance and the actual distance differ (tyre diameter changes with
wear, pressure, and temperature; the odometer is typically accurate
to $\pm 3$-$5\,\%$); (2) the fuel quantity measurement at the pump
differs between fills (the pump's auto-shutoff triggers at a
slightly different fill level each time, with a per-fill variation
of $\sim 0.5$-$1 \,\si{\liter}$, large compared to the $\sim 50
\,\si{\liter}$ tank); (3) the dashboard algorithm computes
consumption from injector pulse width and air-mass measurement,
which is a model of fuel flow rather than a direct measurement;
the model's accuracy is typically $\pm 5$-$10\,\%$. The first
measurement to repeat: the pump-to-pump fuel quantity, by filling
the same tank from the same pump on consecutive days with no driving
between. If the two fills agree to $\pm 0.5 \,\si{\liter}$, the
fuel-flow measurement is reproducible and the dashboard model is
likely the discrepancy; if they disagree by more, the pump auto-
shutoff is the dominant source and the fuel-quantity measurement
is not reproducible in the first place.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A laboratory pipette consistently delivers $98 \,\si{\micro\liter}$
when set to $100 \,\si{\micro\liter}$, with a spread of
$\pm 1 \,\si{\micro\liter}$. The pipette is used in a clinical assay
that has reported drift in its results over the last six months. State
whether the pipette is the cause and what evidence would distinguish
it from other candidates.

\paragraph{Rubric.} The pipette is precise (small spread,
$\pm 1\,\%$) but inaccurate (systematic $-2\,\%$ bias). A
\emph{constant} $2\,\%$ bias would produce a constant offset in
the assay result, not drift; drift implies a time-varying
component. The pipette is unlikely to be the proximate cause of
drift unless its bias has been growing over time, in which case
repeating the calibration weekly over the next six weeks would
show a trend. Other candidates: reagent degradation (assay
reagents drift with storage time and temperature); calibration of
the assay's reference standard (drift in the standard appears as
drift in every measurement against it); operator change (different
operators have different systematic pipetting habits); instrument
drift in the reading device (a spectrophotometer's lamp degrades
with use; recalibration tracks this). Distinguishing evidence:
historical pipette-calibration records (if available) show whether
the $2\,\%$ bias has been growing or constant; control-sample
trending across the drift period shows whether the reference is
drifting; cross-validation against a second pipette in the same
assay tests the pipette hypothesis directly.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Failure analysis}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
Read the executive summary of the Mars Climate Orbiter mishap report
\cite{acc:nasa-mco-mib}. Identify (a) the named root cause as the
report states it, (b) at least one contributing factor the report
identifies but does not call root, (c) one organisational cause
implicit in the report's narrative.

\paragraph{Rubric.} A credible reading identifies: (a) root cause as
the report states it, the failure to use metric units in the coding
of a ground software file (SM\_FORCES), used in trajectory models
that required newton-seconds but received pound-seconds; (b)
contributing factors the report enumerates, including the inadequate
verification of the navigation model against in-flight data, the
sparse small-forces telemetry, the under-staffing of the navigation
team, and the absence of independent cross-checks at the operations
interface; (c) implicit organisational cause, the fragmentation of
responsibility across the contractor (Lockheed Martin) for the
ground software and JPL for the navigation model, with no single
party accountable for end-to-end unit consistency. The report names
several recommendations on the verification, audit, and modelling
fronts; the organisational fragmentation is acknowledged less
directly.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
The Air Canada Flight 143 incident involved a unit conversion error,
an unserviceable fuel quantity processor, and procedural shortcuts
\cite{acc:lockwood-gimli1985}. State, in one sentence each, what
each of the three contributed. Then state which of the three a
measurement discipline of the kind described in section 1.5 would
have caught on its own and which would still require a procedural
change.

\paragraph{Rubric.} (1) The unit conversion error (using
$1.77 \,\text{lb/L}$ where the calculation required $\sim 0.80
\,\si{\kilogram\per\liter}$) understated the required fuel mass by
a factor of $\sim 2.2$; (2) the unserviceable fuel quantity processor
removed the automatic onboard cross-check that would normally have
flagged the discrepancy at the gate; (3) the procedural shortcut
that authorised dispatch without the failed processor working
removed the institutional barrier against the manual-calculation
error. The seven-component measurement discipline of section 1.5
would have caught (1) by enforcing the unit on every entry in the
calculation log and (2) by recording the instrument's calibration
status; the procedural authorisation in (3) is institutional and
requires a procedural change, not a measurement discipline alone.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
The Ariane 5 Flight 501 inquiry report states the cause as a software
exception in a routine that converted a 64-bit floating-point number
to a 16-bit signed integer \cite{acc:ariane501-ifr}. Argue, in 200
to 300 words, whether this is a measurement failure, a software
failure, or a verification failure. State which framing produces the
most useful corrective action.

\paragraph{Rubric.} A credible argument identifies that each framing
captures part of the failure. As measurement failure: the inertial
reference system was measuring a quantity (the horizontal bias)
that exceeded the range of its representation; the engineering
discipline of stating the range of every measured quantity before
the integration would have surfaced the mismatch. As software
failure: the type conversion was unprotected, the exception was
unhandled, and the backup SRI ran the same software and failed for
the same reason; defensive software practices (range checks,
exception handlers, type-conversion guards, dissimilar redundancy)
would have prevented the cascading failure. As verification failure:
the SRI software was qualified for the Ariane 4 flight envelope and
reused on Ariane 5 without re-qualification against the new
envelope; the discipline of validation-envelope tracking would have
flagged the gap. The most useful corrective action is the third
framing, because the proximate engineering decision was the reuse
of qualified software outside its qualification envelope. Software
practices and measurement discipline both contributed, but the
single change that would have prevented the failure was the
verification of the reused module against the new flight envelope
before integration.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Judgment}\pathtag{standard}

\begin{exercise}
The reader is presented with a beam designed to carry $10 \,\si{\kilo
\newton}$. The drawing is dimensioned in millimetres; the
specification is in newtons; the supplier's catalogue gives
dimensions in inches and allowable loads in lbf. Argue, in one
paragraph, what you do.

\paragraph{Discussion.} The working engineer converts the supplier
catalogue once, in writing, at the procurement interface, and
proceeds with the project in SI. The conversion is performed by a
second engineer or by an automated tool and the result is recorded
in the bill of materials with both the source value and the
converted value, so the audit trail survives. The temptation to
``work in inches because the supplier does'' is the failure mode
the chapter has described: every later calculation that touches
the beam's dimensions or load capacity would otherwise carry the
unit-conversion risk. The procurement interface is the right
location for the conversion, because it is the only point at which
the supplier's catalogue intersects the engineering work.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A senior colleague tells the reader that ``the measurement is
obvious; you do not need to write down the conditions.'' Write a
one-paragraph reply that takes the colleague seriously, agrees where
agreement is honest, and disagrees where disagreement is honest.

\paragraph{Discussion.} A credible reply concedes that some
measurements have stable conditions that genuinely do not require
recording for the immediate purpose (a routine calibration check
in a temperature-controlled lab under known protocol). It
disagrees on the broader principle: the recording serves the next
engineer, not the current one, and the current engineer is rarely
the one who has to defend the measurement after a failure. The
recording is also a defence against the engineer's own future
self, who will not remember the conditions in six months. The
discipline is to record by default and document the exceptions
deliberately, rather than skip by default and document only when
remembered. The senior colleague's point is valid for routine work
in well-characterised conditions; the chapter's discipline is
correct for the cases the routine eventually generalises to.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Identify a measurement in your immediate environment that you have
been making for years without realising it (e.g., judging when the
kettle is hot enough to brew tea, judging when the car is too far
from the curb, judging when an email is too long). State the implicit
quantity, the implicit instrument, and the implicit uncertainty.
Comment on whether this measurement could be improved by being made
explicit.

\paragraph{Discussion.} The exercise is open by design; the
reader's answer is the deliverable. A representative response: the
implicit quantity is the temperature of the kettle water; the
implicit instrument is the sound of the rolling boil; the implicit
uncertainty is the difference between the boiling-noise threshold
and the actual $100 \,\degC$, perhaps $\pm 2 \,\degC$. The
measurement could be made explicit with a thermometer for tea-
specific temperatures (green tea at $\sim 75 \,\degC$ benefits from
explicit measurement; black tea at $100 \,\degC$ does not). The
broader pattern: the craftsman's knowledge of section 1.2 is often
precise but unstated; making it explicit converts it into the
engineer's knowledge that can be audited, transferred, and
improved. The conversion is not always worth the effort, but it is
worth recognising the conversion exists.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Argue, in 200 to 300 words, whether the seven-component measurement
habit described in section 1.5 is too much for a working engineer
to maintain at the pace of real engineering. Take a position. Defend
it.

\paragraph{Discussion.} A credible argument names the actual cost
(the seven-component recording takes $\sim 30 \,\text{seconds}$
per measurement at a calibration log; in a production environment
this can become $\sim 10$ minutes per shift across all
measurements) and weighs it against the benefit (audit-defensibility,
diagnostic capability after a failure, traceability for regulatory
purposes). A position that the discipline is too much is defensible
in production environments where the regulatory and audit
requirements do not justify the cost (e.g., routine hobbyist
measurements); it is indefensible in production engineering where
failure consequences are non-trivial. A position that the discipline
is appropriate is defensible in any setting where the measurement
could be the subject of a future inquiry; the cost of recording is
small compared to the cost of an undefended failure. The chapter's
position is that the discipline is appropriate by default with
exceptions documented deliberately. The reader's argument is
credible if it engages with the cost-benefit at the level of
specific engineering settings rather than at the level of
principle.
\end{exercise}
