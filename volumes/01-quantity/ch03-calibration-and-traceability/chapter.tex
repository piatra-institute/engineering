\noindent
A measurement reports a number with a unit. The number's meaning
comes from a chain of comparisons that connects the reader's
instrument to a definition fixed in international agreement. This
chapter describes that chain: how a calibration is performed, how
working laboratory instruments inherit their meaning from primary
standards, how primary standards inherit theirs from defining
constants, and how the entire structure stays trustworthy across
decades, jurisdictions, and orders of magnitude.

\begin{mastery}
\textit{Historical sketch: NIST, BIPM, and how the modern
traceability chain came to be.}

The Bureau international des poids et mesures (BIPM) was
established by the Metre Convention of 20 May 1875, signed in Paris
by representatives of seventeen states. The original mission was to
maintain the international prototypes of the metre and the
kilogram and to disseminate copies to signatory states. The BIPM
sits today in Sevres, outside Paris, under the authority of the
General Conference on Weights and Measures (CGPM, in French
abbreviation); the CGPM meets every four years and decides the
formal evolution of the SI \cite{std:bipm-si2019}.

The United States's National Bureau of Standards (NBS) was
established by congressional act in 1901 in response to a recurring
problem: federal contracts in the late nineteenth century could not
be enforced because the country lacked a national metrology
institute with which contractors could compare their working
standards. The bureau initially occupied a single building on the
Mall in Washington, DC, and held primary standards for length,
mass, electrical quantities, and temperature; the standards
themselves were ultimately calibrated against the BIPM artefacts in
Paris. The bureau was renamed the National Institute of Standards
and Technology (NIST) in 1988 as part of the broader Omnibus Trade
and Competitiveness Act \cite{std:nist-traceability}.

Two structural developments shape current practice. The first is
the 1999 CIPM Mutual Recognition Arrangement (MRA), under which
participating national metrology institutes mutually accept each
other's calibration and measurement capabilities (CMCs) based on
documented peer comparisons. The MRA replaced a former system in
which each country independently verified each other country's
standards; the new system rests on shared technical evidence and
documented peer review. The second is the 2019 SI redefinition,
which moved the seven SI base units from artefact and
realisation-method definitions to fixed-constant definitions
\cite{std:bipm-si2019}. The redefinition was the culmination of
two decades of preparatory metrology work: each constant whose
value was to be fixed had to be measured to sufficient precision
that no working calibration would observably change when the
definition switched. The IPK's role as the artefact reference for
the kilogram ended on 20 May 2019, but the kilogram itself did not
visibly change, by design.

A working laboratory in 2026 inherits both structural developments.
Its accreditation under ISO/IEC 17025 traces, through the local
accreditation body (UKAS, ANAB, DAkkS, A2LA, and others), through
the local NMI's MRA-validated CMCs, to the BIPM. The constants
underlying its calibration certificates were measured to many
decimal places in the preparatory work for the 2019 redefinition;
the certificates state the constants implicitly, as the defining
basis of the SI.
\end{mastery}

\section{What a calibration is}\pathtag{core}

\engterm{Calibration} is the operation that establishes a relation
between an instrument's indication and quantity values provided by
measurement standards, with uncertainties stated on both sides of
the relation. The international vocabulary of metrology defines
calibration as the operation that, under specified conditions, in a
first step establishes a relation between the quantity values with
measurement uncertainties provided by measurement standards and
corresponding indications with associated measurement uncertainties,
and in a second step uses this information to establish a relation
for obtaining a measurement result from an indication
\cite{std:jcgm-vim}.

The definition has two steps.

A calibration begins as a comparison. The instrument produces an
indication; the reference provides a value with stated uncertainty;
the calibration records the relation between the two. Adjustment is
a separate operation. It changes the instrument after calibration
has shown what change is needed.

The report should identify the instrument, the reference, the date,
the conditions, the procedure, the indications, the corresponding
reference values, and the uncertainties. A recalibration interval
may appear on the certificate when required by the customer,
regulator, or quality system. Without records of calibration and
use, the owner has an instrument, but not enough evidence to claim
traceability for a measurement result.

A calibration also depends on the reference's documented status. The
laboratory voltage source is calibrated against a transfer standard;
the transfer standard is calibrated through a higher-level electrical
standard; the primary realisation is tied to Josephson and quantum
Hall effects and the SI constants. Each undocumented link adds an
undocumented uncertainty to the result below it.

\begin{archetype}[Interface]
The interface archetype appears here as the calibration interface.
An interface is a place where a physical quantity becomes a
representation that another system can use. A calibration interface
relates a mass, voltage, length, frequency, or temperature to an
instrument indication, with uncertainty attached to the relation. If
the interface is omitted, the account has not explained how the
quantity became usable.
\end{archetype}

\subsection{Calibration is not adjustment, and is not verification}

Three operations adjacent to calibration are routinely confused with
it. We distinguish them by their purpose:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \engterm{Calibration} establishes a relation between
    indication and reference. It produces information about the
    instrument; it does not change the instrument.
  \item \engterm{Adjustment} changes the instrument so that its
    indication corresponds, under specified conditions, to the value
    of the quantity being measured. Adjustment changes the
    instrument; it requires a prior calibration to know what change
    to make.
  \item \engterm{Verification} provides objective evidence that the
    instrument fulfils specified requirements. Verification
    compares calibration results against specified tolerances; it
    decides whether the instrument is acceptable for use.
\end{itemize}

A calibration produces a relation; an adjustment changes a setting;
a verification produces a yes-or-no judgement against a
specification; validation asks whether the measurement is fit for
its intended use.

\section{Primary, secondary, working standards}\pathtag{core}

A \engterm{measurement standard} is a realisation of the definition
of a quantity, with a stated value and measurement uncertainty, used
as a reference. Some standards are artifacts, some are instruments
or systems, and some are realised through quantum or thermodynamic
effects. Standards form a hierarchy.

\input{volumes/01-quantity/ch03-calibration-and-traceability/figures/fig-traceability-pyramid.tex}

\subsection{Primary standards}

A \engterm{primary standard} is a standard whose value has the
highest metrological qualities and whose value is accepted without
reference to other standards of the same quantity. In the post-2019
SI, primary standards realise the seven defining constants. The
realisation hardware is operated by national metrology institutes
(NMIs) and designated institutes, with mutual comparisons among them
\cite{std:bipm-si2019, std:bipm-cipm-mra}.

Examples of primary realisations under the post-2019 SI:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item The kilogram, realised through Kibble balances (which compare
    mechanical and electrical power) and through X-ray-crystal-
    density measurements of $^{28}$Si single crystals. Either method
    expresses mass in terms of the Planck constant
    $h$ \cite{std:bipm-si2019, web:nist-kibble-balance}.
  \item The metre, realised through frequency-stabilised laser
    interferometry against the cesium-133 hyperfine transition; the
    metre's definition is via the speed of light $c$ and the second
    \cite{std:bipm-si2019}.
  \item The second, realised through cesium-133 fountain clocks
    operating at major NMIs (NIST, NPL, PTB, and others); cesium
    fountain primary standards report fractional uncertainties on
    the order of $10^{-16}$ \cite{web:nist-cesium-fountain}.
  \item The volt, realised through Josephson voltage standards: arrays
    of Josephson junctions whose voltage steps depend only on the
    elementary charge and the Planck constant.
  \item The ohm, realised through the quantum Hall effect.
  \item The kelvin, realised through several primary thermometry
    methods (acoustic gas thermometry, dielectric-constant gas
    thermometry, radiation thermometry), each tied to the Boltzmann
    constant \cite{std:bipm-si2019, web:bipm-cct-its90}.
\end{itemize}

Primary realisation is specialised work. National metrology
institutes and designated institutes maintain selected realisations,
compare them internationally, and publish calibration and measurement
capabilities. The dissemination system rests on those realisations,
their comparisons, and the documented uncertainty budgets that
connect them to working laboratories \cite{std:bipm-cipm-mra}.

\subsection{Secondary standards}

A \engterm{secondary standard} is a standard whose value is
established by calibration with respect to a primary standard or
another higher-order reference for the same quantity. It
disseminates the unit through a documented calibration chain rather
than by definitional authority. Secondary standards may be physical
artifacts (a mass set, a voltage source, a length gauge, a frequency
reference) or laboratory instruments whose values are established by
calibration against a higher reference.

A secondary standard's calibration is recorded on a certificate
that names the primary standard, the date and conditions of
calibration, the measured value, the uncertainty, and the recommended
recalibration interval. A secondary standard with a current,
NMI-issued calibration certificate is the ordinary means by which a
working laboratory acquires access to the SI.

\subsection{Working standards}

A \engterm{working standard} is a standard used routinely to
calibrate or check the working instruments in a laboratory. Working
standards are calibrated against secondary standards (or, in some
laboratories, directly against primary standards), and they in turn
calibrate the laboratory's working instruments.

Most calibration laboratories operate at the working-standard layer.
A calibration laboratory in a manufacturing plant calibrates the
plant's instruments against its working standards; the working
standards are calibrated against secondary standards held by an
external accredited calibration body; the secondary standards are
calibrated against primary standards at an NMI; the primary
standards realise the SI defining constants.

A simple teaching chain has three to five visible links, but real
chains vary. A user may have a short chain through a direct NMI
calibration, or a longer chain through accredited laboratories,
reference standards, transfer standards, and internal checks. A
reader who has done a calibration in a working laboratory has
touched only the working-standard end. A reader who has done a
calibration at an accredited body has touched the secondary end. A
reader who has worked at an NMI has touched the primary end.

\section{The traceability chain and NIST (and BIPM)}\pathtag{core}

\engterm{Metrological traceability} is the property of a measurement
result whereby the result can be related to a reference through a
documented unbroken chain of calibrations, each contributing to the
measurement uncertainty \cite{std:jcgm-vim, std:nist-traceability}.
The chain is the operational realisation of the standards hierarchy
described above.

A traceable measurement result is one whose calibration certificate
can be followed link-by-link from the working instrument back to a
primary realisation. Each link in the chain has its own calibration
certificate. Each certificate names the higher-level reference and
contributes its own uncertainty to the cumulative uncertainty of the
final measurement. The chain's existence is what distinguishes a
measurement from an opinion; the chain's documentation is what
makes the measurement defendable in a courtroom, in a regulatory
audit, or in a dispute between two engineers.

\subsection{Who maintains the chain}

The chain is maintained by a global system of institutions, in
descending order of authority:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item The Bureau International des Poids et Mesures (BIPM), in
    S\`evres, France. The BIPM is the international body
    coordinating metrology under the Metre Convention of 1875. It
    maintains some primary realisations directly, conducts
    international comparisons among national metrology institutes,
    and operates the CIPM Mutual Recognition Arrangement
    \cite{std:bipm-cipm-mra}.
  \item The General Conference on Weights and Measures (CGPM),
    which adopts the SI's structural decisions. The CGPM is the
    political body whose 26th meeting in 2018 fixed the seven
    defining constants in their current form
    \cite{std:bipm-si2019}.
  \item National metrology institutes and designated institutes.
    NIST, NPL, PTB, NRC, NMIJ, NIM, VNIIM, CSIR-NPLI, and other
    bodies operate national or designated metrology functions; some
    countries use more than one designated institute for different
    fields. These institutes maintain realisations, participate in
    comparisons, and issue calibration certificates within published
    scopes \cite{std:bipm-cipm-mra}.
  \item Accredited calibration laboratories, assessed against
    ISO/IEC 17025 \cite{std:iso-iec-17025} by recognised accreditation
    bodies. These laboratories disseminate calibration services to
    industry and research within their accredited scopes.
  \item Working laboratories within manufacturers, research
    institutes, and service organisations, which calibrate their
    working instruments against secondary standards held by the
    accredited bodies or, where their volume justifies it, by the
    NMI directly.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{The CIPM Mutual Recognition Arrangement}

A manufacturer wants a calibration certificate issued under one
national metrology system to be intelligible and acceptable in
another. The CIPM Mutual Recognition Arrangement (CIPM MRA),
signed at S\`evres on 14 October 1999, supports that by recognising
NMI standards, calibration certificates, and published calibration
and measurement capabilities through comparisons, quality systems,
and peer review \cite{std:bipm-cipm-mra}.

Under the MRA, NMIs and designated institutes participate in periodic
key comparisons (international round-robin measurements of the same
quantity) whose results are published in the BIPM's KCDB (Key
Comparison Database). Legal or regulatory acceptance still depends
on the regulator, the accreditation scope, and the specific
requirement being applied; the MRA establishes the technical and
institutional basis for recognition without by itself overriding
domestic legal frameworks.

\subsection{ISO/IEC 17025 accreditation}

For laboratories that are not NMIs, the operational requirement is
accreditation under ISO/IEC 17025 \cite{std:iso-iec-17025}. The
standard specifies the management-system requirements (records,
calibration intervals, traceability documentation, personnel
competence, document control) and the technical requirements
(measurement uncertainty methodology, equipment, environmental
conditions, methods, sampling) that an accredited calibration
laboratory must meet.

ISO/IEC 17025 accreditation has direct and recurring cost:
application, assessment, corrective action, proficiency testing,
documentation, and surveillance. Assessment cycles are set by
accreditation bodies, so the cycle varies by jurisdiction. UKAS, for
example, uses annual surveillance with periodic full reassessment;
other accreditation bodies use different cycles. The accreditation
can be withdrawn when surveillance findings indicate the laboratory
has departed from the standard.

A reader who buys a calibration service from an accredited laboratory
acquires a certificate that names the accredited scope, the methods,
the references, and the uncertainty. A reader who buys a calibration
service outside an accredited scope must inspect the evidence
directly: method, reference standards, uncertainty budget,
competence, and traceability statement. Accreditation packages that
evidence in a recognised form; it does not replace the user's duty
to read the certificate.

\section{Calibration vs validation vs verification}\pathtag{standard}

Working engineering separates calibration, validation, and
verification because each answers a different audit question. The
vocabulary comes from VIM and quality-system standards
\cite{std:jcgm-vim}; sector-specific sources cover validation and
qualification at a level the general standards do not.

\engterm{Calibration} establishes a relationship between an
indication and a reference value. It is a measurement-system
operation, and we have already defined it above.

\engterm{Validation} confirms, through the provision of objective
evidence, that the requirements for a specific intended use or
application have been fulfilled. Validation is fitness-for-purpose:
it asks whether the measurement, the method, or the system is
suitable for its application. A method that is correctly calibrated
may still fail validation, because the calibrated quantity is not
the quantity the application actually requires. A pH probe
calibrated against pH-7 buffer is calibrated; whether it is
\emph{validated} for measuring the pH of a patient's blood depends
on whether the matrix interferences, the temperature compensation,
the response time, and the safety considerations have all been
documented to be acceptable for that use.

\engterm{Verification} provides objective evidence that specified
requirements have been fulfilled. Verification is conformance: it
asks whether a measurement, method, or system meets a specification.
Verification is mechanical relative to validation: a verification
procedure compares results against criteria, and produces a yes
or no.

The distinctions matter operationally. In a regulated environment
(medical devices, aerospace, nuclear, pharmaceuticals), an
instrument typically has a calibration certificate, a validation
record, and a verification log. Each plays a different role: the
calibration says how the instrument relates to the SI; the
validation says the calibrated instrument is suitable for the
application; the verification says the instrument continues to
meet specification under operational conditions.

A typical chain of evidence for a regulated measurement: the
manufacturer's calibration certificate provides traceability to the
SI; the user's validation report, drawing on the calibration
certificate plus application-specific tests, demonstrates fitness
for the intended use; the user's verification log, populated daily
or per-batch, demonstrates ongoing conformance.

\begin{mastery}
The fourth term, \engterm{qualification}, is used in
pharmaceutical and life-science contexts to mean the documented
demonstration that a system, equipment, or facility consistently
meets predetermined specifications. Qualification has substages
(installation qualification, operational qualification, performance
qualification) familiar from FDA's process-validation vocabulary.
The structure is parallel to the calibration-validation-verification
distinction; the vocabulary differs by industry. A reader working in
regulated industries learns the local terminology first.
\end{mastery}

\subsection{Worked workflow: a pH probe in three regulatory tiers}

The distinction between calibration, validation, and verification is
easiest to see by walking through one instrument across three
regulatory tiers. Consider a pH probe used in three contexts:
educational research, industrial process control, and clinical
diagnostics.

\engterm{Tier 1: educational research.}
In an undergraduate teaching laboratory, the pH probe is calibrated
weekly against pH 4 and pH 10 buffers from the lab's standard
buffer set. The calibration consists of immersing the probe in each
buffer, recording the millivolt reading, and computing the
calibration line (slope and offset) from the two points. The
calibration certificate, if there is one, is the lab notebook entry
showing the calibration line and the date.

No validation is performed: the instrument is used for the
measurement it was designed for, and the application's accuracy
requirement (typically $\pm 0.1\,\text{pH}$ for educational work) is
much looser than the probe's specified accuracy. No verification is
performed in the formal sense, though the calibration itself acts
as an informal verification.

\engterm{Tier 2: industrial process control.}
In a paint manufacturer's mixing room, the same pH probe model is
used to monitor a slurry whose viscosity, opacity, and electrolyte
content differ from the calibration buffers. The probe is
calibrated weekly against traceable buffers (accredited-lab
calibrated, with documented uncertainty), and the calibration
record is filed in the laboratory's quality management system.

A validation report establishes that the probe is fit for measuring
the slurry's pH. The report addresses several application-specific
concerns: matrix interference (the slurry's solids may foul the
probe's junction; the validation tests how quickly the probe
recovers after cleaning); response time (the slurry's viscosity may
slow the probe's diffusive equilibration; the validation tests
response on representative samples); temperature compensation (the
slurry runs at $35\,\si{\degreeCelsius}$, not the buffer's
$25\,\si{\degreeCelsius}$; the validation tests the temperature
compensation circuit's accuracy). The validation report is
reviewed and approved before the probe enters service.

Daily verification consists of a quick-check measurement against
a single pH-7 buffer at the start of each shift. The result is
recorded on a control chart. An out-of-spec verification triggers
recalibration; an in-spec verification permits production
measurement to proceed.

\engterm{Tier 3: clinical diagnostics.}
In a hospital blood-gas analyser, the pH probe is calibrated
automatically every $30\,\si{\minute}$ against on-instrument buffer
solutions whose composition is certified by the manufacturer. The
calibration is logged automatically; out-of-spec calibrations
trigger an instrument alarm and remove the analyser from service.

The validation, performed by the manufacturer before clinical
release, addresses regulatory requirements under the relevant
medical-device authority (FDA, EMA, MHRA, depending on jurisdiction).
The validation report covers analytical performance (linearity,
precision, accuracy, interferences, carry-over), clinical performance
(correlation with reference methods on patient samples across the
relevant clinical range), and risk analysis (effect of incorrect
measurements on clinical decisions). The hospital, before deploying
the analyser, performs its own deployment-specific validation
covering the local patient population's clinical range and the
local laboratory's quality-control workflow.

Verification is per-patient: each blood-gas sample is reported with
its measurement uncertainty, and the result is compared against the
patient's clinical-decision thresholds. Out-of-clinical-range
results trigger clinical follow-up; out-of-analytical-control
results trigger analyser troubleshooting.

The same instrument operates in all three contexts. The difference
is the surrounding chain of evidence the application demands. The
educational use needs a calibration line. The industrial use needs
a calibration line and a validation report. The clinical use needs
all three plus a regulatory-compliant quality management system
around them. The vocabulary supports the audit at each tier.

\section{Drift, recalibration intervals, the cost of
calibration}\pathtag{core}

A calibration is a snapshot. Instruments drift after calibration:
electronic components age, mechanical bearings wear, optical
surfaces accumulate film, environmental exposure shifts component
parameters, packaging stresses re-distribute over time. Drift is the
mechanism that makes recalibration intervals necessary.

\subsection{Drift mechanisms}

Drift mechanisms vary by quantity and instrument type, but a few
patterns recur:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \engterm{Component aging}: electronic components (resistors,
    capacitors, voltage references) drift on a logarithmic timescale
    after manufacturing. The first year typically sees the largest
    drift; subsequent years show progressively smaller annual
    drift, often parameterised as a fractional change per
    decade-of-age.
  \item \engterm{Mechanical wear}: bearings, contacts, springs, and
    sliding surfaces wear with use. The wear rate depends on duty
    cycle and operating environment; an instrument used twice a
    week wears at a different rate from one used continuously.
  \item \engterm{Surface contamination}: optical surfaces and
    high-precision mechanical surfaces accumulate films from
    handling, atmospheric deposition, and outgassing.
    Contamination usually shifts the calibration in a slowly
    monotonic direction until cleaning resets it.
  \item \engterm{Thermal cycling}: instruments stored or operated
    across a temperature range can experience permanent shifts as
    components transit phase-stress regimes. The effect is usually
    bounded but not always reversible.
  \item \engterm{Mechanical shock}: a dropped instrument or a
    shipping impact can produce a step shift in calibration that
    appears as a discrete event rather than gradual drift.
\end{itemize}

\subsection{Setting recalibration intervals}

The recalibration interval for an instrument is set by the rate at
which the instrument drifts beyond the acceptable tolerance for its
application. The procedure is, in principle, straightforward:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Calibrate the instrument repeatedly over an extended period
    without intermediate adjustment, recording each calibration's
    deviation from the previous reference.
  \item Estimate the rate of drift and its uncertainty from the
    history.
  \item Compute the interval at which the cumulative drift would
    reach the application's acceptable tolerance.
  \item Set the recalibration interval at a fraction of that
    computed interval, with the fraction chosen to give an
    acceptable risk of an instrument exceeding tolerance between
    calibrations.
\end{enumerate}

Many laboratories begin with a manufacturer recommendation, customer
requirement, regulatory requirement, or local default interval, then
adjust the interval using calibration history and risk. The general
rule is drift risk versus measurement stakes, not a universal
calendar interval; sourced examples should be cited by instrument
class and jurisdiction rather than asserted as universal practice.

\subsection{When statistical control suffices}

A laboratory that uses an instrument continuously has a different
option: statistical process control on the instrument's own output.
If the laboratory measures known control samples alongside its
working measurements and the control results stay within a defined
band, the instrument is operating within its calibration. If the
control results drift, the instrument is recalibrated regardless of
the calendar interval.

Statistical control reduces unnecessary recalibration on stable
instruments and triggers earlier-than-scheduled recalibration on
drifting ones. Control charts (Shewhart, EWMA, CUSUM) are the
operational tools; we take them up in chapter 8 of this volume on
statistics for engineers.

\subsection{The cost of calibration}

Calibration costs money. The actual price varies by accredited
status, scope, points measured, turnaround, on-site work, and
jurisdiction; a sourced 2026 price table from named providers is the
right reference rather than a generic range, and live prices are
better placed in a companion note than in the permanent text.

Annual recalibration of a working laboratory's instrument inventory
is a meaningful operating cost. Small laboratories may spend
modest amounts per year on calibration services; high-volume
laboratories spend orders of magnitude more; national-scale
metrology operations are larger still. The cost is one factor in
the recalibration-interval decision: too-frequent recalibration is
wasted money; too-infrequent recalibration risks unacceptable drift.
The interval that balances the two is, in practice, set by
regulatory requirement first and economic optimisation second.

\begin{estimation}
\textbf{What is the recalibration interval for a kitchen scale
used to weigh meal portions to within $\pm 5 \,\si{\gram}$?}

\smallskip
\textit{Estimate, before reading on.}

\smallskip
A typical household kitchen scale has a manufacturer-claimed
specification around $\pm 1 \,\si{\gram}$ at $100 \,\si{\gram}$ and
$\pm 5 \,\si{\gram}$ at $1 \,\si{\kilogram}$. Drift mechanisms for a
strain-gauge kitchen scale include load-cell creep, electronics
aging, and zero-point drift from temperature and humidity exposure.
Manufacturer specifications typically state initial accuracy without
naming a drift rate.

A working estimate starts from the user's tolerance, not from the
scale's display resolution. If the application allows
$\pm 5 \,\si{\gram}$ and the scale repeats within about
$\pm 1 \,\si{\gram}$ under a local check, the immediate risk is
small. A yearly check with two or three known masses is usually
more defensible than asserting that a household scale follows a
formal recalibration interval. If the check shows a bias near the
tolerance, the scale is no longer fit for that use.

If the application requires $\pm 1 \,\si{\gram}$, the tolerance is
the same size as the scale's likely repeatability, and the correct
engineering answer is a better instrument or a lower-stakes
measurement. The headroom-to-tolerance ratio is the lever;
resolution requirements above the instrument's specification cannot
be reached by recalibration alone.
\end{estimation}

\subsection{Worked case: a small QC laboratory designs its calibration schedule}

A pharmaceutical quality-control laboratory measures the active
ingredient content of finished tablets using ultraviolet absorbance.
The laboratory's measurement uncertainty target is $\pm 1\%$
($k = 2$) on the active ingredient mass per tablet. The
instruments involved are a spectrophotometer (absorbance), an
analytical balance (sample mass), and a temperature-controlled
sample cuvette holder. The question the laboratory must answer:
which standards does it buy, at what tier of traceability, and how
often does it recalibrate each instrument?

\engterm{Step 1: derive the per-instrument uncertainty target from
the application's tolerance.}
The total measurement uncertainty is the quadrature combination of
the contributions from each instrument plus the method
contribution. With three roughly comparable instrument
contributions and a smaller method term, each instrument's
expanded uncertainty must lie around $\pm 0.5\%$ to give a combined
$\pm 1\%$. The standard uncertainty at $k = 1$ is therefore
$\pm 0.25\%$ per instrument.

\engterm{Step 2: select standards at the appropriate tier.}
For each instrument the laboratory consults the NMI directory of
accredited calibration providers and selects the lowest tier whose
uncertainty satisfies the application's working margin.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Spectrophotometer: photometric accuracy traceable to NIST
    SRM 930 (a set of neutral-density glass filters with assigned
    transmittance values at standard wavelengths). The accredited
    calibration uncertainty is around $\pm 0.2\%$; the laboratory
    buys the filter set and an annual recalibration of the filters
    against the SRM. The instrument's wavelength scale is calibrated
    separately against a holmium oxide reference solution.
  \item Analytical balance: traceable to mass standards calibrated
    against the local NMI's working kilogram. The accredited
    calibration uncertainty for a class E1 weight set at
    $100\,\si{\milli\gram}$ is in the low microgram range; the
    laboratory purchases an E2 working weight set (one tier down,
    around ten microgram uncertainty) and has it recalibrated every
    two years.
  \item Temperature-controlled cuvette holder: traceable to ITS-90
    fixed points at the gallium and indium points. A calibrated
    platinum resistance thermometer (PRT) at one-tier uncertainty
    serves as the working reference; the PRT is recalibrated every
    two years.
\end{itemize}

\engterm{Step 3: set recalibration intervals from drift history and
risk.}
The instrument manufacturers publish typical drift rates: the
spectrophotometer's photometric accuracy drifts at roughly
$0.05\%/$year; the balance drifts at roughly $1\,\si{\micro\gram}/
\si{\month}$ under continuous use; the PRT drifts at roughly
$0.005\,\si{\kelvin}/$year. Each rate is below its instrument's
allocated uncertainty over one year, so the calendar-driven
recalibration interval is one year for each instrument.

The laboratory layers a statistical-control check on top: every
operating day, the spectrophotometer measures a known photometric
control filter and the result is plotted on a Shewhart chart; the
balance measures a known check weight and the result is plotted on
a CUSUM chart; the cuvette holder temperature is checked against
the ice point twice weekly. Statistical-control out-of-spec triggers
recalibration regardless of the calendar.

\engterm{Step 4: choose in-house vs accredited-lab vs vendor
calibration.}
For each instrument the laboratory does a total-cost-of-ownership
calculation. In-house calibration requires capital investment in
working standards plus analyst time; accredited-lab calibration
requires a service contract plus instrument downtime; vendor
calibration is convenient but ties the laboratory to a specific
supplier and to the supplier's calibration uncertainty.

The numerical comparison for the balance: in-house calibration with
a purchased E1 weight set costs approximately \$8{,}000 capital and
\$1{,}500 every two years for recharacterisation of the weights,
amortised over the eight-year service life of the balance and an
estimated 1{,}000 balance calibrations over that life, yielding
roughly \$12 per calibration. Accredited-lab calibration costs
approximately \$400 per session, with two calibrations needed per
year over the eight-year life: \$6{,}400 total, or \$48 per
calibration if the balance is calibrated only annually and only the
recharacterisation cost is amortised. For this volume of work the
in-house route is the lower-cost option; for a laboratory with
fewer balances or lower calibration frequency, the accredited-lab
route can be cheaper.

\engterm{Step 5: document and audit.}
The calibration schedule is filed as a controlled document in the
laboratory's quality management system, reviewed annually by the
quality-assurance officer, and audited by the regulatory inspector
during the laboratory's biennial inspection. The audit checks
schedule adherence, recalibration uncertainty against the
application's tolerance, and traceability chain to the relevant
SI realisation.

The reference implementation of the cost-vs-interval calculation
is in \texttt{code/recalibration\_cost.py}, which takes a drift
rate, tolerance, calibration cost, and out-of-tolerance cost and
returns the optimal interval. The reference uncertainty-chain
calculation is in \texttt{code/chain\_uncertainty.py}, which
propagates uncertainties from primary standard through working
standard to the instrument.

\textbf{What is the calibration-and-recalibration cost for a
mid-sized US semiconductor fab's metrology fleet?}

\smallskip
\textit{Estimate, before reading on.}

\smallskip
A mid-sized 300\,mm semiconductor fab operates approximately
$10{,}000$ instruments (mass-flow controllers, pressure
transducers, temperature probes, voltage references, leak

thickness gauges, alignment systems). Annual calibration cost per

\,\text{million}/\text{yr}$. Add the in-house metrology workforce
($\sim 50$ engineers at $\$150{,}000$ each fully loaded = $\$7.5
\,\text{million}/\text{yr}$) and the reference-equipment depreciation

\section{Cross-domain examples: voltage, mass, length, frequency,
temperature}\pathtag{standard}

At the primary-standard end, each quantity has its own apparatus,
comparison programme, and uncertainty budget. Five examples show how
different the realisations are.

\subsection{Voltage}

The volt is realised through Josephson junctions. A Josephson junction
is a thin insulating barrier between two superconductors; under
microwave irradiation, the voltage across the junction takes
quantised values $V_n = n f / K_J$, where $f$ is the microwave
frequency, $n$ is an integer, and $K_J = 2 e / h$ is the Josephson
constant. The Josephson constant depends only on the elementary
charge $e$ and the Planck constant $h$, both of which are defining
constants in the post-2019 SI \cite{std:bipm-si2019}. The
relationship makes the volt a derived realisation whose accuracy is
limited by the frequency reference rather than by an artefact: a
frequency reference at fractional uncertainty $10^{-14}$ delivers a
voltage reference at the same fractional uncertainty, modulo the
junction's electronic stability.

A Josephson voltage standard array consists of thousands of
junctions in series; the array's output is a precisely-determined
voltage at any chosen integer-step value. National metrology
institutes operate Josephson arrays as primary voltage standards;
the realised uncertainty depends on the system, the comparison
method, and the documented uncertainty budget. NIST's Programmable
Josephson Voltage Standard (PJVS) at Gaithersburg generates
voltages from $-10\,\si{\volt}$ to $+10\,\si{\volt}$ at uncertainties
below the part-per-billion level, current as of 2026
\cite{web:nist-pjvs}.

The dissemination chain. A national metrology institute's Josephson
array calibrates a secondary voltage standard, typically a
Zener-based reference such as the Fluke 732B or its commercial
successors. The Zener reference has internal-stability uncertainty
around $0.5$ to $2$ parts per million per year; it is shipped to
accredited calibration laboratories or operated as the laboratory's
local working standard. The accredited laboratory's Zener references
are calibrated annually or biennially against the NMI's Josephson
array, with documented uncertainty budgets covering thermal
coefficient, transport stress, and analyst-time effects.

The working laboratory's digital multimeter (DMM) is calibrated
against the laboratory's Zener reference. A 7.5-digit bench DMM
specification at $10\,\si{\volt}$ runs around $2$ to $5$ parts per
million one-year accuracy; the calibration certificate states the
indication-vs-applied-voltage table for several test points,
together with the expanded uncertainty at each point. The customer
who buys the DMM and uses it without sending it for recalibration
inherits the manufacturer's stability claim plus whatever drift the
unit accumulates between calibrations.

The voltage traceability chain is the simplest of the working
electrical quantities because it rests on two of the SI defining
constants and a quantum-mechanical resonance condition; the chain
for resistance (via the quantum Hall effect) and for current (via
the volt and the ohm and Ohm's law) builds on the same foundation.
Volume IV Chapter 5 develops the underlying physics.

\subsection{Mass}

The kilogram, since 2019, is realised through methods tied to the
fixed value of the Planck constant, including Kibble balances and
silicon-sphere XRCD measurements \cite{std:bipm-si2019,
web:nist-kibble-balance}. A Kibble balance compares mechanical and
electrical power: the weight of a test mass against gravity is
balanced by the electromagnetic force on a coil carrying current in
a magnetic field; the same coil, moved at constant velocity through
the same field, generates an electromotive force. The combination of
weighing-mode and moving-mode measurements yields a mass whose
voltage and resistance measurements connect to Josephson and quantum
Hall effects, and so to the SI defining constants.

The full chain runs as follows. A Kibble balance at a national
metrology institute realises the kilogram at the primary level with
fractional uncertainty around a few parts in $10^{8}$, current as
of 2026. A platinum-iridium working standard (a survivor of the
artefact era, now demoted to working-reference status) is
calibrated against the Kibble balance every few years. Working
laboratory mass sets (class E1, then E2, then F1, etc. in the OIML
classification) are calibrated against the working standard
through a dissemination chain that may run two or three steps
before reaching the customer's laboratory balance. Each step adds
uncertainty: a class E2 weight at $1\,\si{\kilo\gram}$ is typically
calibrated at $\pm 1.5\,\si{\milli\gram}$, three orders of magnitude
worse than the Kibble realisation. The chain is engineered so that
each user gets the working precision required for the application,
not the primary precision the Kibble balance could in principle
deliver.

Several national metrology institutes and the BIPM operate primary
mass realisations. The international prototype of the kilogram
(IPK), the platinum-iridium cylinder used as the artifact reference
from 1889 until the revised SI took effect on 20 May 2019, is no
longer the definition; the cylinder has been preserved and is now
itself calibrated against Kibble-balance and XRCD realisations. The
cylinder's displacement from a defining role ended a 130-year
dependence of the world's mass measurements on a single physical
object \cite{web:bipm-kilogram-history}. The IPK's slow mass drift
against its sister copies (documented at roughly $50\,\si{\micro
\gram}$ over the 130-year period) was one of the practical
arguments for the redefinition: an artefact whose own mass cannot
be verified without circular reference is a poor anchor for a unit.
The IPK case is registered as a standards-history reference in the
named-cases registry at
\repopath{docs/research/accidents/ipk-mass-drift-1889-2019.md}.

The post-redefinition dissemination chain has no measurable
discontinuity at the 2019 boundary: the redefinition's preparatory
work tied the new constants to the old artefact at every NMI
simultaneously, so a calibration certificate issued in 2018 and a
certificate issued in 2020 differ only in the cited constants, not
in their numerical content. The redefinition was deliberately
engineered for continuity \cite{std:bipm-si2019}.

\subsection{Length}

The metre is realised through frequency-stabilised laser
interferometry. The metre's definition pins it through the speed of
light $c$ and the second's definition: a metre is the length light
travels in vacuum during $1/c$ of a second, with $c = 299{,}792{,}458
\,\si{\meter\per\second}$ exact and the second realised through
cesium-133 hyperfine-transition fountain clocks. National metrology
institutes operate iodine-stabilised helium-neon lasers and femtosecond
optical-frequency-comb references whose absolute frequencies are
known to fractional uncertainty around $10^{-14}$, with corresponding
length scales realised through interferometric measurement.

The reader's working length standard is typically a gauge block, a
laser distance meter, or a coordinate measuring machine, calibrated
against secondary length standards held by a calibration laboratory
and ultimately against the institute's interferometric realisation.

The dissemination chain for length has more steps than the voltage
chain because length measurements span eleven orders of magnitude
in working practice (from sub-nanometre semiconductor lithography
through kilometre-scale geodetic surveying). At the primary level,
the NMI's iodine-stabilised helium-neon laser is calibrated against
the optical-frequency comb; at the secondary level, gauge blocks
are calibrated against the iodine-laser interferometer; at the
working level, customer instruments (micrometers, coordinate
measuring machines, laser distance meters) are calibrated against
the gauge blocks. Each step adds uncertainty: a gauge block calibrated
at primary level carries an uncertainty around $20\,\si{\nano\meter}$;
a CMM calibrated against a gauge-block set carries an uncertainty an
order of magnitude larger; a hand-held laser distance meter for
construction work, an order of magnitude beyond that.

The silent failure mode in length metrology is thermal expansion.
Steel has a linear thermal expansion coefficient of approximately
$11.5 \times 10^{-6}\,\si{\per\kelvin}$, so a $1\,\si{\meter}$ steel
gauge block at $1\,\si{\kelvin}$ above the $20\,\si{\degreeCelsius}$
standard temperature reads $11.5\,\si{\micro\meter}$ longer than its
calibrated value. Precision length measurements are conducted in
temperature-stabilised laboratories at the standard temperature,
typically controlled to within $\pm 0.1\,\si{\kelvin}$; the gauge
block's temperature is logged on the calibration certificate, and
the user applies a temperature correction at the time of
measurement. A measurement that ignores the thermal correction
carries an uncontrolled error that can dominate the calibrated
uncertainty.

\subsection{Frequency}

The second is realised through cesium-133 fountain atomic clocks at
the major national metrology institutes. A fountain clock launches
a cloud of cesium atoms upward through a microwave cavity; the
atoms interact with the cavity's microwave field on the way up and
on the way down; the resonance condition for the cesium-133
hyperfine transition determines the cavity's frequency to fractional
uncertainty around $10^{-16}$. The defining constant is
$\Delta\nu_{\text{Cs}} = 9{,}192{,}631{,}770 \,\si{\hertz}$, exact
\cite{std:bipm-si2019}.

Frequency is the SI quantity realised at the lowest fractional
uncertainty of any base quantity; consequently, where engineering
quantities can be expressed as frequencies (timing intervals,
relative phases, ratios that reduce to frequency comparisons), they
can be measured at uncertainties orders of magnitude better than
direct measurements of position or amplitude. The trick of recasting
a measurement as a frequency comparison is one of the central tools
of precision metrology.

\subsection{Temperature}

The kelvin's realisation is the most operationally diverse of the
seven base units. Since 2019 the kelvin is defined through the
Boltzmann constant $k_{\text{B}} = 1.380\,649 \times 10^{-23}
\,\si{\joule\per\kelvin}$. Realisation at primary level is through
acoustic gas thermometry (measuring the speed of sound in a gas of
known composition and pressure to extract the gas-kinetic
temperature), dielectric-constant gas thermometry (measuring the
temperature dependence of the dielectric constant), or radiation
thermometry (Planck's law fits to spectral radiance from a
blackbody source) \cite{std:bipm-si2019}.

For working laboratories, the kelvin is commonly disseminated
through the International Temperature Scale of 1990 (ITS-90), a
practical scale defined by fixed points, interpolation instruments,
and interpolation equations over specified ranges
\cite{web:bipm-cct-its90}. Its fixed points include cryogenic points,
the triple point of water, the melting point of gallium, and the
freezing points of indium, tin, zinc, aluminium, silver, gold, and
copper. ITS-90 approximates thermodynamic temperature over its
defined ranges and remains the working scale for many calibrations.
Working thermocouples, platinum resistance thermometers, and
radiation pyrometers are calibrated against ITS-90 fixed points or
ITS-90-calibrated reference instruments.

The diversity of realisation methods reflects the diversity of
temperature engineering: a temperature instrument operating at
$4 \,\si{\kelvin}$ in a cryogenic experiment, at $300 \,\si{\kelvin}$
in a clinical context, and at $1500 \,\si{\kelvin}$ in a
turbomachinery test cell sees three different realisation
technologies tied to one defining constant.

A worked example of ITS-90 dissemination. A pharmaceutical
laboratory needs to verify that an autoclave reaches and holds
$121\,\si{\degreeCelsius}$ for $15\,\si{\minute}$. The laboratory
buys a platinum resistance thermometer (PRT) with a calibration
certificate stating the indication-vs-temperature relationship at
six points, including ITS-90's gallium triple point
($29.7646\,\si{\degreeCelsius}$, fixed by ITS-90), the indium
freezing point ($156.5985\,\si{\degreeCelsius}$, fixed by ITS-90),
and the triple point of water ($0.01\,\si{\degreeCelsius}$, fixed
by ITS-90). The PRT's expanded uncertainty at $121\,\si{\degreeCelsius}$
is $\pm 0.02\,\si{\kelvin}$ at $k = 2$, derived from interpolation
between the indium freezing point ($156.6\,\si{\degreeCelsius}$)
and the gallium triple point ($29.8\,\si{\degreeCelsius}$).

The PRT is calibrated against the indium and gallium fixed-point
cells held at the accredited laboratory's primary metrology bench;
the fixed-point cells were calibrated against the local NMI's own
fixed-point cells, themselves part of the national ITS-90
realisation. The calibration certificate states the
indication-vs-temperature points, the interpolation equation, the
fixed-point cell certifications, and the expanded uncertainty. The
pharmaceutical laboratory uses the PRT as its working reference
temperature standard, against which working autoclave probes are
checked. The end-user autoclave probe's calibration ultimately
traces, through the PRT, through the fixed-point cells, through the
NMI's national realisation, to the Boltzmann constant defining
$k_{\text{B}}$. The chain operates daily without the user
explicitly invoking it; the user invokes it explicitly only when an
audit requires the documented chain.

\section{Uncertainty in the calibration chain}\pathtag{core}

A calibration certificate's central number is not the value but the
uncertainty. The value tells the customer what the instrument
reads relative to the reference; the uncertainty tells the customer
how tightly they may rely on the calibration in their downstream
budget. The discipline that produces both numbers is the
\engterm{calibration uncertainty budget}, the working artefact this
section makes operational.

Chapter 4 introduces uncertainty budgets at the working-engineer
level. This section narrows them to the specific structure of a
calibration measurement: an instrument being compared against a
reference, with the comparison itself contributing uncertainty
alongside the reference's own contribution and the chain of
predecessors above it.

\subsection{The chain budget's structure}

A calibration uncertainty budget partitions the certificate's
reported uncertainty into four canonical contributions:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \engterm{Reference uncertainty}: the uncertainty of the
    standard used to perform the calibration, taken from its own
    calibration certificate. This component is Type B in the
    working calibration's own evaluation, even though it was
    Type A in the upstream calibration that produced it.
  \item \engterm{Comparison uncertainty}: the uncertainty introduced
    by the calibration procedure itself, including operator
    technique, instrument-to-reference coupling effects, and the
    measurement chain's own internal noise. This component is
    typically Type A, evaluated by repeating the comparison
    multiple times and computing the standard deviation.
  \item \engterm{Environmental uncertainty}: contributions from
    temperature, humidity, vibration, electromagnetic interference,
    and other environmental effects that influence the comparison.
    These are typically Type B contributions evaluated from
    instrument specifications or independent measurements of the
    environmental quantity.
  \item \engterm{Instrument-under-test uncertainty}: the
    repeatability, hysteresis, drift between checks, and other
    intrinsic contributions from the instrument being calibrated.
    These are Type A contributions from the calibration data plus
    Type B contributions from the instrument's prior history.
\end{itemize}

The combined standard uncertainty is the quadrature sum of the
four contributions (with their sensitivity coefficients, when the
contributions are not in the same units as the output). The
expanded uncertainty at the laboratory's chosen coverage factor
(typically $k = 2$ for accredited services) is reported on the
certificate.

\subsection{Worked chain budget: a $10\,\si{\volt}$ DMM calibration}

A typical calibration scenario: an accredited laboratory calibrates
a six-and-a-half-digit benchtop DMM at the $10\,\si{\volt}$ DC point
against a transfer-grade Zener voltage reference that the laboratory
itself had calibrated against a primary Josephson voltage standard
at a national metrology institute six months earlier.

The combined standard uncertainty is computed by quadrature:
$u_{c} = \sqrt{0.5^{2} + 0.4^{2} + 0.3^{2} + 0.2^{2} + 0.15^{2}
+ 0.1^{2}} \approx 0.78\,\si{\micro\volt}$. The expanded

The certificate would report this as: "At $10\,\si{\volt}$ DC the
instrument reads $V_{\text{ref}} + 0.3\,\si{\micro\volt}$ with
expanded uncertainty $1.6\,\si{\micro\volt}$ at $k = 2$" (the
$0.3\,\si{\micro\volt}$ being the laboratory's measured calibration
offset of the DMM against the reference).

\subsection{The certificate to the customer's budget}

The customer who buys the calibrated DMM and uses it in their own
measurement inherits the certificate's expanded uncertainty as a
Type B contribution to their own budget. The flow is downward
through the chain: the NMI's Josephson realisation gives the
laboratory's Zener a Type B uncertainty of perhaps $0.3\,\si{\micro
\volt}$; the laboratory's calibration of the customer's DMM
inherits the Zener uncertainty as a Type B contribution and adds
its own comparison contribution, giving the customer an expanded
uncertainty of $1.6\,\si{\micro\volt}$; the customer's own
measurement using the DMM adds their own comparison contributions
and gives the eventual measurement an even larger expanded
uncertainty.

At each step the uncertainty grows. The growth is what makes the
chain expensive and the metrology infrastructure necessary. A
calibration chain whose end-user uncertainty is $1\,\si{\milli
\volt}$ requires the NMI's Josephson realisation to be at the
microvolt level: three orders of magnitude of headroom for the
chain's accumulated growth. Without the headroom, the chain
delivers no useful precision at the end-user level.

\subsection{Reading a calibration certificate properly}

A working engineer who receives a calibration certificate should
extract five pieces of information before relying on it:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item The measured value and its expanded uncertainty, at the
    stated coverage factor.
  \item The reference against which the calibration was performed,
    and that reference's own traceability to a primary realisation.
  \item The date of the calibration and the recommended
    recalibration interval, plus the manufacturer's drift
    specification for the period since calibration.
  \item The environmental conditions during the calibration, and
    the corrections required if the instrument is to be used at
    different conditions.
  \item The accreditation status of the issuing laboratory and the
    scope of accreditation that covers the calibration.
\end{enumerate}

A certificate that does not provide all five is incomplete. The
working engineer who carries the certificate into a critical
application is contractually entitled to ask the laboratory for
any missing piece before placing weight on the calibration. A
certificate is the contract; the contract should be readable.

\section{Failure: when the standard itself was wrong}\pathtag{core}

The traceability chain assumes that each link is correctly
calibrated against the link above it. The chain is maintained by
people, instruments, and procedures, all of which can fail. A
chapter on calibration ends with cases where the standard itself
was the source of the error.

\subsection{The artifact-standard divergence: the IPK, 1889--2019}

The international prototype of the kilogram (IPK) was the world's
mass standard from 1889 until the revised SI took effect on 20 May
2019. BIPM records divergence among prototypes by the second
verification in 1946 and a median difference of about
$25 \,\si{\micro\gram}$ for the original prototypes in the
1989--1991 verification \cite{web:bipm-kilogram-history,
std:bipm-si2019}. Larger century-scale figures sometimes quoted for
official copies require their own provenance.

The technical lesson is that an artifact definition cannot
distinguish unit drift from artifact drift using artifacts alone. A
definition that pins the unit to a physical object cannot ask
whether the object has changed; the object's change \emph{is} the
unit's change. The 2019 redefinition replaced the artifact with the
Planck constant; the IPK is now itself calibrated against
Kibble-balance and XRCD realisations. Mass measurements made before
2019 against the IPK remain technically traceable, but their
long-term consistency with measurements made after 2019 depends on
the calibration history of each individual artifact and on whether
the IPK-versus-Planck-constant offset at the moment of redefinition
has been characterised \cite{web:nist-kibble-balance}.

\subsection{The test-instrument-as-failed-standard: Hubble, 1990}

The Hubble Space Telescope launched in April 1990 with a primary
mirror figured to the wrong shape. The NASA Optical Systems Board of
Investigation traced the error to the reflective null corrector
used during polishing: a field lens was mispositioned by about
$1.3 \,\si{\milli\meter}$ along the optical axis, and auxiliary
tests that showed the discrepancy were discounted
\cite{acc:nasa-hubble-optical-systems-1990}. The missing barrier
was independent verification of the test instrument before it
became the authoritative reference.

The Hubble case is recorded in the named-cases registry at
\repopath{docs/research/accidents/hubble-primary-mirror-1990.md}; we
return to the case in Volume X with its full organisational
treatment.

\subsection{Drifting transfer standards}

A chronic failure begins when a laboratory's reference standard
drifts undetected and downstream calibrations inherit the bias. The
laboratory's secondary standard is recalibrated against an external
primary at the recommended interval, but between recalibrations it
drifts in a way the laboratory's internal checks fail to detect.
Every working instrument calibrated against the secondary during the
drift period inherits the drift.

When the next external recalibration reveals the secondary's drift,
the laboratory must assess affected certificates, notify customers
where required, and decide whether corrected results or recalls are
necessary. Customers in turn must trace forward through products,
batches, or measurements made with the affected instruments and
decide whether the cumulative effect requires a product recall, a
regulatory notification, or a correction-factor adjustment to
historical data.

The discipline that catches drift earlier is the laboratory's own
internal control: cross-checks among the laboratory's secondary
standards, intermediate calibration checks against alternative
references, and statistical-control charts on the calibration
results themselves. A laboratory whose only check on a secondary
standard is the next external recalibration discovers slow drift
only when the recall is already required.

\section{Project}\pathtag{core}

\begin{project}[Trace one personal measurement to a national
standard]
The reader picks one measurement that they make, or that is made on
their behalf, in their daily life. They then trace the measurement's
calibration chain back as far as it can be traced, document the
chain, and identify the gaps.

\textbf{Track}. Analysis with optional household component. The
optional component is a single measurement using the chosen
instrument; the bulk of the project is research into the chain.
\textbf{Hazard class}: none.
\textbf{Tools}. Paper, pencil, internet research access for
standards documents and manufacturer calibration certificates,
optionally one household measuring instrument.

\textbf{Choosing the measurement}. Pick one of the following or
equivalent:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item A bathroom scale reading. Trace through the scale's
    manufacturer specifications, the load cell's calibration class,
    the secondary mass standards used to calibrate the load cell at
    the manufacturer, and the national mass standards above those.
  \item A kitchen thermometer reading. Trace through the
    thermistor's or thermocouple's calibration source, the ITS-90
    fixed points used at calibration, and the thermodynamic kelvin
    above those.
  \item A smartphone clock reading. Trace as far as the device and
    network documentation allow: operating-system time service,
    cellular or NTP source, server stratum or carrier time source,
    and the reference clock above it. Expect gaps; this is a
    gap-finding exercise rather than a clean calibration chain.
  \item A GPS position reading. Trace the timing chain, satellite
    ephemeris source, receiver assumptions, and geodetic reference
    frame. Treat this as a gap-finding exercise rather than a clean
    calibration chain.
  \item A workplace instrument the reader uses regularly: a
    micrometer, a torque wrench, a multimeter, a pipette. Trace
    through the workplace's calibration procedure, the calibration
    laboratory's certificates, and the national standards above
    those.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Procedure}. For the chosen measurement:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Identify the instrument and locate any calibration
    certificate, calibration label, or manufacturer specification
    available.
  \item Document the chain link by link, going as far up the chain
    as the available documentation permits.
  \item For each link, record: the upper-level reference, the date
    of calibration, the calibrating organisation, the measured
    value, the uncertainty, the recalibration interval.
  \item Identify each gap in the chain (a link for which
    documentation is unavailable, or for which the upper-level
    reference is not specified).
  \item For each gap, propose how the gap could be filled in
    principle (which document or organisation would carry the
    information).
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Reflection (1500 words)}. The reader writes a 1500-word
narrative addressing:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item How far up the chain documentation existed; where the
    chain became invisible; what would be required to make the
    invisible portion visible.
  \item What the reader's confidence in the measurement would
    change if any specific link in the chain failed, and which link
    would most affect that confidence.
  \item Whether the cost-and-effort of documenting the chain seems
    proportionate to the measurement's stakes.
  \item One measurement in the reader's life that the reader cannot
    realistically trace to a national standard, and what that
    implies about the measurement's status.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Deliverable}. The traceability-chain document and the
reflection. The general editor's reference traces (one per
suggested measurement category) are published online; the reader is

\textbf{Time}. Three to six hours of research, plus two to three

\end{project}

\section{Exercises}

The exercises train calculation, derivation, estimation, simulation,
design, diagnosis, failure analysis, and judgment. Calculation and
derivation problems have full solutions; open-ended problems have
rubrics or reference write-ups. Exercises are tagged with their
reader-path tier.

\subsection*{Calculation}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
A calibration certificate states that a multimeter reads
$1.0023 \,\si{\volt}$ when applied to a $1.0000 \,\si{\volt}$
reference, with an expanded uncertainty of $\pm 0.0010 \,\si{\volt}$
at coverage factor $k = 2$. State (a) the calibration correction the
user should apply to readings, (b) the standard uncertainty implied
at $k = 1$, (c) the corrected reading and its uncertainty for an
indication of $5.0150 \,\si{\volt}$.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
(a) Bias: indication $-$ reference $= 1.0023 - 1.0000 = +0.0023
\,\si{\volt}$. The correction is the negative of the bias:
$-0.0023 \,\si{\volt}$. The corrected reading is indication plus
correction.
(b) Standard uncertainty at $k = 1$: $u = U / k = 0.0010 / 2 =
0.0005 \,\si{\volt}$.
(c) Assuming the bias is approximately constant across the
calibrated range (a simplifying assumption; in practice a
range-dependent correction may apply), corrected reading
$= 5.0150 - 0.0023 = 5.0127 \,\si{\volt}$. Uncertainty is the
calibration's standard uncertainty at the corrected value:
$5.0127 \pm 0.0005 \,\si{\volt}$ at $k = 1$, equivalently
$\pm 0.0010 \,\si{\volt}$ at $k = 2$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A pressure gauge has a manufacturer-stated accuracy of $0.5\,\%$
of full-scale, with full-scale at $10\,\text{bar}$. Express the
accuracy in absolute units. Express it as a percentage of indicated
value at indications of $1$, $5$, and $9\,\text{bar}$. State which
operating range is most affected by the as-percentage-of-full-scale
specification.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Absolute accuracy: $0.5\,\% \times 10 = 0.05 \,\text{bar}$ over the
entire range.
At $1 \,\text{bar}$: $0.05/1 = 5\,\%$ of indicated value.
At $5 \,\text{bar}$: $0.05/5 = 1\,\%$ of indicated value.
At $9 \,\text{bar}$: $0.05/9 \approx 0.56\,\%$ of indicated value.
The low end of the range is most affected: a percent-of-full-scale
specification produces poor relative accuracy at small indications.
The engineering response is to size the gauge so its operating
range sits in the upper half of full scale, or to select a gauge
with a percent-of-reading specification when low-end accuracy is
required.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A platinum resistance thermometer is calibrated at the triple point
of water and the melting point of gallium. For the narrow interval
between those points, write a local linear interpolation in the
resistance ratio $W(T) = R(T) / R(273.16 \,\si{\kelvin})$. Then
explain why ITS-90 uses specified interpolation functions and
additional fixed points rather than a single linear rule when the
range extends toward $200 \,\si{\degreeCelsius}$
\cite{web:bipm-cct-its90}.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Linear interpolation: $T(W) = T_{\text{tp,W}} + (T_{\text{mp,Ga}} -
T_{\text{tp,W}}) \cdot (W - W_{\text{tp,W}}) / (W_{\text{mp,Ga}} -
W_{\text{tp,W}})$, where $T_{\text{tp,W}} = 273.16 \,\si{\kelvin}$,
$T_{\text{mp,Ga}} = 302.9146 \,\si{\kelvin}$, and $W_{\text{tp,W}}
= 1$ by definition. The rule is acceptably linear across this
$\sim 30$-K interval because platinum's resistance-temperature
relation is smooth and the interval is small. Over a wider range,
$R(T)$ deviates noticeably from linearity due to the Callendar-Van
Dusen polynomial structure; ITS-90 prescribes higher-order
deviation functions plus a sub-range of fixed points (mercury
triple point, indium melting, tin melting, zinc, aluminium, silver)
to keep the interpolation uncertainty below $\sim 1 \,\si{\milli
\kelvin}$ across the working range \cite{web:bipm-cct-its90}.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A working laboratory's secondary voltage standard is rated at
$10.000\,000 \,\si{\volt}$ with a drift limit of $\pm 0.5 \,\si
{\micro\volt}$ per year. It was last calibrated $24$ months ago.
Compute the worst-case drift allowance, then compute the standard
uncertainty if the drift is modelled as uniformly distributed
within that allowance.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Worst-case drift after $24$ months: $\pm 1.0 \,\si{\micro\volt}$
(twice the annual allowance, in either direction). Modelled as
uniform on $[-1.0, +1.0] \,\si{\micro\volt}$, the standard
uncertainty is the half-width divided by $\sqrt{3}$:
$u = 1.0/\sqrt{3} \approx 0.577 \,\si{\micro\volt}$. The standard's
value at use should be reported as $10.000\,000 \,\si{\volt} \pm
0.58 \,\si{\micro\volt}$ at $k = 1$ from the drift contribution
alone; further contributions (the original calibration uncertainty,
temperature effects, etc.) combine in quadrature.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A torque wrench is specified to $\pm 4\,\%$ of indicated value over
its rated range of $20$ to $200 \,\si{\newton\meter}$. A user sets
the wrench to $90 \,\si{\newton\meter}$ and the calibration
certificate reports a measured value of $87.6 \,\si{\newton\meter}$
with a stated uncertainty of $\pm 1.2 \,\si{\newton\meter}$. State
whether the wrench is operating within its specification, the
correction the user should apply, and the corrected uncertainty for
the indicated $90 \,\si{\newton\meter}$ setting.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Specification at $90 \,\si{\newton\meter}$: $\pm 4\,\% \times 90 =
\pm 3.6 \,\si{\newton\meter}$. Calibration deviation: $|87.6 - 90|
= 2.4 \,\si{\newton\meter}$, within the $\pm 3.6$ specification.
The wrench is within tolerance. Correction: when the wrench reads
$90 \,\si{\newton\meter}$ it delivers $87.6 \,\si{\newton\meter}$;
to deliver $90 \,\si{\newton\meter}$, set the wrench to $90 \times
(90/87.6) = 92.5 \,\si{\newton\meter}$. The corrected delivered
torque has uncertainty $\pm 1.2 \,\si{\newton\meter}$ from the
calibration; under repeated use, additional uncertainty from
between-calibration drift and operator variation must be combined.
For safety-critical fasteners with $\pm 5\,\%$ tolerance, the
$\pm 1.2/90 \approx 1.3\,\%$ wrench-uncertainty is acceptable but
leaves modest margin against the joint tolerance.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Derivation}\pathtag{standard}

\begin{exercise}
Show that for an instrument whose drift accumulates linearly at rate
$d$ per unit time, with random variation of standard deviation
$\sigma$ at any given time, the recalibration interval $\tau$ that
keeps the cumulative deviation below tolerance $\Delta$ with
confidence corresponding to $k$ standard deviations is
\[
\tau = \frac{\Delta - k\sigma}{d}.
\]
State the conditions under which this expression is valid.

\paragraph{Solution.}
The recalibration interval formula is a working result with five
explicit conditions. The solution states the model, derives the
formula, and explains where each condition enters.

\emph{Step 1: the drift-plus-noise model.} At time $t$ since the
last calibration, the instrument's reading minus the true value
is modelled as
\[
X(t) = d \cdot t + \varepsilon(t),
\]
where $d$ is the constant drift rate and $\varepsilon(t)$ is the
random component at time $t$, modelled as Gaussian with mean zero
and standard deviation $\sigma$. The drift component is
deterministic; the random component is the run-to-run variation
of the calibration check.

\emph{Step 2: the upper bound on deviation.} For an instrument
in service at time $t$, the upper $k$-sigma bound on the
absolute deviation is
\[
|X(t)| \leq d \cdot t + k\sigma,
\]
with probability $1 - 2\Phi(-k)$ for a Gaussian noise component
($\Phi$ the standard normal CDF). For $k = 2$ the probability is
about $95\%$; for $k = 3$, about $99.7\%$.

\emph{Step 3: solve for the interval.} The recalibration interval
$\tau$ is the largest $t$ for which the upper bound is at or below
the application's tolerance $\Delta$:
\[
d\tau + k\sigma = \Delta \quad\Rightarrow\quad
\tau = \frac{\Delta - k\sigma}{d}.
\]
The solution exists and is positive only when $\Delta > k\sigma$,
i.e., when the application's tolerance is wider than the
instrument's random scatter alone. If $\Delta \leq k\sigma$, no
recalibration interval suffices and the instrument must be
replaced by one with smaller random variation.

\emph{Step 4: the five validity conditions.} The derivation
assumes (i) linear drift, (ii) independent (uncorrelated) random
component over time, (iii) Gaussian random component (or other
distribution where $k$ corresponds to the chosen probability),
(iv) stationary $\sigma$ over the interval, and (v) the
calibration check at $t = 0$ is exact (no offset from the last
calibration). Each condition can fail in practice.

\emph{Step 5: failure modes.} Capacitor and reference-aging effects
typically follow $d(t) = d_{0}/(t + t_{0})$ rather than constant
drift, with high initial drift settling to a steady rate; the
linear-drift assumption underestimates the cumulative deviation
in the high-drift phase. Mechanical instruments often see
autocorrelated noise from operator technique or thermal
hysteresis; the independent-noise assumption understates the
shorter-timescale uncertainty. Heavy-tailed noise (electronic
glitch or impulse pickup) makes the $k$-sigma bound inadequate;
the engineering response is to use a more conservative bound or to
filter the outliers from the calibration data. Non-stationary
$\sigma$ from environmental variation requires fitting $\sigma(t)$
from the calibration history; the formula then uses the average
or worst-case $\sigma$ over the interval.

\emph{Step 6: the engineering decision.} The formula is the
starting point for a recalibration schedule, not the answer. A
working laboratory tests the linear-drift assumption empirically
by recalibrating the instrument at several intervals and checking
the deviation; departures from linearity are investigated and the
schedule adjusted. The formula gives the analyst the lever
($\tau \propto 1/d$) and the conservative starting point.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
For a measurement chain with $N$ links, each contributing an
independent uncertainty $u_i$, derive the combined uncertainty $u_c$
at the bottom of the chain. State the condition under which the
combined uncertainty is dominated by a single link, and identify
that link.

\paragraph{Solution.}
The chain-uncertainty combination is a direct application of the
variance-addition rule for independent random variables. The
derivation and its working consequences come in four parts.

\emph{Step 1: model the chain.} A measurement chain with $N$
independent links each contributes its own random component
$\varepsilon_{i}$ with variance $u_{i}^{2}$. The cumulative
deviation at the bottom of the chain is the sum
$\varepsilon = \sum_{i=1}^{N} \varepsilon_{i}$.

\emph{Step 2: compute the combined variance.} For uncorrelated
contributions, variances add:
\[
u_{c}^{2} = \Var\left(\sum_{i=1}^{N} \varepsilon_{i}\right) =
\sum_{i=1}^{N} \Var(\varepsilon_{i}) = \sum_{i=1}^{N} u_{i}^{2}.
\]
Taking the square root gives the combined standard uncertainty
$u_{c} = \sqrt{\sum u_{i}^{2}}$, the root-sum-square (RSS) rule.

\emph{Step 3: identify when a single link dominates.} The
contribution of link $i$ to the combined variance is $u_{i}^{2}/
u_{c}^{2}$. When one link has uncertainty $u_{j}$ much larger than
the others, that link's squared contribution dominates the sum.
A working threshold: if $u_{j} \geq 3 \max_{i \neq j} u_{i}$, then
$u_{j}^{2}$ is at least nine times any other variance term, and
the combined uncertainty $u_{c} \approx u_{j}$ to within roughly
$5\%$. The dominant link is the largest individual contributor.

\emph{Step 4: the engineering implication.} The variance-addition
rule has a fundamental engineering consequence: \emph{reducing a
small contribution has negligible effect on the combined
uncertainty}. If $u_{j}$ dominates, reducing $u_{j}$ by a factor of
$2$ reduces $u_{c}$ by roughly the same factor; reducing a
non-dominant $u_{i}$ by a factor of $2$ reduces $u_{c}$ by less
than $1\%$. The right calibration improvement is the one targeted
at the dominant link.

\emph{Step 5: where the dominant link typically sits.} In a real
traceability chain, the uncertainty contributions grow from the top
(primary NMI realisation, $\sim 10^{-9}$ fractional) to the bottom
(working laboratory instrument, $\sim 10^{-4}$ to $10^{-5}$
fractional). The dominant link is usually at the bottom, because
the working standard sees the most use (drift accumulation), the
most environmental exposure (temperature, humidity, vibration),
and the longest time between recalibrations. The chain's
dominant-link calibration improvement is almost always made at the
working-instrument level, not at the primary-realisation level.
The cost-versus-yield logic of section 3.5 follows directly.

\emph{Step 6: the correlated extension.} If two links share a
common error source (the same calibration reference, the same
operator technique), they are correlated. The combined variance
acquires cross-terms: $u_{c}^{2} = \sum u_{i}^{2} + 2 \sum_{i<j}
\rho_{ij} u_{i} u_{j}$. Positive correlation enlarges $u_{c}$
beyond the RSS rule; negative correlation reduces it. The
uncorrelated formula is conservative when correlations are
negative and optimistic when positive; a serious chain budget
identifies the correlations explicitly.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
The Kibble balance relates mass to the Planck constant through
mechanical and electrical-power balance. Show that the mass $m$ in
terms of measured quantities is
\[
m = \frac{U I}{g v},
\]
where $U$ is the induced voltage in moving mode, $I$ is the current
in weighing mode, $g$ is the local gravitational acceleration, and
$v$ is the coil's velocity in moving mode. Verify that the
expression has the correct dimensions and identify which Kibble-
balance measurements link to the Planck constant.

\paragraph{Solution.}
The Kibble balance is the post-2019 primary realisation of the
kilogram. The derivation extracts the mass from the two operating
modes, verifies dimensions, and traces the Planck link.

\emph{Step 1: write the weighing-mode force balance.} In weighing
mode, the test mass $m$ sits in a magnetic field $B$ produced by
a permanent magnet. A current $I$ flows through a coil of length
$L$ in the field. The force on the coil is $F = BLI$. Balancing
this against gravity on the test mass gives
\[
m g = B L I.
\]

\emph{Step 2: write the moving-mode voltage equation.} In moving
mode, the test mass is removed and the coil is driven through the
same magnetic field at velocity $v$. Faraday's law gives the
induced EMF
\[
U = B L v.
\]

\emph{Step 3: eliminate the geometry-flux product $BL$.} From the
moving-mode equation, $BL = U/v$. Substituting into the weighing-
mode equation gives
\[
m g = \frac{U}{v} \cdot I, \quad\text{or}\quad
m = \frac{U I}{g v}.
\]
The geometry-flux product $BL$, which depends on the coil winding
detail and the magnet's flux pattern, has been eliminated. The
mass is now expressed in terms of electrical quantities $U, I$
(measurable through electrical metrology) and mechanical
quantities $g, v$ (measurable through gravimetry and laser
interferometry).

\emph{Step 4: verify the dimensions.} The base units are
$[M], [L], [T], [I]$.
\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item $[U] = [M][L]^{2}[T]^{-3}[I]^{-1}$ (voltage; from
    $W = qV$ and $W = m \cdot \si{\text{velocity}}^{2}$).
  \item $[I] = [I]$ (current; SI base).
  \item $[U I] = [M][L]^{2}[T]^{-3}$ (power; the dimensional
    check matches the working physics that $UI$ is the
    instantaneous power in the moving-mode comparison).
  \item $[g] = [L][T]^{-2}$ (acceleration).
  \item $[v] = [L][T]^{-1}$ (velocity).
  \item $[g v] = [L]^{2}[T]^{-3}$.
  \item $[U I / (g v)] = [M]$. Correct.
\end{itemize}

\emph{Step 5: trace the Planck-constant link.} The voltage $U$ is
realised through the Josephson constant $K_{J} = 2e/h$ and a
microwave frequency $f$: $V = nf/K_{J}$ for integer $n$. The
current $I$ is realised by passing a Josephson voltage through a
quantum-Hall resistance $R_{K} = h/e^{2}$: $I = V/R_{K}$. The
product $UI$ then contains the combination
\[
UI = \frac{n_{1} f_{1}}{K_{J}} \cdot \frac{n_{2} f_{2}/K_{J}}{R_{K}}
= n_{1} n_{2} f_{1} f_{2} \cdot \frac{h}{4 e^{2} \cdot h/e^{2}}
= n_{1} n_{2} f_{1} f_{2} \cdot \frac{h}{4}.
\]
The constants $e$ cancel; the Planck constant $h$ survives. The
mass realisation $m = UI/(gv)$ therefore depends on the fixed
value of $h$ and on measured frequencies (atomically realised at
$\sim 10^{-16}$ uncertainty), measured gravity (gravimetrically
realised at $\sim 10^{-9}$ fractional uncertainty), and measured
velocity (interferometrically realised through laser
distance-time products).

\emph{Step 6: the working consequence.} The Kibble balance gives
$m$ in terms of a fixed-by-definition Planck constant and four
measurable quantities. No artefact is required. The accuracy floor
is set by the combined uncertainty of the four measurements, and
in current state-of-the-art operation reaches a few parts in
$10^{8}$ on a $1\,\si{\kilo\gram}$ test mass. The discipline is
the post-2019 dissemination chain the chapter describes: a Kibble
balance at the national metrology institute realises the
kilogram; calibrated working masses inherit the realisation through
the accredited chain.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Derive the relationship between Josephson voltage steps and the
elementary charge: starting from the Josephson constant $K_J = 2e/h$
and the relation $V_n = nf/K_J$, show that a Josephson voltage
standard's output voltage depends only on the microwave frequency
and the chosen integer step.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
$K_J = 2e/h$ is a combination of two exact-by-definition SI
constants in the 2019 SI; $K_J$ is itself exact. The output
voltage of a Josephson junction under microwave irradiation at
frequency $f$ takes quantised values $V_n = n f / K_J = n f h /
(2e)$. Substituting the defining values $h = 6.626\,070\,15 \times
10^{-34}$ J s and $e = 1.602\,176\,634 \times 10^{-19}$ C, the
voltage step at $n = 1$ and $f = 1 \,\text{GHz}$ is $\approx
2.067834 \,\si{\micro\volt}$. The output voltage depends only on
the chosen $n$ and the microwave frequency, both of which are
known to high precision (atomic clocks reach $\sim 10^{-15}$
fractional frequency uncertainty). A Josephson voltage standard
typically operates with $n$ in the range $10^{4}$-$10^{5}$ and
$f \sim 70$-$95 \,\text{GHz}$, producing standard voltages of
$1$ to $10 \,\si{\volt}$ with sub-parts-per-billion uncertainty.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the length at which a fractional frequency uncertainty of
$10^{-7}$ would correspond to a length uncertainty of
$1 \,\si{\milli\meter}$ in an ideal time-of-flight distance meter.
Then name at least three real error sources that dominate before

$\Delta L / L = \Delta f / f = 10^{-7}$. For $\Delta L = 10^{-3}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the recalibration interval for a household thermometer
used to take body temperatures, given a drift rate inferred from

\paragraph{Reference estimate.}
A typical consumer digital thermometer is specified for $\pm 0.1
\,\si{\degreeCelsius}$ initial accuracy and is silent on long-term

Celsius}$/yr (a conservative inferred rate from comparable
electronic sensors), and the user wants to stay within $\pm 0.2

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the time delay introduced by GPS-signal propagation from a
satellite to a ground receiver, and state how that delay relates to
the calibration of GPS-derived position. Compare to the precision
of the cesium clocks aboard the satellites.

\paragraph{Reference estimate.}
GPS satellites orbit at $\sim 20{,}200 \,\si{\kilo\meter}$ altitude.
Slant range to a ground receiver is typically $20{,}000$ to
$25{,}000 \,\si{\kilo\meter}$. Propagation delay at speed of light:
$\sim 20{,}000{,}000 / 3 \times 10^{8} = 67 \,\si{\milli\second}$.
Position is computed from the differences in arrival times across
multiple satellites; a timing error of $\Delta t$ corresponds to
range error $c \cdot \Delta t$: $1 \,\si{\nano\second}$ of timing
error gives $30 \,\si{\centi\meter}$ of range error, $1 \,\si{\micro
\second}$ gives $300 \,\si{\meter}$. The onboard cesium and rubidium
clocks have fractional stability $\sim 10^{-13}$ to $10^{-14}$ over
relevant time scales; the relativistic corrections (general and
special relativity combined produce a $\sim 38 \,\si{\micro\second}/
\text{day}$ blue-shift, applied via on-board offset) must be
applied to deliver positions to metre-scale accuracy. The clock
precision is well beyond what would be needed for direct timing;
the precision is consumed by atmospheric delays, multipath,
satellite-clock-drift modelling, and receiver-clock-bias estimation.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Simulation}\pathtag{standard}

\begin{exercise}
Simulate an instrument whose bias drifts linearly at rate $d$ and
whose readings include Gaussian random variation with standard
deviation $\sigma$. For several recalibration intervals, estimate
the probability that the instrument exceeds tolerance before the
next calibration. Plot probability against interval for at least
three values of $\sigma / \Delta$, where $\Delta$ is the tolerance.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} See \texttt{code/drift\_recalibration.py}.
Monte Carlo simulation over $10^4$ trials with $d = 1.0$/yr,

\begin{exercise}
Simulate a five-link traceability chain in which each link
contributes an independent standard uncertainty. Compare equal-link
uncertainties with a chain dominated by one weak link, and plot
the combined uncertainty in each case. Comment on which chain
configuration is more robust to an unknown bias at one link.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} See \texttt{code/chain\_uncertainty.py}.
For equal links $u_i = 0.5$, $u_c = \sqrt{5 \times 0.25} \approx
1.12$. For one dominant link $u_1 = 2.0$ and four small $u_i = 0.1$,
$u_c = \sqrt{4 + 0.04} \approx 2.01$, dominated by the weak link.
Equal-link configuration is more robust to an unknown bias at any
single link because no single link's failure shifts $u_c$ by more
than $\sqrt{1/5} \approx 45\,\%$. The dominant-link configuration
amplifies any unknown bias at the weak link by approximately the
factor of the link's contribution. The engineering insight: a chain
is only as good as its weakest link in the bias sense, but the
combined uncertainty has a quadrature-averaging structure that
favours equalising the contributions.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Build a simple cost model for recalibration interval choice. Include
the per-calibration cost, the downtime cost during calibration,
and an estimated cost for discovering an out-of-tolerance condition
late (rework, recall, regulatory exposure). Identify the interval
that minimises expected cost under your assumptions and the
parameter regime where the answer changes.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Expected annual cost per instrument: $C(\tau) = C_{\text{cal}}/\tau
+ p_{\text{oot}}(\tau) \cdot C_{\text{loss}}$, where
$C_{\text{cal}}$ is per-calibration cost, $\tau$ is the interval in
years, $p_{\text{oot}}(\tau)$ is the probability of being
out-of-tolerance during the interval (from the simulation in the
previous exercise), and $C_{\text{loss}}$ is the cost of a single
out-of-tolerance event. For $C_{\text{cal}} = \$300$, $C_{\text{loss}}
= \$10{,}000$, and the $\sigma/\Delta = 0.1$ drift profile: the
expected cost is minimised near $\tau \approx 2$-$3$ yr. As
$C_{\text{loss}}$ rises (regulatory, safety-critical), the optimal
interval shortens; as $C_{\text{cal}}$ falls (in-house
calibration), it lengthens. Most regulatory regimes fix the
interval at a defensible value (often 1 year) rather than
optimise; the optimisation is most relevant for instruments
outside formal regulatory tolerance.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Design}\pathtag{standard}

\begin{exercise}
Design a local verification procedure for a household kitchen scale
using one or more low-stakes references: a calibration mass with
stated tolerance, coins whose official mass and wear condition are
considered, or a sealed package whose labelled net mass is treated
only as a rough check. State the procedure step by step, identify
the dominant uncertainty source, and explain why this is a
verification check rather than an accredited calibration.

\paragraph{Rubric.} Procedure: (1) zero the scale at empty; (2)
place a reference at the low end of the scale's range (e.g., a
2.5 g US quarter); record reading; (3) repeat with a sequence of
references spanning the scale's range; (4) compare reading vs
nominal mass for each; (5) declare verification PASS if every
reading is within the user-chosen tolerance, otherwise FAIL.
Dominant uncertainty source: the coin reference itself (US Mint
tolerance is $\pm 4\,\%$ on circulation coins; wear can shift mass
beyond the original tolerance). Why this is verification, not
accredited calibration: no documented traceability of the reference
to a national standard; no formal uncertainty budget; no
calibration certificate; no environmental control (temperature,
vibration). The procedure provides a fitness-for-purpose check at
household stakes and is appropriate for that use. For commercial
food preparation or pharmaceutical compounding, accredited
calibration is required.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Design a recalibration-interval policy for a small dental clinic's
digital thermometers, blood-pressure cuffs, and pulse-oximeters,
given the clinical-acceptance tolerances for each measurement.
State the regulatory framework you assume and the cost trade-offs
you considered.

\paragraph{Rubric.} Regulatory framework: US FDA QSR (21 CFR 820)
or EU MDR for medical devices used in clinical settings; both
require documented calibration intervals and traceability.
Clinical tolerances: thermometer $\pm 0.1\,\degC$ (ASTM E1112);
blood-pressure cuff $\pm 3 \,\text{mmHg}$ (ANSI/AAMI SP10);
pulse oximeter $\pm 2\,\%$ SpO$_2$ at $80\,\%$-$100\,\%$ saturation
(ISO 80601-2-61). Recommended intervals: thermometer 1 yr (or per
manufacturer); blood-pressure cuff 1 yr (or every 6 months for
high-use units); pulse oximeter 1 yr or after major shock event.
Cost trade-off: annual external calibration of 5 thermometers,
3 cuffs, 2 oximeters at $\sim \$100$ each $\approx \$1000$/yr.
In-house calibration with a traceable reference: $\sim \$3000$
upfront for reference equipment, $\sim \$200$/yr labour, breaks
even at year 2-3. Most small clinics choose external accredited
calibration for cost and audit-defensibility.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Design a verification routine that a manufacturing quality
laboratory could run daily on one of its critical instruments to
detect drift between formal recalibrations. Specify the control
sample, the tolerance band, the action thresholds, and the
escalation procedure when the routine flags a drift.

\paragraph{Rubric.} Control sample: a stable reference whose
nominal value sits in the instrument's working range and whose
uncertainty is at least $3\times$ smaller than the instrument's
tolerance (e.g., a NIST-traceable Zener voltage reference for a
multimeter check). Tolerance band: 1/3 of the instrument's
formal calibration tolerance, applied as a warning band. Action
thresholds: green within $\pm 1/3$ tolerance; yellow within
$\pm 2/3$; red beyond. Daily procedure: log the reading on the
control sample at the same conditions; plot on a control chart
(SPC); apply Western Electric or Nelson rules to detect non-random
patterns. Escalation: red triggers immediate instrument removal
from service and external recalibration; yellow triggers
investigation within 1 week (environmental, operator,
sample-degradation cause analysis); pattern signals on the green
band trigger a documented review at the next scheduled
calibration. The control-chart method catches drift earlier than
the formal annual recalibration and reduces the rework risk that
the cost model in the previous simulation exercise priced.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Diagnosis and reverse engineering}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
A calibration laboratory's annual external recalibration of its
secondary voltage standard reveals that the standard has drifted
$0.8 \,\si{\micro\volt}$ over the year, larger than the typical
drift of $0.3 \,\si{\micro\volt}$ for that standard. State three
plausible mechanisms for the larger-than-expected drift and propose
the next measurement that distinguishes them.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Three plausible mechanisms: (1) the Zener diode (typical voltage
reference at this level) is approaching end-of-life; aging
accelerates near failure. (2) The instrument's temperature
control has drifted (e.g., a failed oven thermostat), exposing the
Zener to wider temperature swings. (3) The instrument has suffered
a thermal or mechanical shock event (e.g., a power surge or a
move between buildings) that produced a step shift rather than
continuous drift. Next measurement to distinguish: review the
instrument's continuous logging history (most working standards
log internal temperature and reference voltage). A monotonic drift
across the year supports aging; a step plus stable thereafter
supports a shock event; correlated drift with internal temperature
supports the oven hypothesis. If no logging exists, repeat the
calibration under controlled-temperature conditions at the
external lab and compare to the original certificate's conditions.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A working multimeter's calibration certificate, dated $14$ months
ago, has a one-year recalibration interval. The manufacturer's
specification claims a one-year accuracy of $\pm 0.05\,\%$ of
reading. State whether the multimeter can still be used for
legally-defendable measurements and what would be required to
restore its calibrated status.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
The multimeter's calibrated status has lapsed: the recalibration
interval (typically defined as 1 year from the previous calibration
date) was exceeded 2 months ago. Legally-defendable measurements
made after the lapse cannot be supported by the existing
calibration certificate. The manufacturer's accuracy claim is
typically a one-year specification under stated conditions; outside
that window the manufacturer makes no claim. To restore calibrated
status: (1) send the instrument for external recalibration at an
accredited laboratory; (2) when received, verify the as-found data
shows the instrument was within manufacturer specification during
the lapsed period (the lab's report distinguishes ``as found''
from ``as left''); (3) if the as-found data is within
specification, the lapsed-period measurements may be retroactively
defended on the calibrated-period bridge argument; if not, the
lapsed-period measurements must be flagged as suspect, and any
products or decisions made on them reviewed.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A user receives a calibration certificate for a torque wrench that
states the calibration was performed against a "calibration
master" but does not specify the master's traceability. State what
questions the user should ask the calibrating laboratory before
accepting the certificate as evidence of traceability.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Questions to ask: (1) Is the calibrating laboratory ISO/IEC 17025
accredited, and for what scope? (an accredited laboratory's scope
explicitly lists which calibrations are covered by its
accreditation; torque wrench calibration may or may not be).
(2) What is the calibration master's traceability chain? Specifically,
which higher-level reference (NMI or NMI-traceable) was used to
calibrate it, on what date, with what uncertainty? (3) What is the
calibration master's recalibration interval, and when is its next
recalibration due? (4) What was the uncertainty budget for the
wrench calibration, including the master's contribution? Without
satisfactory answers to all four, the certificate does not
constitute documented traceability. The user should either obtain
the missing information or send the wrench elsewhere for
calibration against a documented reference.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A pH meter that has just been calibrated at pH $4.00$ and pH $7.00$
reads pH $9.50$ when applied to a sample known to be at pH $10.00$.
Identify three plausible causes for the disagreement and propose
the next measurement that distinguishes them.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Three causes: (1) Extrapolation error: the calibration brackets
$[4, 7]$ does not cover the measurement at pH 10; the linear-
interpolation assumption fails outside the calibrated range,
especially as the electrode's Nernstian slope departs from the
ideal at extreme pH. (2) Electrode condition: the glass membrane
may have aged or fouled, producing a sub-Nernstian response that
under-reads at high pH. (3) The known-pH sample is itself drifting
(e.g., a borate buffer that has absorbed CO$_2$ from air, shifting
toward neutrality). Next measurement: extend the calibration with
a pH 10.00 buffer (three-point calibration) and remeasure. If the
reading now agrees with 10.00, the cause was extrapolation; if the
reading still disagrees, replace the electrode and remeasure; if
the new electrode also reads low, the sample is the source.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Failure analysis}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
The Hubble Space Telescope's reflective null corrector was the test
instrument that produced the wrong figure for the primary mirror.
Argue, in 200 to 300 words, whether the failure is primarily a
calibration failure (the RNC was uncalibrated against an
independent reference), a verification failure (the auxiliary
tests were overruled), or a metrology-policy failure (the test
process did not require independent verification of the test
instrument). Cite the OSBI report \cite{acc:nasa-hubble-optical-systems-1990}.

\paragraph{Rubric.} A credible argument identifies that each framing
captures part of the failure. As calibration failure: the
reflective null corrector was a custom instrument whose internal
spacings were set during assembly and not independently verified
against a reference; in the metrology sense, the RNC was
uncalibrated. As verification failure: the two auxiliary optical
tests (refractive null and inverse null) flagged the figure error
and were set aside under cost and schedule pressure; the
verification information existed but was not acted on. As
metrology-policy failure: the process did not require independent
verification of the test instrument; this is the structural cause
that the calibration and verification framings each partially
address. The metrology-policy framing is the most useful corrective
action because it covers both prior failures as instances of the
same systemic gap. The post-Hubble process changes at the NASA
Goddard optical-fabrication community and across the space-optics
industry codified independent verification of test instruments as
a standard requirement; the calibration and verification practices
were addressed by the policy change. Cite the OSBI report
\cite{acc:nasa-hubble-optical-systems-1990}.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
The pre-2019 international prototype of the kilogram, as the
artifact-based mass definition, had no external standard with equal
definitional authority. Describe, in 200 to 300 words, how the
post-2019 SI redefinition addresses that problem, and identify
what new failure modes Kibble-balance and XRCD realisations
introduce that the IPK did not have. Cite the SI Brochure
\cite{std:bipm-si2019} and the BIPM kilogram history record
\cite{web:bipm-kilogram-history}.

\paragraph{Rubric.} The 2019 redefinition fixed the numerical value
of the Planck constant exactly and made the kilogram a derived
quantity realisable by any laboratory with the appropriate
hardware. The IPK's silent drift problem ($\sim 25$-$50 \,\si{\micro
\gram}$ over a century, with the direction undetermined because
the IPK was the definition) became impossible: realisations now
trace to a fixed constant. New failure modes: (1) Kibble-balance
realisation requires precise measurements of voltage, resistance,
gravity, and velocity; any error in any of those propagates to the
realised mass. (2) XRCD requires precise measurement of a silicon
sphere's volume, mass, density, and lattice parameter; any error
in those propagates. (3) Different NMIs operating different
realisations may disagree at the limit of their uncertainties,
requiring inter-NMI comparisons to maintain coherence. (4) The
realisation is no longer transportable in a single artifact; mass
dissemination requires either repeating the realisation or using
secondary mass standards calibrated against a primary realisation.
The trade is from a single irreplaceable artifact's silent drift
to a system whose individual realisations have larger initial
uncertainty but no systemic drift. The BIPM CIPM MRA framework
manages the inter-NMI comparisons \cite{std:bipm-cipm-mra}; the
history is in \cite{web:bipm-kilogram-history}; the SI Brochure
codifies the regime \cite{std:bipm-si2019}.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A drifting secondary standard at a calibration laboratory
contaminates a year of downstream calibration certificates before
the next external recalibration reveals the drift. State the
laboratory's recall procedure, the customer's downstream
obligations, and the type of internal control that would have
detected the drift earlier.

\paragraph{Rubric.} Laboratory recall procedure (per ISO/IEC 17025
clause 7.10): (1) identify the affected calibration certificates by
date range and instrument; (2) notify each customer with the
drift-affected certificate; (3) re-calibrate the affected
instruments using a known-good reference, or re-issue the
certificate with corrected values plus expanded uncertainty
covering the drift exposure; (4) document the root-cause analysis
and corrective action. Customer downstream obligations: (1) review
the affected calibration's use in production records; (2) assess
whether any downstream measurements or product decisions were
affected; (3) if so, trace through the customer's own quality
records and apply corrective action (rework, recall, regulatory
notification). Internal control that would have detected the drift
earlier: a daily or weekly verification routine against a separate
internal control standard (the routine in the previous design
exercise). The control-chart approach catches drift between formal
recalibrations and prevents the year-long contamination scenario.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Judgment}\pathtag{standard}

\begin{exercise}
A manufacturing engineer argues that internal calibration
laboratories should not bother with ISO/IEC 17025 accreditation if
their work is purely internal. Write a one-paragraph response that
takes the argument seriously and identifies the specific cases
where ISO/IEC 17025 accreditation provides value to internal
operations as well as external customers.

\paragraph{Discussion.} A credible response concedes the cost
argument: ISO/IEC 17025 accreditation carries non-trivial
documentation, training, and audit costs that an internal-only
laboratory may genuinely not need. The response identifies four
cases where accreditation does provide internal value: (1)
regulatory exposure (FDA, EU MDR, automotive ISO/TS 16949,
aerospace AS9100; these regimes either require accreditation or
strongly prefer it); (2) liability defence (an accident
investigation will scrutinise the measurement chain; accredited
calibrations are harder to dispute); (3) workforce mobility (an
accredited laboratory's calibration certificates remain valid if
the laboratory is sold, the parent company is acquired, or the
internal use shifts to external customer relationships); (4)
internal discipline (the accreditation audit forces the
documentation hygiene that an internal-only laboratory may let
slide). The cost-benefit depends on the operational stakes; for
high-volume manufacturing with regulatory exposure, the case for
accreditation is strong even when the work is internal.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A senior colleague tells the reader that "calibration is bureaucratic
make-work; the instrument either works or it doesn't." Write a
one-paragraph reply that agrees where agreement is honest and
disagrees where disagreement is honest, with a specific example of
each.

\paragraph{Discussion.} A credible reply concedes that some
instruments operate in regimes where calibration is genuinely
ceremonial: a kitchen ruler's accuracy is set by its manufacture
and is not affected by recalibration; an ohmmeter measuring
$1 \,\text{M}\Omega$ against a $\pm 1\,\%$ tolerance has so much
headroom that recalibration is unlikely to change any decision.
The colleague's point holds for these. The reply disagrees on the
broader claim. Calibration is the documentation of agreement
between an instrument and a reference at a defined moment; the
documentation is what makes the measurement defensible in
audit, regulation, or accident investigation. Without calibration,
the instrument's status is the reader's claim, not the laboratory's
evidence. A specific example: a Boeing maintenance torque wrench
in airline service must have a current calibration certificate
because an airworthiness directive may turn on whether a fastener
was tightened to specification; the calibration is not
bureaucratic, it is the airline's defence against liability. The
distinction the senior colleague misses is between calibration as
discipline and calibration as ritual; the former is engineering,
the latter is a malpractice from which the former must be
distinguished.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A startup is bringing a new clinical-diagnostic instrument to
market. Argue, in 200 to 300 words, whether the startup should
invest in setting up its own calibration laboratory or rely on
external accredited laboratories for all its calibration needs.
State the trade-offs in cost, time-to-market, regulatory exposure,
and operational control.

\paragraph{Discussion.} A credible analysis weighs the trade-offs.
Cost: internal laboratory setup requires $\$100K$-$\$500K$ in
reference equipment plus $\$50K$-$\$150K$/yr in trained personnel;
external calibration costs $\sim \$300$-$\$1500$/instrument/year at
clinical-grade. For a startup with $< 50$ instruments to calibrate,
external is cheaper. Time-to-market: external calibration adds
$2$-$4$ weeks per cycle (ship, calibrate, ship back); internal
allows on-demand calibration. For a startup with frequent
development-test cycles, internal saves substantial calendar time.
Regulatory exposure: FDA QSR requires documented traceability;
both options meet the requirement if executed correctly, but
external accredited laboratories carry their own accreditation
documentation that the startup inherits; internal requires the
startup to maintain ISO/IEC 17025 accreditation itself, which is
substantial overhead. Operational control: internal gives same-day
turnaround and direct access to calibration history; external
introduces vendor dependencies but isolates the calibration
function from product engineering pressure. For a startup at
clinical-trial stage with $\sim 10$-$20$ instruments and infrequent
calibration cycles, external accredited is appropriate; the
break-even shifts toward internal when the company scales to
$\sim 50$+ instruments or when production cycle time becomes
binding. The decision should be revisited at each major scaling
milestone.
\end{exercise}
