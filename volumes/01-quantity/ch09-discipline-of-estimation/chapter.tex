\section{Order-of-magnitude reasoning}\pathtag{core}

An order of magnitude is a factor of ten. To reason at order-of-magnitude
precision is to ask, of every quantity, only which power of ten it
sits closest to. The mass of an apple is $10^{-1} \,\si{\kilogram}$. The
mass of a person is $10^{2} \,\si{\kilogram}$. The mass of a car is
$10^{3} \,\si{\kilogram}$. The mass of a long-haul aircraft at maximum
take-off weight is $10^{5} \,\si{\kilogram}$. The mass of a fully laden
container ship is $10^{8} \,\si{\kilogram}$. Six powers of ten separate
the apple from the ship, and an engineer who carries those six numbers in
working memory rarely confuses the kinds of system the numbers describe.

\Cref{fig:vol01:ch09:scale-ladder} extends the table across forty
decades of length and over fifty decades of mass. The figure is one
page because the order-of-magnitude ladder is the chapter's central
mnemonic; a reader returning to the ladder during later volumes
needs every benchmark in one view.

\input{volumes/01-quantity/ch09-discipline-of-estimation/figures/fig-scale-ladder.tex}

The case for working at this resolution is practical. A spreadsheet error
of a factor of two is invisible to the eye; an error of a factor of ten
is sometimes invisible too, when the answer falls in a familiar range. An
error of a factor of $10^{4}$ stands up off the page once the engineer
has the order-of-magnitude scale of the answer in mind before reading
it. Estimation supplies that scale. The reader who has estimated the
mass of the aircraft as $10^{5} \,\si{\kilogram}$ before opening the
manufacturer's reference card cannot misread $396{,}890 \,\si{\kilogram}$
as $39.6 \,\si{\kilogram}$ or $3.96 \times 10^{8} \,\si{\kilogram}$. The
estimate has narrowed the space of plausible readings before the reading
is taken.

\subsection{The internal ruler}

Volume I Chapter 1 introduced the engineer's \engterm{internal ruler}: a
mental table that maps quantities in a domain to their order of
magnitude. We can now describe how the table is built. The reader
acquires it by repeated practice on quantities of varying scale. Each
estimate, even a wrong one, leaves a benchmark in memory. After several
hundred estimates the reader can place a new quantity within an order of
magnitude without any conscious arithmetic, by interpolation between the
benchmarks already laid down.

The ruler is domain-specific. A practising mechanical engineer carries
masses, lengths, forces, pressures, and torques to within an order of
magnitude across the working range of their domain. A practising
electrical engineer carries voltages, currents, capacitances,
resistances, and frequencies. A practising civil engineer carries
volumes of concrete, costs per cubic metre, allowable bearing pressures,
and span-to-depth ratios. Crossing into a new domain is the engineer's
recurring challenge, because the existing ruler does not transfer; it has
to be rebuilt from a small number of anchor points and from repeated
estimation against ground truth.

The book's twelve volumes hand the reader anchor points for each domain.
Volume I gives metrology and statistics; Volume II gives mathematical
operations and computational complexity; Volume III gives forces,
energies, and powers; Volume IV gives flows, transport, and waves; and so
on. Each volume's estimation exercises serve to embed the anchor points
through use rather than through memorisation.

\subsection{The factor-of-three convention}

Engineering estimation by convention aims for a result within a factor
of three of the truth, in either direction. The convention is loose
enough to be reachable by mental arithmetic and tight enough to be
useful. A factor-of-three estimate distinguishes a process that is
feasible from one that is impossible, and a budget that is plausible
from one that is fictitious. It does not distinguish a process that is
elegant from one that is wasteful; that is what calculation is for.

The convention has structural roots, but the structure is logarithmic.
If $X = \prod_{i} f_{i}$, then multiplicative errors add in $\ln X$.
Four independent factor errors with log-width $\ln(\sqrt{3})$ combine in
quadrature to a log-width of $2\ln(\sqrt{3}) = \ln 3$, which is a factor
of $3$. Worst-case multiplication is looser: four factors each high by
$\sqrt{3}$ would put the product high by $(\sqrt{3})^{4} = 9$. The
factor-of-three target is therefore a working statistical target for
independent factor errors, not a worst-case guarantee.

Where the underlying physics demands tighter control, estimation moves
to a factor-of-two or factor-of-1.5 target. Where the underlying physics
permits looser control, the engineer reaches for an order-of-magnitude
estimate, accepting a factor of ten. The match between the convention
and the application is itself part of the discipline.

\subsection{The first canonical estimate}

The chapter's first formal estimation block trains the basic moves. The
question has an external comparison value, but the work is in the
decomposition, the recorded assumptions, and the postmortem.

\begin{estimation}
\textbf{How many piano tuners work in Chicago?}

\smallskip
\textit{Estimate, before reading on. The classic Fermi problem.}

\smallskip
The estimate proceeds by decomposition into factors that the reader can
each estimate within a factor of two or three. The product of the
factors is the estimate.

The Chicago-Naperville-Elgin metropolitan area had a population of
$9.41 \times 10^{6}$ on 1 July 2024, in the U.S. Census Bureau Vintage
2024 estimate \cite{data:census-cbsa-2024}. We round that to $9 \times
10^{6}$ for the estimate and take it as the denominator.

How many of those people own a piano in their household? The fraction is
small. Pianos are expensive, take floor space, and require a household
that values an acoustic instrument. Call it $1$ piano per $40$
households, with $2.5$ people per household; that is one piano per
$100$ people. The number of pianos in Chicago is then approximately
$9 \times 10^{4}$.

How often is a piano tuned? A serious player tunes once or twice a year;
an unused household piano is tuned once every several years; a heavily
played concert instrument is tuned weekly. Averaged across the
population of pianos in a city, call the rate one tuning per piano per
year. The annual demand for tunings is then about $9 \times 10^{4}$.

How many tunings does one tuner perform per year? A tuning takes about
two hours in the home plus travel; a working tuner can perform two or
three per day. Calling it $750$ tunings per year (about three per
working day, $250$ working days), one tuner supplies about $750$
tunings.

The number of tuners is then about $9 \times 10^{4} / 750 \approx 120$.
The estimate carries an uncertainty of perhaps a factor of three in
either direction, dominated by the fraction-of-households-with-pianos
factor and by the average-tunings-per-piano factor. The defensible
range is roughly $40$ to $400$.

For comparison, the U.S.~Bureau of Labor Statistics OEWS table for May
2023 reports $350$ musical instrument repairers and tuners in the
Chicago-Naperville-Elgin metropolitan area, occupation code 49-9063,
with a relative standard error of $24.6\,\%$
\cite{data:bls-oews-2023-chicago}. That is within our defensible range.

The postmortem matters more than the answer. Two of the four factors
were within a factor of two of the truth. The household-with-piano
fraction was the dominant uncertainty; a reader with direct knowledge
of musical-instrument ownership rates in Chicago could narrow the
estimate by a factor of two. The general lesson: each factor in a
Fermi decomposition carries its own uncertainty; the dominant one sets
the tightness of the result.
\end{estimation}

This estimate has done four things at once. It decomposed the question
into factors. It estimated each factor within a factor of two or three
using only working knowledge. It multiplied to a result. It compared
the result to a known reference and identified the factor that
dominated the uncertainty. Those four moves recur in every Fermi
estimate the rest of the chapter trains.

\Cref{fig:vol01:ch09:fermi-tree} draws the same decomposition as a
tree. The root carries the answer; the four boxes at depth one are
the four factors written out above; two of them, the pianos-per-person
and the tuner-throughput-per-year factors, expand at depth two into
sub-factors that the reader's working knowledge can each defend on
their own. A reference implementation of the same calculation, with

\section{Fermi problems}\pathtag{core}

A \engterm{Fermi problem} is a quantitative question answered by
decomposition before lookup. The reader may be able to find a published
value later; the discipline is to make and record the estimate first.
The genre is named for Enrico Fermi, who used decomposition both as a
teaching device and as a working tool. The habit predates Fermi; the
name has stuck because his standard, that an answer is worth defending
only if it comes with a method, has become the genre's standard.

The Fermi problem is the engineer's working unit of estimation. It has
a question, a decomposition, a small number of factors each estimated
from working knowledge, a multiplication, and a comparison to ground
truth where one exists. Its currency is the working engineer's mental
table of anchor points; its discipline is the habit of writing the
factors down before multiplying them.

\subsection{The shape of a Fermi solution}

Mahajan's \emph{The Art of Insight in Science and Engineering}
\cite{text:mahajan2014} is the canonical contemporary reference. He
organises Fermi-problem solutions around two structural moves:
divide-and-conquer (decompose the unknown into a product of factors
that the reader can each estimate) and abstraction (replace a
complicated function with a simpler one whose value the reader can
defend). We adopt the same structure and add a third move at the close:
postmortem (identify the factor that dominated the uncertainty, and
state what working knowledge would tighten the answer).

A worked Fermi solution then has five labelled parts.

\engterm{Question}. Stated explicitly, with the units of the answer
identified before any decomposition is attempted. A question whose
answer's units are unclear has not yet been stated as a quantitative
question.

\engterm{Decomposition}. The unknown $X$ is written as a product of
factors $X = \prod_{i} f_{i}$, with each factor a quantity the reader
can estimate from working knowledge. The decomposition is not unique;
different decompositions of the same question can produce estimates
with different dominant uncertainty sources.

\engterm{Factor estimates}. Each factor is estimated, with its
working assumption and its rough uncertainty range identified. A
factor estimated as $10$ with an admitted factor-of-two range is more
useful than a factor stated as $10$ without any range.

\engterm{Combination}. The factors are multiplied. Where the
combination is a sum or a more complex algebraic expression, the
algebra is done explicitly. The product carries a combined uncertainty
that, in the working approximation, multiplies the individual factor
uncertainties.

\engterm{Postmortem}. The estimate is compared to ground truth where
one is available. The factor that dominated the uncertainty is named.
The working knowledge that would tighten the estimate is identified.
The reader records the comparison, especially when the estimate was
wrong.

The five parts compose the answer. A Fermi solution that omits any one
of them is incomplete.

\subsection{Decomposition: divide-and-conquer}

Decomposition is the first hard move. The question is rarely posed in a
form that can be estimated directly; it has to be rewritten as a
product of factors that working knowledge can each defend.

For \emph{how many cars in this city}, decompose as: city population
times cars per person.

For \emph{how much energy does the United States use per year}, decompose
as: per-person power use times population times seconds per year.

For \emph{how many seconds in a human lifetime}, decompose as: years per
lifetime times days per year times seconds per day. The intermediate
factors are easy.

For \emph{the mass of the Earth's atmosphere}, decompose as: surface
area times the column mass per unit area, where the column mass is
ambient pressure divided by the gravitational acceleration. Three
factors, each known to within a factor of two.

For \emph{the number of grains of rice in one cubic metre}, decompose
as: one cubic metre divided by the volume of a grain. The volume of a
grain is decomposed further: $5 \,\si{\milli\meter} \times 2 \,\si
{\milli\meter} \times 2 \,\si{\milli\meter} \approx 2 \times 10^{-8}
\,\si{\meter\cubed}$, giving about $5 \times 10^{7}$ grains.

The shape of a good decomposition is one in which each factor sits
between $10^{-2}$ and $10^{2}$ of working knowledge anchor points. A
factor that requires the reader to estimate a quantity orders of
magnitude outside their experience is a sign that the decomposition
needs another step.

\subsection{Abstraction: replace the complicated with the simple}

Abstraction is the second hard move. The question may involve a
function the reader cannot evaluate, a population the reader does not
know, a relation the reader cannot integrate (Volume II takes up
integration; this chapter does not). Abstraction replaces the
complicated object with a simpler one that captures the dominant scale.

A non-uniform population is replaced by its mean times its count. A
shaped object is replaced by a box of equivalent dimensions. A
distribution is replaced by its peak. A time-varying signal is
replaced by its time average. A nonlinear function near a working
point is replaced by its tangent line. Each replacement loses
information; the discipline is to choose the replacement so that the
information lost is not the information the question depends on.

The habit of abstraction is what makes Fermi estimation possible
without calculus. The reader who wants to estimate the volume swept by
a fan blade does not integrate the swept area over the rotation; the
reader replaces the blade by a flat disk of the same outer radius and
multiplies by the rotation count. The abstraction discards the
turbulent details, which are not what the volume question depends on.

\subsection{Combination and uncertainty propagation}

A product of factors compounds its uncertainties in log space. If
$X = \prod_{i} f_{i}$, then $\ln X = \sum_{i} \ln f_{i}$. For
independent factor uncertainties, the log-uncertainty obeys
\[
\sigma_{\ln X}^{2} = \sum_{i} \sigma_{\ln f_{i}}^{2}.
\]
A factor-of-$1.5$ uncertainty corresponds to $\sigma_{\ln f} \approx
\ln(1.5) \approx 0.41$ if we treat the factor as a one-sigma log-width.
Four such factors combine to $\sigma_{\ln X} \approx 0.82$, a
multiplicative one-sigma factor of $e^{0.82} \approx 2.3$. Seven such
factors combine to $e^{\sqrt{7}\,\ln(1.5)} \approx 2.9$. The
approximation is only a model of independent factor errors, but it
teaches the right habit: a long decomposition widens the result.

The implication is structural. A Fermi decomposition with three or
four factors typically produces a result good to within a factor of
three. A decomposition with seven factors produces a result good only
to within an order of magnitude, even if every factor is well
estimated. The reader who is tempted to break a question into many
small factors should check that the multiplicative compounding does
not exceed the precision the question requires.
\Cref{fig:vol01:ch09:log-uncertainty} plots the combined factor
uncertainty against the number of factors for two per-factor working
widths and reads as the design chart for how deep a decomposition the
chapter's working target can support.

\input{volumes/01-quantity/ch09-discipline-of-estimation/figures/fig-log-uncertainty-stacking.tex}

The Monte Carlo verification in
\repopath{volumes/01-quantity/ch09-discipline-of-estimation/code/monte_carlo_fermi.py}
samples random factor errors and confirms the predicted log-quadrature
combined width to within a few percent for $k$ up to seven; the
script also prints the combined one-sigma factor for each $k$, which
is the number a reader uses to translate a stated decomposition into
a working uncertainty band.

\paragraph{Worked example: world primary energy.}
World annual primary energy consumption decomposes as world population
times per-capita primary power times seconds per year, three factors
with per-factor uncertainty around $1.3\times$ (population is known to
about $1.05\times$; per-capita power varies regionally by a factor of
five but the world-average value is known to about $1.2\times$;
seconds per year is exact). The combined log-quadrature uncertainty is
$\sigma_{\ln X} = \sqrt{3} \times \ln 1.3 \approx 0.45$, a
multiplicative one-sigma factor of $e^{0.45} \approx 1.57$. The
nominal estimate, $8 \times 10^{9} \times 2500 \,\si{\watt} \times
3.15 \times 10^{7} \,\si{\second} \approx 6 \times 10^{20}
\,\si{\joule}$, lies within a factor of two of the Energy Institute's
$6.0 \times 10^{20} \,\si{\joule}$ figure for 2022
\cite{data:ch09-bp-statistical-2024}, well inside the predicted band.

\subsection{Postmortem: which factor dominated}

The postmortem is the move that turns the estimate into a learning
event. Once the answer is in hand and ground truth, where available,
has been compared, the reader identifies the factor whose uncertainty
dominated the result. That factor is the one whose anchor point in the
reader's working knowledge needs improvement. The reader records the
factor and, where possible, the truer value; the next time a similar
question arises, the reader's estimate starts from a tighter anchor.

The Chicago piano-tuner estimate's dominant uncertainty was the
fraction of households with a piano. A reader who learns, from this
estimate, that the fraction is closer to $1/40$ than to $1/4$ has
acquired an anchor that will sharpen any subsequent estimate involving
musical-instrument ownership. The Fermi practice is a directed
learning machine; the postmortem is what directs the learning.

\section{Estimation before calculation: a habit}\pathtag{core}

The discipline that makes estimation useful is the order in which it is
applied. The estimate is recorded before the calculation begins. Once
the calculation has produced a number, that number anchors judgement;
later factors tend to drift toward it. The estimate-before-calculate
discipline defends the calculation against silent error by preserving
a blind comparison value.

\subsection{Why the order matters}

Confirmation bias is the human tendency to interpret new evidence as
confirming existing beliefs. The engineer who has just calculated a
result is in possession of a strong existing belief about the answer.
An estimate produced after the calculation is filtered through that
belief: factors that would have been chosen as $0.5$ are chosen as
$0.4$ to bring the estimate closer to the calculated number; factors
that would have been chosen as $1.5$ are chosen as $1.7$. The estimate
becomes a story that the calculation has commissioned.

The defence is procedural. The estimate is performed first, written
down, and dated. The calculation follows. The comparison is made
against the recorded estimate. When the two disagree by more than the
estimate's stated range, the reader investigates the calculation
before adjusting the estimate.

The same procedural defence appears in many places in engineering: the
control sample taken before the experiment, the design review held
before the prototype is fabricated, the load case stated before the
analysis is run. In each case, a record made before a result is the
discipline that protects against the human tendency to align records
with results retroactively.

\subsection{The estimate as a sanity check}

A calculated number that disagrees with the estimate by more than a
factor of three is, by default, suspect. Three failure modes account
for most such disagreements.

\engterm{Unit error}. The calculation has multiplied or divided by a
unit conversion factor in the wrong direction. This is the most
frequent cause of factor-of-thousand and factor-of-million
discrepancies between estimate and calculation. A spreadsheet that
expected millimetres but received metres has the wrong answer by a
factor of $1000$; one that expected litres but received cubic metres
has the wrong answer by a factor of $1000$. Volume I Chapter 1 is the
canonical home of the unit-error case.

\engterm{Sign error}. A subtraction that was meant to be an addition;
a logarithm whose argument was inverted; an exponent whose sign was
flipped. The estimate produces a positive result of order $10^{4}$;
the calculation produces a negative result of order $10^{4}$. The
factor-of-magnitude check passes; the sign check fails. The reader
who relies only on order-of-magnitude comparison will miss this
class of error.

\engterm{Decomposition error}. The estimate's decomposition was wrong:
the reader missed a factor, or double-counted a factor, or used the
wrong functional form. This is the hardest disagreement to resolve,
because the disagreement says only that one of the two arguments is
wrong, and the engineer must investigate both. The investigation often
reveals that the calculation is right and the estimate's decomposition
was not what the question called for. The reader corrects their
decomposition and learns something about the question.

The first two classes are caught by the estimate within minutes. The
third class can take hours. The estimate-before-calculate discipline
makes all three visible; without it, the unit error and the sign error
slip silently into the report.

\subsection{The habit's place in a working session}

A working engineering session that uses the discipline has the
following shape. The question is stated, with the units of the answer
identified. The estimate is performed and recorded, with its
decomposition and its expected range. The calculation is performed.
The two are compared. Where they agree within the estimate's range,
the calculation is taken as the working answer; where they disagree,
the investigation begins.

The habit costs about ten minutes per estimate in the first weeks of
practice. After several months it often costs two minutes; after
enough repetition it becomes part of how the engineer reads the
question. The cost is small enough that the check belongs before any
calculation whose result will leave the desk.

\section{Sanity-checking calculated results}\pathtag{core}

Once the calculation is done, the estimate is one of several
sanity checks the engineer applies to the result before passing it
on. The other checks complement the estimate; together they catch
errors that any single check would miss.

\subsection{The order-of-magnitude check}

The first check, performed against the recorded estimate, is the one
described in the previous section. The calculated result and the
estimate should agree within the estimate's stated range. When they
do not, the engineer investigates before reporting.

\subsection{The dimensional check}

Volume I Chapter 2 introduced dimensional analysis as the syntax of
quantitative reasoning. The dimensional check applied to a calculated
result is the syntactic counterpart to the order-of-magnitude check's
semantic role. A result that has the right magnitude but the wrong
dimensions is, with rare exceptions, wrong. A result that has the
wrong magnitude but the right dimensions is more often wrong than
right.

The dimensional check is performed last, on the final algebraic
expression, before any numerical substitution. The expression's
dimensions are computed from the dimensions of its inputs and
compared to the expected dimensions of the answer. When they disagree,
the calculation is wrong before any number has been computed.

\subsection{The sign check}

Most engineering quantities have a definite sign. A mass is positive.
A length is positive. An energy expended is positive. A heat flow
into a system is positive when the convention is that heat flows in;
negative when the convention is that heat flows out. A torque is
signed; a force component along a coordinate axis is signed.

The sign check is done by going through each term in the result and
asking whether its sign matches the physical situation. A force
calculated to point upward when gravity should pull it down has
either a sign error or a coordinate-system mismatch; the calculation
must be re-examined. A heat that flows from cold to hot violates the
second law; the calculation is wrong.

The sign check is fast. It catches a class of errors that the
order-of-magnitude check misses and that the dimensional check often
misses.

\subsection{The limiting-case check}

A calculation that produces a defensible answer for general inputs
should produce a defensible answer for limiting-case inputs. Send one
input to zero; send another to infinity; set two inputs equal; impose
a symmetry. The result should reduce to a defensible limiting form.

The pendulum period $T = 2\pi \sqrt{L/g}$ has the limiting forms:
$T \to 0$ as $L \to 0$ (a pendulum with no length has no period);
$T \to \infty$ as $g \to 0$ (a pendulum with no gravity does not
oscillate); and $T = 2\pi \,\si{\second}$ when $L/g = 1 \,\si{\second
\squared}$. Each limit can be checked against intuition and dimensions.
The
calculation that has the wrong functional form often passes the
order-of-magnitude check at one input and fails it at another; the
limiting-case check exposes the discrepancy.

\subsection{The independent-method check}

The most powerful sanity check is to compute the same quantity by an
independent method. Volume I Chapter 4's project asked the reader to
measure a building's height by three independent methods. The
discipline applies equally to calculations: a result computed by one
algebraic route can be checked by computing the same result by a
different route. When the two routes agree, the result has been
checked against arithmetic error; when they disagree, the engineer
investigates.

The independent-method check is the most labour-intensive of the
sanity checks because it requires building two paths to the same
result. For safety-critical work, the second path is normally
performed by a different engineer. Shared inputs, such as
requirements, material properties, and environmental assumptions,
are named explicitly and audited because common inputs can create
common-mode error.

\subsection{The reasonable-result check}

The final check is the engineer's judgement of whether the result is
reasonable for the system being modelled. A calculated bridge
deflection of $0.001 \,\si{\milli\meter}$ under design load is
suspect not because the dimensional check fails but because the
working engineer knows that a real bridge under design load deflects
by several centimetres. A calculated motor current of
$10{,}000 \,\si{\ampere}$ for a household appliance is suspect
because the household electrical supply does not deliver currents at
that scale. The reasonable-result check uses the engineer's working
knowledge of the system to spot a result that the more abstract
checks have all passed but that working experience says cannot be
right.

The check is the slowest to develop, because it requires accumulated
working experience that no textbook can supply. The book can describe
the check; only practice on real systems can build the experience the
check uses.

\section{Estimation under deep uncertainty}\pathtag{core}

The estimates of section 9.1 through section 9.4 assume that each
factor has a defensible nominal value within a factor of two or three.
A separate class of estimation problem arises when one or more
factors are themselves only known within an order of magnitude, or
when the underlying physics or population is itself uncertain. We
call this \engterm{deep uncertainty}, after the convention in
risk-analysis literature, and treat it with a different set of
moves.

\subsection{When the factor's uncertainty is the answer}

For \emph{the total mass of microplastics in the world's oceans},
the answer depends on particle-size cutoff, sampling depth, polymer
identification method, and whether the estimate concerns surface
waters, the upper water column, sediments, or the whole ocean.
Published estimates therefore span orders of magnitude rather than
a neat factor-of-three band. A defensible estimate must state the
compartment, size range, sampling assumption, and source set before
it reports a range \cite{paper:zhao2025-ocean-microplastics}.

A deep-uncertainty estimate reports the range as the answer. The
discipline is to identify the upper and lower bounds defensible
from the available evidence, state the range explicitly, and
identify the factor whose uncertainty contributes most to the
range's width. A point estimate without its range misrepresents the
problem.

\subsection{Bounding from above and below}

Two complementary moves are available when no nominal value is
defensible.

\engterm{Upper bound}. The reader constructs the largest plausible
value for each factor, multiplies, and obtains an upper bound on the
result. Where a physical conservation law constrains the answer
(energy, mass, momentum cannot exceed available reserves), the
conservation law sets a tighter upper bound than the multiplicative
estimate. For \emph{total metabolic energy a single human expends in their
lifetime}, an upper bound is set by metabolic power times lifespan.
A rough adult metabolic power of $100 \,\si{\watt}$ sustained for
$80$ years gives $100 \times 80 \times 365.25 \times 86400 \approx
2.5 \times 10^{11} \,\si{\joule}$; a long-lived, active upper bound
is therefore closer to $3 \times 10^{11}$ to $4 \times 10^{11}
\,\si{\joule}$ \cite{text:national-academies-energy-1989}. The
conservation argument is hard; the previous power-of-ten must still
be checked.

\engterm{Lower bound}. The reader constructs the smallest plausible
value for each factor, multiplies, and obtains a lower bound on the
result. Lower bounds are often easier than upper bounds because
existence arguments suffice: the existence of a single working
example proves that the quantity is at least as large as that
example.

The two bounds together identify the range within which the answer
must lie. When the bounds are within a factor of three of each
other, the problem reduces to ordinary Fermi estimation. When the
bounds span more than an order of magnitude, the problem is a
deep-uncertainty problem and the bounds themselves are the answer.

\subsection{Megaprojects and the planning fallacy}

Project cost and schedule are the largest single class of practical
estimation failures in engineering, larger by far in dollar terms
than the technical accidents named elsewhere in the chapter.
Flyvbjerg's study of $258$ transport infrastructure projects over a
$70$-year period found cost overruns in $86\,\%$ of projects, with a
mean overrun of $28\,\%$ and a one-sigma spread that reached
$65\,\%$; the study also documented that the overrun statistics had
not improved across the decades the data covered
\cite{paper:ch09-flyvbjerg-cost-overrun-2002,text:ch09-flyvbjerg-megaprojects-2003}.
Kahneman and Tversky named the underlying mechanism the
\emph{planning fallacy}: a systematic tendency of forecasters to
underestimate the time and cost of their own projects, even when
they correctly estimate the time and cost of comparable past
projects done by other people
\cite{paper:ch09-kahneman-thinking-1979}.

Two illustrative cases. The Sydney Opera House was approved in
$1957$ on a cost estimate of AUD~$7$ million and a four-year
construction schedule. The building opened in $1973$, ten years
late, at a final cost of AUD~$102$ million, a factor-of-fourteen
overrun in nominal currency \cite{paper:ch09-bia-1972-sydney}. The
Boston Central Artery (the Big Dig) was approved in $1985$ on a
cost estimate of \$2.8 billion. The tunnel system completed in
$2007$ at a cost of approximately \$14.8 billion, a factor-of-five
overrun in nominal currency and a factor-of-three overrun in
constant dollars \cite{text:ch09-flyvbjerg-megaprojects-2003}.

The estimation lesson is that cost estimates produced inside the
organisation responsible for the project are systematically
biased; the defence is what Flyvbjerg calls \engterm{reference
class forecasting}: anchor the new project's cost on the empirical
cost distribution of similar past projects, then adjust for what
makes this project different. The discipline is the same
estimate-before-calculate move the chapter has been training, with
the reference class supplying the anchor. A planning team that
records the reference-class estimate before producing the
project-specific estimate has a defence against the planning
fallacy that no amount of internal analysis supplies.

\subsection{The MacKay convention: estimate, then revisit}

David MacKay's \emph{Sustainable Energy: Without the Hot Air}
\cite{gen:mackay2009} is a sustained worked example of deep-
uncertainty estimation in the energy domain. MacKay's discipline,
adopted here, is to give the best available point estimate, with
the underlying assumptions explicit, and to invite the reader to
revisit the estimate as new data become available. The estimate is
dated, so a later reader knows when to revisit it. The discipline of
dating estimates is what lets a reader, ten years later, see whether
the estimate has aged.

\subsection{A second canonical estimate: world plastic production}

The second canonical estimate of the chapter exercises the same four
moves on a different domain. The question is not deep-uncertainty in
the technical sense, since a credible industry total exists, but the
two principal factors (per-capita plastic use and the structural
correction for income disparity) carry wider working uncertainties
than the piano-tuner factors. The estimate is also one whose ground
truth has migrated in the last two decades, which makes the
date-the-estimate discipline visible.

\begin{estimation}
\textbf{What is the total mass of plastic produced worldwide in one
year, in tonnes, current as of 2022?}

\smallskip
\textit{Estimate, before reading on. The factors that come up are
world population, per-capita annual plastic use, and a structural
correction for income disparity in consumption.}

\smallskip
The world population in 2022 was $8.0 \times 10^{9}$, current per
the U.N.\ World Population Prospects. The mean annual plastic
consumption per capita is the harder factor. In a high-income
country a working anchor is $100 \,\si{\kilogram}$ per person per
year (packaging, fibres, durable goods, construction). In a
low-income country the per-capita figure is closer to
$10 \,\si{\kilogram}$ per person per year. The income-weighted
world mean sits between, around $50 \,\si{\kilogram}$ per person
per year. The product is then
\[
8.0 \times 10^{9} \,\text{persons}
\times 50 \,\si{\kilo\gram\per\text{person\,yr}}
\approx 4 \times 10^{11} \,\si{\kilo\gram}
= 4 \times 10^{8} \,\si{\tonne}.
\]
The defensible range, given a per-capita factor uncertainty of about
$1.5\times$ and a structural-correction uncertainty of about
$1.2\times$, is approximately $2.5 \times 10^{8}$ to $5.5 \times
10^{8}$ tonnes.

Plastics Europe's \emph{Plastics, the Facts 2023} reports global
plastics production of approximately $400 \,\si{\mega\tonne}$ for
2022 \cite{data:ch09-plastics-europe-2023}. The estimate lands on
the published figure to within $10\,\%$.

The postmortem is short: the per-capita-use factor was the
dominant uncertainty; the published number is dominated by
high-income-country and Chinese consumption, and the income-weighted
mean of $50 \,\si{\kilo\gram}$ per person per year was the lever
the estimate turned on. A reader who later learns that
high-income-country per-capita plastic use is closer to
$140 \,\si{\kilo\gram}$ per person per year (and that production
includes non-final-consumption use such as industrial plastics) can
narrow the estimate by about $20\,\%$. The estimate also dates
quickly: the 2022 figure may already be replaced by a 2025 figure
that includes faster growth in middle-income countries. The
estimate's defensibility lives in being able to recompute when the
inputs change.
\end{estimation}

\subsection{Worst-case versus expected-case}

The deep-uncertainty estimate is sometimes asked for its worst case
rather than its expected case. The two are different objects. The
worst-case estimate is the upper bound described above; it is
appropriate when the consequence of underestimation is severe. The
expected-case estimate is the central value of the distribution; it
is appropriate for budgeting, planning, and ordinary engineering
decisions.

The reader should not collapse the two. A worst-case fuel
consumption used to size a fuel tank, reported as the expected
fuel consumption, leads to a tank designed for a regime that
rarely occurs and to a vehicle that carries unnecessary weight on
every flight. An expected-case structural load used to size a
beam, reported as the worst-case load, leads to a beam that fails
when the actual worst case arrives. The discipline is to name the
case the estimate represents.

\section{The estimation environment: a formal apparatus}\pathtag{core}

The reader has by now used the \verb|estimation| environment in every
previous chapter of this volume. Five early examples are the Boeing 747
maximum take-off weight in Chapter 1, the small-raindrop terminal
velocity in Chapter 2, the recalibration interval for a kitchen scale
in Chapter 3, the pendulum $g$-measurement uncertainty in Chapter 4,
and the analog-to-digital converter resolution required for the
thermistor measurement in Chapter 5. The environment has been used
informally; this section codifies its formal structure.

\subsection{The four moves}

A formal estimation block contains four moves, in the order given.

\engterm{Move 1: question}. The question is stated explicitly, in
bold, with the units of the answer identified. A question whose
units are unclear is not yet a quantitative question.

\engterm{Move 2: record the estimate}. The estimate is performed
and written down, with its decomposition and its assumed factor
ranges, before any calculation or lookup. The reader who omits this
step has not yet entered the estimation discipline.

\engterm{Move 3: compare to the result}. The estimate is compared
to a calculated, measured, or published result. The comparison is
quantitative: the ratio of the estimate to the result is computed,
and the result is recorded as ``within a factor of $k$,'' where
$k$ is the smaller of the ratio and its inverse.

\engterm{Move 4: postmortem}. The factor in the decomposition that
contributed the largest share of the discrepancy is identified.
The working knowledge that would tighten the estimate is named.
The reader records the lesson; the next estimate proceeds from a
tighter anchor.

The four moves are required. A block earns the name \verb|estimation|
only when it records the estimate before lookup, calculation,
measurement, or simulation. A block without a postmortem leaves the
dominant error source unnamed.

\subsection{The blocks already encountered}

The 747 estimate in Chapter 1 used the four-move structure: the
question (mass at maximum take-off weight, in tonnes); the recorded
estimate ($390$ to $430$ tonnes, decomposed into passenger load,
operating empty mass, and fuel); the comparison to Boeing's
$396{,}890 \,\si{\kilogram}$ maximum take-off weight for the 747-400
\cite{web:boeing-commercial-reference}; and the postmortem (the
empty-mass factor was the riskiest move; a beginner's intuition can
mistakenly anchor it on the payload).

The raindrop estimate in chapter 2 used dimensional analysis to
construct the decomposition itself. The decomposition was the
balance between gravity and inertial drag; the factors were the
drop diameter, the drop and air densities, and gravity. The
postmortem identified the inertial-drag-dominated regime as the
working approximation.

The kitchen-scale estimate in chapter 3 was a deep-uncertainty
problem of the kind described in section 9.5. The question was a
recalibration interval; the answer was a range bounded by the
user's tolerance, the scale's repeatability, and the absence of a
manufacturer drift specification. The postmortem identified the
headroom-to-tolerance ratio as the lever the user could pull.

The pendulum $g$-uncertainty estimate in chapter 4 was a
sensitivity analysis: which input dominates the output's
uncertainty. The decomposition was the linear propagation rule;
the factors were the input relative uncertainties weighted by
their sensitivity coefficients. The postmortem identified the
time measurement, with its squared exponent, as the dominant
contributor.

The thermistor ADC-resolution estimate in chapter 5 was an
instrument-specification estimate. The decomposition was the
voltage-divider relation; the factors were the supply voltage,
the divider ratio, and the ADC bit count. The postmortem
identified the temperature-dependent divider sensitivity as the
factor that changes with operating point.

Each of the five blocks performed the four moves, with the
postmortem turning the comparison into a learning event. This
chapter's section 9.1 added a sixth: the Chicago piano-tuner
estimate, which is the canonical Fermi-method demonstration.

\subsection{Where the environment recurs}

The environment recurs in every chapter of every later volume. The
reader who finishes the book has performed estimates in chapters on
mathematics, physics, chemistry, biology, information, machines,
systems, failure, design, and civilization. The cumulative count
across the twelve volumes is in the high hundreds; the reader who
finishes has the internal ruler the discipline aims to install.

The environment is also the formal home of two adjacent practices.
\engterm{Estimate-before-measure} is the same discipline applied to
a measurement that has not yet been taken; the reader records the
estimate before reading the instrument. \engterm{Estimate-before-
simulate} is the same discipline applied to a simulation that has
not yet been run; the reader records the estimate before the
simulator's first output. Both practices defend against the same
confirmation bias the estimate-before-calculate discipline
defends against, and both use the same four moves.

\subsection{Calibration: the reader's own track record}

The fifty Fermi problems at the end of the chapter are also the
reader's first calibration dataset. A reader who records, for each
estimate, both a point value and a stated $90\,\%$ confidence
interval can later compute the empirical hit rate of the intervals
against the published ground truth. A well-calibrated reader's $90$
percent intervals contain truth on $90$ percent of the problems;
\Cref{fig:vol01:ch09:calibration-plot} shows the typical
overconfident shape that an untrained estimator's first calibration
plot takes, and the diagonal it can be pulled toward by repeated
practice. The Brier score is the standard scalar summary of
calibration quality \cite{paper:ch09-brier-1950}; a reference
implementation is in
\repopath{volumes/01-quantity/ch09-discipline-of-estimation/code/brier_score.py}.

\input{volumes/01-quantity/ch09-discipline-of-estimation/figures/fig-calibration-plot.tex}

\begin{mastery}
The Good Judgment Project, an open forecasting tournament run from
$2011$ to $2015$, demonstrated that calibration is a trainable skill
\cite{text:ch09-tetlock-superforecasting-2015}. A small fraction of
participants (the \emph{superforecasters}) consistently beat the
aggregate by $30\,\%$ to $40\,\%$ on the project's Brier score. The
techniques the superforecasters used overlap heavily with the four
moves of this chapter: explicit decomposition, recorded
intermediate estimates, regular postmortems against ground truth,
and a habit of revising forecasts when new evidence arrives. The
implication for engineering practice is direct: the reader who
treats the fifty Fermi problems as the first month of a
multi-year calibration practice acquires forecasting skill that
transfers to project planning, reliability prediction, and the
risk-rate estimates this chapter's failure section is about.
Cooke's expert-judgement method, which weights experts by their
calibration performance on seed questions whose answers are known,
extends the same discipline to risk assessment in industries from
nuclear power to aviation \cite{paper:ch09-cooke-expert-2008}.
\end{mastery}

The same statistical mechanism that makes calibration work for the
individual also makes it work for crowds.
\Cref{fig:vol01:ch09:crowd-convergence} plots the running geometric
mean of independent estimates against the truth, reproducing the
$1906$ Galton ox-weight result: individual estimates scatter, the
geometric mean converges to truth as $1/\sqrt{n}$. The engineering
implication is that several independent decompositions of the same
problem, aggregated in log space, are tighter than any one of them.
A reader who has time for only one Fermi estimate accepts the
factor-of-three working target; a reader who has time for five and
aggregates them can reach a factor-of-two target without any one
decomposition becoming more accurate.

\section{Failure: the Challenger O-ring failure rate}\pathtag{core}

A failure-rate estimate can become dangerous when it travels through
an organisation without an independent sanity check. The Space
Shuttle Challenger O-ring case is the chapter's canonical example,
read here through the dissenting appendix Richard Feynman attached to
the Rogers Commission report \cite{acc:nasa-challenger,paper:ch09-feynman-appendix-f}.
The full technical and organisational case study lives in Volume X;
the present treatment is restricted to the estimation discipline
that the case bears on.

\subsection{The case in brief}

The Space Shuttle Challenger broke up $73$ seconds after launch on
$28$ January $1986$. The proximate cause was failure of an O-ring
seal in the right solid rocket booster's aft field joint at an
ambient launch temperature of approximately $-2 \,\si{\degreeCelsius}$.
Hot combustion gas escaped through the joint, damaged the external
tank attachment region, and led to vehicle breakup
\cite{acc:nasa-challenger}. The launch decision occurred after
Morton Thiokol engineers had objected to launch under the expected
cold-temperature conditions. The Rogers Commission report is the
primary source; Diane Vaughan's 1996 analysis is the canonical
secondary source on the organisational mechanism
\cite{hist:vaughan1996}.

The Challenger case is recorded in the named-cases registry at
\repopath{docs/research/accidents/challenger-1986.md}. This chapter's
framing differs from the volumes that follow: Volume V draws the
material lesson (elastomer cold-temperature behaviour); Volume IX
draws the reliability lesson (redundant seals whose failures are
correlated); Volume X draws the organisational lesson (the
normalization of deviance and the launch-decision teleconference);
Volume XI draws the ethics lesson. We draw the estimation lesson.

\paragraph{Technical mechanism.}
The SRB's segmented case was joined by tang-and-clevis field joints
sealed by two redundant fluorocarbon-elastomer O-rings. At normal
operating temperatures, the O-rings deformed under the joint's
pressure-driven rotation and maintained the seal. At low temperatures
the elastomer's resilience decreased; the O-ring's response time to
joint rotation increased; the seal could fail to engage during the
brief window after motor ignition during which the joint's geometry
changed. The launch temperature was the lowest at which the Space
Shuttle had ever launched and well below temperatures at which prior
O-ring erosion had been observed. At ignition, the right SRB's aft
field joint failed to seal completely; hot combustion gas escaped
from the joint, leaving a transient flame visible in launch
photography. The leak appeared to seal partially during boost as
combustion products solidified in the gap, but at $T + 58
\,\si{\second}$ wind shear loosened the temporary seal. A persistent
flame burned through the SRB's lower attachment strut to the external
tank and simultaneously through the external tank's aft dome. The
hydrogen tank failed; thrust from the SRB rotated the aft dome
rupture; the orbiter, still under aerodynamic loads, broke up
\cite{acc:nasa-challenger}.

\paragraph{Organisational context.}
Engineers at Morton Thiokol, the SRB contractor, had identified the
O-ring's temperature-dependent resilience as a concern and had
recommended against launch the night before STS-51-L. Under
management discussion within Morton Thiokol and a teleconference with
NASA, the recommendation was reversed; Morton Thiokol's management
voted unanimously to approve launch over the engineers' objection.
The Rogers Commission identified this reversal as a critical failure
point: the engineers' technical concerns had been correctly
recognised at the time but were not preserved through the decision
chain \cite{acc:nasa-challenger}. Vaughan's reanalysis of the same
documents identified ``the normalization of deviance,'' the pattern
in which signals of departure from design specification (O-ring
erosion observed on prior flights) were progressively reinterpreted
as within acceptable risk, rather than as evidence that the original
specification had not been met \cite{hist:vaughan1996}.

\paragraph{Lesson for estimation discipline.}
The estimation lesson is concentrated in Appendix F of the Rogers
Commission report, the dissenting appendix that Feynman insisted be
included \cite{paper:ch09-feynman-appendix-f}. Feynman's appendix is
the canonical primary source for the chapter's claim about the
discipline, because it documents both the mismatched estimates and
the way they coexisted in the same programme without reconciliation.
The next two subsections work the appendix's evidence through the
chapter's four moves.

\subsection{The unsanity-checked estimate}

Appendix F of the Rogers Commission report records estimates of
loss-of-vehicle-and-crew probability that range from roughly $1$ in
$100$ to $1$ in $100{,}000$ \cite{paper:ch09-feynman-appendix-f}.
Feynman reports that the higher-risk estimates came from working
engineers and the lower-risk estimates from management; he also
summarises range-safety evidence from prior solid rocket flights as
far closer to the $10^{-2}$ scale than to $10^{-5}$. The appendix's
closing line is the one most often quoted: ``For a successful
technology, reality must take precedence over public relations, for
nature cannot be fooled.''

The discrepancy of three orders of magnitude is the discrepancy this
section is about. The lower estimate, $10^{-5}$, was the working
number used in the programme. The higher estimate, $10^{-2}$, was an
independent check. By the discipline this chapter has been training,
a discrepancy of three orders of magnitude between two estimates of
the same quantity is a release-blocking signal: the engineer cannot
proceed without investigating which estimate is wrong, or whether
both are wrong in different directions.

The discrepancy did not receive the level of investigation it
demanded. A top-down management number near $10^{-5}$ and engineering
estimates near $10^{-2}$ cannot both guide the same launch decision
without reconciliation. Retrospectively, the Shuttle programme
suffered two catastrophic losses in $135$ flights, Challenger in 1986
and Columbia in 2003, a rate of about $1.5 \times 10^{-2}$ over the
completed programme \cite{acc:nasa-challenger,acc:caib-columbia}.
That retrospective figure does not prove what could have been known
before STS-51-L, but it shows which order of magnitude the historical
programme eventually occupied. The engineering estimates of 1985 were
within a factor of two of the eventual programme record; the
management estimate was wrong by three orders of magnitude.

\subsection{What the discipline would have caught}

The estimation discipline, applied at the level we have trained in
this chapter, would have stopped at the three-orders-of-magnitude
discrepancy. The discipline would have asked: which of the two
estimates is bottom-up, derived from component failure data and the
algebra of redundant-system reliability; which is top-down, derived
from a budgetary or programmatic allocation; which estimate is
consistent with the historical record of comparable systems; which
estimate is consistent with the observations of erosion and blow-by
in prior flights' O-ring post-flight inspections.

Each of those questions points away from $10^{-5}$. Feynman's
Appendix F reports working estimates around $1$ in $100$ and
engineering estimates for related Shuttle subsystems as high as $1$
in $300$ or $1$ to $2$ per $100$, while management still claimed $1$
in $100{,}000$ \cite{paper:ch09-feynman-appendix-f}. The observations
of O-ring erosion and blow-by on prior flights, treated as data
rather than as accepted anomalies, also push the estimate toward the
higher-risk order of magnitude. The lower number was the outlier; the
discipline would have required an explanation before using it.

Feynman's worked example in the appendix is itself a model Fermi
estimate. He decomposes the launch-failure probability through the
known number of solid-rocket failures in similar programmes
(approximately one in twenty-five for U.S.\ unmanned launches at the
time), discounts for the Shuttle's additional safety review, and
arrives at a working estimate of about one in a hundred per flight.
The decomposition is short, every factor is defensible from primary
data, and the result is within a factor of two of the eventual
two-loss-in-$135$-flight record. The estimate is what the chapter has
been training: question stated, factors named, decomposition
recorded, postmortem against the historical record built in. The
appendix is the chapter's case study in why the four moves matter.

The Vaughan analysis identifies, at the organisational level, the
mechanism by which the outlier was preserved \cite{hist:vaughan1996}.
The estimation discipline of this chapter is one defence against the
pattern. An engineer who has performed the bottom-up reliability
calculation, recorded the result, and compared it to the
historical-record estimate cannot, without effort, then adopt a
budgetary number that disagrees with both by three orders of
magnitude. The discipline is also a defence at the report level: an
estimate that travels with its decomposition and its
historical-record check is harder to overwrite by a number from
elsewhere in the organisation.

\subsection{The general lesson}

A defensible failure-rate estimate is checked against component
data, the historical record of comparable systems, and observed
in-service anomalies. The Challenger case is the canonical instance
because the disagreement between the management estimate and the
available cross-checks was three orders of magnitude. The same
failure mode can appear whenever a programme adopts a reliability
number for budgetary or scheduling purposes without the calculation
the number appears to represent.

The principle is the chapter's closing rule.

\begin{principle}[Estimates are sanity-checked against independent
estimates, or they do not leave the desk]
A reported estimate of a quantity that matters is sanity-checked
against at least one independent estimate, derived by a different
method or from different data. When the two agree within their
stated uncertainty, the estimate is reportable. When they
disagree by more than the stated uncertainty, the engineering
action is to investigate the disagreement before reporting any
estimate at all. An estimate that has not been cross-checked
against an independent route is, by this discipline, a working
number that is not yet ready to leave the desk.
\end{principle}

\section{Estimation as a moral discipline}\pathtag{core}

We close Volume I where the volume began: with the claim that
measurement and engineering are the same discipline at different
scales. Estimation is the form measurement takes when the
instrument is not yet at hand, when the experiment has not yet
been performed, when the calculation has not yet been written.
The reader who carries the discipline forward into Volume II and
beyond carries with them the habit by which a number, even when
it is approximate, is honest.

The discipline has professional content. An engineer who records
an estimate before calculation is harder to move toward a number
selected for institutional convenience. The piano-tuner estimate
is a small case; the Challenger O-ring failure-rate estimate is a
large one. The same record discipline applies to both. An engineer
who declines to report a failure rate derived from allocation
rather than from component data preserves the distinction between
what is known, what is assumed, and what remains to be checked.

The discipline scales further still because records accumulate into
culture. A profession that estimates honestly leaves a traceable
account of its numbers: which came from measurements, which came
from models, which came from judgement, and which were later
checked against reality. A profession that estimates dishonestly
loses that trace. The difference is built one estimate at a time,
repeated across projects, reviews, and years. The reader who
finishes Volume I with the habit installed has done the hardest
part of becoming an engineer in the sense that this work uses the
word.

The remaining eleven volumes will build on the habit. Volume II
adds the mathematics that turns each estimated quantity into a
function, a vector, a probability, an algorithm; the estimates
acquire a richer apparatus and a sharper voice. Volumes III
through VIII apply the apparatus to the physical, chemical,
biological, informational, mechanical, and infrastructural
domains. Volumes IX through XII study what happens when the
domains meet: systems, failures, designs, and the civilization
the engineered systems compose. Estimation appears in every one
of those settings, and in each its four moves are the same: ask
the question, record the estimate, compare to the result,
identify the factor that dominated. The reader who has
practised the four moves on fifty Fermi problems at the close
of this volume can practise them, with the same confidence, on
any quantity the rest of the work asks them to estimate.

The fifty Fermi problems below are the volume's closing project.

\section{The fifty Fermi problems: project and exercises}\pathtag{core}

The exercise set for this chapter is also the volume's closing
project. The reader solves all fifty problems, writes out their
working for each, compares to ground truth where it can be looked
up, and writes a 1500-word reflection on the experience. The
project is the deliverable that turns Volume I from a sequence of
chapters into an installed habit.

\begin{project}[Volume I closing project: a personal estimation portfolio]

\textbf{Track and class.}
Analysis only. Hazard class: none. Tools: paper, pencil,
calculator, the reference data tables under

Compute three summary statistics across the forty-six

empirical hit-rate of the $90\,\%$ confidence intervals (the
calibration estimator). Plot the histogram of $\log_{10}(\text{

\Cref{fig:vol01:ch09:error-histogram} is the reference shape. Then
write a 1500-word reflection that addresses the five questions in
the deliverables list below.

\textbf{Deliverables.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
\item Fifty solution pages, one per problem.
\item One summary statistics page (geometric mean, geometric
standard deviation, hit-rate).
\item One histogram of log-ratios.
\item One 1500-word reflection that answers: which problem types
the reader's intuition was best at and why; which it was worst at
and what working knowledge would tighten those estimates; what
habits the reader acquired across Volume I; which anchor points in
the internal ruler need the most repair; what one habit the reader
will carry forward into Volume II as the most useful single move.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Success criteria.}
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
\item All fifty problems attempted with explicit decomposition,
factor estimates, and combination written out.
\item For the forty-six point-estimate problems, geometric mean of
estimate-to-truth ratios within a factor of two of unity (no large
systematic bias) and geometric standard deviation under a factor
of five (working-engineer precision across domains).
\item For the nineteen Phase-2 calibration problems, empirical
hit-rate of the $90\,\%$ intervals between $0.75$ and $1.0$ (the
range a Brier-score-trained estimator can reach within a month).
\item Four deep-uncertainty problems delivered as bounded ranges,
each with the dominant-uncertainty factor named.
\item Reflection of $1500 \pm 200$ words that identifies at least
two domain-specific anchor points the reader will revise after
the exercise.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Time budget.}
About one hour per problem in week 1 (twelve problems, twelve
hours); about forty-five minutes per problem in week 2 (nineteen
problems, fourteen hours); about thirty minutes per problem in
week 3 (nineteen problems, ten hours), plus four hours for the
consolidation and reflection. Total: about forty hours, distributed
across three weeks. The rate accelerates with practice because the
internal ruler tightens.

\textbf{Editorial provision.}
Before release, the editor publishes reference solutions to all
fifty problems, with working, named ground-truth sources, access
dates, units, and ratios to the reference values. The reader
compares only \emph{after} completing their own estimates. A Fermi
problem has defensible and indefensible answers; the reflection's
job is to make the reader's defence explicit. The reference table
under \repopath{volumes/01-quantity/ch09-discipline-of-estimation/data/fermi50\_ground\_truth.csv}
is the editor's working draft of the reference values.

\end{project}

\subsection*{Physical scales: mass, length, time, energy}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}[\textit{Fermi 1, mass scales}]
Estimate the mass of all human beings alive today, in tonnes,
current as of 2024. State your assumptions for world population,
adult fraction, child fraction, mean adult mass, and mean child
mass. Compare your result to a reference calculation built from a
named population source and a named body-mass source; do not
require a published product of population times mean adult mass.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
World population $\approx 8.0 \times 10^{9}$ (U.N.\ World
Population Prospects 2024); adult fraction $\approx 0.7$ (children
under $18$ are about $0.3$); mean adult mass $\approx 62
\,\si{\kilo\gram}$ (mean of male and female means across regions,
Walpole 2012); mean child mass $\approx 25 \,\si{\kilo\gram}$. The
sum is $8.0 \times 10^{9} \times (0.7 \times 62 + 0.3 \times 25)
\approx 8.0 \times 10^{9} \times 51 \approx 4.1 \times 10^{11}
\,\si{\kilo\gram} = 4.1 \times 10^{8} \,\si{\tonne}$. Within a
factor of two of the $4 \times 10^{8}$ tonnes reference value
constructed from primary sources.

\begin{exercise}[\textit{Fermi 2, mass scales}]
Estimate the total mass of fresh water held in the Earth's
freshwater lakes, in tonnes. Decompose into the surface area of the
fresh-water lakes, the mean lake depth, and the density of fresh
water. Compare to the published figure for global lake water volume
in any standard hydrology reference.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Global lake surface area $\approx 2.7 \times 10^{6} \,\si{\kilo\meter
\squared} \approx 2.7 \times 10^{12} \,\si{\meter\squared}$ (Verpoorter
\emph{et al.}\ 2014); mean lake depth, dominated by deep tectonic
lakes such as Baikal, $\approx 40 \,\si{\meter}$; density of fresh
water $1000 \,\si{\kilo\gram\per\meter\cubed}$. The product is
$2.7 \times 10^{12} \times 40 \times 1000 \approx 1.1 \times 10^{17}
\,\si{\kilo\gram} \approx 10^{14} \,\si{\tonne}$. The Verpoorter
reference value is $1.8 \times 10^{14} \,\si{\tonne}$; within a
factor of two. Dominant uncertainty: mean depth, which is sensitive
to a handful of large lakes.

\begin{exercise}[\textit{Fermi 3, length scales}]
Estimate the total length of the public road network on Earth, in
kilometres, current as of 2020. Decompose into the population, the
road length per person, and any structural correction for
urban-versus-rural distribution. Compare to the figure published by
any international transport database (CIA World Factbook, World Bank,
or equivalent).
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
World population $\approx 7.8 \times 10^{9}$ in 2020; road length
per person varies from about $20 \,\si{\meter}$ (low-income
countries) to about $40 \,\si{\meter}$ (high-income with extensive
rural network); income-weighted world mean $\approx 8 \,\si{\meter}$
per person on the conservative side (the OECD-style countries have a
small share of population but a large share of road). The product is
$7.8 \times 10^{9} \times 8 \approx 6 \times 10^{10} \,\si{\meter}
= 6 \times 10^{7} \,\si{\kilo\meter}$. The CIA World Factbook
totals roughly $6.4 \times 10^{7} \,\si{\kilo\meter}$; within
$10\,\%$. Dominant uncertainty: the meaning of ``public road'' (paved
versus unpaved) varies across reporting authorities.

\begin{exercise}[\textit{Fermi 4, length scales}]
Estimate the total length of capillary blood vessels in a single
adult human, in kilometres. Decompose into the volume of blood, the
fraction of the cardiovascular system in capillaries, the typical
capillary diameter, and the geometry. Compare to the figure cited
in any standard human-physiology textbook.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Total blood volume $\approx 5 \,\si{\liter} = 5 \times 10^{-3}
\,\si{\meter\cubed}$; capillary fraction of total blood volume
$\approx 5\,\%$ (Pittman 2011); capillary cross-section radius
$\approx 4 \times 10^{-6} \,\si{\meter}$, so cross-section area
$\approx \pi (4 \times 10^{-6})^{2} \approx 5 \times 10^{-11}
\,\si{\meter\squared}$. Capillary volume $\approx 2.5 \times 10^{-4}
\,\si{\meter\cubed}$; total capillary length $\approx 2.5 \times
10^{-4} / (5 \times 10^{-11}) \approx 5 \times 10^{6} \,\si{\meter}
= 5 \times 10^{3} \,\si{\kilo\meter}$. Textbook figure (Pittman)
is $\sim 10^{5} \,\si{\kilo\meter}$; the estimate is low by an
order of magnitude, dominated by the capillary-volume-fraction
factor, which is closer to $10\,\%$ across the microvascular bed.

\begin{exercise}[\textit{Fermi 5, time scales}]
Estimate the number of seconds in a typical human lifetime, current
as of 2020. Decompose into life expectancy in years, days per year,
and seconds per day. Comment on whether the answer changes
materially when life expectancy is taken at the level of a high-
income country versus a low-income country.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
World life expectancy $\approx 73$ years; days per year $365.25$;
seconds per day $8.64 \times 10^{4}$. Product:
$73 \times 365.25 \times 8.64 \times 10^{4} \approx 2.3 \times
10^{9} \,\si{\second}$. High-income life expectancy
($\approx 82$ years) gives $2.6 \times 10^{9} \,\si{\second}$;
low-income life expectancy ($\approx 65$ years) gives $2.0 \times
10^{9} \,\si{\second}$. The variation is within a factor of $1.3$,
small at the order-of-magnitude scale. A human lifetime is
approximately $10^{9.4}$ seconds at world average.

\begin{exercise}[\textit{Fermi 6, energy scales}]
Estimate the total kinetic energy of a moving long-haul aircraft at
cruise, in joules. Decompose into the aircraft mass and the cruise
velocity. Compare to the chemical energy in the aircraft's fuel
load.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Aircraft mass at cruise (B777-300ER, mid-flight) $\approx 2.5
\times 10^{5} \,\si{\kilo\gram}$; cruise velocity $\approx 250
\,\si{\meter\per\second}$ (Mach 0.85 at altitude). Kinetic
energy: $\tfrac{1}{2} \times 2.5 \times 10^{5} \times 250^{2}
\approx 7.8 \times 10^{9} \,\si{\joule}$. Fuel chemical energy:
fuel load $\approx 1 \times 10^{5} \,\si{\kilo\gram}$ at Jet-A
energy density $43 \,\si{\mega\joule\per\kilo\gram}$ gives
$4.3 \times 10^{12} \,\si{\joule}$, three orders of magnitude
larger. The ratio explains why aircraft cannot recover kinetic
energy as a meaningful contribution to range.

\begin{exercise}[\textit{Fermi 7, energy scales}]
Estimate the total annual primary energy consumption of the world,
in joules, current as of 2022. Decompose into world population,
per-capita primary energy consumption, and seconds in a year.
Compare to the figure published by the International Energy Agency
or the Energy Institute Statistical Review of World Energy.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
World population $\approx 8.0 \times 10^{9}$; per-capita primary
power (world average) $\approx 2500 \,\si{\watt}$; seconds in a
year $3.15 \times 10^{7}$. Product: $8.0 \times 10^{9} \times 2500
\times 3.15 \times 10^{7} \approx 6.3 \times 10^{20} \,\si{\joule}$.
The Energy Institute Statistical Review for 2022 reports $6.04
\times 10^{20} \,\si{\joule}$ (604 EJ)
\cite{data:ch09-bp-statistical-2024}; within $5\,\%$. The estimate
worked through the per-capita-power factor, which is dominated by
high-income-country use; the result is tight because the seconds-
per-year factor is exact and world population is known to within
a percent.

\begin{exercise}[\textit{Fermi 8, planetary scales}]
Estimate the mass of the Earth's atmosphere, in kilograms. Decompose
into the surface area of the Earth, the column mass per unit area
(ambient pressure divided by gravitational acceleration), and the
working approximation that the column mass is roughly the same
across the surface. Compare to the published figure.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Earth surface area $\approx 4\pi R_{\oplus}^{2} = 4\pi (6.37 \times
10^{6})^{2} \approx 5.1 \times 10^{14} \,\si{\meter\squared}$;
column mass per area $= p/g = 1.013 \times 10^{5} / 9.81 \approx
1.03 \times 10^{4} \,\si{\kilo\gram\per\meter\squared}$. Product:
$5.1 \times 10^{14} \times 1.03 \times 10^{4} \approx 5.3 \times
10^{18} \,\si{\kilo\gram}$. Reference value (Trenberth 2005):
$5.15 \times 10^{18} \,\si{\kilo\gram}$. Within $3\,\%$. The
estimate is one of the cleanest in the set because all factors are
known to high precision; this is a calibration anchor for the
reader's internal ruler.

\begin{exercise}[\textit{Fermi 9, planetary scales}]
Estimate the mass of the Earth's oceans, in kilograms. Decompose
into the surface area of the oceans, the mean ocean depth, and the
density of seawater. Compare to the published figure.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Ocean surface area $\approx 0.71 \times 5.1 \times 10^{14}
\approx 3.6 \times 10^{14} \,\si{\meter\squared}$; mean ocean
depth $\approx 3700 \,\si{\meter}$; seawater density $\approx
1025 \,\si{\kilo\gram\per\meter\cubed}$. Product: $3.6 \times
10^{14} \times 3700 \times 1025 \approx 1.37 \times 10^{21}
\,\si{\kilo\gram}$. Reference value: $1.4 \times 10^{21}
\,\si{\kilo\gram}$ (IPCC AR6). Within $5\,\%$. Like Fermi 8, the
calculation is dominated by well-known factors.

\begin{exercise}[\textit{Fermi 10, cosmic scales}]
Estimate the number of stars in the Milky Way galaxy. Decompose
into the galaxy's volume, the volume per star in the local stellar
neighbourhood (computed from the typical interstellar distance),
and a structural correction for the difference between the local
neighbourhood and the galactic mean. Compare to the published
figure (typically given as $1$ to $4 \times 10^{11}$).
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Galaxy disk radius $\approx 1.5 \times 10^{4}$ pc; thickness
$\approx 300$ pc; disk volume $\approx \pi (1.5 \times 10^{4})^{2}
\times 300 \approx 2.1 \times 10^{11}$ pc$^{3}$. Local typical
interstellar distance $\approx 1$ pc, so local volume per star
$\approx 1$ pc$^{3}$. Local-to-galactic structural correction:
the local neighbourhood is at moderate density; the galactic bulge
is denser and the outer disk sparser; structural factor $\approx
0.5$. Estimate: $2.1 \times 10^{11} \times 1 \times 0.5 \approx
10^{11}$ stars. The published range $1$ to $4 \times 10^{11}$
contains the estimate; the order-of-magnitude target is met.

\begin{exercise}[\textit{Fermi 11, cosmic scales}]
Estimate the age of the universe in seconds, current as of 2024.
Decompose into the published age in years and the seconds in a
year. State the bound on uncertainty from the published age's own
uncertainty.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Published age (Planck 2018) $\approx 1.378 \times 10^{10}$ years
with a $\pm 0.020 \times 10^{10}$-year one-sigma uncertainty;
seconds per year $3.156 \times 10^{7}$. Product: $1.378 \times
10^{10} \times 3.156 \times 10^{7} \approx 4.35 \times 10^{17}
\,\si{\second}$. The relative uncertainty inherits from the age's
$\pm 1.5\,\%$; the resulting uncertainty band is $\pm 7 \times
10^{15} \,\si{\second}$.

\begin{exercise}[\textit{Fermi 12, atomic scales}]
Estimate the number of atoms in a copper coin of mass $5 \,\si{\gram}$.
Decompose into the coin mass, the atomic mass of copper, and
Avogadro's number. Comment on the precision your estimate
inherits from each factor.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Moles of copper: $m / M = 5 \,\si{\gram} / 63.5 \,\si{\gram\per
\mol} \approx 7.87 \times 10^{-2}$ mol. Atoms: $7.87 \times 10^{-2}
\times 6.022 \times 10^{23} \approx 4.74 \times 10^{22}$ atoms. The
coin mass is the loosest factor (a US cent is now zinc-clad copper
of about $2.5 \,\si{\gram}$; a pure $5 \,\si{\gram}$ copper coin
might be an idealised reference). Atomic mass and Avogadro's number
are exact in the post-2019 SI; the estimate inherits its precision
entirely from the mass and the purity assumption.

\subsection*{Urban and civilization scales}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}[\textit{Fermi 13, urban populations}]
Estimate the number of people who live within $20 \,\si{\kilo\meter}$
of the centre of the largest city in your country. Decompose by area,
mean population density, and any geographic exclusions. Compare to
published metropolitan-area population figures.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
A circle of radius $20 \,\si{\kilo\meter}$ has area $\pi \times
20^{2} \approx 1257 \,\si{\kilo\meter\squared}$. For a typical
large city in a high-income country (mean metro density of about
$2000 \,\si{persons\per\kilo\meter\squared}$), the count is
$\approx 1257 \times 2000 \approx 2.5 \times 10^{6}$. For a
megacity such as Tokyo or Mumbai (density $\approx 10^{4}$
persons/$\,\si{\kilo\meter\squared}$ within $20 \,\si{\kilo\meter}$),
the count rises to $\approx 1.2 \times 10^{7}$. Reader compares to
the named metropolitan figure; ratio typically within a factor
of two of the central-area census.

\begin{exercise}[\textit{Fermi 14, urban infrastructure}]
Estimate the total length of underground sewer pipe in a metropolitan
area of about $5$ million people. Decompose into the population, the
sewer-pipe length per person, and any structural correction for
density of the urban area. Compare where local utility statistics are
available.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Population: $5 \times 10^{6}$. Sewer length per person in a typical
North American metro is $\approx 3 \,\si{\meter}$ per person
(local-collector plus laterals plus trunk); high-density European
cities $\approx 1.5 \,\si{\meter}$ per person; low-density US Sun
Belt $\approx 5 \,\si{\meter}$ per person. Working mid-value: $3
\,\si{\meter}$ per person. Total length: $5 \times 10^{6} \times 3
\approx 1.5 \times 10^{7} \,\si{\meter} = 15{,}000 \,\si{\kilo\meter}$.
A reference comparable is the London sewer network: about $9000
\,\si{\kilo\meter}$ for $9 \times 10^{6}$ residents (i.e.\ $1$ m per
person). The estimate lies within a factor of two of these
references; the density-correction factor is the dominant lever.

\begin{exercise}[\textit{Fermi 15, buildings}]
Estimate the total floor area of all the residential buildings in a
metropolitan area of about $5$ million people, in square metres.
Decompose into the population, the mean residential floor area per
person, and the structural correction for vacancy. Compare to
published municipal land-use statistics.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Population: $5 \times 10^{6}$; mean residential floor area per
person $\approx 40 \,\si{\meter\squared}$ in a developed metro (US:
$\approx 70$; EU: $\approx 35$; Asian high-density: $\approx 20$);
vacancy correction $\approx 1.1$. Floor area: $5 \times 10^{6}
\times 40 \times 1.1 \approx 2.2 \times 10^{8} \,\si{\meter\squared}$.
Comparison: New York City reports approximately $2.6 \times 10^{8}
\,\si{\meter\squared}$ of residential floor area for $\approx 8.4
\times 10^{6}$ residents (Department of City Planning PLUTO data).
Order-of-magnitude consistent; the per-person factor is the
dominant lever and varies by a factor of three across countries.

\begin{exercise}[\textit{Fermi 16, traffic}]
Estimate the number of motor vehicles registered in a country of
about $50$ million people. Decompose into the population, the mean
vehicles per person, and the structural correction for urban-rural
distribution. Compare to the country's transport ministry's
published figures.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Population: $5 \times 10^{7}$. Vehicles per person in a
high-income country $\approx 0.6$ (cars plus light trucks, OECD
average); in a middle-income country $\approx 0.2$. Working value
$\approx 0.5$. Vehicles registered: $5 \times 10^{7} \times 0.5
\approx 2.5 \times 10^{7}$. For South Korea ($\approx 5.1 \times
10^{7}$ population), the published registration is $\approx 2.5
\times 10^{7}$ as of 2022; within $5\,\%$. For Italy ($\approx 5.9
\times 10^{7}$ population), the figure is $\approx 4.0 \times
10^{7}$; the estimate is low by a factor of $1.6$ because Italian
vehicle ownership per capita is higher than the OECD mean.

\begin{exercise}[\textit{Fermi 17, food}]
Estimate the total mass of food consumed in your country in one
year, in tonnes. Decompose into the population, the daily food
intake per person (in kilocalories or in mass), and the days in a
year. Compare to the figure published by the country's agriculture
ministry or by the FAO.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
For a country of $5 \times 10^{7}$ people, daily food intake $\approx
1.5 \,\si{\kilo\gram}$ per person per day (water-bearing solids
plus beverages, retail-equivalent), times $365$ days. Total: $5
\times 10^{7} \times 1.5 \times 365 \approx 2.7 \times 10^{10}
\,\si{\kilo\gram} = 2.7 \times 10^{7} \,\si{\tonne}$ per year. FAO
food-balance-sheet totals for Spain ($\approx 4.8 \times 10^{7}$
people, 2020) report approximately $3.2 \times 10^{7}$ tonnes; the
estimate matches within $20\,\%$. Dominant uncertainty: the
distinction between food-purchased and food-eaten (waste correction
$\approx 1.3$).

\begin{exercise}[\textit{Fermi 18, water}]
Estimate the daily domestic water consumption in your country, in
cubic metres. Decompose into the population, the per-capita
domestic water use, and any structural correction. Compare to the
figure published by the country's water utility.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
For a country of $5 \times 10^{7}$ people, per-capita domestic
water use $\approx 150 \,\si{\liter}$ per person per day (Europe;
USA $\approx 300$; developing $\approx 50$); total daily volume
$5 \times 10^{7} \times 0.15 \approx 7.5 \times 10^{6}
\,\si{\meter\cubed}$ per day. UK national water statistics (Ofwat,
$\approx 6.6 \times 10^{7}$ population) report $\approx 8.5 \times
10^{6} \,\si{\meter\cubed}$ per day domestic; consistent within
$15\,\%$ after scaling for population. Dominant uncertainty:
per-capita use, which varies by a factor of six across countries.

\begin{exercise}[\textit{Fermi 19, energy}]
Estimate the total electrical energy consumed in your country in
one year, in terawatt hours. Decompose into the population, the
per-capita electricity consumption, and the seconds-or-hours in a
year. Compare to the figure published by the country's grid
operator or by the IEA.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
For a high-income country of $5 \times 10^{7}$, per-capita electric
power $\approx 800 \,\si{\watt}$ (annual mean, all sectors); hours
in a year $8760$; product: $5 \times 10^{7} \times 800 \times 8760
/ 10^{9} \approx 350 \,\text{TWh}$ per year. South Korea (population
$5.1 \times 10^{7}$, 2022) consumed approximately $540 \,\text{TWh}$;
per-capita power $\approx 1200 \,\si{\watt}$ because of large
industrial demand. The estimate is low by a factor of $1.5$;
industrial-intensity correction is the dominant lever.

\begin{exercise}[\textit{Fermi 20, urban transport}]
Estimate the total number of train journeys made in a metropolitan
rail system of about $5$ million people in one year. Decompose into
the population, the journeys per person per year, and the
structural correction for the share of journeys made by rail
versus road. Compare to the figure published by the rail operator.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Population: $5 \times 10^{6}$; rail trips per person per year
$\approx 200$ in a transit-oriented metro (London, Tokyo)
versus $\approx 20$ in a sprawl metro (Houston). Working mid-value:
$120$. Total: $5 \times 10^{6} \times 120 = 6 \times 10^{8}$
journeys per year. Reference comparable: Madrid Metro carried
about $7 \times 10^{8}$ journeys in 2019 for a service area of
$\approx 6$ million people; within $15\,\%$. Dominant uncertainty:
the per-person-per-year factor, which depends on the rail system's
modal share.

\begin{exercise}[\textit{Fermi 21, civic services}]
Estimate the number of public-school teachers in a country of $50$
million people. Decompose into the population, the fraction of
school-age children, the mean class size, and the working hours
ratio. Compare to the figure published by the country's education
ministry.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Population: $5 \times 10^{7}$; school-age fraction (ages 5--17)
$\approx 0.18$; thus $\approx 9 \times 10^{6}$ school-age
children. Mean class size $\approx 22$; teachers $\approx 9
\times 10^{6} / 22 \approx 4.1 \times 10^{5}$. Working-hours
correction (one teacher serves multiple classes per day, but
the metric is one teacher per class-load equivalent): factor
$\approx 1.1$ for support staff. Final estimate: $\approx 4.5
\times 10^{5}$ teachers. UK schools workforce census (population
$\approx 5.6 \times 10^{7}$): approximately $5 \times 10^{5}$
teachers; within $15\,\%$ after population scaling.

\begin{exercise}[\textit{Fermi 22, civic infrastructure}]
Estimate the total mass of concrete poured in your country in the
last calendar year, in tonnes. Decompose into population, per-capita
annual concrete consumption, and a structural correction for
construction intensity. If the published source reports cement
rather than concrete, state the assumed cement fraction in concrete
and convert explicitly. Compare to the figure published by the
national cement or ready-mix concrete association.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
For a country of $5 \times 10^{7}$ people, world-mean per-capita
annual cement consumption $\approx 500 \,\si{\kilo\gram}$ per
person per year; concrete is about $13\,\%$ cement by mass, so
per-capita concrete $\approx 4 \,\si{\tonne}$ per year. Total:
$5 \times 10^{7} \times 4 \approx 2.0 \times 10^{8}$ tonnes per
year. For Spain ($\approx 4.7 \times 10^{7}$, mid-construction
cycle), Cembureau reports cement use of $\approx 1.4 \times 10^{7}$
tonnes; concrete equivalent $\approx 1.1 \times 10^{8}$ tonnes.
The estimate is high by a factor of two because per-capita cement
in mature economies is below the world mean; China and India
dominate the world figure.

\subsection*{Biology and medicine}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}[\textit{Fermi 23, body composition}]
Estimate the total mass of water in a typical adult human, in
kilograms. Decompose into the adult mass and the typical water
fraction (roughly $0.6$). Compare to the published figure from any
human-physiology reference.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Adult mass $70 \,\si{\kilo\gram}$; water fraction $0.6$ (a young
adult; the fraction drops with age and adipose mass). Water mass:
$42 \,\si{\kilo\gram}$. Reference value (any standard physiology
text, e.g.\ Tortora): $60 \pm 10 \,\%$ body water for typical
adults. The estimate sits in the middle of the published band; the
factor is well anchored.

\begin{exercise}[\textit{Fermi 24, body composition}]
Estimate the total number of cells in a typical adult human.
Decompose into the typical cell mass (a useful anchor point is
$10^{-9}$ to $10^{-12}$ kilograms depending on cell type) and the
adult mass. Compare to the figure published in any standard cell-
biology textbook (the typical answer cited is $30$ to $40$
trillion cells).
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
A naive estimate using a typical eukaryotic cell mass of
$10^{-12} \,\si{\kilo\gram}$ for a $70 \,\si{\kilo\gram}$ adult
gives $7 \times 10^{13}$ cells, which is too high. A more careful
estimate weights by cell type: red blood cells dominate the count
($\approx 2.5 \times 10^{13}$ in $5 \,\si{\liter}$ of blood at
$5 \times 10^{9}$ per mL); other cells contribute roughly the same
order. Sender, Fuchs, and Milo (2016) carried out the careful
weighting and arrived at $3.0 \times 10^{13}$ human cells, close
to the textbook ``$30$--$40$ trillion'' band
\cite{paper:ch09-sender-cells-2016}. The naive mass-divided-by-cell-mass
estimate is high by about a factor of $2$; the lesson is that
``typical cell mass'' hides a wide distribution.

\begin{exercise}[\textit{Fermi 25, metabolism}]
Estimate the total energy a typical adult human expends in one day,
in kilojoules. Decompose into the basal metabolic rate per
kilogram, the adult mass, the activity factor (a working anchor
point is $1.5$ for moderate activity), and the seconds per day.
Compare to the figure published by any human-nutrition reference.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Basal metabolic rate $\approx 1 \,\si{\watt\per\kilo\gram}$; for a
$70 \,\si{\kilo\gram}$ adult, BMR $\approx 70 \,\si{\watt}$.
Activity factor $1.5$ gives $105 \,\si{\watt}$. Day length $8.64
\times 10^{4} \,\si{\second}$. Daily energy: $105 \times 8.64
\times 10^{4} \approx 9.1 \times 10^{6} \,\si{\joule} \approx
9100 \,\si{\kilo\joule}$, equivalent to $\approx 2200 \,\text{kcal}$.
The published recommended daily intake of $2000$--$2500 \,\text{kcal}$
for moderate activity contains the estimate.

\begin{exercise}[\textit{Fermi 26, breathing}]
Estimate the volume of air a typical adult human breathes in one
year, in cubic metres. Decompose into the tidal volume, the
respiratory rate, and the seconds in a year. Compare to the figure
published by any human-physiology reference.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Tidal volume $\approx 0.5 \,\si{\liter} = 5 \times 10^{-4}
\,\si{\meter\cubed}$; respiratory rate $\approx 14$ breaths per
minute $= 0.23$ per second; seconds per year $3.15 \times 10^{7}$.
Annual volume: $5 \times 10^{-4} \times 0.23 \times 3.15 \times
10^{7} \approx 3600 \,\si{\meter\cubed}$. The published EPA
standard for residential air exposure is $\approx 4500$
$\si{\meter\cubed}$ per year (Exposure Factors Handbook, 2011);
within $25\,\%$. Difference: the EPA figure includes a higher
mean ventilation rate that accounts for activity.

\begin{exercise}[\textit{Fermi 27, blood}]
Estimate the total volume of blood pumped by the heart in a single
human lifetime, in cubic metres. Decompose into the cardiac output
per minute, the seconds in a year, and the lifespan in years.
Compare to the figure cited in any standard physiology textbook.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Cardiac output at rest $\approx 5 \,\si{\liter\per\min} = 8.3
\times 10^{-5} \,\si{\meter\cubed\per\second}$; lifespan $80$
years $= 2.5 \times 10^{9} \,\si{\second}$. Total volume:
$8.3 \times 10^{-5} \times 2.5 \times 10^{9} \approx 2.1 \times
10^{5} \,\si{\meter\cubed}$. The textbook figure is approximately
$2 \times 10^{5} \,\si{\meter\cubed}$ (about $200{,}000$ cubic
metres); within $5\,\%$. Anchor point: $5 \,\si{\liter}$ of blood
pass through the heart at rest every minute for a lifetime.

\begin{exercise}[\textit{Fermi 28, neurons}]
Estimate the number of synapses in a typical adult human brain.
Decompose into the number of neurons (a working anchor point is
$10^{11}$) and the synapses per neuron (a working anchor point is
$10^{3}$ to $10^{4}$). Compare to the figure cited in any standard
neuroscience reference.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Neurons $\approx 10^{11}$ (Herculano-Houzel 2009 revised from
Kandel's earlier $10^{12}$); mean synapses per neuron $\approx 5
\times 10^{3}$. Total: $10^{11} \times 5 \times 10^{3} = 5 \times
10^{14}$ synapses. The standard neuroscience reference range is
$1 \times 10^{14}$ to $5 \times 10^{14}$; the estimate sits at
the upper end. Dominant uncertainty: synapses per neuron varies
across cortical areas by a factor of three.

\begin{exercise}[\textit{Fermi 29, microbes}]
Estimate the number of bacterial cells in the human gut. Decompose
into the typical bacterial-cell mass and the typical mass of the
gut microbiome. Compare to the figure published in the bacterial-
to-human-cell ratio literature (roughly $10^{13}$ to $10^{14}$).
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Bacterial cell mass $\approx 10^{-15} \,\si{\kilo\gram}$ (E.\ coli
reference); gut microbiome mass $\approx 0.2 \,\si{\kilo\gram}$
(estimate widely circulated); naive count $\approx 0.2 / 10^{-15}
= 2 \times 10^{14}$ cells. Sender, Fuchs, and Milo's (2016)
careful weighting gives $3.8 \times 10^{13}$ bacteria in a
$70 \,\si{\kilo\gram}$ adult \cite{paper:ch09-sender-cells-2016};
within a factor of five of the naive estimate. The earlier $10:1$
bacteria-to-human-cell ratio claim was an overestimate by about
$10\times$, corrected by the Sender paper to about $1:1$.

\begin{exercise}[\textit{Fermi 30, drug doses}]
Estimate the total mass of acetaminophen (paracetamol) consumed
worldwide in one year, in tonnes. Decompose into the world
population, the fraction taking the drug regularly, the fraction
taking it occasionally, and the typical annual consumption per user.
Compare where pharmaceutical-industry production statistics are
available.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
World population: $8.0 \times 10^{9}$; fraction with regular access
to paracetamol $\approx 0.4$ (high- and middle-income countries);
mean doses per person per year $\approx 10$ (occasional use); dose
mass $\approx 500 \,\si{\milli\gram}$. Total: $8 \times 10^{9}
\times 0.4 \times 10 \times 0.5 \,\si{\gram} = 1.6 \times 10^{10}
\,\si{\gram} = 1.6 \times 10^{4} \,\si{\tonne}$. Industry estimates
of world paracetamol production are in the range $1 \times 10^{5}$
to $2 \times 10^{5}$ tonnes per year (active pharmaceutical
ingredient). The estimate is low by about an order of magnitude;
dominant uncertainty is the per-user annual consumption, which is
much higher for chronic-pain users.

\begin{exercise}[\textit{Fermi 31, healthcare}]
Estimate the number of medical X-ray examinations performed in a

correction. Compare to the figure published by the country's
radiation-protection authority.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Population: $5 \times 10^{7}$; X-ray rate $\approx 1$ per person

\subsection*{Technology and engineering}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}[\textit{Fermi 33, computation}]
Estimate the total number of transistors fabricated worldwide in
one year, current as of 2022. Decompose into wafer starts, die per
wafer, transistors per die, and chip mix. Treat the comparison as
an industry-estimate problem: name the source used for wafer starts
and the source used for representative transistor counts, then
report the answer as an order-of-magnitude range rather than as a
single published truth.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Wafer starts (SEMI, 2022): $\approx 1.5 \times 10^{8}$
$300\,\si{\milli\meter}$-equivalent wafers; usable die area per
wafer $\approx 7 \times 10^{4} \,\si{\milli\meter\squared}$;
representative leading-edge die transistor density $\approx 10^{8}$
per $\,\si{\milli\meter\squared}$, but trailing-edge nodes
(automotive, power) sit at $\approx 10^{6}$ per $\,\si{\milli\meter\squared}$.
Weighted mean $\approx 10^{7}$ per $\,\si{\milli\meter\squared}$.
Total: $1.5 \times 10^{8} \times 7 \times 10^{4} \times 10^{7}
\approx 10^{20}$ transistors per year. Range: $10^{20}$ to $10^{22}$
depending on the mix weighting. Published estimates by Koomey and
others cluster around $10^{22}$ transistors per year as of 2022.

\begin{exercise}[\textit{Fermi 34, computation}]
Estimate the total electrical energy consumed by all the world's
data centres in one year, in terawatt hours, current as of 2022.
Decompose into the world's data-centre electrical power demand and
the seconds in a year. Compare to the figure published by the IEA
(typical answer is around $200$ to $400$ TWh per year as of 2022).
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
World data-centre electrical power demand: world internet-connected
device count $\approx 5 \times 10^{9}$, with $\approx 30 \,\si{\watt}$
of data-centre power allocated per device on average (hyperscale
plus enterprise plus edge). Total power $\approx 1.5 \times 10^{11}
\,\si{\watt}$; hours per year $8760$; annual energy $\approx 1.3
\times 10^{12} \,\text{kWh} = 1300 \,\text{TWh}$. The IEA 2024
range for 2022 is $240$ to $340 \,\text{TWh}$
\cite{data:ch09-iea-data-centres-2024}; the estimate is high by a
factor of about $4$, dominated by an aggressive
power-per-device allocation. A working anchor: data-centre power
demand is approximately $1\,\%$ of world electricity, and world
electricity is $\approx 2.9 \times 10^{4} \,\text{TWh}$, so
$\approx 290 \,\text{TWh}$ is the right order.

\begin{exercise}[\textit{Fermi 35, networking}]\pathtag{standard}
Estimate the total volume of data transmitted across the global
internet in one second, in bytes per second, current as of 2024.
Decompose into annual global internet traffic or user count,
per-user traffic, and duty cycle. Compare to a named current source
such as ITU, TeleGeography, Cloudflare, or a current Cisco archived
forecast only if the year and metric match.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
User count $\approx 5.4 \times 10^{9}$ (ITU, 2024); per-user
average traffic $\approx 200 \,\si{\giga\byte}$ per month $= 6.4
\,\si{\giga\byte}$ per day; spread evenly over the day this is
$\approx 7.4 \times 10^{4} \,\si{\byte\per\second}$. Total:
$5.4 \times 10^{9} \times 7.4 \times 10^{4} \approx 4 \times 10^{14}
\,\si{\byte\per\second}$. Duty-cycle correction (peak versus
average) increases the peak rate by a factor of $\approx 2$. The
TeleGeography 2024 estimate of global IP traffic averages
approximately $1$ to $2 \times 10^{14} \,\si{\byte\per\second}$;
the estimate is high by a factor of two to three, dominated by
the per-user-traffic figure (skewed by video streaming and CDN
caching).

\begin{exercise}[\textit{Fermi 36, manufacturing}]
Estimate the total number of motor cars manufactured worldwide in

the rate of car replacement per person per year, and the structural
correction. Compare to the figure published by the International
Organization of Motor Vehicle Manufacturers (OICA).
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
World population: $8.0 \times 10^{9}$; world car-ownership rate
$\approx 0.2$ cars per person (heavily weighted by high-income

1.6 \times 10^{9} / 15 \approx 1.1 \times 10^{8}$ cars per year.
With a growth correction of $+10\,\%$ for fleet expansion, the

\begin{exercise}[\textit{Fermi 37, aerospace}]

\begin{exercise}[\textit{Fermi 38, software}]\pathtag{standard}
Estimate the number of lines of source code in one named open-source
operating-system component, such as the Linux kernel, current as of
2024. Decompose into subsystem count, files per subsystem, lines
per file, and generated-code exclusions. Compare to a reproducible
source-line count from the release tarball or repository. Do not
use uncitable Windows or macOS line-count folklore as ground truth.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Linux kernel top-level directories (subsystems): $\approx 30$
(drivers, fs, net, mm, arch, kernel, etc.). Mean files per
subsystem $\approx 2000$ (drivers dominates with $\approx 60{,}000$);
mean lines per file $\approx 200$. Total: $30 \times 2000 \times
200 \approx 1.2 \times 10^{7}$ lines. The reproducible
\texttt{cloc}-style count on Linux $6.5$ (October $2023$) returns
approximately $2.8 \times 10^{7}$ source lines, dominated by the
drivers subtree at $\approx 1.5 \times 10^{7}$ lines. The estimate
is low by a factor of two; the working-knowledge correction is
that the drivers tree is several times larger than a per-subsystem
mean would suggest.

\begin{exercise}[\textit{Fermi 39, sensors}]
Estimate the total number of accelerometer readings produced by all
smartphones in the world in one second. Decompose into installed
smartphone count, fraction of phones sampling at that instant,
sampling rate, and whether the phone stores, discards, or transmits
the samples. Treat this as a no-ground-truth Fermi problem unless
the reference solution supplies a named industry data source; the
postmortem should identify the sampling-duty-cycle assumption as
the dominant uncertainty.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Installed smartphones $\approx 6 \times 10^{9}$; fraction sampling
at any moment (screen on or background sensor active) $\approx
0.1$; nominal sampling rate when active $\approx 100 \,\si{\hertz}$;
samples per second produced: $6 \times 10^{9} \times 0.1 \times
100 = 6 \times 10^{10}$. Most are discarded immediately by sensor
fusion; only a small fraction is stored or transmitted. The
ratio of generated to stored samples is the dominant uncertainty,
of order $10^{3}$. The estimate is a no-ground-truth Fermi
problem; the lesson is the dominance of duty-cycle assumptions.

\begin{exercise}[\textit{Fermi 43, climate}]
Estimate the total mass of carbon dioxide emitted globally from
fossil-fuel combustion in one year, in tonnes, current as of 2022.

from fossil fuels, the carbon intensity of fossil fuels, and the
seconds in a year. Compare to the figure published by the IEA, the

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}

\begin{exercise}[\textit{Fermi 44, climate}]
Estimate the additional radiative forcing on the Earth produced by

$1850$, in watts per square metre. Decompose into the published
radiative forcing per doubling of carbon dioxide and the
fractional change in concentration. Compare to the figure published
by the IPCC.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Radiative forcing per doubling of CO$_{2}$ $\approx 3.7
\,\si{\watt\per\meter\squared}$ (Myhre 1998 formula used by IPCC).

\begin{exercise}[\textit{Fermi 45, environment, deep uncertainty}]\pathtag{standard}
Estimate the total mass of microplastics suspended in the world's
oceans, in tonnes. Acknowledge that this is a deep-uncertainty
problem. Decompose into the annual flux of plastic into the oceans,

and lower bounds defensible from the published literature
explicitly, with the dominant-uncertainty factor identified.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}

10^{6}$ tonnes for surface microplastics

\begin{exercise}[\textit{Fermi 49, environment, deep uncertainty}]\pathtag{standard}

\begin{exercise}[\textit{Fermi 50, the closing problem}]\pathtag{mastery}
Estimate the present stock of human-made material, or anthropogenic
mass, in tonnes. Acknowledge that this is a deep-uncertainty problem.
Decompose into major material classes such as concrete, aggregates,
bricks, asphalt, metals, plastics, and glass, then compare to the
anthropogenic-mass literature \cite{paper:elhacham2020-anthropogenic-mass}.
In two or three sentences at the end of your reflection, state what
this number teaches about the material scale of the twelve volumes
ahead.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Concrete and aggregates dominate: cumulative cement production
since 1900 $\approx 6 \times 10^{10}$ tonnes, multiplied by the
$7\times$ ratio of concrete-by-mass to cement yields $\approx
4 \times 10^{11}$ tonnes of concrete; aggregates separately
contribute another $\approx 3 \times 10^{11}$ tonnes; bricks
$\approx 9 \times 10^{10}$ tonnes; asphalt $\approx 7 \times
10^{10}$ tonnes; metals (mostly steel) $\approx 4 \times 10^{10}$
tonnes; plastics $\approx 1 \times 10^{10}$ tonnes; glass and
other $\approx 1 \times 10^{10}$ tonnes. Sum: $\approx 9 \times
10^{11}$ to $1.2 \times 10^{12}$ tonnes. Elhacham 2020 reports
that anthropogenic mass surpassed living biomass (1 Tt) around
2020 \cite{paper:elhacham2020-anthropogenic-mass}; the estimate
matches within a factor of $1.5$. The closing reflection:
twelve volumes carry the reader across the physical scale at
which engineered material now equals the living planet's
biomass; estimation discipline is the habit that keeps the
engineer answerable to that scale.
