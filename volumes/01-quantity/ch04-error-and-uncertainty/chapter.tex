A measurement returns a value and an uncertainty. The value says
where the measurement lands; the uncertainty says how wide the
defensible interval is. This chapter makes the second quantity
operational: we compute it, propagate it through measurement models,
and reject uncertainty figures selected for institutional convenience
rather than derived from evidence.

\section{Random vs systematic error}\pathtag{core}

The international vocabulary of metrology distinguishes
\engterm{random error} (a component of measurement error that
varies in an unpredictable manner across repeated measurements) from
\engterm{systematic error} (a component of measurement error that,
in repeated measurements, remains constant or varies in a
predictable manner) \cite{std:jcgm-vim}. The distinction is
operational: it determines which mitigation works.

Independent random error is reduced by averaging. If a quantity is
measured $n$ times under repeatability conditions and the random
component is independent across measurements, the standard
uncertainty of the mean is the sample standard deviation divided by
$\sqrt{n}$. The central-limit-theorem argument is taken up below; the
operational consequence is narrower than the slogan: more data
reduces only the independent random component.

Systematic error is not reduced by averaging. If a thermometer reads
$0.2 \,\si{\degreeCelsius}$ high because of a calibration offset,
averaging a thousand readings produces a mean that is still
$0.2 \,\si{\degreeCelsius}$ high. The mechanism that reduces
systematic error is calibration against a reference that does not
share the offset, plus correction or adjustment. The discipline
that prevents systematic error from being mistaken for random error
is the separation of the two in the uncertainty budget.

\subsection{Mechanisms}

The mechanism matters because it tells us which corrective action has
a chance of working.

Random-error mechanisms include thermal noise in electronic
instruments, quantisation noise in digital instruments (the last
digit fluctuates because the underlying quantity is rarely exactly
on a quantisation step), parallax in analog instruments,
hand-tremor in manually-positioned instruments, repeatability
limits in mechanical instruments, and the effects of brief
environmental fluctuations.

Systematic-error mechanisms include calibration offset, calibration
drift between recalibrations, instrument-specific bias from
component aging, environmental gradient (a temperature gradient
across a measurement system biases all readings in the same
direction), method-specific bias (the chosen procedure systematically
under- or overestimates the quantity), and the operator's
interpretive bias.

A mechanism that begins as random can become systematic if the
underlying cause shifts. A thermometer in a draft has random
fluctuations from the airflow; a thermometer in a steady-state warm
room has a systematic bias from the room's temperature gradient.
The distinction is in the time scale and the consistency of the
underlying cause, not in the mechanism's name.

\subsection{Type A and Type B in this language}

Chapter 1 introduced the GUM's Type A and Type B distinction at a
teaching level \cite{std:iso-iec-guide98}. We restate it here in
the language of random and systematic error.

\engterm{Type A evaluation} estimates uncertainty by statistical
analysis of observed data, usually repeated measurements. If the
independent random component has standard deviation $\sigma$ in a
single measurement, the standard uncertainty of the mean of $n$
measurements is estimated as $s/\sqrt{n}$.

\engterm{Type B evaluation} estimates uncertainty from other
information: instrument specifications, calibration certificates,
manufacturer data, documented experience, previous measurements, and
physical models. A calibration correction taken from a certificate
is commonly a Type B contribution, even when the effect being
corrected is a systematic bias.

The distinction between Type A and Type B is the method of
evaluation, not the random-versus-systematic character of the
effect. A bias can be estimated by Type A methods when repeated
calibration data, intercomparisons, or controlled condition changes
expose it statistically. A random contribution can enter a Type B
evaluation when previous data, a specification, or a physical model
gives the standard uncertainty without a new repeated-measurement
campaign.

\begin{archetype}[Uncertainty, first formal introduction]
Uncertainty is the engineering habit of carrying a second quantity
beside the reported value. The value says where the measurement
lands; the uncertainty says how wide the defensible landing zone is.
Later chapters attach the same habit to structural loads,
temperatures, material properties, reliability estimates, and
failure rates. In this chapter the pattern becomes formal: a number
reported without its uncertainty is not yet a usable measurement.
\end{archetype}

\section{Distribution of error: Gaussian, uniform, asymmetric}\pathtag{core}

A standard uncertainty is a single number that summarises a
distribution. The distribution itself carries more information.
Three distribution shapes recur in engineering measurement.

\subsection{Gaussian}

The Gaussian (normal) distribution, $\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^{2})$,
is the canonical distribution of measurement error in the limit of
many small independent contributions. Its probability density is

\[
p(x) = \frac{1}{\sigma\sqrt{2\pi}} \exp\!\left(-\frac{(x - \mu)^{2}}
{2\sigma^{2}}\right).
\]

The Gaussian is fully characterised by its mean $\mu$ and its
standard deviation $\sigma$. The probability that a single draw
falls within $\pm 1\sigma$ of the mean is approximately $0.683$;
within $\pm 2\sigma$, $0.954$; within $\pm 3\sigma$, $0.997$. These
coverage probabilities map directly to the reporting conventions
introduced in section 4.6.

The Gaussian is appropriate when the underlying error mechanism is
the sum of many small independent contributions, none of which
dominates. The central limit theorem (section 4.4) guarantees the
limiting distribution under those conditions.

\subsection{Uniform}

The uniform distribution on an interval $[a, b]$ has density

\[
p(x) = \frac{1}{b - a} \quad \text{for } a \leq x \leq b, \quad 0
\text{ otherwise}.
\]

Its mean is $(a + b)/2$ and its standard deviation is
$(b - a)/(2\sqrt{3})$. The uniform distribution is appropriate when
the error is bounded above and below and the available information
gives no preference for any value within the bounds. Quantisation
error is often modelled as uniform over one quantisation interval
when the input is not locked to the quantiser and sufficient
variation or dithering is present. A specification that gives a
maximum and minimum without a most-likely value is commonly treated,
under the GUM's rectangular-distribution convention, as uniform
between those bounds \cite{std:iso-iec-guide98}.

A frequent Type B evaluation: a manufacturer states a maximum error
of $\pm a$ without a distribution. The standard uncertainty is then
$a/\sqrt{3}$, computed from the uniform distribution on
$[-a, +a]$.

\subsection{Asymmetric distributions}

The following examples are standard statistical models, not automatic
assignments. The measurement report should name the mechanism or
cite the convention that justifies the distribution
\cite{text:taylor1997}.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \engterm{Lognormal}: the logarithm of the quantity is
    Gaussian. Multiplicative-error mechanisms (compound effects,
    biological growth, financial returns, particle-size
    distributions) often produce lognormal errors.
  \item \engterm{Half-normal}: the absolute value of a Gaussian.
    Errors that are bounded below by zero (deviations, magnitudes,
    distances to a reference) sometimes follow this distribution.
  \item \engterm{Weibull}: lifetime distributions, fatigue-cycle
    counts, time-to-failure data.
  \item \engterm{Poisson}: counting processes at low rates (radiation
    counts, defect counts, network packet arrivals). For a Poisson
    distribution with mean $\lambda$, the variance is also
    $\lambda$ and the standard deviation is $\sqrt{\lambda}$.
\end{itemize}

For asymmetric distributions, reporting a single $\pm u$ figure
loses information. The discipline is to report either both bounds
explicitly or the median plus the asymmetric percentile range.

\subsection{Heavy tails}

Some distributions have tails heavy enough that the ordinary
finite-variance central limit theorem does not apply. The Cauchy
distribution is the clean example: it has no finite mean and no
finite variance, and the average of independent Cauchy variables
remains Cauchy-distributed. A measurement model with Cauchy-like
error is not repaired by taking more ordinary averages. The
practical rule is simple: before applying Gaussian tools, check that
the variance is finite and that the observed tails are compatible
with the model.

\section{Propagation of uncertainty: linear and Monte Carlo}\pathtag{core}

Most engineering quantities are computed from measured inputs. A
volume is computed from a measured radius and a measured height; an
acceleration is computed from a measured velocity change and a
measured time interval; a power is computed from a measured voltage
and a measured current. Each input has its own uncertainty. The
output's uncertainty must be computed from the inputs'
uncertainties. The discipline that does the computation is
\engterm{propagation of uncertainty}.

\subsection{Algebraic propagation rules}

A reader at this point in Volume I has algebra and trigonometry but
not yet calculus, which Volume II takes up from first principles.
The algebraic propagation rules below cover the bulk of practical
uncertainty propagation a working engineer encounters; the
generalisation to arbitrary functions of several inputs uses partial
derivatives and is treated formally in Volume II Chapter 4. The
GUM \cite{std:iso-iec-guide98} states the general formula for
reference, included as a mastery box at the end of this section; for
calculations in this chapter we use the algebraic special cases.

\engterm{Sum and difference}. For $y = a x_{1} \pm b x_{2}$ with
$a, b$ constants and uncorrelated inputs $x_{1}, x_{2}$,

\[
u_{c}^{2}(y) = a^{2} u^{2}(x_{1}) + b^{2} u^{2}(x_{2}).
\]

The uncertainties of additive terms combine in quadrature, weighted
by the squared coefficients. A useful consequence: if two nominally
equal-uncertainty terms add, the combined standard uncertainty is
$\sqrt{2}$ times either term, not twice it.

\engterm{Product and ratio}. For a product of powers
$y = a x_{1}^{p_{1}} x_{2}^{p_{2}} \cdots$ with uncorrelated inputs
and small relative uncertainties, the relative uncertainty satisfies

\[
\left(\frac{u_{c}(y)}{y}\right)^{2} = \sum_{i} p_{i}^{2}
\left(\frac{u(x_{i})}{x_{i}}\right)^{2}.
\]

The exponent $p_{i}$ on each input is the input's
\engterm{sensitivity coefficient} in the relative-uncertainty rule:
the contribution to the combined relative uncertainty is the
exponent squared, times the relative uncertainty of the input,
squared, summed. For a simple product $y = x_{1} x_{2}$ both
exponents are $1$, so the relative uncertainties add in quadrature.
For $y = x^{2}$, the exponent is $2$ and the relative uncertainty
doubles before quadrature. For $y = \sqrt{x}$, the exponent is
$1/2$ and the relative uncertainty halves. For $y = 1/x$, the
exponent is $-1$ and the relative uncertainty enters with the same
magnitude (the sign drops in the square).

If the inputs are correlated, covariance terms must be added; the
simple quadrature rule no longer applies.

\engterm{Correlated difference: common-mode cancellation}. For
$y = x_{1} - x_{2}$ with correlation coefficient $\rho$,

\[
u_{c}^{2}(y) = u^{2}(x_{1}) + u^{2}(x_{2}) - 2\rho u(x_{1}) u(x_{2}).
\]

When $\rho = +1$ and $u(x_{1}) = u(x_{2})$, the common uncertainty
component cancels. Difference measurements exploit this: subtracting
two readings from the same drifting instrument can reject the shared
drift while preserving the change between the readings. Comparator
measurements in metrology are built around this common-mode
cancellation.

\begin{mastery}
The general GUM linear propagation formula. For an output quantity
$y$ that is an arbitrary function $y = f(x_{1}, x_{2}, \ldots,
x_{N})$ of $N$ input quantities $x_{i}$ with standard uncertainties
$u(x_{i})$, the combined standard uncertainty satisfies

\[
u_{c}^{2}(y) = \sum_{i=1}^{N}\left(\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_{i}}
\right)^{2} u^{2}(x_{i}) + 2\sum_{i=1}^{N-1}\sum_{j=i+1}^{N}
\frac{\partial f}{\partial x_{i}} \frac{\partial f}{\partial x_{j}}
u(x_{i}, x_{j}),
\]

where $u(x_{i}, x_{j})$ is the covariance between inputs $i$ and
$j$, and the partial derivatives are evaluated at the input
estimates. The formula is a first-order Taylor approximation. It
specialises to the algebraic rules above for sums (linear $f$) and
products (logarithmic-derivative manipulation). Volume II Chapter 4
introduces partial derivatives and Taylor expansion; the general
formula and its non-algebraic applications belong there. For
calculations in this volume, the algebraic rules above suffice.
\end{mastery}

\begin{estimation}
Before calculating, estimate which input will dominate the uncertainty
in $g = 4\pi^{2} L / T^{2}$. The length uncertainty is about $10^{-3}$
relative. The time uncertainty is about $0.005/2 \approx 2.5 \times
10^{-3}$ relative, and the exponent $-2$ doubles that contribution
before quadrature. We should expect a combined relative uncertainty
near $5 \times 10^{-3}$, so a $g$ value near $10 \,\si{\meter\per
\second\squared}$ should carry a standard uncertainty near $0.05 \,
\si{\meter\per\second\squared}$.
\end{estimation}

\subsection{Worked example: pendulum period gives $g$}

A simple pendulum of length $L$ has small-amplitude period
$T = 2\pi\sqrt{L/g}$, so $g = 4\pi^{2} L / T^{2}$. Suppose the
reader measures $L = 1.000 \,\si{\meter}$ with
$u(L) = 0.001 \,\si{\meter}$ and $T = 2.007 \,\si{\second}$ with
$u(T) = 0.005 \,\si{\second}$.

The expression $g = 4\pi^{2} L^{1} T^{-2}$ is a product of powers,
with constant prefactor $4\pi^{2}$, exponent $+1$ on $L$, and
exponent $-2$ on $T$. The product-and-ratio rule above gives the
relative uncertainty directly:

\[
\left(\frac{u_{c}(g)}{g}\right)^{2} = (1)^{2}
\left(\frac{u(L)}{L}\right)^{2} + (-2)^{2}
\left(\frac{u(T)}{T}\right)^{2} =
\left(\frac{u(L)}{L}\right)^{2} + 4
\left(\frac{u(T)}{T}\right)^{2}.
\]

Plugging in the numbers: $u(L)/L = 0.001$ and
$u(T)/T \approx 0.0025$, so $(u_{c}(g)/g)^{2} = 10^{-6} +
4 \times 6.25 \times 10^{-6} = 2.6 \times 10^{-5}$, giving
$u_{c}(g)/g \approx 5 \times 10^{-3}$. The mean value
$g = 4\pi^{2} \times 1.000 / 2.007^{2} \approx 9.80 \,\si{\meter\per
\second\squared}$, so $u_{c}(g) \approx 0.05 \,\si{\meter\per
\second\squared}$.

The factor of $4$ on the time term comes from squaring the exponent
$(-2)$. The time measurement is the dominant uncertainty source even
though its absolute uncertainty is small, because the squared
exponent amplifies its relative uncertainty fourfold. A reader who
wants better $g$ measures $T$ more accurately, not $L$.

\subsection{When linear propagation fails}

Three conditions break the linear approximation:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Strong nonlinearity in $f$ over the range of input
    uncertainties. Small input uncertainties may still produce
    asymmetric output distributions if $f$ is steeply curved.
  \item Large input uncertainties such that first-order Taylor terms
    no longer dominate the neglected second-order terms. A practical
    check is to compare the model evaluated at plausible high and
    low input values with the tangent-line prediction; if the
    residual is comparable to the propagated standard uncertainty,
    linear propagation is no longer the right tool.
  \item Heavy-tailed input distributions where the variance is not
    representative of the distribution.
\end{itemize}

When linear propagation fails, the GUM's Supplement 1
(JCGM 101:2008) describes the alternative: Monte Carlo propagation
of distributions \cite{std:jcgm-101}.

\subsection{Monte Carlo propagation}

The Monte Carlo method propagates the entire input distribution
rather than just its variance. The procedure:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Specify a probability distribution for each input quantity
    based on Type A and Type B evaluations.
  \item Draw a random sample from each input distribution. The
    samples should be drawn jointly when inputs are correlated,
    using the joint distribution.
  \item Compute the output quantity from the sampled inputs.
  \item Repeat steps 2--3 for $M$ trials. Choose $M$ from a
    convergence or numerical-tolerance criterion; for simple models,
    $10^{5}$ to $10^{6}$ trials are common starting values, but the
    report should state the criterion actually used.
  \item Estimate the output's mean, standard deviation, and any
    desired percentile range from the resulting empirical
    distribution.
\end{enumerate}

The Monte Carlo method does not assume linearity and does not
require analytic partial derivatives. It does require computational
effort and well-specified input distributions. JCGM 101 gives the
canonical formulation \cite{std:jcgm-101}; we will use it
operationally in chapter 8 when we cover statistics for engineers
and again in chapter 9 when we cover estimation as a discipline.

\begin{mastery}
The choice between linear propagation and Monte Carlo is an
engineering judgment. Linear propagation is fast, exposes the
dominant sensitivity coefficient, and is easy to document. Monte
Carlo is better when the input distributions and model are specified
but the output distribution is nonlinear, asymmetric, truncated, or
censored. The method still has sampling error, so the report must
state the trial count or convergence criterion. For safety-critical
work, the method is chosen from the uncertainty model and the
decision consequence, not from fashion.
\end{mastery}

\section{The central limit theorem in practice}\pathtag{core}

The central limit theorem (CLT) states that, under regularity
conditions, the sum or mean of many independent random variables
tends toward a Gaussian distribution after centering and scaling.
The working conditions are finite variance and no dominating
contribution; the Lyapunov or Lindeberg forms make that last
condition precise.

The theorem is the reason engineering measurement reports so often
quote a Gaussian uncertainty. The reading on a digital voltmeter is
the sum of many small noise contributions; the displacement of a
mechanical instrument is the sum of many small mechanical effects;
the timing measurement of a stopwatch is the sum of many small
timing-jitter sources. Each individual contribution may be far from
Gaussian, but the sum tends Gaussian under the CLT's conditions.

\subsection{Conditions}

The CLT requires:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Independence of the individual contributions. Correlated
    contributions do not satisfy the CLT in its simplest form.
    Long-range-correlated noise is one place engineering
    measurement deviates from CLT predictions.
  \item Finite variance of each contribution. Heavy-tailed
    distributions (Cauchy, Pareto with shape parameter less than
    $2$) violate this and produce non-Gaussian sums regardless of
    sample size.
  \item No dominating contribution. The Lyapunov condition (a more
    precise statement of "no single variable dominates") must hold;
    in practice, this means no single noise source contributes a
    variance comparable to the sum of all others.
\end{itemize}

When all three conditions hold, the CLT applies and the Gaussian
approximation is good. When one fails, the empirical distribution
of the sum will deviate from Gaussian; the reader should check.

\subsection{Sample size and the t-distribution}

The CLT is an asymptotic result. For finite samples, the sample
mean may be approximately Gaussian, but the uncertainty of that
mean is usually estimated from the same data. When the parent
population is Gaussian and the population standard deviation is
unknown, the standardized statistic built from the sample mean and
sample standard deviation follows Student's $t$-distribution with
$n - 1$ degrees of freedom. For $n \geq 30$, the $t$ critical values
are close to Gaussian critical values for many routine intervals;
for $n < 30$, and especially for $n < 10$, the correction is large
enough to matter.

A working laboratory rule: for samples of $n \geq 30$, use Gaussian
coverage factors; for $n < 30$, use $t$-distribution coverage
factors with $n - 1$ degrees of freedom. The $t$-table is in any
statistics text and in the NIST/SEMATECH e-Handbook
\cite{web:nist-eshandbook}.

\section{Significant figures, rounding, reporting}\pathtag{standard}

A reported measurement value is a string of digits with an
associated uncertainty. The convention by which the digits and the
uncertainty are written carries information about the measurement;
violating the convention loses that information.

\subsection{Significant figures}

Significant figures are a coarse uncertainty proxy. A reading of
$5.32 \,\si{\volt}$ asserts three significant figures: the
uncertainty is implied to be roughly $\pm 0.005 \,\si{\volt}$ in
the third digit. The convention is sometimes sharper than the
underlying measurement supports (a reading of $5.32 \,\si{\volt}$
on an instrument whose actual uncertainty is $\pm 0.05 \,\si{\volt}$
overstates precision by an order of magnitude), and sometimes
laxer (a reading of $5 \,\si{\volt}$ on an instrument resolving
$0.01 \,\si{\volt}$ wastes precision).

The discipline is to use significant figures only as a quick
communication of approximate precision. When the precision matters,
report an explicit uncertainty alongside the value.

\subsection{Rounding}

One common reporting rule is round-half-to-even (banker's rounding):
when the discarded digit is exactly $5$ with no further nonzero
digits, round to the nearest even digit. Use this rule when the
applicable standard, laboratory procedure, or software environment
specifies it; otherwise state the rounding convention. A separate
convention applies to uncertainties: report an uncertainty with one
significant figure, or with two when the leading digit is $1$ or $2$
or when the second digit changes the decision. Thus
$0.05246 \,\si{\volt}$ would usually become $0.05 \,\si{\volt}$;
$0.0146 \,\si{\volt}$ would usually become $0.015 \,\si{\volt}$
\cite{std:nist-sp811}.

\subsection{Reporting conventions}

Three notations for a measurement and its uncertainty are common.

\engterm{Value plus or minus uncertainty}: $5.32 \pm 0.05 \,\si
{\volt}$. The clearest form when the uncertainty is symmetric. The
coverage factor (typically $k = 2$ for a $95\%$ expanded
uncertainty) should be stated either inline or in a footnote.

\engterm{Value with uncertainty in parentheses}: $5.32(5) \,\si
{\volt}$. The digit(s) in parentheses give the uncertainty in the
last digit(s) shown. So $5.32(5)$ means $5.32 \pm 0.05$;
$5.32(15)$ means $5.32 \pm 0.15$. This form is compact and is the
convention in most physics and metrology papers.

\engterm{Value with separate uncertainty column}: tabular form, used
when many measurements are reported together. The header specifies
the coverage factor.

In all three forms, the value and the uncertainty should be rounded
to consistent decimal places. A measurement of $5.3247 \,\si{\volt}$
with uncertainty $\pm 0.05 \,\si{\volt}$ should be written as
$5.32 \pm 0.05 \,\si{\volt}$, not $5.3247 \pm 0.05 \,\si{\volt}$.
Reporting more digits in the value than the uncertainty supports is
a form of false precision.

\section{Confidence intervals as engineering tools}\pathtag{standard}

A \engterm{confidence interval} is a range of values, computed from
a sample, that is intended to contain the true value of the
measured quantity with a stated coverage probability. The standard
frequentist definition: a $95\%$ confidence interval is one that,
in repeated experiments, contains the true value $95\%$ of the
time.

The frequentist definition is not what most working engineers mean
when they say "$95\%$ confidence interval." Most working engineers
mean: "I am $95\%$ confident the true value lies in this interval."
The two interpretations are different. The frequentist statement is
about the procedure (the interval-computing method, in repeated
application, has a $95\%$ success rate); the engineering statement
is about the specific interval (the probability that this
particular interval contains the true value). The frequentist
framework does not, strictly, support the engineering statement
without an additional Bayesian assumption about the prior
distribution of the true value.

For routine laboratory reporting, the practical action is to name
the interval type and its assumptions. A GUM expanded uncertainty is
reported with its standard uncertainty, coverage factor, effective
degrees of freedom where relevant, and distributional assumption. A
Bayesian credible interval is reported with its prior and likelihood
model. The reader should not translate one statement into the other
silently.

\subsection{Coverage factors}

The GUM convention is to report a \engterm{standard uncertainty}
$u(y)$ at coverage factor $k = 1$ (corresponding to approximately
$68\%$ coverage for a Gaussian) and an \engterm{expanded
uncertainty} $U(y) = k \cdot u(y)$ at a stated coverage factor.
The most common expanded-uncertainty conventions:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item $k = 2$: approximately $95\%$ coverage for a Gaussian.
    The default in most metrology and calibration reports.
  \item $k = 3$: approximately $99.7\%$ coverage for a Gaussian.
    Used in safety-critical contexts where the consequence of an
    out-of-range value is severe.
  \item Other values for non-Gaussian distributions, with the
    actual coverage probability stated rather than implied.
\end{itemize}

A reported expanded uncertainty without a stated coverage factor is
ambiguous. The reader should not infer $k$ from the plus-minus sign;
ask for the coverage factor, coverage probability, and distributional
basis, or treat the report as incomplete.

\subsection{Manufacturer accuracy specifications}

A manufacturer's accuracy specification may be a maximum permissible
error, a calibration-condition claim, a coverage interval, or a
shorthand tied to a particular standard. The plus-minus sign alone
does not identify a standard uncertainty. A reader who uses a
specification in an uncertainty budget should record the assumption
explicitly: rectangular half-width, Gaussian expanded uncertainty
with stated $k$, or another distribution justified by the data sheet
or calibration certificate. Treating a maximum-error specification
as a standard uncertainty can understate the uncertainty by a large
factor.

\section{The cost of a smaller error bar}\pathtag{core}

Reducing uncertainty costs money, time, and effort. The cost is not
linear in the inverse of the uncertainty. The reader who wants
half the error bar typically cannot get there with twice the effort.

\subsection{Random uncertainty: the $1/\sqrt{n}$ scaling}

For uncertainty dominated by independent random contributions, the
standard uncertainty of the mean of $n$ measurements scales as
$1/\sqrt{n}$. To halve the uncertainty, the reader needs $4n$
measurements. To reduce the uncertainty by a factor of ten, the
reader needs $100 n$ measurements. The first factor-of-two
reduction is comparatively cheap; the next factor-of-two becomes
expensive; the next is unaffordable for most working laboratories.

\subsection{Systematic uncertainty: the wall}

Random uncertainty's $1/\sqrt{n}$ scaling has a hard floor: the
systematic uncertainty. No amount of additional measurement reduces
a calibration offset, an environmental gradient bias, or a
methodological bias. The systematic component sets a wall that
random-error reduction cannot pass.

To reduce a systematic uncertainty, the reader must change the
instrument, the procedure, the environment, or the method.
Recalibration against a tighter reference reduces calibration-offset
contributions; environmental control reduces gradient
contributions; method changes reduce methodological contributions.
Each change typically requires capital investment, training, or
both.

\subsection{The cost-of-precision curve}

The cost of measurement at a target precision plotted against the
target precision typically shows three regions:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item A flat region at coarse precisions, where the measurement
    is easily accomplished with off-the-shelf instruments and
    standard procedures.
  \item A rising region in the middle range, where the random
    uncertainty must be reduced by additional measurement and the
    systematic uncertainty must be reduced by procedural
    refinements.
  \item A wall at the high-precision end, where each additional
    increment of precision requires new instruments, new methods, or
    operation at the limits of the relevant national metrology
    institute's capabilities.
\end{itemize}

The position of the wall depends on the quantity. Frequency
measurements at fractional uncertainty around $10^{-16}$ are routine
for national time and frequency laboratories, while optical-clock
comparisons have reached the low $10^{-18}$ range and selected
optical-clock systematic uncertainties below $10^{-18}$, current as
of 2026 \cite{web:nist-optical-clock}. Kilogram realisations by
$1 \,\si{\kilogram}$ Kibble balances at national metrology institutes
are typically reported at uncertainties on the order of a few parts
in $10^{8}$, current as of 2026 \cite{web:nist-kibble-balance}. A
reader specifying a measurement should know which side of the
current metrology frontier the requirement occupies.

\subsection{Engineering economics}

The optimum precision is the precision at which the marginal cost
of further reduction equals the marginal value of further
reduction. The marginal value depends on the application: a clinical
diagnostic that misdiagnoses by $5\%$ at the current precision and
by $1\%$ at half the current uncertainty has a clear marginal-value
calculation. A consumer-electronics measurement that is already
within manufacturing tolerance has near-zero marginal value of
further precision.

The discipline that prevents over-precision is to specify the
required uncertainty as part of the measurement plan, then design
to that specification. The discipline that prevents under-precision
is to verify the required uncertainty against the application's
tolerance before deploying the measurement. Both disciplines
require honest engagement with the cost-of-precision curve.

\section{Failure: error bars that were politically chosen}\pathtag{core}

The failure mode here is quieter than a missing measurement or a
unit error. The report contains numbers, the numbers look defensible,
and the uncertainty figure is smaller than the evidence permits
because schedule, money, legal exposure, or institutional pressure
has entered the uncertainty budget.

\subsection{The Hubble auxiliary-test override}

The Hubble Space Telescope launched in April 1990 with a primary
mirror figured to the wrong shape. The NASA Optical Systems Board of
Investigation traced the failure to the reflective null corrector
used during polishing: a field lens was mispositioned by about
$1.3 \,\si{\milli\meter}$ along the optical axis, and the resulting
mirror figure error was about $2 \,\si{\micro\meter}$ at the outer
edge \cite{acc:nasa-hubble-optical-systems-1990}. Auxiliary optical
tests, including an inverse null corrector and a second null
corrector, showed clear indications of the problem, but the
fabrication process treated the reflective null corrector as
authoritative.

For this chapter, the uncertainty lesson is the treatment of
disagreement. A measurement system with independent cross-checks
that disagree should not report an uncertainty based only on the
preferred instrument. The report should either widen the uncertainty
to account for the disagreement or document why the auxiliary tests
are inapplicable. The OSBI record supports the narrower statement
that Hubble's process discounted the auxiliary evidence under cost
and schedule pressure; it does not by itself give us a complete
published uncertainty budget for the mirror.

The Hubble case is recorded in the named-cases registry at
\repopath{docs/research/accidents/hubble-primary-mirror-1990.md}.

\subsection{The chronic class}

The same pattern appears in weaker forms whenever the reporting
process can benefit from a smaller interval: a test scenario
selected to preserve a passing result, a specification quoted
without its coverage basis, a sampling margin reported as if it
included non-response bias, a cost estimate collapsed from a range
to a single favorable point. One named case carries the pattern at
regulatory scale: Volkswagen Group's 2009--2015 light-duty diesel
vehicles were equipped with engine-control software that detected
the EPA's certified emissions test cycles and switched the
emissions-control system into a compliant calibration only during
testing; under ordinary driving the same vehicles emitted nitrogen
oxides at up to forty times the regulatory limit
\cite{acc:epa-vw-nov-2015, acc:doj-vw-2017}. Each example, named or
unnamed, requires the same audit question: what data and model
produced the interval?

The common failure is an uncertainty budget that answers to pressure
rather than evidence. Prevention is procedural: derive the
uncertainty from the data and model, document the Type A, Type B,
or Monte Carlo route, report the dominant component, and make the
calculation reproducible.

\subsection{The principle, restated}

\begin{principle}[Uncertainty derives from data, not preference]
The uncertainty of a measurement is computed from the data, the
instruments, the procedures, and the propagation of input
uncertainties through the measurement model. The uncertainty is
not chosen, negotiated, or selected for institutional convenience.
A reported uncertainty without a documented derivation is an
incomplete measurement result; the engineering action is to request
the uncertainty budget before using the number in a decision.
\end{principle}

\section{Project}\pathtag{core}

\begin{project}[Building height by three methods, with error bars]
The reader measures the height of a chosen building by three
independent methods, computes the uncertainty for each, and
compares the three results to a published reference height. The
project tests the chapter's main mechanism: independent measurements
with documented uncertainty propagation should agree within their
combined error bars.

\textbf{Track}. Household and analysis (hybrid). The reader spends
time outdoors at street level near the building; no climbing, no
working at height, no instruments above battery voltage.
\textbf{Hazard class}: low.
\textbf{Tools}. Smartphone (clinometer or theodolite app, compass,
GPS, camera with EXIF or known focal length), tape measure
(preferably $\geq 10 \,\si{\meter}$), a stopwatch, a meter rule,
and a published reference height for the building (Wikipedia,
OpenStreetMap, building permit records, building owner's
documentation).

\textbf{Procedure}. The reader chooses a building whose published
height is well documented and whose base is accessible from a
distance of at least one building-height. They then perform three
of the following methods, computing each result and its standard
uncertainty using the linear propagation formula from section 4.3.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \engterm{Trigonometric method}. Measure a baseline distance
    $d$ from the reader's position to the base of the building.
    Measure the elevation angle $\theta$ from the reader's eye level
    to the top of the building using a smartphone clinometer.
    Compute height $h = d \tan\theta + h_{\text{eye}}$, where
    $h_{\text{eye}}$ is the reader's eye height above the ground.
    Propagate uncertainties from $u(d)$, $u(\theta)$, and
    $u(h_{\text{eye}})$ to $u(h)$ using the algebraic rules of
    section 4.3 applied to the small-angle approximation
    $\tan\theta \approx \theta$ near working angles, or by the
    finite-difference method (compute $h$ for nominal inputs and for
    inputs perturbed by their standard uncertainties; the spread is
    the propagated uncertainty estimate).
  \item \engterm{Shadow method}. On a sunny day, measure the
    building's shadow length $L_{\text{b}}$ and the reader's shadow
    length $L_{\text{r}}$ at the same time. Use the proportionality
    $h_{\text{building}} / h_{\text{reader}} = L_{\text{b}} /
    L_{\text{r}}$. Propagate uncertainties from $u(L_{\text{b}})$,
    $u(L_{\text{r}})$, and $u(h_{\text{reader}})$ to
    $u(h_{\text{building}})$.
  \item \engterm{Photographic method}. From a known distance $d$,
    take a photograph of the building with a known focal length and
    a known mapping from pixels to sensor length. Convert the
    measured pixel height to a sensor-plane height $s_{\text{image}}$
    in the same length units as the focal length $f$. For small
    angles and a camera aimed approximately perpendicular to the
    building face, use $h \approx d \cdot s_{\text{image}} / f$;
    otherwise compute the angular height from the camera geometry
    and use the trigonometric method. Propagate uncertainties from
    $d$, pixel measurement, focal length, sensor scaling, and camera
    alignment.
  \item \engterm{Hand-rule method}. First calibrate the reader's own
    hand rule: measure arm length and the width of the fist or
    thumb to compute its angular width. Stand at a safe measured
    distance where the building's apparent angular size can be
    compared with that calibrated angle. Compute the height from the
    angle and distance, and propagate the larger angular uncertainty
    explicitly.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Record}. Each method's record includes: measured input
quantities with their standard uncertainties, the computed height,
the propagated standard uncertainty, the expanded uncertainty at
$k = 2$, and the dominant uncertainty source identified by
sensitivity analysis (which input's exponent or coefficient amplifies
its relative uncertainty most strongly into the result).

\textbf{Analysis}. The reader produces a small comparison table
giving each method's result with $k = 2$ expanded uncertainty,
the published reference height with its source, and a column
showing whether the reference falls within each method's expanded
uncertainty range. Where the methods disagree by more than their
combined expanded uncertainty, the reader investigates why.

\textbf{Reflection (1500 words)}. The reader writes a 1500-word
narrative addressing:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Which method gave the smallest error bar and why.
  \item Which method's dominant uncertainty source surprised the
    reader.
  \item Where two methods disagreed beyond their combined
    uncertainty, what mechanism the reader identifies as the most
    likely cause of the disagreement.
  \item Whether the published reference height fell within all
    three methods' expanded uncertainty, and what to do if it
    did not.
  \item One measurement in the reader's life or work where the
    same three-method discipline would change the reader's
    confidence in the result.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Deliverable}. The three method records, the comparison
table, and the reflection. The general editor's reference set of
three-method computations for a representative building is
published online; the reader is encouraged to compare \emph{after}
completing their own.

\textbf{Time}. One to two hours per method outdoors, plus two to
three hours of analysis and writing. Total approximately six to
nine hours, distributed across two or three days.
\end{project}

\section{Exercises}

The exercises train calculation, derivation, estimation, simulation,
design, diagnosis, failure analysis, and judgment. Calculation and
derivation problems have full solutions; open-ended problems have
rubrics or reference write-ups. Exercises are tagged with their
reader-path tier.

\subsection*{Calculation}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
A digital voltmeter reads $5.32 \,\si{\volt}$ with a manufacturer-
specified accuracy of $\pm (0.05\,\% \text{ of reading} + 1 \text{
last digit})$. The last digit is at the $10 \,\si{\milli\volt}$
position. Compute the standard uncertainty assuming the
specification is at $k = 2$ and the underlying distributions are
Gaussian.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Two thermometers read the same room temperature: thermometer A
reads $21.4 \,\si{\degreeCelsius}$ with $u_{A} = 0.2 \,\si{\degreeCelsius}$,
thermometer B reads $21.7 \,\si{\degreeCelsius}$ with $u_{B} = 0.3
\,\si{\degreeCelsius}$. Compute the weighted mean and its standard
uncertainty, assuming the two readings are independent.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A length is measured as $L = 1.250 \,\si{\meter}$ with a maximum-
error specification of $\pm 0.002 \,\si{\meter}$ and no stated
distribution. Treat the error as uniformly distributed on
$[-0.002, 0.002] \,\si{\meter}$ and compute the standard uncertainty.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A rectangle has measured side lengths $a = 12.5 \,\si{\centi\meter}
\pm 0.1 \,\si{\centi\meter}$ and $b = 8.3 \,\si{\centi\meter} \pm
0.1 \,\si{\centi\meter}$ (both at $k = 1$). Compute the area and
its standard uncertainty.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A current $I = 2.0 \,\si{\ampere} \pm 0.05 \,\si{\ampere}$ flows
through a resistor $R = 100 \,\si{\ohm} \pm 1 \,\si{\ohm}$. Compute
the dissipated power and its standard uncertainty, assuming the
two measurements are uncorrelated. Identify which input contributes
more to the power's uncertainty.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A measurement has a value of $9.876 \,\si{\meter\per\second\squared}$
with a standard uncertainty of $0.043 \,\si{\meter\per\second
\squared}$. Report the value at coverage factor $k = 2$ in (a) the
$\pm$ form, (b) the parenthesis form, with both rounded to
appropriate decimal places.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Derivation}\pathtag{standard}

\begin{exercise}
Starting from the algebraic sum rule, derive the combined standard
uncertainty for $y = a x_{1} + b x_{2} + c x_{3}$ with all inputs
uncorrelated. State the conditions under which the derivation is
valid (linearity, independence, finite variance) and identify the
case in which the formula understates the combined uncertainty.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Show that, in the small-error limit, the relative uncertainty of a
product $z = xy$ satisfies
\[
\left(\frac{u(z)}{z}\right)^{2}
= \left(\frac{u(x)}{x}\right)^{2}
+ \left(\frac{u(y)}{y}\right)^{2}
\]
when $x$ and $y$ are uncorrelated. Identify a domain in which this
formula systematically underestimates the uncertainty.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Derive the propagation rule for $z = x + y$ when $x$ and $y$ have
correlation coefficient $\rho$. State the result, and identify the
case in which the uncertainty of $z$ is smaller than either $u(x)$
or $u(y)$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
For a quantity $y = f(x)$ with $x$ uniformly distributed on
$[a, b]$, derive the standard uncertainty of $y$ assuming $f$ is
linear over the interval. Compare to the result for $x$ Gaussian
with the same standard deviation.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Show that the standard uncertainty of the mean of $n$ independent
measurements, each with standard uncertainty $\sigma$, is
$\sigma / \sqrt{n}$. Then consider the simpler correlated case in
which every pair of measurements has the same correlation
coefficient $\rho$. Derive the standard uncertainty of the mean and
state why positive correlation reduces the benefit of averaging.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Estimation}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the standard uncertainty of a smartphone clinometer's
elevation angle reading using assumed values supplied in the
problem: bias of order $10^{-3} \,\si{\radian}$ after calibration
and short-term noise of order $10^{-4} \,\si{\radian}$. State
whether your result is controlled by bias or random noise, and name
the measurement you would perform to replace these assumed values
with data.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate how many independent voltage measurements would be required
to reduce a standard uncertainty of $10 \,\si{\milli\volt}$ to
$1 \,\si{\milli\volt}$, assuming the dominant uncertainty is
random with no systematic component. Then estimate the same
required count when half the uncertainty is systematic.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the standard uncertainty of the mean height of a
representative adult population from a sample of $30$ measurements
each with standard uncertainty $0.5 \,\si{\centi\meter}$. State
the assumptions about between-individual variability versus
within-individual measurement uncertainty.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the height of the tallest building you can see from the
window of your home or workplace, with an explicit error bar
derived from your own assumptions about distance and angular
size. Compare your error bar to the published reference height.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the relative uncertainty in a stopwatch-timed measurement
of the period of a pendulum, given a stopwatch reaction time of
$\sim 0.2 \,\si{\second}$. State the conditions under which the
reaction-time uncertainty is the dominant component of the
period's uncertainty.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Simulation}\pathtag{mastery}

\begin{exercise}
In a language of the reader's choice, simulate $10^{4}$ independent
samples of $z = x + y$ where $x \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$ and
$y \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$ are independent. Compute the empirical
standard deviation of $z$ and compare to the analytical result
$\sqrt{2}$. Then repeat with $\rho = 0.5$ and $\rho = -0.5$
correlation between $x$ and $y$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Simulate $10^{4}$ independent measurements of a quantity drawn from
a uniform distribution on $[-1, 1]$. Estimate the empirical
distribution of the mean of $n$ such measurements for $n = 1, 5,
10, 50, 100$, and compare to the Gaussian prediction from the
central limit theorem. Identify the value of $n$ at which the
sample mean's distribution becomes visually indistinguishable from
Gaussian.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Implement a Monte Carlo propagation per JCGM 101 for the pendulum
$g$-measurement of section 4.3. Use Gaussian distributions for $L$
and $T$ with the standard deviations stated in the worked example.
Compute the empirical standard deviation of $g$ and compare to the
linear-propagation result $0.05 \,\si{\meter\per\second\squared}$.
Comment on whether the linear approximation is appropriate for this
problem.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Simulate the distribution of the sample mean of $n = 5$ samples
drawn from a Cauchy distribution. Plot the empirical distribution
of the sample mean for $1000$ trials and identify the failure of
the central limit theorem. Comment on what averaging more samples
would, and would not, accomplish.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Design}\pathtag{standard}

\begin{exercise}
Design a measurement plan to determine the average mass of an
object in a population of $100$ nominally identical units with an
expanded uncertainty of $U = 0.5 \,\si{\gram}$ at $k = 2$. State
the required standard uncertainty of the mean, the instrument
accuracy or calibration uncertainty, the number of units sampled,
the number of measurements per sampled unit, and the procedure for
separating between-unit variability from measurement repeatability.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Design an uncertainty budget for a household refrigerator
temperature measurement with target expanded uncertainty
$U = 0.5 \,\si{\degreeCelsius}$ at $k = 2$. List each input source,
including thermometer calibration, sensor resolution, sampling
location, time-of-day variation, defrost cycle, and door opening,
with an estimated standard uncertainty contribution. Combine the
contributions and identify which component dominates.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Design a comparison protocol for a clinical-laboratory measurement
of blood glucose by two methods. Given an acceptance criterion
supplied by the problem statement (for example, $\pm 15\%$ of the
reference value at glucose concentrations above
$5.55 \,\si{\milli\mol\per\liter}$), state how the laboratory would
establish whether the two methods agree within their combined
uncertainty, and what investigation follows when they disagree.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Diagnosis and reverse engineering}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
A laboratory reports a measurement of $5.32 \pm 0.001 \,\si
{\volt}$. The instrument is a digital multimeter rated at $\pm
0.05\,\%$ of reading. State whether the reported uncertainty is
plausible, and identify the most likely error in the report.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A pressure measurement gives $P = 100 \,\si{\kilo\pascal}$ with
$u(P) = 0.5 \,\si{\kilo\pascal}$ at $k = 1$. The same pressure
measured by an independent method gives $P = 102 \,\si{\kilo\pascal}$
with $u(P) = 0.4 \,\si{\kilo\pascal}$ at $k = 1$. State whether
the two measurements are consistent within their combined
uncertainty. If not, propose three plausible mechanisms for the
disagreement.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A spreadsheet computes $y = (x_{1} + x_{2}) / x_{3}$ from inputs
with stated uncertainties $u_{1}, u_{2}, u_{3}$. The spreadsheet
reports the standard uncertainty of $y$ as $u_{1} + u_{2} + u_{3}$
divided by $x_{3}$. Identify the error and write the correct
propagation formula.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A torque-wrench specification claims $\pm 4\%$ at $k = 1$. The
manufacturer tests the wrench against a reference and the
measurements scatter at $\pm 4\%$ at $k = 2$. State whether the
wrench meets its specification and what additional information
the user needs to make the comparison.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Failure analysis}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
The Hubble Space Telescope's primary-mirror figure measurement
relied on the reflective null corrector while two auxiliary
optical tests indicated discrepancies. Argue, in 200 to 300
words, what the reported uncertainty on the mirror's figure should
have been if the auxiliary-test results had been incorporated.
Cite the OSBI report \cite{acc:nasa-hubble-optical-systems-1990}.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A polling firm reports a $\pm 3\%$ margin of error on a poll of
$1{,}000$ respondents and concludes that two candidates are
"statistically tied" within the margin. Argue, in 200 to 300
words, whether the stated margin of error correctly characterises
the poll's uncertainty, and identify at least three systematic
sources that the margin does not include.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A regulatory agency reports a cost-benefit analysis with a single
point estimate of net benefit of $\$1.2$ billion. The underlying
analysis produced a range from $\$0.4$ to $\$2.1$ billion. State
what the single point estimate communicates that the range does
not, and identify the discipline that would prevent the
single-estimate report.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Judgment}\pathtag{standard}

\begin{exercise}
A senior colleague reports a measurement as $5.32 \,\si{\volt}$
with no uncertainty. Write a one-paragraph response that takes the
colleague seriously, identifies the specific information missing
to interpret the result, and proposes a minimum acceptable form
for the report.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A startup is preparing a regulatory submission and the regulator
expects a $\pm 5\%$ uncertainty on a key measurement. The startup's
data supports $\pm 8\%$. Argue, in 200 to 300 words, what the
startup should do, why narrowing the reported uncertainty to
$\pm 5\%$ would be a discipline failure even if it satisfies the
regulator, and what the startup's options are.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Argue, in one paragraph, whether engineering training should
emphasise random or systematic error reduction. Take a position
and defend it with a specific example from your own experience or
a documented case.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A data sheet states `accuracy: $\pm 0.8\%$ of reading' with no
coverage factor and no distribution. Write the uncertainty-budget
entry three ways: as a rectangular half-width, as a $k = 2$ Gaussian
expanded uncertainty, and as an unresolved specification requiring
manufacturer clarification. Compare the resulting standard
uncertainties and state which assumption a conservative engineer
would use absent further information.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Run a Monte Carlo propagation for $y = x_{1} - x_{2}$ with equal
standard uncertainties and correlations $\rho = 0$, $0.5$, $0.9$,
and $0.99$. Compare the empirical standard deviation with the
correlated-difference formula
$u_{c}^{2}(y) = u^{2}(x_{1}) + u^{2}(x_{2}) - 2\rho u(x_{1})
u(x_{2})$, and explain why common-mode cancellation depends on
positive correlation between the two readings.
\end{exercise}
