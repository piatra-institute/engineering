\section{Regenerative medicine: state of the art}\pathtag{core}

\engterm{Regenerative medicine} is the discipline of restoring lost or
damaged biological structure and function by stimulating, replacing, or
augmenting the body's own repair processes. It is not a single
technology. It is a family of approaches, each of which makes a
different bet about which level of the biological hierarchy is the
right intervention point. The clinical reality of the late 2020s is
that several of these approaches deliver useful interventions and none
of them yet delivers the general capability the field's vocabulary
promises. The engineer who reads the field's literature must hold the
two facts together.

\subsection{Four interventional levels}

We organise current practice around four interventional levels. Each
level corresponds to a class of method, a class of regulatory pathway,
and a class of failure mode.

\engterm{Cell-level intervention.} A cell therapy delivers viable cells
that perform a missing or damaged function. The cells may be the
patient's own (autologous), from a matched donor (allogeneic), or
derived from an established line. They may be unmanipulated, expanded
in culture, gene-edited, or differentiated from pluripotent precursors.
Examples that have crossed the regulatory threshold include autologous
chondrocyte implantation for cartilage defects, mesenchymal stromal
cell preparations for graft-versus-host disease (regional approvals),
and CAR-T cell therapies for B-cell malignancies (US approval Kymriah
2017, Yescarta 2017) \cite{paper:v6c13-june-cart-2018,
text:v6c13-atala-principles-regen}. CAR-T is a cell-level intervention
that delivers a programmed killing function; the cells themselves are
the therapy.

\engterm{Tissue-level intervention.} Tissue engineering builds a
structure outside the body, or in situ, that performs the role of a
missing tissue. The classical formulation combines a scaffold (a porous
material that defines shape and provides mechanical and chemical
context), cells (the living phase that will remodel or populate the
scaffold), and signals (growth factors, mechanical loading, electrical
or chemical cues). Clinical examples that have reached patients include
skin substitutes (Apligraf, Dermagraft, approved 1998 onward), bladder
augmentation in pediatric cases reported by Atala and colleagues in
2006, and tracheal reconstruction (an active field with documented
failures as well as successes through the 2010s)
\cite{paper:v6c13-atala-bladder-2006, text:v6c13-atala-principles-regen}.

\engterm{Organ-level intervention.} Organ engineering aims at a
replacement organ. Decellularisation-recellularisation methods strip a
donor or porcine organ of its cells, leaving an extracellular-matrix
scaffold, then reseed with patient or donor cells. Bioprinting deposits
cells and matrix in a designed three-dimensional structure. Whole-organ
work has produced laboratory demonstrations (rat heart decellularisation
and partial recellularisation, Ott and colleagues 2008; pig kidney
xenotransplant clinical attempts in the 2020s) but no general-purpose
clinical replacement organ \cite{paper:v6c13-ott-decell-2008}. The
engineering distance between a perfused decellularised matrix and a
functioning organ is large and is dominated by vasculature,
innervation, immunology, and time.

\engterm{Morphogenetic intervention.} Morphogenetic engineering aims at
the control system that builds the structure in the first place. The
bet is that an injury whose default outcome is a scar can be redirected
to an outcome that rebuilds the original structure if the right signals
are delivered to the right cells at the right time. The interventions
are typically chemical (small molecules that open or close ion
channels, modulate developmental pathways), bioelectric (drugs or
optogenetic actuators that change membrane potential patterns across
tissue), or genetic (transient expression of regulatory factors). The
target is not a missing cell type or a missing tissue. The target is
the rule that generates the cell pattern. Section 13.2 takes this
level seriously.

The four levels are not in competition. A cell therapy may be the right
intervention for a single missing function; tissue engineering may be
right for a load-bearing replacement; morphogenetic intervention may be
right for a regeneration-competent structure such as a digit tip or, in
non-mammalian models, a limb. The engineer chooses the level by asking
where the failure was, not by asking which technique is in fashion.

\subsection{What works clinically, as of 2026}

We name the clinical state in a single paragraph and date it. As of
2026, the regenerative-medicine interventions with the strongest
evidence base are: hematopoietic stem cell transplantation (in
continuous use since the 1950s and the discipline's oldest cell
therapy); chondrocyte-based cartilage repair (multiple approved
products since the 1990s, modest but real effect sizes); allogeneic and
autologous skin substitutes for chronic wounds (approved in the 1990s,
real but variable clinical outcomes); CAR-T cell therapies for select
hematologic malignancies (2017 onward, high response rates with
specific toxicities); and gene-modified autologous cell therapies for
selected inherited disorders (sickle cell disease 2023 approvals of
Casgevy and Lyfgenia in the US and UK)
\cite{paper:v6c13-frangoul-casgevy-2021, paper:v6c13-june-cart-2018}.
Several whole-tissue interventions remain investigational with variable
trajectories: pancreatic islet transplantation has approved products in
some jurisdictions for type 1 diabetes; corneal limbal stem cell
transplantation (Holoclar approved in the EU in 2015 for limbal stem
cell deficiency); and intervertebral disc and meniscus replacement
products under various national approvals.

The list is shorter than the field's vocabulary suggests. The 1990s and
2000s saw a generation of papers and a generation of clinical trials
that promised more than they delivered. Reading them now is part of an
engineer's training: the gap between a laboratory demonstration and a
clinical product is wider than it looks, and the failure modes recur.

\subsection{What does not work, and why}

Three failure patterns dominate the regenerative-medicine literature.
We name them now and return to specific cases in Section 13.6.

\engterm{The integration failure.} A graft, a printed tissue, or a
seeded scaffold survives short-term but does not integrate with host
vasculature, innervation, or mechanical context. The intervention
delivers material; the host does not accept it as part of itself. The
failure is usually invisible at the bench (the cells are alive in
culture) and visible only in vivo (the construct fails to perfuse, to
conduct, or to remodel). Tracheal-reconstruction reports through the
2010s collect many examples of this pattern
\cite{paper:v6c13-deluca-tissue-failure}.

\engterm{The control failure.} Cells delivered to a wound are alive,
genetically correct, in the right number, and do the wrong thing. They
fail to differentiate, fail to migrate, differentiate into the wrong
cell type, or proliferate without limit. The intervention provided the
right component without the right control signal. Fetal-cell grafts
for Parkinson's disease in the 1990s produced this pattern at clinical
scale: cells that survived, integrated partially, and in a subset of
recipients produced graft-induced dyskinesias, which are an active
control failure of the grafted tissue
\cite{paper:v6c13-freed-fetal-parkinson-2001,
paper:v6c13-olanow-fetal-parkinson-2003}.

\engterm{The morphogenetic failure.} A structure of the wrong shape is
built. The cells are correct; the integration is acceptable; the
pattern is wrong. A regenerated digit has the wrong segment count; a
seeded scaffold develops fibrotic tissue where it should have developed
muscle; a printed kidney has tubules but not nephrons. The intervention
solved the cell-supply and survival problem and left the shape problem
untouched. This is the failure mode that motivates the morphogenetic
research programme of Section 13.2.

These three failures are nested, not parallel. The integration failure
hides the control failure (you cannot fail at control if you fail at
integration first); the control failure hides the morphogenetic failure
(you cannot generate a shape if your cells will not differentiate at
all). A field that has solved integration sees control failures emerge;
a field that has solved control sees morphogenetic failures emerge.
Reading the literature in this order makes its history legible.

\subsection{The vocabulary problem}

The field's vocabulary is loose. \engterm{Regeneration} sometimes means
the rebuilding of a structure (a salamander limb), sometimes the
replacement of cells in a tissue (the constant turnover of intestinal
epithelium), sometimes the recovery of a function (return of motor
control after stroke). \engterm{Stem cell} sometimes means a
hematopoietic stem cell with a specific surface phenotype, sometimes a
loose preparation of bone-marrow-derived cells, sometimes a pluripotent
cell line. \engterm{Bioengineered organ} sometimes means a
decellularised matrix and sometimes a printed construct and sometimes
an in-vitro tissue assembly the size of a pea.

For an engineer entering the field, two disciplines help. The first is
to read primary papers, not press releases or review articles whose
selection bias hides the failure half of the record. The second is to
treat every claim of ``regeneration'' as a question: regeneration of
what, by what mechanism, demonstrated at what scale, with what control,
and with what failure modes? Few claims survive the question intact.

\begin{principle}[State both halves before betting on either]
Every regenerative-medicine claim has a working half and a not-working
half. The working half is what the paper reports. The not-working half
is everything the paper did not measure, did not report, or measured
and excluded from analysis. An engineer reading the literature reads
both halves. A field whose vocabulary mostly references the working
half is a field whose engineering is not yet stable.
\end{principle}

\section{Bioelectric morphogenesis (Levin's programme)}\pathtag{standard}

The research programme led by Michael Levin at Tufts University
proposes that the bioelectric properties of cells (resting membrane
potentials, gradients of voltage across tissue, and the ion-channel
and gap-junction networks that maintain them) carry pattern information
that controls anatomical outcomes
\cite{paper:v6c13-levin-bioelectric-2014,
paper:v6c13-pezzulo-levin-2015,
paper:v6c13-mathews-levin-2017,
paper:v6c13-levin-comp-boundary-2019,
paper:v6c13-levin-life-death-2021,
paper:v6c13-levin-darwin-agential-2023}.
The claim is empirical and falsifiable in its specific instantiations
and is conjectural in its strongest form. We separate the two in the
exposition that follows.

\subsection{The empirical claim}

Cells maintain a resting membrane potential, the difference in electric
potential between cytoplasm and extracellular space, typically in the
range $-30$ to $-90\,\text{mV}$ in vertebrate tissue. The potential is
not a passive consequence of metabolism; it is maintained by ion pumps
and channels (notably the Na$^+$/K$^+$ ATPase, the proton-pumping
ATPase V-ATPase, and a large family of potassium, chloride, and proton
channels) and it varies systematically across developmental and
regenerative time courses \cite{paper:v6c13-levin-bioelectric-2014}.

The empirical claim has three parts that have been tested in published
work since the early 2000s. First, the membrane potential of individual
cells, mapped across a tissue with voltage-sensitive dyes such as
DiBAC$_4$(3) or genetically encoded indicators, shows non-random
spatial patterns that correlate with developmental anatomy
\cite{paper:v6c13-adams-vmem-2007}. Second, perturbing these patterns
with ion-channel agonists, antagonists, or genetic manipulations of
specific channels (HCN, Kv, glycine receptor and others) produces
specific anatomical changes: ectopic eyes formed at non-cranial
locations after sustained depolarisation of competent cells in
\textit{Xenopus} embryos, and induction of regeneration in normally
non-regenerating tail amputations after the application of a sodium
gradient \cite{paper:v6c13-pai-eye-2012, paper:v6c13-tseng-tail-2010}.
Third, the regeneration of planaria (small flatworms with high
regenerative capacity) can be redirected to produce two-headed or
no-headed animals after gap-junction blockade during a critical window;
the redirected morphology persists through subsequent regenerative
cycles even after the blocker has cleared, suggesting that a
bioelectric pattern is read and re-instantiated as the cells divide
\cite{paper:v6c13-oviedo-planarian-2010}.

The third class of result is the most interesting for an engineer. It
turns the bioelectric pattern into a heritable state that lives in the
tissue and propagates across cell generations without genetic change.
The framing is that the gap-junction-connected cell network is
processing positional information and the network's state is a memory
that determines what the regenerated structure will be.

\subsection{From correlation to control}

The line from ``voltage maps correlate with anatomy'' to ``voltage maps
specify anatomy'' is the line a research programme must cross to count
as engineering-relevant. Levin's group has crossed it for several
specific outcomes:

\begin{itemize}
\item In \textit{Xenopus} embryos, expression of the human
glycine-gated chloride channel GlyR in non-eye anterior tissue,
followed by ligand activation that induces hyperpolarisation, produces
ectopic eye-like organs at remote sites \cite{paper:v6c13-pai-eye-2012}.
The cells are not committed eye precursors; the depolarisation alone is
sufficient to trigger the eye developmental cascade in cells that are
competent to respond.
\item In Xenopus tadpoles, sustained re-polarisation of the wound
membrane by H,K-ATPase activation extends the regenerative window for
the tail and rescues a normally non-regenerative stage
\cite{paper:v6c13-tseng-tail-2010}.
\item In planaria, transient blockade of gap-junction communication
with octanol or related agents during regeneration redirects the
animal to form two heads instead of a head-tail pair. The two-headed
morphology is stable across subsequent rounds of regeneration even
though the blocker is no longer present, until a second targeted
intervention switches the pattern back
\cite{paper:v6c13-oviedo-planarian-2010}.
\end{itemize}

The interventions are surgical only in the sense that they apply at a
specific tissue location and time window. The substrate is
unmodified-genome wild-type animal; the intervention is a small
molecule or a transiently expressed channel. The result is a different
morphology.

\subsection{Engineering reading of the empirical record}

We read the empirical record as engineers. Three observations matter.

First, the manipulations work on biologically competent substrates.
GlyR-mediated depolarisation produces an eye in a region that has
relevant developmental potential; the same intervention would not
produce an eye on a leaf. The bioelectric signal is a switch into a
state the system can already access.

Second, the manipulations are upstream of large biological cascades.
The downstream genetic and biochemical program for ``build an eye'' or
``build a head'' is intact in the substrate. The intervention selects
which of several possible cascades to run.

Third, the manipulations are partial: success rates in published work
are often in the tens of percent, with substantial morphological
variability among successful animals. As of 2026 these are
laboratory-grade outcomes, not engineering-grade outcomes. The gap
between ``we can produce a two-headed planarian in 20\% of treated
animals'' and ``we can specify the anatomy of a regenerating limb to
manufacturing tolerances'' is the engineering distance the field still
has to close.

\subsection{The conjectural claim: bioelectricity as a control language}

The empirical record supports a stronger conjecture that Levin and
colleagues make explicit in their review and synthesis papers
\cite{paper:v6c13-pezzulo-levin-2015,
paper:v6c13-levin-comp-boundary-2019,
paper:v6c13-levin-life-death-2021,
paper:v6c13-levin-darwin-agential-2023}. The conjecture is that the
bioelectric state of the gap-junction-connected cell network is a
\textit{language} for specifying anatomical outcomes. Cells read this
language and act on it; investigators can write in it to redirect
outcomes. The language is at a higher level of abstraction than the
underlying biochemistry and genetics. It plays the role of a control
layer.

The conjecture is engineering-relevant because it changes the
interventional surface. A control language gives the engineer a
high-level interface to a low-level system. A reader of an electrical
schematic does not need to understand silicon physics; a writer in a
high-level programming language does not need to understand register
allocation. If anatomy has a control language and we can read and
write it, then regenerative interventions can be specified at the
language level (build this shape) and translated by the substrate (run
the cellular execution).

The conjecture is far from established at the level of generality at
which it is sometimes stated. The published evidence supports specific
instantiations (the eye, the head, the tail), not a general anatomical
specification language. The conjecture's strongest formulation is a
research hypothesis; its engineering use is to motivate intervention
classes and to suggest experiments that would falsify or refine it.

\begin{archetype}[Control: morphogenetic field as a control system]
A regenerative tissue is a control system. The plant is a population of
cells whose state spans gene expression, metabolism, mechanics, and
membrane potential. The output is anatomical shape, measured at a
length scale much larger than a cell. The setpoint is what the tissue
``thinks'' it is supposed to be, encoded in some combination of
genetics, biochemistry, mechanics, and bioelectricity. The controller
is the cell network's joint behaviour. The disturbance is the injury.

Read this way, an engineering intervention can act at any layer of the
control system: change the actuator (the cells), change the plant
(scaffold and matrix), change the setpoint (the bioelectric pattern),
or change the controller (the rules cells use to read patterns). The
choice of layer is the same choice an engineer makes in any control
problem: at which level is the disturbance, and at which level is the
intervention cheapest, fastest, and least disruptive to the rest of
the system?
\end{archetype}

\subsection{Time scales, length scales, and gradients}

The bioelectric morphogenetic programme operates on length scales from
single cells (microns) to entire embryos (millimetres) and on time
scales from minutes (channel kinetics) to days or weeks (anatomical
outcomes). We name the scales explicitly because the engineering
interface depends on them.

\engterm{Length scales.} Single-cell membrane potential is set by ion
gradients across a single bilayer; the relevant length is the membrane
thickness, around $5\,\text{nm}$. Tissue-scale voltage gradients are
maintained across distances of $100\,\micro\meter$ to several
millimetres, mediated by gap junctions, extracellular currents, and
asymmetric distributions of pumps and channels. The voltage gradient
itself, expressed in millivolts per micrometre, is small (often
$0.1$--$10\,\text{mV}/\text{mm}$ across a tissue) but is read by cells
that are nanometre-scale antennas in the same field
\cite{paper:v6c13-levin-bioelectric-2014}.

\engterm{Time scales.} Channel-opening kinetics are millisecond-scale;
changes in steady-state cell potential settle over seconds to minutes;
tissue-scale voltage patterns establish over minutes to hours;
anatomical outcomes that follow a perturbation appear over days to
weeks. An intervention that holds a perturbation for ten minutes is a
different intervention from one that holds it for ten days.

\engterm{Gradients.} The bioelectric field is a scalar field of
membrane potential plus an associated current density. The cells read
both the value (am I depolarised?) and the spatial pattern (am I in a
gradient, and which way does it point?). The shape of the field
matters, not only its magnitude.

\subsection{The mechanistic open questions}

Several mechanistic questions remain open as of 2026 and are visible in
the published literature.

\engterm{How does a cell read a tissue-scale voltage?} A single cell's
membrane potential is set primarily by that cell's ion homeostasis. The
mechanism by which a cell senses where it sits in a tissue-wide
gradient is not fully resolved. Candidate mechanisms include voltage-
and pH-sensitive transcription, membrane-curvature coupling to
voltage, mechanically gated channels responding to differential
pressure, and calcium signalling driven by voltage-sensitive channels.

\engterm{What is the gap-junction code?} Gap junctions allow exchange
of small molecules and ions between connected cells, and their
connectivity is dynamic. The hypothesis is that connectivity patterns
plus voltage patterns together specify regions of tissue with distinct
identities. The specific molecular events that distinguish ``connected
to neighbour with similar potential'' from ``connected to neighbour
with different potential'' are not all known.

\engterm{How does the pattern persist across cell generations?} The
planarian two-headed phenotype is heritable across cycles of
regeneration without genetic change. The proposed mechanism is that
the bioelectric pattern, distributed across the gap-junction network,
re-establishes after each division and biases the next regenerative
cycle to the same outcome. This is a candidate for an epigenetic
memory at the tissue level. The molecular implementation of this
memory is not established.

These are research questions, not engineering questions. The engineer
who would build something based on Levin's programme reads them as the
boundary of what is currently specifiable.

\begin{estimation}
\textbf{Question.} Estimate the bioelectric voltage gradient across a
regenerating planarian wound. State assumptions and units.

\textbf{Answer sketch.} A planarian is approximately $1$--$10\,\text{mm}$
long. The blastema (the regenerating tissue at the wound site) extends
over $\sim 100\,\micro\meter$ to $500\,\micro\meter$. Resting
potentials in non-excitable cells are in the range $-30$ to
$-70\,\text{mV}$. The bioelectric maps from voltage-sensitive dyes show
local variations of order $10$ to $40\,\text{mV}$ across the
blastema-mature-tissue boundary \cite{paper:v6c13-adams-vmem-2007,
paper:v6c13-oviedo-planarian-2010}. The gradient is therefore
approximately

\[
\nabla V \sim \frac{20\,\text{mV}}{200\,\micro\meter}
\approx 100\,\text{mV}/\text{mm} = 0.1\,\text{mV}/\micro\meter.
\]

This is small as a laboratory electric field but is the signal the
cell network reads. The estimate is to one significant figure;
tissue-scale voltage measurements carry $\pm 30\%$ uncertainty from
dye calibration alone. The estimate suffices to guide an experimental
design (a perturbation must change membrane potential by tens of
millivolts to register as a significant signal) and is too coarse to
serve as a specification (a clinical intervention would need to
specify the gradient to a tighter tolerance, which is currently not
achievable).
\end{estimation}

\section{The anatomical compiler concept}\pathtag{standard}

The term \engterm{anatomical compiler} appears in Levin's published
synthesis papers as a framing for a long-horizon goal of the research
programme \cite{paper:v6c13-levin-life-death-2021,
paper:v6c13-levin-darwin-agential-2023}. A compiler in computing
translates a high-level specification (source code) into a low-level
execution (machine instructions). An anatomical compiler, by analogy,
would translate a high-level anatomical specification (build this
shape) into low-level cellular execution (these cells, in these
positions, in this differentiation state, connected this way, doing
this work).

The analogy is illuminating and misleading in different ways. We
develop it carefully.

\subsection{The compiler analogy, stated carefully}

A traditional software compiler accepts a source program in a language
whose semantics are formally specified, performs lexing, parsing,
semantic analysis, optimisation, and code generation, and emits machine
code that, when executed on a target processor, computes what the
source said. The compiler is a function from one well-defined language
to another. The languages are specified at the symbol level; the
translation is deterministic to within optimisation choices.

An anatomical compiler, by analogy, would accept a specification of an
anatomical target (in some language we do not yet have), translate it
through layers of representation (perhaps bioelectric patterns at the
intermediate level), and dispatch a sequence of cellular actions that
build the target shape in living tissue. The source program is a
desired anatomy; the target machine is a population of cells in their
local environment.

The analogy is illuminating in several respects. It separates the
specification of \textit{what} from the execution of \textit{how},
which is the same separation that lets a software engineer ignore
register allocation and focus on algorithms. It makes the engineering
intervention point clear: change the source program (write new
specifications) rather than rewriting every cell's behaviour. It
suggests that the intermediate representations might be discoverable
by the same kind of probing that compiled-binary reverse-engineering
uses.

The analogy is misleading in other respects. The substrate is not a
deterministic processor; it is a population of self-interested cells
whose lineage history, metabolic state, and physical context all
affect their response. The source language is not yet specified. The
machine instructions are not exposed by an instruction-set
documentation; they are inferred from observation. The compiler itself,
in the analogy, does not exist; what exists is a partial catalogue of
input-output pairs (we know that intervention $A$ produces outcome $B$
in substrate $C$).

\subsection{Three readings of the compiler conjecture}

We separate three readings of the anatomical-compiler conjecture
because they have different evidence requirements and different
engineering consequences.

\engterm{Reading 1: the metaphor.} The compiler is a vivid analogy for
the relation between high-level anatomical specification and low-level
cellular execution. As a metaphor it is useful for talking about the
research programme. It commits to no specific mechanism. This reading
is supported by every paper in the bioelectric morphogenesis literature
in the sense that it is consistent with all of them.

\engterm{Reading 2: the empirical hypothesis.} Some specific bioelectric
or biochemical layer in the cell network plays the role of an
intermediate representation between high-level pattern (the target
anatomy) and low-level execution (gene expression, cell
differentiation, cell migration). The hypothesis is testable;
experimental evidence includes the planarian two-headed result and the
Xenopus eye result. The evidence is partial and species-specific; the
layer is not fully mapped; the read-write semantics are not all known.

\engterm{Reading 3: the engineering claim.} A practical compiler can be
built: a system that accepts target anatomies in some specification
language, translates them through the intermediate layer, and emits
interventions that reliably build those anatomies in target organisms.
This reading is conjectural with respect to all but the simplest
outcomes. As of 2026 there is no general anatomical compiler; there
are demonstrations that ablate or duplicate specific structures in
specific model organisms.

A reader who collapses these three readings into one will either
overestimate the state of the science (Reading 1 dressed as Reading 3)
or underestimate the research programme (Reading 3 failures cited
against Reading 2). The engineering critique in this chapter's project
exercise depends on keeping them separate.

\subsection{What the source language might look like}

If a future anatomical compiler exists, what would its source language
specify? We list five candidate elements drawn from the developmental
biology and morphogenesis literature.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Topology.} The connectivity of the target structure: how
many segments, how many digits, what is connected to what. Topology
is the least redundant specification; small changes have large
effects.
\item \textbf{Polarity.} The intrinsic directionality of the structure:
dorsal-ventral, anterior-posterior, proximal-distal. Polarity in
current bioelectric work appears to correlate with voltage-gradient
direction.
\item \textbf{Scale.} The size of the target structure in absolute
units or relative to body size. Scale specification is a hard problem
in developmental biology; the mechanisms by which embryos sense and
maintain proportion are partially known.
\item \textbf{Composition.} Which tissue types appear where: bone here,
cartilage here, vasculature here. Composition is the level closest to
current tissue-engineering practice.
\item \textbf{Function.} What the structure is supposed to do:
load-bearing, sensing, secretion. Functional specification implies
all of the above and adds usage constraints.
\end{enumerate}

A useful source language would let an engineer specify these elements
modularly; a useful compiler would translate them into a sequence of
interventions; a useful runtime would execute the interventions and
verify the outcome. None of these layers exists for general use.
Single-purpose tooling exists at the research-laboratory scale for
particular outcomes.

\begin{estimation}
\textbf{Question.} Estimate the information content required to
specify a salamander limb. Use a coarse bound; state your assumptions.

\textbf{Answer sketch.} A salamander forelimb has tens of bones,
dozens of muscles, hundreds of nerves and vessels at major-branch
resolution, and approximately $10^7$ to $10^9$ cells depending on
species and age. A bone-and-major-soft-tissue description at
millimetre resolution would specify perhaps $10^4$ to $10^5$ voxels of
tissue identity, each requiring a few bits for tissue type and a few
bits for orientation. The information at this resolution is on the
order of $10^5$ to $10^6$ bits, roughly 10--100 kilobytes.

Cell-resolution specification is far larger. A $10^8$-cell limb, with
20 bits per cell to specify position and type, would require $2\times
10^9$ bits, around $200\,\text{MB}$. This is well within current
information-storage capabilities and well beyond current
biological-specification capabilities.

Two observations follow. First, the millimetre-scale description is
not large; if the source language operates at that resolution, the
specification is short. Second, the cell-scale description is large
but bounded; the substrate is responsible for filling in
sub-specification detail (which cell goes where, exactly), and the
compiler does not need to emit cell-level instructions. The strong
form of the compiler conjecture is precisely that low-level detail is
recovered by the substrate from a much higher-level specification.

The estimate is order-of-magnitude. The actual minimum-description
length depends on the structure of the source language and the
redundancy of the substrate's interpretation, neither of which is
established. The exercise is meant to fix scale, not to bound a
deliverable.
\end{estimation}

\subsection{Why the compiler analogy is engineering-useful}

Even if the analogy turns out to be wrong in detail, it is
engineering-useful in three specific ways.

First, it makes the level of intervention explicit. An engineer who
asks ``at what level is my source specification?'' can choose between
direct cell manipulation (low level, high specificity, low
scalability), tissue-scale signal delivery (intermediate level,
moderate specificity, moderate scalability), and high-level pattern
specification (high level, unknown specificity, potentially high
scalability). The compiler analogy clarifies the tradeoff.

Second, it makes the engineering goals testable. A pattern-language
intervention that is supposed to produce a specific structure is
falsified by producing the wrong structure or no structure at all.
Without the framing, an intervention that fails partially can be
reported as a qualified success. With the framing, the failure pattern
is the diagnostic.

Third, it makes the safety and ethical questions concrete. A compiler
that emits arbitrary structures emits structures we do not want as
well as structures we do want. The control of \textit{what gets
written} becomes a separate engineering problem from the control of
\textit{how the writing executes}. We treat both in Section 13.5.

\section{Engineering challenges in morphogenetic control}\pathtag{standard}

The engineering distance between Levin's published demonstrations and a
working anatomical compiler is large. We catalogue the principal
challenges. The list is meant to be honest rather than complete; an
engineer entering the field will find each of these problems in
specific concrete form.

\subsection{Specification challenges}

\engterm{No source language.} The most basic challenge is that there is
no established language in which to write anatomical specifications.
Computer-aided design (CAD) provides a specification language for
mechanical structures: solid models, dimensions, tolerances, surface
finishes. There is no comparable formal language for biological
structures. Voxelised volumetric specifications (medical imaging
formats such as DICOM) describe existing anatomy; they do not specify
target anatomy in a constructive form.

\engterm{No formal semantics.} A specification is useful only if its
meaning is determinate. ``Build a limb'' leaves underspecified: which
limb, what size, what proportions, what muscle attachment points, what
nerve routing, what blood supply. Each underspecification is a degree
of freedom the substrate or the compiler must resolve. A specification
language that lets the substrate resolve everything has low engineering
content; a specification language that resolves everything has very
high information requirements and may not match biological flexibility.

\engterm{Verification.} A specification's correctness can be checked
only against a built structure. If a specification produces a wrong
structure, the engineer must distinguish a specification error from a
compiler error from an execution error. The diagnosis problem
multiplies with the size of the toolchain.

\subsection{Translation challenges}

\engterm{No catalogue of input-output pairs.} A compiler's translation
tables are populated by the careful work of identifying which inputs
produce which outputs in which substrate. The published bioelectric
morphogenesis literature provides a small number of these pairs (the
planarian two-headed phenotype, the Xenopus ectopic eye, the rescued
tail regeneration) in particular model organisms. Generalising any of
these to mammalian tissue or human clinical context requires its own
research programme; transferring a planarian result to a human is not a
software porting problem.

\engterm{Substrate variability.} The substrate is not a standardised
machine. Cell populations differ between individuals; tissue states
differ between healthy and injured contexts; immune environments differ
between recipients. The translation step in any anatomical compiler
must absorb this variability or fail. Software compilers target a
standardised instruction set; an anatomical compiler does not.

\engterm{Pattern stability.} The bioelectric pattern in a tissue is
maintained by active processes (pump activity, gap-junction state,
extracellular currents). The pattern is therefore susceptible to
perturbation by anything that affects these processes: drugs,
inflammation, ageing, mechanical stress. A compiler whose intermediate
representation is unstable is a compiler whose outputs are unreliable.

\subsection{Execution challenges}

\engterm{Resolution and addressability.} A bioelectric intervention is
applied to a population of cells. Specifying \textit{which cells}
receive the intervention is technically difficult at length scales
below a few hundred micrometres. Optogenetic actuators provide
single-cell addressability in experimental settings but require genetic
modification. Pharmacological interventions are systemic by default.
The control surface is coarse where the biology demands fine.

\engterm{Time-window control.} Many morphogenetic interventions work
only during specific developmental or regenerative time windows.
Outside the window the substrate is unresponsive or responds
differently. The compiler must therefore not only know what to write
but when. The window-knowledge problem multiplies the
research-and-tooling cost.

\engterm{Side effects.} An intervention that depolarises cells in a
target tissue may depolarise cells in adjacent tissues, may affect
cells whose role is not understood, may have off-target effects on
downstream pathways. Side effects in software compilers are a known
class of bug; side effects in anatomical interventions can be permanent
or lethal.

\subsection{Safety and reversibility}

\engterm{Reversibility is not automatic.} A compiled program that runs
incorrectly can be killed and re-run. A built tissue that is wrong is
in the patient. The cost of an error is asymmetric. This asymmetry
drives the field's clinical-translation timeline more than the
technology's underlying capability does.

\engterm{Cancer risk.} Interventions that drive proliferation,
de-differentiation, or pattern change are exactly the interventions
whose miscarriage produces tumours. The bioelectric programme's strong
results in regeneration come with a corollary risk: the same mechanisms
that rebuild a structure can drive uncontrolled growth if the
regulation breaks down. Safety engineering for morphogenetic
interventions is in its infancy.

\engterm{Immune response.} Even autologous interventions can provoke
immune response if the modified cells display non-self markers or if
the rebuilt structure exposes hidden antigens. Allogeneic interventions
require either immune suppression (with its own risks) or tolerance
induction (which is an active research field).

\subsection{Verification and regulatory framing}

\engterm{What counts as success?} A regulatory pathway requires
endpoints that are measurable and clinically meaningful. A bioelectric
intervention's intermediate effect (a voltage pattern, a gene
expression change) is not by itself a clinical endpoint. The field
needs measurement infrastructure for outcomes (a limb regrew with what
function, what stability, what longevity) and biomarkers that correlate
with longer-term outcomes.

\engterm{Trial design.} A clinical trial for a morphogenetic
intervention does not fit the classical small-molecule template (give
drug, measure dose-response, demonstrate safety, demonstrate efficacy
against control). The intervention is multi-modal, the timeline is
long, the outcome is structural rather than physiological in a
classical sense. New trial designs are needed and are being developed
incrementally for cell and gene therapies.

\engterm{Regulatory pathway.} Cell and gene therapies have established
regulatory pathways (US FDA's Center for Biologics Evaluation and
Research, EMA's Advanced Therapy Medicinal Product framework).
Morphogenetic interventions, depending on their nature, may fit under
these pathways, under medical-device pathways, under combination-product
pathways, or may require new frameworks. The regulatory engineering
work is itself an engineering challenge.

\begin{principle}[Morphogenesis is engineering with biological substrate: the substrate is computational, not just mechanical]
The cells in a tissue are not passive material on which the engineer
acts. They run a program. The program is partially specified by
genetics, partially by the local environment, partially by bioelectric
and biochemical state inherited from the developmental history. An
intervention that ignores the program will be either overruled (the
program continues without effect) or destructive (the program is
corrupted). An intervention that addresses the program is the
substrate's collaborator rather than its opponent. The engineering
problem of morphogenetic control is to identify the program's
read-write interface, characterise its semantics, and design
interventions that the program will accept.
\end{principle}

\subsection{Tooling and instrumentation gaps}

We name the principal tooling gaps as of 2026.

\engterm{Voltage measurement at scale.} Voltage-sensitive dyes are
useful for short experiments in small organisms; they bleach, they
perturb membrane properties, they cannot be used continuously over days
in clinical contexts. Genetically encoded voltage indicators (ASAP
family, Archon, voltron) provide better stability and specificity but
require genetic modification. Direct electrode-based measurements give
point data without spatial coverage. A clinical tool for tissue-scale
voltage measurement does not exist.

\engterm{Spatially patterned intervention.} Producing a specified
voltage pattern across a tissue, rather than a uniform depolarisation,
requires either multi-electrode arrays, optogenetic actuators with
spatial light delivery, or magnetothermal genetics. Each is at the
demonstration stage; none is a clinical tool. The intervention
bandwidth is therefore limited.

\engterm{Computational models.} Simulation tools for bioelectric
patterning, gap-junction networks, and morphogenetic outcome prediction
exist (BETSE for bioelectric modelling, NetLogo implementations for
agent-based pattern formation, Compucell3D for cellular Potts
modelling) but no integrated tool maps from a target morphology to an
intervention plan. A compiler in the classical sense is missing.

\engterm{Verification assays.} A morphogenetic outcome is verified by
imaging, histology, electrophysiology, and behavioural assay. None of
these is fast or cheap. A compiler that emits a thousand candidate
interventions cannot be tested against a thousand outcomes in
reasonable time.

\section{Long-horizon framing: what it would mean to compile anatomy}\pathtag{standard}

We step back from the engineering challenges and ask what the
long-horizon framing actually claims. A compiler that worked would
change several things at once. Listing them clarifies what is at stake.

\subsection{What a working compiler would change}

\engterm{Trauma and regeneration.} Burns, amputations, congenital
malformations, and certain forms of organ failure are currently
addressed by replacement (transplant, prosthetic), by reconstruction
(surgical tissue rearrangement), or by acceptance. A working anatomical
compiler would address a subset of these by directing in-situ
regeneration of the missing structure. The clinical addressable
population is large; the technical bar is high.

\engterm{Drug discovery.} A subset of drug development today aims at
restoring function lost to degeneration. A compiler could shift the
emphasis from molecules that slow degeneration to interventions that
direct regeneration. The economic model of drug development would
change.

\engterm{Aging.} A substantial fraction of age-related disease is
structural: cartilage loss, vascular damage, neurodegeneration. A
compiler operative on tissue-scale structure could in principle
address several of these. The framing of aging as an engineering
problem is not new and is not specific to bioelectric morphogenesis;
the compiler programme is one of several research lines that
contribute.

\engterm{Synthetic biology.} The relationship between synthetic biology
(which engineers individual cells or populations to perform functions)
and a hypothetical anatomical compiler is complementary. Synthetic
biology builds the components; the compiler arranges them into
structures. The two would be deployed together.

\engterm{Ethical and legal categories.} A compiler that builds tissues,
organs, or limbs touches questions of identity, consent, ownership,
and access. Some of these are extensions of existing biomedical
ethics; some are new. We name them here without attempting a complete
account.

\subsection{What a partial compiler would change}

The strongest forms of the conjecture are decades away at best. A more
realistic medium-term scenario is a partial compiler: a system that can
specify and instantiate a small number of structures, in restricted
biological contexts, with bounded reliability. Such a system would
already change practice.

\engterm{Skin and cartilage at predictable shape.} Current tissue
engineering produces approximate shapes that the host remodels (often
imperfectly) into something like the target. A partial compiler that
specified shape to working tolerances would improve outcomes for burn
patients, congenital reconstruction, and post-surgical scar management.

\engterm{Digit-tip regeneration.} Human distal fingertip regeneration
is well documented in young patients and at conservative amputation
levels \cite{paper:v6c13-illingworth-digit-1974}. A partial compiler
that extended the addressable window to older patients and more
proximal amputations would be a substantial clinical advance.

\engterm{Targeted pattern restoration in degenerated tissue.}
Conditions in which a normal structure has been replaced by a wrong
structure (fibrotic scar, cataract, certain forms of vascular
restructuring) are candidates for pattern-restoration interventions
even without full regeneration.

\engterm{Tissue-pattern adjuncts in oncology.} Tumours involve
breakdown of morphogenetic control. Interventions that restore
bioelectric or other pattern signals could in principle act as
adjuncts to existing oncologic therapy. This is currently a research
hypothesis rather than a clinical option.

\subsection{The information-theoretic frame}

We name two information-theoretic observations because they shape the
long-horizon framing.

\engterm{Anatomical specification is compressible.} The mature human
body has approximately $3\times 10^{13}$ cells (an order of magnitude
estimate). The full anatomical specification at cell-level resolution
would be enormous. The human genome encodes approximately $3\times
10^9$ base pairs, of which a small fraction is regulatory. The genome
plus developmental context plus the substrate's local rules produce
the anatomy. The compressibility ratio is enormous, and most of the
work is done by the substrate. A compiler need not specify cell-level
detail; it must specify the high-level constraints that the substrate
fills in.

\engterm{Anatomical execution is noisy.} No two individuals are
identical at high resolution. Identical twins differ in fingerprint
patterns, in microvascular routing, in fine cortical structure. The
substrate is a noisy executor of a compressed specification. A
compiler must accept this noise as part of its semantics; an
intervention that specifies cell positions to micrometre tolerance is
fighting the substrate.

These two observations frame the engineering target. A working compiler
specifies at a compression level the substrate can interpret and
accepts the noise that the substrate's interpretation introduces.
Demanding more than this is fighting the biology; demanding less leaves
the compiler with too little control.

\begin{estimation}
\textbf{Question.} Estimate the compute cost for a putative
anatomical-compiler simulation that verifies a candidate intervention
before deployment. State assumptions.

\textbf{Answer sketch.} A simulation of a regenerative event in a
millimetre-scale structure with cell-resolution dynamics requires
tracking on the order of $10^5$ to $10^7$ cells, each with state for
gene expression (perhaps 100 variables), bioelectric properties (5--10
variables), and mechanical properties (5--10 variables). The state
vector is therefore around $10^7$ to $10^9$ floating-point numbers.
Time integration over a regenerative event lasting days, at minute
resolution, requires perhaps $10^4$ steps.

If each step's computation is dominated by cell-cell signalling with
$\sim 10$ neighbours and $\sim 100$ operations per neighbour, the
per-step cost is around $10^4$ floating-point operations per cell, or
$10^9$ to $10^{11}$ operations total. Over $10^4$ time steps, the
simulation cost is $10^{13}$ to $10^{15}$ floating-point operations. A
modern GPU performs $10^{13}$ to $10^{14}$ operations per second
(current as of 2026), so the simulation runs in seconds to hours per
intervention candidate. A campaign that screens $10^3$ candidates
costs hours to weeks.

This compute estimate is not the limiting factor. The limiting factor
is model fidelity: the simulation reproduces the intervention's effect
only if the model captures the relevant biology, and current models do
not. A larger compute budget on a wrong model produces wrong answers
faster. The estimation is to guide engineering planning, not to
suggest that compute is the bottleneck.
\end{estimation}

\subsection{Coupling to other engineering disciplines}

A working compiler would not stand alone; it would couple to other
engineering disciplines along established interfaces.

\engterm{To imaging.} Specification and verification both depend on
imaging the target site at relevant resolution. Medical imaging
(MRI, CT, ultrasound, optical coherence tomography) provides relevant
infrastructure. A compiler that consumes imaging data and emits
imaging-verifiable outcomes fits the existing clinical infrastructure
better than one that requires new diagnostics.

\engterm{To synthetic biology.} As above. A compiler emits
interventions that may include engineered cell populations; synthetic
biology builds those populations. The interface is the cell as a
deliverable.

\engterm{To pharmacology.} Small-molecule modulators of ion channels
are the workhorse of bioelectric experiments. Their pharmacology,
side-effect profiles, and clinical histories already exist for many
candidate molecules. A compiler that emits pharmacological
prescriptions fits existing regulatory and clinical structures.

\engterm{To medical devices.} Multi-electrode arrays, optogenetic
delivery systems, and patterned light sources are medical-device
technologies. A compiler that emits device prescriptions creates an
interface to the medical-device engineering discipline.

\engterm{To clinical care.} The patient is the system. Workflow,
informed consent, follow-up, and long-term monitoring are part of the
compiler's interface to the clinical world. A compiler that ignores
this interface produces interventions that cannot be deployed.

\section{Failure: morphogenetic interventions that did not work, and what they teach}\pathtag{core}

A field's failure record is its most reliable teacher. We name three
classes of failure, each with at least one specific case from the
published literature. The chapter does not name new accidents; the
cases are sufficient as paper-level citations and several have
multi-decade follow-up that we can read.

\subsection{The Astoria-class electrical-stimulation claims}

A line of work in the 1970s through the 1990s, beginning with reports
from R.\,O.\,Becker and Joseph A.\,Spadaro and a small constellation of
collaborators, proposed that direct-current electrical stimulation
could induce regeneration of amputated structures (including digit
tips and partial limbs) in mammals
\cite{paper:v6c13-becker-currents-1972,
paper:v6c13-spadaro-bone-1974}. The claims were dramatic; the
laboratory reports included radiographic evidence of regenerated
structures in animal models; the work received substantial attention.

The engineering failure modes were several. First, the reported
outcomes were not reproducible at the strong claims' level. Subsequent
work has not generally replicated mammalian limb regeneration after
electrical stimulation alone. Second, the mechanism proposed (a
``current of injury'' that, when supplemented, drives regeneration)
was not well distinguished from secondary effects (wound healing, scar
remodelling) in the original studies. Third, the field did not develop
standardised end-point measurements; the line between ``a small bump
grew on the stump'' and ``regeneration'' was not held to a consistent
definition.

The lesson is methodological: an intervention whose success criterion
is not specified ahead of time cannot fail in a useful way. The
bioelectric programme that descended from this line (and that now
includes Levin's work) has been more careful about end-points,
reproducibility, and species specificity. The bioelectric framing is
the same; the discipline is different.

\subsection{Fetal-cell grafts for Parkinson's disease}

In the 1980s and 1990s, several groups grafted dopamine-producing fetal
midbrain cells into the striatum of patients with advanced Parkinson's
disease. Open-label studies suggested benefit; controlled trials
(Freed et al.\ 2001 NEJM; Olanow et al.\ 2003) gave a more complex
picture: modest benefit for some patients, no benefit for others, and
a subset of patients who developed \engterm{graft-induced dyskinesias},
abnormal involuntary movements attributable to the graft itself
\cite{paper:v6c13-freed-fetal-parkinson-2001,
paper:v6c13-olanow-fetal-parkinson-2003}.

The engineering failure modes were three. First, the intervention
delivered cells without delivering the regulatory context in which
those cells normally operate. Surviving grafts secreted dopamine; they
did not modulate secretion in response to behavioural state, because
the host did not innervate them as a normal nigrostriatal pathway. The
graft's open-loop dopamine release in a system that normally has
closed-loop regulation explains the dyskinesia subset. Second, the
intervention was given to patients with advanced disease whose
remaining circuitry was already abnormal; a working graft in a
non-working circuit is a different intervention from a working graft
in a healthy circuit. Third, the cell preparations varied between
trials in ways that were not fully characterised at the time
\cite{paper:v6c13-barker-fetal-stemcell-2015}.

The lesson for a future anatomical compiler is that delivering the
right cells is not equivalent to delivering the right function. A
compiler that emits cells without emitting the regulatory rewiring
required to integrate them is a compiler that produces control
failures. The fetal-cell story is therefore part of the compiler
programme's pre-history: it is a case study in why morphogenetic
control, not cell delivery alone, is the target.

\subsection{Over-interpretation episodes in regenerative medicine}

A pattern of over-interpretation appears repeatedly in the
regenerative-medicine literature. Specific examples have included
early bone-marrow-derived ``transdifferentiation'' claims in the
2000s (cells reported to convert from one lineage to another in vivo,
later largely attributed to cell fusion or technical artefact)
\cite{paper:v6c13-wagers-transdiff-2004,
paper:v6c13-anversa-cardiac-stem-retraction}; certain stem-cell-derived
tracheal reconstruction reports and clinical applications in the early
2010s, several of which were retracted or otherwise corrected
following misconduct investigations
\cite{paper:v6c13-deluca-tissue-failure}; and reports of cardiac
stem-cell regeneration originating in the Anversa laboratory, which
were retracted or expression-of-concerned in significant volume in
2018--2019 after institutional investigation
\cite{paper:v6c13-anversa-cardiac-stem-retraction}.

The engineering failure mode is not a single technical error. It is a
system-level failure of the field's incentives and verification
practices. Strong claims attract attention, attention attracts funding
and clinical interest, clinical interest attracts patients, and the
infrastructure for retraction and correction is slower than the
infrastructure for publication and translation. The lesson for the
compiler programme is that strong claims about morphogenetic
intervention will have similar incentive pressures and similar failure
pressure if the field does not build verification infrastructure ahead
of intervention claims.

The chapter's named-cases meta-line is ``none required'' because no
single named accident anchors the failure discussion; the failures are
documented at paper level across multiple cases and multiple
laboratories. The discipline of citing the primary papers rather than
narrating a stylised case study is intentional. A field in motion is
read in the primary literature; the case study format is for failures
that have stabilised in the historical record.

\subsection{What the failures have in common}

Three patterns recur in the failures we cited.

\engterm{Underspecified success criteria.} In each case, what counts as
success was either not specified ahead of the experiment or was
specified loosely enough that ambiguous results could be reported as
success. A compiler programme needs success criteria stated as
falsifiable predictions before the intervention.

\engterm{Mechanism-output gap.} In each case, a proposed mechanism
(current of injury, dopaminergic restoration, transdifferentiation)
was not held to a quantitative read-out that would distinguish it
from alternatives. A compiler programme needs intermediate-step
measurements that constrain the model.

\engterm{Translation pressure.} In each case, premature translation
from animal model to human application contributed to the failure's
public consequences. A compiler programme that builds out the
translation infrastructure ahead of the strong claims will avoid this
pressure point.

\subsection{What the failures teach a compiler programme}

Read as engineering lessons, the failures teach the following.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
\item Specify what success means ahead of the intervention. A
qualitative endpoint becomes any endpoint when the experiment is
inconclusive.
\item Build measurement infrastructure before intervention
infrastructure. A measurement that lets the engineer detect a wrong
outcome is the prerequisite for the compiler's diagnostic feedback.
\item Treat the substrate's autonomous control as the dominant
process. Cells continue running their program after the intervention;
an intervention that depends on the program staying suspended is
brittle.
\item Make every claim about a model organism explicit about the
species, the stage, and the genotype. Bioelectric results in planaria
or Xenopus do not transfer to mammalian tissue without specific
re-derivation.
\item Build the translation infrastructure (animal-to-human, lab-to-
clinic) at the same pace as the technical infrastructure. Strong
claims under-supported by translational evidence collapse publicly.
\end{enumerate}

These are the principles the chapter's project critique applies to the
CERB position paper.

\section{Closing: living matter as the next engineering frontier}\pathtag{core}

The book treats engineering as the discipline by which measured reality
becomes reliable intervention. Living matter is the substrate where
that discipline meets its hardest test. The substrate is not passive;
it has its own programs, its own memory, its own development
trajectory. Engineering at this level is not the engineering of an
object that does what we say. It is the engineering of a collaborator
whose autonomy is part of the problem and part of the solution at once.

\subsection{The intellectual framing}

The chapter has argued three claims.

First, regenerative medicine as it stands in 2026 is a discipline with
working clinical interventions and a longer record of partial
successes and characteristic failures than the field's vocabulary
suggests. An engineer entering the discipline reads both the working
half and the not-working half.

Second, Michael Levin's bioelectric morphogenesis programme is an
empirically grounded research line whose published results support a
specific claim (bioelectric patterns participate in morphogenetic
control in particular model systems) and whose stronger framings (the
anatomical compiler conjecture) are conjectural and engineering-useful
in equal measure.

Third, the engineering challenges of building a working anatomical
compiler are substantial and named. They are not the kind of
challenges that yield to a single breakthrough; they are the kind that
yield to a sustained engineering discipline applied to a substrate
whose semantics are partially unknown.

The book's thesis applies to this domain in a specific form: living
matter becomes reliable intervention only by careful, measured work
that respects the substrate's autonomous control. The engineering
frontier is not a domain where the engineer overcomes biology. It is a
domain where the engineer joins biology's existing program as a
deliberate, accountable collaborator.

\subsection{The institutional grounding}

The Piatra Institute hosts a project called the Centre for Engineering
of Regenerative Biology (CERB), whose published position paper outlines
a research and intervention programme inspired in part by the
bioelectric-morphogenesis line and by the broader compiler framing.
The chapter's project (below) asks the reader to read that position
paper and to write a one-page engineering critique. The institute is
the sponsor of this book; the chapter's intellectual scaffolding
stands or falls on the published science we have cited, not on the
institute's own promotional material. The reader is invited to
evaluate CERB on the engineering criteria the chapter has developed.

\subsection{What the closing chapter is for}

A closing chapter of a volume serves two purposes. First, it gathers
the volume's themes (cellular machinery, metabolism, genetics, proteins
as machines, tissue and organ engineering, biomechanics, the nervous
and immune systems, populations and ecology, synthetic biology,
bioreactors, bioinformatics, biocompatibility) into a single forward
frame: a discipline that builds and rebuilds living structure. Second,
it sets the volume in the larger book: the engineering of life sits
next to the engineering of information (Volume VII), of machines
(Volume VIII), of systems (Volume IX), of failure (Volume X), of
design (Volume XI), and of civilization (Volume XII). The chapters
ahead address the discipline at progressively larger scales; the
chapter behind addresses the discipline at the scale of cells and
tissues; this chapter is the hinge.

A reader who finishes the book and turns back to this chapter will
read it differently. The book by then has framed engineering as
disciplined intervention under constraint, failure, scale, and
responsibility. The living substrate is the case where every term of
that framing operates at the same time. The chapter that closes the
life volume should leave the reader uncertain about the timeline and
certain about the discipline.

\section{Project}\pathtag{core}

\begin{project}[Critique the CERB position paper from an engineering perspective]

The reader reads the Piatra Institute's CERB position paper (published
on the institute's site at \texttt{piatra-policies/cerb}) and writes a
one-page engineering critique. The exercise is the volume's closing
intellectual artefact.

\textbf{Track}. Read-and-critique (analytical). No wet work, no
clinical contact, no laboratory access. The deliverable is a written
critique.

\textbf{Hazard class}. None. Reading and writing.

\textbf{Phases}.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
\item \textbf{Reading.} Read the CERB position paper end to end. Read
it twice. Take notes on: what intervention level is being claimed
(cell, tissue, organ, morphogenetic); what specifications, if any, are
stated as falsifiable; what mechanisms are claimed; what verification
infrastructure is described; what failure modes are addressed and
which are silent.
\item \textbf{Re-reading source material.} Re-read Sections 13.2--13.6
of this chapter. Re-read Levin 2021 ``Life, death, and self''
\cite{paper:v6c13-levin-life-death-2021} and Pezzulo and Levin 2015
\cite{paper:v6c13-pezzulo-levin-2015}. The point is to recover the
intellectual content the CERB paper draws from, so that the critique
can distinguish positions the position paper has earned from positions
it has inherited.
\item \textbf{Identifying the engineering questions.} List the
engineering questions an engineer would ask of any intervention
programme at this scale. The chapter has given a starter list:
specification language, formal semantics, verification, catalogue of
input-output pairs, substrate variability, pattern stability,
resolution, time-window control, side effects, reversibility, cancer
risk, immune response, success criteria, trial design, regulatory
pathway, measurement infrastructure, mechanism-output gap. The reader
selects the questions most relevant to the CERB paper's specific
claims.
\item \textbf{Mapping the position paper against the questions.} For
each selected question, state what the position paper says (with a
direct quotation or page reference), what the reader's engineering
critique is, and what evidence would change the critique.
\item \textbf{Drafting.} Write a one-page critique. The page is the
constraint. One-page critiques force the writer to choose: a paragraph
of context, three or four engineering points, a closing position. The
reader is allowed to attach a longer supporting document, but the
critique itself is one page.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Deliverable}. A one-page engineering critique, plus an
optional supporting document of any length. The critique answers, at
minimum:

\begin{itemize}
\item At what intervention level (cell, tissue, organ, morphogenetic)
is CERB's programme positioned?
\item Which of the engineering challenges in Section 13.4 does CERB
address explicitly, which does it address implicitly, which does it
not address?
\item Which of the failure patterns in Section 13.6 is the programme
most at risk of repeating? What protective measure should CERB take?
\item What would the reader fund, change, or stop in the programme if
the reader were the institute's engineering board?
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Success criteria}.

\begin{itemize}
\item The critique cites the CERB paper by section or page, not by
gist.
\item The critique distinguishes the empirical, the conjectural, and
the engineering claims.
\item The critique names at least one specific engineering principle
from Sections 13.1--13.5 against which it evaluates a position-paper
claim.
\item The critique names at least one specific failure pattern from
Section 13.6 as a risk the programme should manage.
\item The critique closes with a stance: what the reader would do as
the institute's engineering board.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Reading list pointers}.

\begin{itemize}
\item Chapter sources, primary: Levin 2014, 2019, 2021, 2023; Pezzulo
and Levin 2015; Mathews and Levin 2017
\cite{paper:v6c13-levin-bioelectric-2014,
paper:v6c13-levin-comp-boundary-2019,
paper:v6c13-levin-life-death-2021,
paper:v6c13-levin-darwin-agential-2023,
paper:v6c13-pezzulo-levin-2015,
paper:v6c13-mathews-levin-2017}.
\item Field reviews: Atala's \textit{Principles of Regenerative
Medicine} (text reference) for current clinical state
\cite{text:v6c13-atala-principles-regen}.
\item Failure cases: Freed 2001 and Olanow 2003 for fetal-graft
control failures \cite{paper:v6c13-freed-fetal-parkinson-2001,
paper:v6c13-olanow-fetal-parkinson-2003}; De Luca 2019 for the
tracheal-reconstruction failures
\cite{paper:v6c13-deluca-tissue-failure}.
\item Regulatory and translational framing: relevant FDA and EMA
guidance documents on cell and gene therapy; the primary reading list
will depend on the specific clinical scope CERB articulates.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{What this project is not}. It is not a literature review of
regenerative medicine. It is not a critique of Levin's published
science. It is not an evaluation of the Piatra Institute as a host. It
is an engineering critique of a single position paper using the
engineering principles this chapter has developed.

\textbf{Self-check}. The reader who writes a critique that could be
written without reading the position paper has not read the position
paper carefully enough. The reader who writes a critique that
contradicts the chapter's named principles has either found a
chapter-level error or has misread the chapter. Either outcome is
worth recording.

\end{project}

\section{Exercises}

\subsection*{Calculation}\pathtag{standard}

\begin{exercise}
A tissue voltage gradient of $30\,\text{mV}/\text{mm}$ is established
across a $500\,\micro\meter$ region of regenerating tissue. Estimate
the potential difference between cells at opposite ends of the region.
Express the answer in millivolts and state whether the difference is
above the typical noise floor of voltage-sensitive-dye measurements
($\pm 5\,\text{mV}$).

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
The potential difference is
\[
\Delta V = (30\,\text{mV}/\text{mm}) \times 0.5\,\text{mm} = 15\,\text{mV}.
\]
The difference is approximately three times the noise floor and is
therefore measurable but not comfortably so. A measurement campaign
should report a confidence interval rather than a point value at this
ratio.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A gap-junction-coupled cell network has a coupling time constant
$\tau = 100\,\text{ms}$ for ion equilibration between adjacent cells.
Estimate the time for a perturbation applied at one end of a
$200\,\micro\meter$-long tissue (with $10\,\micro\meter$ cells) to
propagate as a steady-state shift across the tissue. Assume the
propagation is diffusion-like with one cell-coupling step per $\tau$.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
The number of cells across the tissue is
$N = 200\,\micro\meter / 10\,\micro\meter = 20$ cells. For a
diffusion-like process, the equilibration time scales as $N^2 \tau$:
\[
t \sim N^2 \tau = 400 \times 0.1\,\text{s} = 40\,\text{s}.
\]
This is fast on the time scale of developmental events (hours to
days). The bioelectric pattern can therefore re-establish a steady
state on a time scale much shorter than the morphogenetic outcome it
specifies.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Derivation}\pathtag{standard}

\begin{exercise}
Starting from the Nernst equation for a single permeable ion species,
\[
E_X = \frac{RT}{zF}\ln\frac{[X]_o}{[X]_i},
\]
derive an expression for the resting membrane potential of a cell
permeable to two species (potassium and sodium) using the
Goldman-Hodgkin-Katz equation. State explicitly which species
dominate at typical resting conditions and which dominate at the
peak of an action potential. Discuss the engineering relevance of
this distinction for bioelectric patterning.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
The GHK equation for two species is
\[
V_m = \frac{RT}{F}\ln\frac{P_K [K^+]_o + P_{Na} [Na^+]_o}
{P_K [K^+]_i + P_{Na} [Na^+]_i}.
\]
At rest, $P_K \gg P_{Na}$ in most cells, so $V_m \approx E_K$ (close
to $-70\,\text{mV}$). During an action potential, $P_{Na}$
transiently exceeds $P_K$ and $V_m$ swings toward $E_{Na}$ (around
$+50\,\text{mV}$). The engineering relevance is that bioelectric
patterning manipulations typically operate on the resting potential by
modulating $P_K$ or $P_{Cl}$ (mostly through specific channel agonists
or antagonists or through targeted expression of exogenous channels
such as GlyR). Action potentials and pattern manipulations operate on
different time scales and through different channel populations; an
intervention that disrupts excitability will also disrupt patterning,
which is a side effect a compiler must consider.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Estimation}\pathtag{standard}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the energy budget of maintaining a tissue voltage gradient.
Consider a $1\,\text{mm}^3$ tissue volume with $10^6$ cells, each
maintaining a transmembrane potential of $-60\,\text{mV}$. The
dominant cost is Na$^+$/K$^+$-ATPase pump activity. Estimate the ATP
consumption rate per unit volume.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
A typical mammalian cell consumes around $10^9$ ATP per second under
resting conditions, of which $\sim 20$--$40\%$ supports the
Na$^+$/K$^+$ pump and other ion homeostasis (current as of 2026
standard textbook estimates) \cite{text:v6c13-alberts-mbc}. Take
$3\times 10^8$ ATP/s for ion homeostasis per cell. For $10^6$ cells,
the rate is $3\times 10^{14}$ ATP/s/$\text{mm}^3$. Each ATP
hydrolysis releases $\sim 50\,\text{kJ}/\text{mol}$, or $\sim
8\times 10^{-20}\,\text{J}$ per molecule. The volumetric power
dissipation is
$3\times 10^{14} \times 8\times 10^{-20}\,\text{J}/\text{s}/\text{mm}^3
\approx 2.4 \times 10^{-5}\,\text{W}/\text{mm}^3 = 24\,\text{W}/\text{L}$.

This is comparable to whole-body resting metabolism per volume
(which is around $1\,\text{W}/\text{kg}$ for a $70\,\text{kg}$ human),
suggesting the per-cell estimate is broadly consistent. The energy
budget is not the limiting factor for maintaining a bioelectric
pattern; the limiting factors are pattern stability and
addressability.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the number of distinct bioelectric ``addresses'' a tissue
with $N$ cells can support, given measurement noise of $\pm
5\,\text{mV}$ and a dynamic range of $80\,\text{mV}$ between
maximally hyperpolarised and maximally depolarised cells. Discuss the
implication for a compiler's source-language expressiveness.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
The number of distinguishable voltage levels per cell is
$80\,\text{mV} / 10\,\text{mV} = 8$ (taking $\pm 5\,\text{mV}$ as a
$10\,\text{mV}$ window). For $N$ cells independently set, the address
space size is $8^N$, but spatial correlation due to gap-junction
coupling and biological constraint reduces the effective dimension by
perhaps an order of magnitude. For a 100-cell region, the
unconstrained address space is $8^{100} \approx 10^{90}$; the
effective biological space is much smaller but still vast.

The implication is that bioelectric pattern space is informationally
ample: the compiler is not constrained by address-space size but by
the much harder problem of which addresses correspond to which
anatomical outcomes. The combinatorial-explosion problem is replaced
by a correspondence problem.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Simulation}\pathtag{standard}

\begin{exercise}
Implement a one-dimensional reaction-diffusion model of a tissue
voltage gradient. The state variable is membrane potential $V(x, t)$
on a domain $0 \leq x \leq L$ with diffusive coupling and a local
restoring term: $\partial V/\partial t = D \partial^2 V/\partial x^2
- k(V - V_{\text{rest}})$. Use $D = 10\,\micro\meter^2/\text{s}$,
$k = 0.1\,\text{s}^{-1}$, $V_{\text{rest}} = -60\,\text{mV}$,
$L = 1\,\text{mm}$, and a Dirichlet boundary condition
$V(0, t) = -30\,\text{mV}$ at one end (modelling a local
depolarising intervention). Solve to steady state and plot the
spatial profile. Repeat for $k = 1\,\text{s}^{-1}$ and discuss how
the steady-state spread depends on the dimensionless ratio
$\sqrt{D/k}$.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
The steady-state equation $D V'' - k(V - V_{\text{rest}}) = 0$ has
solution $V(x) = V_{\text{rest}} + (V(0) - V_{\text{rest}})
e^{-x/\lambda}$ with $\lambda = \sqrt{D/k}$. For
$D = 10\,\micro\meter^2/\text{s}$ and $k = 0.1\,\text{s}^{-1}$,
$\lambda = 10\,\micro\meter$, meaning the depolarisation decays
within tens of micrometres. For $k = 1\,\text{s}^{-1}$, $\lambda =
3.2\,\micro\meter$, much sharper. The lesson is that the local
restoring kinetics dominate spatial spread on the time scales of the
intervention, and the bioelectric pattern can be sharper than the
underlying diffusion process would suggest. The code asset
\texttt{voltage\_gradient\_1d.py} implements the numerical solution
and plots both profiles.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Implement a two-dimensional Fisher-KPP wound-healing simulation:
$\partial u/\partial t = D \nabla^2 u + r u (1 - u/K)$ on a square
domain with a circular initial wound (a region with $u = 0$
surrounded by healthy tissue at $u = K$). Use $D =
100\,\micro\meter^2/\text{s}$, $r = 10^{-4}\,\text{s}^{-1}$, $K = 1$,
a domain of $2\,\text{mm}$ side, and an initial wound radius of
$500\,\micro\meter$. Plot the wound radius as a function of time and
compare to the analytical Fisher-KPP wave speed $c = 2\sqrt{Dr}$.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
The Fisher-KPP wave speed is $c = 2\sqrt{Dr} = 2\sqrt{100 \times
10^{-4}}\,\micro\meter/\text{s} = 0.2\,\micro\meter/\text{s}$. The
wound radius shrinks at approximately this rate after the initial
transient. For a $500\,\micro\meter$ initial wound, closure takes
$\sim 500\,\micro\meter / 0.2\,\micro\meter/\text{s} \approx
2500\,\text{s} \approx 40\,\text{min}$, faster than real wound
closure because the model omits many in vivo factors (cell density
constraints, ECM remodelling, immune response). The simulation
illustrates how a simple model captures wave-like spread but
underestimates real time scales. Code asset
\texttt{wound\_healing\_growth.py} implements the simulation.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Design}\pathtag{standard}

\begin{exercise}
Design a measurement system for a hypothetical clinical use of a
bioelectric morphogenetic intervention. The clinical scenario: a
patient has a partial digit-tip amputation, and a bioelectric
intervention (a hydrogel-delivered hyperpolarising agent) is applied
for 14 days. Specify: what is measured, at what time points, with
what instrument, with what accuracy target, and what failure mode
each measurement detects.

\paragraph{Discussion.}
A defensible specification: (1) wound-site voltage by minimally
invasive voltage-sensitive optical sensor at days 0, 1, 3, 7, 14,
21, 28 (target accuracy $\pm 5\,\text{mV}$; detects intervention
delivery failure and pattern instability); (2) tissue thickness and
shape by 3D optical coherence tomography weekly for 12 weeks
(accuracy $\pm 50\,\micro\meter$ in depth; detects morphogenetic
outcome); (3) standard wound healing measures (closure rate, signs
of infection, perfusion) at weekly clinical visits (detects
integration failure); (4) systemic blood markers (CRP, complete blood
count) at weeks 1, 4, 12 (detects immune response); (5) pain and
function assessment at all clinical visits (detects clinical
acceptability). The measurement battery is designed to detect each of
the failure modes in Section 13.4 separately. The discussion may
extend to which of these are tractable now and which require
development.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Design a falsification protocol for the strong form of the anatomical
compiler conjecture. Specify what intervention would be applied, what
outcome would be measured, and what result would falsify the strong
form (a general compiler exists) while leaving the empirical
hypothesis (specific bioelectric interventions produce specific
outcomes) intact.

\paragraph{Discussion.}
A defensible protocol: (1) apply a battery of bioelectric
interventions calibrated against a fixed target morphology in a
model organism (planaria, Xenopus) with established response
profiles, then transfer the same battery to a mammalian regeneration
model (digit-tip in young mice) and a second mammalian model
(cardiac-regeneration window in neonatal mice); (2) score
morphological outcomes against the target with quantitative shape
metrics; (3) the strong form is falsified if no transfer-to-mammalian
effect achieves target-morphology specification (even partially) while
the planaria or Xenopus interventions reproduce baseline; the
empirical hypothesis survives because the species-specific
demonstrations remain intact. The discussion may critique the
protocol's power (is one mammalian species enough?) and propose
extensions.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Design a small-team research roadmap (12 months, 5-person team) that
would advance the anatomical compiler programme by addressing one of
the engineering challenges in Section 13.4. Choose the challenge
explicitly and justify the choice. Specify deliverables, milestones,
and what success would look like at month 12.

\paragraph{Discussion.}
A defensible roadmap might address \emph{verification assays}: build a
high-throughput morphology-scoring pipeline for planarian
regeneration outcomes, deliverables being (a) an automated
image-segmentation tool that quantifies head, tail, eye-spot, and
pharynx morphology, (b) calibration against expert scoring on a
500-animal dataset, (c) deployment for two intervention campaigns.
Success at month 12: the pipeline scores 1000 animals/week at
agreement-with-expert above 95\% on standard morphologies and 80\% on
perturbed morphologies. Team composition: one senior scientist, two
engineers, one bioinformatician, one experimental biologist. The
discussion may compare this with alternative roadmaps (for example,
tooling for voltage measurement at scale).
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Diagnosis}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
An experimental campaign reports that a bioelectric intervention in
planaria produces a two-headed phenotype in 30\% of treated animals.
The remaining 70\% are unaffected or display intermediate phenotypes
(one head and one partial head, no eye spots, asymmetric body axes).
Diagnose the campaign: what are three engineering hypotheses for the
30\% success rate, and what intermediate measurement would
distinguish among them?

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Three hypotheses: (1) animal-to-animal variability in baseline
bioelectric state; (2) variable delivery of the intervention (dose,
timing, exposure); (3) downstream stochasticity in the cascade that
reads the bioelectric perturbation. Distinguishing measurements:
(1) baseline voltage mapping of each animal before intervention
correlates pre-state with post-outcome; (2) post-intervention voltage
mapping correlates intervention-state with outcome; (3) molecular
read-out of downstream targets (Wnt pathway activation, eye-field
gene expression) correlates cascade-state with outcome. The right
intermediate measurement isolates which step of the
specify-translate-execute pipeline is variable.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Judgement}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
A startup proposes a ``skin-regrowth'' product for cosmetic use, with
a press release that describes the technology as ``an anatomical
compiler for skin.'' The product's published evidence consists of an
unpublished press deck citing improved patient satisfaction in a small
open-label series and an in vitro skin-equivalent model showing
``regenerative gene expression.'' State three engineering questions
you would ask the startup's leadership before investing or
recommending the product. State what answer to each question would
change your position.

\paragraph{Discussion.}
Three questions a working engineer should ask: (1) ``What is your
falsifiable specification of success? Show me the pre-registered
primary endpoint, the comparator, and the effect size that counts as
success.'' (2) ``Show me your failure data: how many cases produced
the wrong outcome, what wrong outcomes were observed, and what is the
known mechanism for each?'' (3) ``What is the regulatory pathway, and
what is your evidence that the intervention meets the relevant
agency's criteria?'' Answers that would change position: (1) a
pre-registered double-blind randomised trial with prespecified
endpoints would warrant further interest; (2) honest disclosure of
failure modes, including specific patient outcomes, would warrant
engagement; (3) a clear regulatory pathway with prior agency
interaction would warrant cautious enthusiasm. Press-release-grade
evidence is not engineering-grade evidence; the engineer's job is to
read the claim down to the evidence and refuse to invest the
discipline's vocabulary on the gap.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A clinical-trial protocol proposes to evaluate an
electrical-stimulation device for amputation-stump regeneration based
on the qualitative endpoint ``any radiographic evidence of new bone
or soft-tissue formation at the stump within 12 weeks.'' State why
this endpoint is engineering-inadequate and propose two specific
replacement endpoints.

\paragraph{Discussion.}
The endpoint is inadequate because ``any radiographic evidence'' is
not falsifiable: callus, scar tissue, bone spurs, and small ossicles
all produce radiographic features and none counts as regeneration in
the engineering sense. The endpoint also fails to specify what counts
as failure, so all outcomes are reportable as success. Two replacement
endpoints: (1) quantitative volumetric increase of bone tissue,
measured by serial CT with a specified minimum effect size and
confidence interval; (2) a functional endpoint such as length restored
to within a stated tolerance of the pre-amputation length, with the
missing function (grasp, sensation) measured by a clinical scale.
Both endpoints are pre-specifiable, falsifiable, and clinically
meaningful.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Levin's published programme treats the bioelectric state of the
gap-junction-connected cell network as a candidate for a control
language. A critic argues that this is ``merely a metaphor'' and that
the empirical work is consistent with simpler, non-language
explanations (for example, ``channels gate developmental cascades; the
language framing adds nothing''). State the critic's strongest form of
the argument, then state the strongest engineering reason to keep the
language framing as a research-programme heuristic. State the class of
experimental result that would settle the question.

\paragraph{Discussion.}
The critic's strongest form: every published bioelectric outcome can
be explained by specific channel manipulations triggering specific
developmental cascades, without any need for a higher-level
``language.'' The metaphor adds interpretive load without explanatory
content. The strongest engineering reason to keep the framing: the
language framing predicts the existence of read-write semantics and
positional information, which has guided successful interventional
design (the planarian two-headed result is anticipated by the
framing); a research programme is justified by the interventions it
suggests, not by the metaphor it uses. The class of result that would
settle the question: a demonstration that the same bioelectric
pattern in different cellular contexts produces consistently
predictable anatomical outcomes, indicating context-independent
read-out. Conversely, a demonstration that the outcomes depend
entirely on cell-type-specific cascade access would falsify the
context-independence required for a true control language. As of
2026 neither demonstration has been made at the relevant generality.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Failure analysis}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
Read the abstracts of Freed et al.\ 2001 and Olanow et al.\ 2003 on
fetal-cell grafts for Parkinson's disease
\cite{paper:v6c13-freed-fetal-parkinson-2001,
paper:v6c13-olanow-fetal-parkinson-2003}. Identify three engineering
errors at the level of intervention design (not at the level of
clinical execution). For each error, state what an anatomical
compiler would have to do differently to avoid the same failure.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Three engineering errors at the design level: (1) The intervention
delivered a cell population without delivering the regulatory
architecture in which it normally functions; the graft secreted
dopamine in an open-loop manner because the host did not innervate
it as a normal pathway. A compiler must emit not only the cells but
the integration plan. (2) The intervention's success criterion was
loose, leaving room for over-interpretation in open-label studies
that the later controlled trials had to correct. A compiler programme
must specify outcomes as falsifiable predictions before each clinical
attempt. (3) The clinical population had advanced disease with
already-abnormal host circuitry; a working intervention in a
non-working circuit is a different intervention. A compiler programme
must specify the substrate state required for the intervention to
succeed, and the trial design must enrol substrates that meet that
requirement. Each error has a direct compiler-programme remedy. The
common thread is that the cell-delivery framing was too simple for the
problem the intervention was actually trying to solve.
\end{exercise}
