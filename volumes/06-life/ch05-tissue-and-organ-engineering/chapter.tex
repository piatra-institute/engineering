\noindent
The chapter scales from the cell to the organism. A single cell is
the unit; a tissue is a population of cells organised into a working
collective; an organ is a collection of tissues organised to perform
a body-scale function; an organ system is a network of organs that
collectively maintains a physiological variable. Engineering the
intermediate scales is the subject of tissue engineering. We develop
the hierarchy, the extracellular matrix that holds it together, the
mechanics of tissues, the vascularisation constraint that limits all
tissue construction, the scaffold-and-print methods that the field
uses, the stem-cell and organoid technologies that supply the
cellular material, and the failure modes that haunt the translational
clinic.

\section{The hierarchy: cells, tissues, organs, systems}\pathtag{core}

\engterm{Tissue}. A tissue is a population of cells, generally of one
or a few types, organised in a characteristic pattern and embedded in
an extracellular matrix. Histologists divide tissues into four
classical types: \engterm{epithelial} (sheets of cells covering
surfaces and lining cavities, with apical-basal polarity), \engterm{connective}
(cells dispersed in a substantial extracellular matrix, including
bone, cartilage, tendon, ligament, fat, and the loose connective
tissue between organs), \engterm{muscle} (contractile cells:
skeletal, cardiac, smooth), and \engterm{nervous} (neurons and glial
support cells). The four-type classification dates from the
nineteenth-century histologists and remains the working vocabulary;
modern tissue engineering adds more nuanced subclassifications based
on cell lineage and developmental origin.

\engterm{Organ}. An organ is a structurally bounded assembly of
tissues that performs a defined body-scale function. The human heart
is composed of cardiac muscle (the bulk), epithelial endocardium
(the inner lining), epicardial fibroblasts and adipocytes (the outer
surface), a coronary vasculature (vessels providing oxygen to the
heart muscle itself), and an electrical conduction network of
specialised cardiomyocytes (the sinoatrial node, atrioventricular
node, His-Purkinje system). Engineering an organ-equivalent in vitro
requires reproducing not just the dominant cell type but the entire
tissue mosaic in the appropriate geometry.

\engterm{System}. An organ system is a coordinated set of organs that
maintains a physiological function. The cardiovascular system
(heart, arteries, veins, capillaries) maintains tissue perfusion;
the respiratory system (lungs, airways, diaphragm) maintains gas
exchange; the digestive system (mouth, stomach, intestines, liver,
pancreas) processes food and absorbs nutrients; the urinary system
(kidneys, ureters, bladder, urethra) maintains fluid and electrolyte
balance; the nervous system (brain, spinal cord, peripheral nerves)
coordinates the others (Chapter~\ref{vol06:ch07}). System-level
engineering (organ-on-chip platforms, body-on-chip systems) attempts
to recapitulate inter-organ interactions in miniature culture.

\engterm{Reading the hierarchy as scaling}. The transition from cell
to system spans seven orders of magnitude in length scale (a
$10\,\si{\micro\meter}$ cell to a $1\,\text{m}$ adult human) and
twelve orders of magnitude in mass (a $10^{-12}\,\text{g}$ cell to a
$70\,\text{kg}$ adult). The engineering constraints change at each
scale. Cells are limited by diffusion across the plasma membrane;
tissues by diffusion across the tissue thickness; organs by the
geometry of their internal transport network (vasculature); systems
by inter-organ feedback loops. The volume's scaling archetype
appears in its canonical biological form in this hierarchy: each
scale solves the scaling-up problem of the previous scale by
inventing a new transport mechanism.

\section{The extracellular matrix}\pathtag{standard}

The \engterm{extracellular matrix} (ECM) is the protein-and-polysaccharide
gel that fills the space between cells and gives tissues their
mechanical, biochemical, and signalling properties. The ECM is
synthesised by the tissue's cells and remodelled continuously by the
combination of synthesis, secretion, and degradation by specific
enzyme families (the matrix metalloproteinases).

\engterm{Principal components}. The ECM contains five principal
classes of macromolecule. \engterm{Collagens} (a family of $\sim 28$
homologous proteins, of which type I, II, III, and IV are the most
abundant) form the load-bearing fibrils of connective tissue. Type-I
collagen in bone, tendon, and skin has a tensile strength of
approximately $100\,\text{MPa}$ and a Young's modulus of $1$ to
$2\,\text{GPa}$ along the fibre direction. \engterm{Elastin} forms
the elastic fibres of skin, lung, and arterial walls; elastin can
stretch reversibly to $130\,\%$ of its resting length. \engterm{Proteoglycans}
(a core protein with attached glycosaminoglycan chains) bind water
and provide compressive resistance; aggrecan in cartilage is the
canonical example, with chondroitin sulfate side chains that hold
water at $80\,\%$ of cartilage's mass. \engterm{Glycoproteins}
(fibronectin, laminin, vitronectin) mediate cell-matrix attachment
through specific cell-surface receptors (integrins). \engterm{Hyaluronic
acid} is a large unbranched polysaccharide that contributes to the
viscoelasticity of soft tissues.

\engterm{Specialised matrices}. Bone matrix contains collagen
($\sim 30\,\%$ by mass) plus the mineral hydroxyapatite
(Ca$_5$(PO$_4$)$_3$(OH), $\sim 60\,\%$ by mass) plus water. The
collagen provides toughness; the mineral provides stiffness; the
composite has properties superior to either component alone. Tendon
matrix is approximately $70\,\%$ collagen by dry mass, oriented in
parallel along the load direction. Cartilage matrix has high
proteoglycan content and low cell density, with a Young's modulus
of $0.5$ to $1\,\text{MPa}$ and a friction coefficient below
$0.01$ when lubricated. Skin matrix is a mixture of collagen,
elastin, and ground substance with regional variation in composition
that maps onto regional differences in mechanical demands.

\engterm{Matrix as instruction}. The ECM is not only a structural
filler; it is an active instruction substrate for the cells embedded
in it. Matrix stiffness directly influences cell fate: mesenchymal
stem cells cultured on substrates of stiffness comparable to brain
tissue ($\sim 1\,\text{kPa}$) differentiate toward neurons, those on
muscle-like stiffness ($\sim 10\,\text{kPa}$) toward myocytes, those
on bone-like stiffness ($\sim 30\,\text{kPa}$) toward osteoblasts.
The discovery, by Engler, Sen, Sweeney, and Discher in 2006, was one
of the most surprising in cell biology and has driven a decade of
work on mechanotransduction. Matrix composition similarly signals to
cells through integrin-mediated attachment and through the local
concentration of bound growth factors. The engineer designing a
scaffold for tissue regeneration must specify the matrix's mechanical
properties, composition, and bound-signal profile, not just its
geometry \cite[ch.~24]{text:langer-tissue-engineering}.

\section{Tissue mechanics}\pathtag{core}

Tissues are mechanical objects with stress-strain relations that the
engineer must measure and model. The relations are usually non-linear
(stress-strain curves are not Hookean for most tissues), anisotropic
(properties differ along different directions), viscoelastic (the
response depends on the rate of loading), and history-dependent
(prior loading alters the current response). The mechanics of soft
tissues are the subject of Y.~C. Fung's foundational monograph
\cite{text:fung-biomechanics} and Chapter~\ref{vol06:ch06} will
return to them in the context of biomechanics; the present chapter
develops the constitutive-modelling framework that the engineering
of scaffolds and synthetic tissues requires.

\engterm{Stress and strain}. We use the standard continuum-mechanics
definitions. \engterm{Cauchy stress} $\sigma$ is force per unit
current area; \engterm{Green strain} $E$ is a finite-strain measure
appropriate to large deformations. For small deformations
(strain $\le 5\,\%$), the linearised infinitesimal-strain framework
is adequate; for the larger deformations typical of soft tissues
($> 10\,\%$), finite-strain measures are necessary.

\engterm{Hyperelastic constitutive models}. A common framework for
soft tissues is hyperelasticity: the stress is derived from a
strain-energy density function $W(E)$ such that $\sigma_{ij} =
\partial W/\partial E_{ij}$. For an isotropic tissue, $W$ depends
only on the strain invariants $I_1$, $I_2$, $I_3$. Common forms
include the neo-Hookean model
$W = \frac{\mu}{2}(I_1 - 3)$, the Mooney-Rivlin model
$W = c_1(I_1 - 3) + c_2(I_2 - 3)$, and the Fung exponential form
$W = \frac{1}{2} c (e^{Q} - 1)$ with $Q$ a quadratic form in the
strain components. The Fung model fits a wide range of soft tissues
because the exponential captures the strain-stiffening behaviour
typical of collagen-containing tissues
\cite[ch.~7]{text:fung-biomechanics}. Each model has two to six fitting
parameters that the engineer measures by uniaxial or biaxial tension
tests on the tissue of interest.

\engterm{Viscoelasticity}. Soft tissues dissipate energy when
deformed; the dissipation appears as hysteresis in the loading-unloading
cycle. The standard viscoelastic models (Kelvin-Voigt, Maxwell,
standard linear solid) combine springs and dashpots in different
topologies. The relaxation modulus $G(t)$ of a typical soft tissue
follows a power law $G(t) \sim t^{-\alpha}$ with $\alpha \approx 0.1$
to $0.3$ over many decades in time. The quasi-linear viscoelastic
(QLV) framework of Fung, with a strain-dependent elastic response
and a strain-independent relaxation function, captures most of the
phenomenology.

\engterm{Anisotropy}. Tissues organised by fibres (tendon, ligament,
arterial wall, cardiac muscle) are mechanically anisotropic. The
constitutive models for anisotropic tissues include the Holzapfel-Gasser-Ogden
(HGO) family, which adds collagen fibre contributions to a
neo-Hookean ground substance. The HGO model and its variants are the
working standard for arterial mechanics and have been incorporated
into commercial finite-element codes.

\engterm{Tissue-specific properties}. The principal soft tissues have
characteristic mechanical properties at small strain: brain
$\sim 1\,\text{kPa}$, fat $\sim 5\,\text{kPa}$, muscle (passive)
$\sim 10\,\text{kPa}$, liver $\sim 1$ to $5\,\text{kPa}$, skin
$\sim 100\,\text{kPa}$, cartilage $\sim 500\,\text{kPa}$ to
$1\,\text{MPa}$, tendon $\sim 1\,\text{GPa}$ along the fibre,
cortical bone $\sim 15\,\text{GPa}$. The five orders of magnitude
span reflect the structural composition: protein-rich, water-rich
tissues are soft; collagen-rich tissues are stiff; mineralised
tissues are stiffer still.

\begin{principle}[Tissue mechanics is constitutive, not Hookean]
The mechanical behaviour of a soft tissue is a non-linear, anisotropic,
viscoelastic constitutive relation that the engineer measures and
fits for each tissue type. The Hookean approximation applies only at
small strain; the engineering models (Fung, Mooney-Rivlin, HGO) are
the working standard for tissue mechanics. A scaffold whose stiffness
matches the target tissue in the small-strain regime may still fail
the strain-stiffening or anisotropy requirements at physiological
loads. The engineer specifies the constitutive model, not just one
modulus value.
\end{principle}

\section{Vascularisation as design constraint}\pathtag{core}

The single hardest engineering problem in tissue construction is
vascularisation. A piece of tissue thicker than the diffusion limit
($\sim 200\,\si{\micro\meter}$ for oxygen delivery in metabolically
active tissue) cannot survive without an internal blood supply. The
vasculature must extend through the tissue with sufficient density
that no cell is more than $\sim 100\,\si{\micro\meter}$ from a
capillary, must connect to the host's circulation upon implantation,
must withstand the blood pressure ($\sim 100\,\text{mmHg}$ peak
systolic in the arterial tree), and must maintain anti-thrombotic
function over the lifetime of the implant.

\engterm{The diffusion limit}. The maximum tissue thickness that can
survive on diffusion from one face is set by the steady-state
oxygen-diffusion equation. The oxygen consumption rate per unit
volume in metabolically active tissue is approximately
$10^{-3}\,\text{mol}/(\text{m}^3\cdot\text{s})$. The oxygen
diffusion coefficient in tissue is $D \approx 2 \times 10^{-9}\,
\text{m}^2/\text{s}$. The arterial oxygen partial pressure is
$\sim 100\,\text{mmHg}$, corresponding to a dissolved-oxygen
concentration of $0.13\,\text{mM}$ in plasma. Solving the diffusion
equation for a slab supplied from one face gives a maximum supply
distance of $\sim 100$ to $200\,\si{\micro\meter}$ before the
oxygen concentration falls to zero at the far face. Tissues thicker
than this must be internally vascularised.

\engterm{Strategies}. Tissue engineering uses four principal
strategies to address the vascularisation constraint. \engterm{Thin
constructs} (skin substitutes, corneal grafts) are below the
diffusion limit and do not require internal vasculature.
\engterm{Pre-vascularised constructs} have a microvascular network
seeded into the scaffold in vitro, with the network anastomosing
(connecting) to host vessels after implantation. \engterm{Channel-based
constructs} have pre-formed channels through the scaffold that act
as artificial vessels; the host endothelialises the channels after
implantation. \engterm{Sequential implantation} grows the construct
in a pre-vascularised host site first (a flap or chamber with its
own blood supply) and only then transfers it to the final location;
this strategy is used in several experimental whole-organ approaches.

\engterm{Vascular tree geometry}. The branching ratio and diameter
scaling of a vascular tree are constrained by Murray's law: at every
bifurcation, the cube of the parent vessel diameter equals the sum
of the cubes of the daughter diameters
($d_{\text{parent}}^3 = d_1^3 + d_2^3$). The law minimises the metabolic
cost of maintaining a blood vessel (which scales with vessel volume)
plus the work cost of pumping blood (which scales with the inverse
of $d^4$ by Poiseuille's law). Murray's law fits the observed
branching of mammalian arterial trees to within $\sim 10\,\%$ across
many orders of magnitude in vessel size and is the engineering basis
of scaffold-design strategies that try to recapitulate natural
vasculature \cite[ch.~5]{text:phillips-physical-biology}.

\engterm{Capillary density}. The human body contains approximately
$10^9$ capillaries with a total length of $50\,000$ to $80\,000\,\text{km}$
and a total exchange surface area of $\sim 100\,\text{m}^2$. The
capillary density is tissue-specific: $2000$ to $3000\,\text{mm}^{-2}$
in cardiac muscle and active skeletal muscle, $300$ to
$500\,\text{mm}^{-2}$ in resting muscle, $200$ to
$1000\,\text{mm}^{-2}$ in nervous tissue, and lower in connective
tissue. The reader designing a vascularised scaffold targets the
capillary density of the tissue being replaced.

\section{Scaffolds and 3D-printed tissue}\pathtag{standard}

A \engterm{scaffold} is a three-dimensional structure that supports
the attachment, growth, and organisation of cells into a tissue. The
scaffold provides the geometric template, the mechanical environment,
and the initial signalling context; the cells perform the eventual
tissue construction.

\engterm{Scaffold materials}. Scaffolds are made from a wide range of
materials, each with its trade-offs. \engterm{Natural polymers}
(collagen, fibrin, hyaluronic acid, alginate) provide native cell-binding
signals and degrade through enzymatic remodelling; their batch-to-batch
variability is the main drawback for translational use.
\engterm{Synthetic polymers} (polylactic acid PLA, polyglycolic acid
PGA, their copolymer PLGA, polycaprolactone PCL) offer reproducible
properties and tunable degradation rates of weeks to years;
their drawback is the absence of native binding signals.
\engterm{Ceramics} (hydroxyapatite, tricalcium phosphate, bioactive
glass) are used for bone scaffolds because they bond directly to
mineralising bone. \engterm{Decellularised tissue} (the native ECM
of a tissue with its cells removed by detergent treatment) provides
the most accurate physiological template but requires donor tissue
and adds immunological complexity.

\engterm{Fabrication methods}. Scaffolds are fabricated by a portfolio
of methods. \engterm{Foaming and freeze-drying} produce porous
sponges with random pore architecture; pore sizes are typically
$100$ to $500\,\si{\micro\meter}$, suitable for cell infiltration.
\engterm{Electrospinning} produces non-woven mats of submicrometre
fibres that mimic the topology of native ECM. \engterm{3D printing}
(extrusion-based, ink-jet, stereolithography, two-photon polymerisation)
produces patient-specific geometries with sub-100-micrometre resolution.
\engterm{Bioprinting} extrudes cell-laden hydrogels directly,
producing constructs that are cell-seeded from the start. The choice
of method depends on the required geometric precision, the required
cell density, and the scale of the construct.

\engterm{The state of the field}. Tissue engineering at the time of
writing (current as of 2025) has produced clinical-grade products in
several areas: skin substitutes (Apligraf, Dermagraft), cartilage
implants (autologous chondrocyte implantation), bone-void fillers,
and corneal grafts. Solid organs (kidneys, livers, hearts, lungs)
remain in pre-clinical development; the vascularisation barrier has
limited progress for the past three decades and remains the dominant
engineering bottleneck \cite[ch.~30]{text:langer-tissue-engineering}.

\section{Stem cells and organoids}\pathtag{standard}

A \engterm{stem cell} is a cell that can both self-renew (produce
more stem cells) and differentiate into one or more specialised cell
types. Stem cells supply the cellular material for tissue engineering
and the regenerative capacity of adult tissues.

\engterm{Stem-cell hierarchy}. The hierarchy of stem-cell potency
spans four levels. \engterm{Totipotent} cells (the early embryo, up
to about eight cells) can give rise to a complete organism plus the
extra-embryonic tissues. \engterm{Pluripotent} cells (the inner cell
mass of the blastocyst, plus laboratory-derived embryonic and
induced-pluripotent stem cells) can give rise to all cell types of
the adult body. \engterm{Multipotent} cells (adult stem cells in the
bone marrow, intestinal crypt, hair follicle, etc.) can give rise to
a restricted set of related cell types. \engterm{Unipotent} cells
(spermatogonial stem cells, basal keratinocytes) can give rise to
only one differentiated cell type. The hierarchy maps onto the
tissue-engineering use case: pluripotent cells supply the broadest
range of cell types but require more careful control; multipotent
cells from the relevant tissue supply more lineage-appropriate cells
but require donor-tissue access.

\engterm{Induced pluripotent stem cells (iPSCs)}. Shinya Yamanaka's
2006 demonstration that adult cells could be reprogrammed to a
pluripotent state by the expression of four transcription factors
(Oct4, Sox2, Klf4, c-Myc) transformed the stem-cell field by removing
the need for embryonic tissue. iPSCs are routinely derived from
patient skin or blood cells and differentiated into therapeutic cell
types for autologous transplantation. The Nobel Prize for the work
was awarded in 2012. Clinical applications using iPSC-derived cells
include retinal pigment epithelium for macular degeneration,
cardiomyocytes for heart-failure patches, and dopaminergic neurons
for Parkinson's disease, with the first clinical trials having
begun in the mid-2010s and continuing at the time of writing.

\engterm{Organoids}. An \engterm{organoid} is a self-organised
three-dimensional culture of stem-cell-derived cells that
recapitulates aspects of an organ's structure and function. Intestinal
organoids (Hans Clevers's 2009 demonstration of crypt-villus
structures from single intestinal stem cells), brain organoids
(Madeline Lancaster's 2013 demonstration of cerebral organoids from
iPSCs), kidney organoids, liver organoids, and lung organoids are
all now standard laboratory models. Organoids do not reproduce
full-organ function (they lack vasculature, immune cells, and
appropriate boundary tissues), but they are valuable in disease
modelling, drug screening, and as building blocks for larger tissue
constructs.

\engterm{Engineering control}. The challenge in stem-cell engineering
is controlling the differentiation: directing the cell to the desired
fate while suppressing alternative fates. The protocols are
combinations of soluble factors, ECM signals, mechanical cues, and
timing. Protocols are typically two to four weeks long and produce
populations of $\sim 80\,\%$ to $99\,\%$ purity of the target cell
type, with the remaining cells comprising off-target lineages or
undifferentiated cells. The undifferentiated cells in particular are
a concern for clinical use because of their teratoma-forming
potential; purification by surface-marker sorting before transplantation
is standard in current clinical protocols.

\begin{archetype}[Scaling and interface at the tissue scale]
Tissue engineering exhibits the scaling and interface archetypes in
their canonical clinical-translation forms. The scaling archetype is
the hierarchical assembly from molecular to cellular to tissue to
organ scales, with each transition requiring a new transport
mechanism (diffusion across the membrane, diffusion across the tissue,
flow through the vasculature). The interface archetype is the
necessity of designing every boundary in the construct: the
cell-matrix interface (integrin signalling, mechanotransduction), the
tissue-vasculature interface (endothelial lining, anti-thrombotic
function), the construct-host interface (immune compatibility,
vascular anastomosis, tissue integration). Tissue engineering is the
volume's most explicit application of the two archetypes to a single
engineering programme.
\end{archetype}

\begin{estimation}
\textbf{Question.} Estimate the volume of vasculature required to
adequately perfuse a $5\,\text{cm} \times 5\,\text{cm} \times 5\,\text{cm}$
engineered liver tissue construct.

\smallskip
\textit{Estimate, before reading on.}

\smallskip
The construct volume is $125\,\text{cm}^3$. Native liver has a
capillary density of approximately $500$ to $1000\,\text{mm}^{-2}$
when sectioned (capillaries per square millimetre of tissue
cross-section).

A $1\,\text{mm}^2$ cross-section with $500$ capillaries each of
diameter $8\,\si{\micro\meter}$ has a capillary volume per
millimetre depth of $500 \times \pi \times (4 \times 10^{-3}\,\text{mm})^2
\times 1\,\text{mm} = 2.5 \times 10^{-2}\,\text{mm}^3$. The
capillary volume fraction is $2.5\,\%$ of the tissue volume.

Adding the larger vessels (the construct must be supplied by
arterioles and venules), which collectively constitute another
$5\,\%$ to $10\,\%$ of tissue volume, gives a total vasculature
fraction of approximately $10\,\%$.

For a $125\,\text{cm}^3$ construct, the vasculature volume is
$\sim 12.5\,\text{cm}^3$. With an additional $1$ to $2\,\text{cm}^3$
for the inlet and outlet vessels, the total vasculature requirement
is approximately $15\,\text{cm}^3$ or $12\,\%$ of the construct.

\smallskip
\textit{Postmortem.} The estimate is consistent with the observed
vasculature volume fraction in native liver tissue ($\sim 10\,\%$,
including hepatic sinusoids and the larger portal-venous and arterial
vessels). The number is large: a tenth of the construct's volume must
be allocated to non-cellular vascular space. The implication is that
engineering a fully cellular tissue construct of liver volume is not
possible without internal vasculature, and that any scaffold design
must reserve $10\,\%$ of the volume for vessels before the cellular
component can be sized. The estimate is the working number for
scaffold design budgets.
\end{estimation}

\section{Failure: rejection, vascularisation failure, tissue death}\pathtag{core}

Tissue-engineering constructs fail by three principal mechanism
classes, each well-characterised in translational experience.

\engterm{Immune rejection}. An implanted tissue construct that does
not match the recipient's HLA (human leukocyte antigen) type elicits
an immune response from the recipient's T-cells. The response
recognises foreign HLA molecules on the donor cells and produces
either a hyperacute rejection (within minutes to hours, mediated by
pre-existing antibodies) or an acute or chronic rejection over days
to months. Strategies to reduce rejection include HLA matching (the
basis of solid-organ transplant compatibility), immunosuppression
(life-long for solid-organ recipients), autologous cell sourcing
(iPSC-derived cells from the patient), and immune-cloaked
constructs (genetically engineered cells with reduced HLA expression).
The immune-rejection failure mode constrains tissue engineering to a
working set of choices: autologous-source constructs avoid the
problem but require months of cell preparation per patient; allogeneic
constructs require either HLA matching or immunosuppression. Chapter~\ref{vol06:ch12}
on biocompatibility develops the full biological-response
engineering required for medical devices, including tissue
constructs.

\engterm{Vascularisation failure}. The implanted construct that fails
to vascularise within days of implantation suffers core necrosis. The
construct's inner cells, beyond the diffusion limit from the surface,
die from oxygen and nutrient starvation before the host vasculature
can grow into them. The dying cells release damage-associated
molecular patterns that recruit immune cells, accelerate tissue
breakdown, and may compromise the surviving outer shell. The
failure mode is the primary reason that solid-organ tissue engineering
has stalled at the centimetre scale for the past three decades.

\engterm{Tissue death and remodelling failure}. Even a successfully
vascularised construct may fail to integrate into a working tissue
because the cells do not survive their first weeks in vivo, do not
remodel the scaffold into a native tissue, or do not maintain their
differentiated state. The mechanisms include inflammatory injury
from the foreign scaffold, mechanical stress that the construct is
not engineered to bear, and gradual loss of cellular phenotype
(dedifferentiation) as the cells adapt to the implant environment.
Long-term clinical follow-up data on tissue-engineered cartilage
implants and bone-substitute scaffolds show that a substantial
fraction of constructs survive at $10$-year follow-up but with
remodelled rather than native tissue properties.

\engterm{Engineering responses}. Each failure mode has well-characterised
engineering responses: HLA matching or autologous sourcing for
rejection, pre-vascularisation or microchannel design for
vascularisation, scaffold stiffness matching and bioactive coating
for tissue integration. The engineering of tissue constructs is, in
practice, an ongoing process of preempting these three failure modes
across the construct's geometry, materials, cell source, and
implantation protocol.

\section{Project}\pathtag{core}

\begin{project}[Design a vascularised scaffold and defend the geometry]
The reader designs a vascularised scaffold for a specified
tissue-engineering target and produces a written defence of the
chosen geometry, materials, and vascularisation strategy.

\textbf{Track}. Design study (paper exercise, no fabrication required).
\textbf{Hazard class}: none.

\textbf{Target}. The reader selects one of three target tissues:
(a) a $2\,\text{cm} \times 2\,\text{cm} \times 0.5\,\text{cm}$ skin
substitute for a chronic non-healing wound; (b) a
$4\,\text{cm} \times 4\,\text{cm} \times 1\,\text{cm}$ cartilage
construct for a knee joint repair; (c) a
$5\,\text{cm} \times 5\,\text{cm} \times 2\,\text{cm}$ liver-tissue
patch for partial hepatic regeneration after surgical resection.

\textbf{Procedure}.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
\item Specify the target tissue's mechanical properties (Young's
  modulus, strain to failure), cellular composition, and vasculature
  requirement.
\item Choose a scaffold material and defend the choice on the basis
  of degradation rate, mechanical match, and cell-attachment
  signals.
\item Design the macroscopic geometry, including any internal
  channels for vascularisation. Specify channel diameters, spacing,
  and the strategy for connecting to host circulation.
\item Specify the cell source (autologous, allogeneic, iPSC-derived)
  and the seeding density.
\item Identify the three most likely failure modes and the engineering
  responses you have built in.
\item Estimate the cost per construct (rough order-of-magnitude) and
  the time from patient sample to implantation.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Deliverable}. A ten- to fifteen-page report with one geometric
diagram, one materials table, one failure-mode analysis table, and a
closing paragraph naming the one engineering uncertainty most likely
to limit the design.

\textbf{Time}. Eight to twelve hours.
\end{project}

\section{Exercises}

\subsection*{Calculation}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
Compute the maximum thickness of a metabolically active tissue
($\dot{q} = 10^{-3}\,\text{mol O}_2/(\text{m}^3\cdot\text{s})$)
supplied by diffusion from one face, with $D_{\text{O}_2} = 2 \times
10^{-9}\,\text{m}^2/\text{s}$ and $[\text{O}_2]_{\text{surface}} =
0.13\,\text{mM}$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Murray's law predicts that $d_{\text{parent}}^3 = d_1^3 + d_2^3$ at
a vascular bifurcation. For a symmetric bifurcation where
$d_1 = d_2$, compute the ratio $d_{\text{daughter}}/d_{\text{parent}}$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A $1\,\text{cm}^3$ tissue construct has a vasculature volume
fraction of $10\,\%$ and a capillary density of $500\,\text{mm}^{-2}$.
Compute the total capillary length in the construct, assuming
$8\,\si{\micro\meter}$ capillary diameter.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Tendon has a Young's modulus of $\sim 1\,\text{GPa}$ along the
fibre and breaks at a tensile stress of $\sim 100\,\text{MPa}$.
Compute the strain at failure and compare to skin (modulus $\sim 0.1\,\text{MPa}$
at small strain, failure at strain $\sim 0.5$).
