\noindent
Biomanufacturing is the engineering of a living production system. A
chemical plant asks molecules to react; a bioprocess asks cells to grow,
maintain themselves, express the product, survive the stress imposed by
the product, and remain genetically stable long enough for the run to be
worth keeping. The reader who finishes this chapter can read a
bioreactor as a balance around biomass, substrate, oxygen, heat, and
product; can name the scale-up variables that do not travel cleanly from
flask to factory; can distinguish microbial, mammalian, and plant
production systems; and can explain why the downstream purification
train often costs more attention than the reactor itself.

\section{Bioreactor types}\pathtag{core}

A \engterm{bioreactor} is a bounded vessel or device in which living
cells, enzymes, or tissues perform a controlled biochemical
transformation. The boundary matters. Feed enters; product, waste, heat,
and gas leave; cells remain, leave, or are recycled depending on the
process. The engineer's first task is to draw that boundary and state
which quantities cross it.

\engterm{Batch reactors}. In a batch culture, the vessel is charged
with medium and inoculum, then run until a harvest condition is reached.
No fresh medium enters during the main production phase. The batch is
simple, inspectable, and easy to isolate from contamination. It is also
finite: the substrate is consumed, inhibitory products accumulate, and
the cells pass through lag, exponential, stationary, and decline phases.
Batch operation fits products tied to a finite growth cycle, processes
where contamination risk dominates, and products whose purification is
organised around one harvest at a time \cite[ch.~3]{text:doran-bioprocess-engineering}.

\engterm{Fed-batch reactors}. Fed-batch operation adds feed during the
run while withholding continuous harvest. The feed rate becomes a
control variable. It can limit substrate to avoid overflow metabolism,
extend the production phase, hold the cells away from inhibitory
concentrations, and tune the growth rate. Fed-batch is the default
industrial compromise for many microbial and mammalian processes:
more controllable than batch, less operationally exposed than continuous
culture \cite[ch.~12]{text:stanbury-fermentation}.

\engterm{Continuous reactors}. In a continuous stirred-tank bioreactor,
fresh medium enters at volumetric flow rate $F$ and culture leaves at
the same rate. The working volume $V$ is constant, and the
\engterm{dilution rate} is
\[
D = \frac{F}{V}.
\]
At steady state, the cells grow at the dilution rate. A chemostat uses
this fact to make growth rate a setpoint. It is a beautiful machine for
studying physiology and evolution; it is a demanding production machine
because contamination, mutation, and drift have indefinite time to act.

\engterm{Perfusion reactors}. In perfusion culture, fresh medium is
fed continuously and product-containing spent medium is removed while
cells are retained by a filter, settler, centrifuge, or membrane. The
cell density can rise far above ordinary batch culture. Perfusion fits
fragile high-value products, including some mammalian-cell products,
where keeping cells in a productive state beats repeated batch start-up.
The price is a more complicated boundary: medium, product, cells, and
waste no longer share the same residence time.

\engterm{Stirred tanks, air-lift reactors, packed beds, and
photobioreactors}. The tank geometry is not decorative. A stirred tank
uses impellers and baffles to mix liquid and disperse gas. An air-lift
reactor uses gas injection and density differences to circulate the
broth with lower shear. A packed bed immobilises cells or enzymes on a
solid support and passes medium through the voids. A photobioreactor
adds light as an input and makes surface area, shading, and heat removal
central design constraints. The vessel type is chosen from the transport
problem, not from a catalogue preference \cite[ch.~8]{text:shuler-kargi-bioprocess}.

\engterm{Single-use systems}. Many modern bioprocesses use disposable
bags, tubing assemblies, filters, and sensors for development and some
production scales. Single-use systems reduce cleaning validation burden
and speed changeover, but they introduce extractables, leachables,
plastic-film oxygen transfer limits, waste handling, and supplier
dependence. They are engineering tradeoffs, not a general upgrade.

\begin{archetype}[Balance: the living phase is inside the account]
The biological phase is not a catalyst drawn as a symbol above the
reaction arrow. It is part of the mass, energy, information, and risk
account. Biomass consumes carbon, nitrogen, oxygen, phosphate, trace
metals, and operator attention. It produces heat, carbon dioxide, acid
or base load, secreted product, intracellular product, extracellular
polymer, and variants of itself. A reactor balance that leaves the
living phase implicit has already lost the process.
\end{archetype}

\section{Mass and heat balances in bioreactors}\pathtag{core}

The generic bioreactor balance is the same balance used throughout this
book:
\[
\frac{d(\text{amount in vessel})}{dt}
= \text{in} - \text{out} + \text{generation} - \text{consumption}.
\]
The difference is that the generation and consumption terms are produced
by a living population whose state changes during the run.

\subsection{Growth kinetics}

Let $X$ be biomass concentration, $S$ substrate concentration, and $P$
product concentration. A common first model for microbial growth uses
Monod kinetics,
\[
\mu(S) = \mu_{\max}\frac{S}{K_S + S},
\]
where $\mu$ is the specific growth rate, $\mu_{\max}$ is the maximum
specific growth rate, and $K_S$ is the substrate concentration at which
$\mu = \mu_{\max}/2$ \cite{paper:monod1949-growth}. The form is the
same saturating shape as Michaelis-Menten enzyme kinetics
\cite{paper:michaelis-menten1913}. The interpretation differs: a whole
cell grows, regulates, and reallocates resources, so $K_S$ is an
empirical parameter, not a single enzyme constant.

For a well-mixed batch culture with one limiting substrate,
\begin{align*}
\frac{dX}{dt} &= \mu(S)X, \\
\frac{dS}{dt} &= -\frac{1}{Y_{X/S}}\mu(S)X - m_S X, \\
\frac{dP}{dt} &= q_P X.
\end{align*}
Here $Y_{X/S}$ is the biomass yield on substrate, $m_S$ is a
maintenance substrate demand, and $q_P$ is a specific product-formation
rate. The equations are already an engineering judgement. They assume
one limiting substrate, no inhibition, homogeneous mixing, no cell
death, and a product rate independent of growth phase. Those assumptions
are acceptable for a first calculation and dangerous as a release
specification.

\subsection{Continuous culture and washout}

For a chemostat with feed substrate concentration $S_f$ and dilution
rate $D$, the balances become
\begin{align*}
\frac{dX}{dt} &= \left(\mu(S) - D\right)X, \\
\frac{dS}{dt} &= D(S_f - S) - \frac{1}{Y_{X/S}}\mu(S)X.
\end{align*}
At nonzero steady state, $\mu(S^\ast)=D$. The reactor turns growth
rate into a knob. Increase $D$, and the culture must grow faster to
remain in the vessel. If $D$ exceeds the maximum growth rate available
at the feed and operating conditions, biomass washes out and the reactor
becomes a sterile pipe.

The washout condition is not a mathematical curiosity. It is the
operating limit of continuous culture. A shift in pH, temperature,
inhibitor concentration, phage burden, or trace nutrient can lower
$\mu_{\max}$ enough that a dilution rate that was safe yesterday
becomes washout today. The operator sees the failure first as falling
optical density, falling oxygen uptake, and product loss.

\subsection{Yield accounting}

A yield is a ratio that only makes sense with its basis named. Biomass
yield on substrate, $Y_{X/S}$, has units such as
$\text{g biomass}/\text{g glucose}$. Product yield on substrate,
$Y_{P/S}$, has units such as $\text{g product}/\text{g glucose}$.
Productivity is a rate, often $\text{g}/(\text{L}\cdot\text{h})$.
Titer is a concentration, often $\text{g}/\text{L}$. A high titer can
come with poor productivity if the run takes too long. A high
productivity can come with poor final titer if the product is harvested
too dilute. A high yield can still be uneconomic if downstream recovery
destroys the product.

The reactor calculation therefore carries at least four quantities:
titer, rate, yield, and recovery. A process that reports only one of
them is hiding the tradeoff.

\subsection{Heat balance}

Cells dissipate heat. Aerobic growth turns reduced carbon into biomass,
CO$_2$, water, product, and heat. Anaerobic fermentation releases less
heat per mole of substrate because more chemical free energy remains in
the product. In either case, a reactor that cannot remove heat cannot
hold its biological setpoint.

A simple heat balance for a well-mixed jacketed reactor is
\[
\rho C_p V \frac{dT}{dt}
= \dot{Q}_{\text{met}} + \dot{Q}_{\text{agitation}}
- U A (T - T_c) - F\rho C_p (T - T_f),
\]
where $T$ is broth temperature, $T_c$ jacket coolant temperature, $T_f$
feed temperature, $U A$ the heat-transfer conductance, and
$\dot{Q}_{\text{met}}$ the metabolic heat release. The agitation term
matters at large scale: impeller power eventually becomes heat in the
broth.

\begin{estimation}
\textbf{A $1000\,\text{L}$ aerobic microbial reactor consumes oxygen
at $0.10\,\text{mol}/(\text{L}\cdot\text{h})$. Estimate the metabolic
heat that must be removed.}

\smallskip
\textit{Estimate, before reading on.}

\smallskip
The oxygen uptake is $100\,\text{mol}/\text{h}$ for the whole vessel.
One mole of O$_2$ is $32\,\text{g}$, so this is
$3.2\,\text{kg}/\text{h}$ of oxygen. Oxidising reduced carbon releases
about $14\,\text{MJ}$ per kilogram of O$_2$ consumed, at order of
magnitude. The heat load is therefore
\[
3.2\,\frac{\text{kg O}_2}{\text{h}}
\times 14\,\frac{\text{MJ}}{\text{kg O}_2}
\approx 45\,\text{MJ}/\text{h}
\approx 12.5\,\text{kW}.
\]
A household kettle is roughly $2\,\text{kW}$. The reactor is carrying
the heat load of several kettles continuously. At pilot scale this is
already a design constraint; at production scale it is a dominant one.
\end{estimation}

\section{Aeration, mixing, scale-up}\pathtag{core}

Aerobic biomanufacturing is usually limited by oxygen transfer before it
is limited by the solubility of glucose. Oxygen is poorly soluble in
water. A fast microbial culture can consume the dissolved oxygen in a
broth in seconds if gas transfer stops. The reactor therefore has to
move oxygen from bubbles into liquid and then from liquid to the cells
fast enough to meet demand.

\subsection{Oxygen-transfer balance}

The standard engineering expression is
\[
\text{OTR} = k_L a (C^\ast - C_L),
\]
where OTR is the oxygen-transfer rate, $k_L$ is a liquid-film
mass-transfer coefficient, $a$ is interfacial area per volume,
$C^\ast$ is the saturation concentration at the gas composition and
pressure, and $C_L$ is the dissolved oxygen concentration in the liquid.
The oxygen uptake rate is
\[
\text{OUR} = q_{O_2} X,
\]
with $q_{O_2}$ the specific oxygen-uptake rate. The oxygen setpoint is
possible only when OTR can exceed OUR with margin.

The symbols hide the engineering work. Increasing aeration raises gas
flow and often $C^\ast$; increasing agitation breaks bubbles and raises
$a$; raising pressure raises oxygen solubility; adding pure oxygen
raises the driving force. Each move has a cost: foaming, shear,
compressor power, heat, cell damage, and regulatory complexity.

\subsection{Mixing time and gradients}

Laboratory reactors are often close to homogeneous. Large reactors are
not. A $2\,\text{L}$ stirred tank can mix in seconds; a
$100\,\text{m}^3$ vessel may contain substrate-rich zones near the feed,
oxygen-poor zones far from spargers, pH gradients around base addition,
and thermal gradients around jackets or coils. Cells that circulate
through those zones experience a periodic environment. The process
model that uses one $S(t)$, one $C_L(t)$, and one pH is an average over
that circulation.

Scale-up therefore asks which average must be preserved. Constant
geometric similarity preserves shape. Constant power per volume
preserves one measure of mixing intensity. Constant tip speed protects
shear-sensitive cells. Constant $k_L a$ protects oxygen transfer.
Constant mixing time protects homogeneity. The list cannot be satisfied
simultaneously across a large scale change \cite[ch.~10]{text:doran-bioprocess-engineering}.

\begin{principle}[Scale-up preserves a chosen failure margin]
A scale-up rule is a decision about which failure mode receives the
largest margin. Constant $k_L a$ says oxygen limitation is the feared
failure. Constant tip speed says shear damage is the feared failure.
Constant power per volume says mixing and suspension are the feared
failure. The engineer states the feared failure explicitly, then accepts
the secondary consequences.
\end{principle}

\subsection{Foam, shear, and gas handling}

Gas makes foam. Proteins, surfactants, cell debris, and extracellular
polymer stabilise the foam. Foam carries cells and product into filters,
vents, and exhaust lines, where it becomes contamination risk and
product loss. Antifoam reduces foam but can reduce oxygen transfer and
complicate downstream purification. A foam probe and antifoam pump are
therefore part of the mass-transfer design, not an accessory.

Shear is equally context-dependent. Bacteria tolerate high agitation.
Filamentous fungi change morphology under shear. Mammalian cells are
larger, membrane-bound, and more shear-sensitive, especially at gas-liquid
interfaces. Protecting mammalian cells often means lower agitation,
gentler sparging, microcarriers, cell-retention devices, or perfusion
formats. The reactor is chosen around the cell's mechanical tolerance.

\section{Sterile process design}\pathtag{standard}

Sterility in biomanufacturing is a process property. It is not a
sentence written after a vessel has been cleaned. A sterile process is
designed so that every material entering the boundary has a defined
sterilisation route, every connection has a defined aseptic procedure,
and every excursion has a defined response.

\subsection{The sterile boundary}

The sterile boundary includes the reactor vessel, inlet filters,
medium lines, gas lines after sterilising filters, harvest lines,
sampling ports, probes, seals, valves, and any disposable assemblies.
The boundary is drawn before the run. If a sampling operation crosses
the boundary, the sampling operation is part of the process design.

Steam-in-place and clean-in-place systems sterilise or clean hard-piped
equipment. Disposable bags and tubing sets arrive gamma-irradiated or
otherwise sterilised by the supplier, then are connected by sterile
welds, aseptic connectors, or closed manifolds. Neither route removes
the need for procedure. A sterile component handled through an open
connection in an uncontrolled environment is no longer part of a sterile
process.

\subsection{Kill kinetics and validation}

Microbial death under heat treatment is commonly approximated as
first-order on a logarithmic scale. If $D$ is the decimal reduction
time, then
\[
N(t) = N_0 10^{-t/D}.
\]
A six-log reduction reduces $10^6$ organisms to an expected count of
one. A twelve-log reduction reduces $10^{12}$ to one, in expectation.
The calculation is not a guarantee that the reactor is sterile. It is
the quantitative core of a sterilisation design that also requires cold
spot identification, biological indicators, material compatibility, and
validated hold times \cite[ch.~7]{text:stanbury-fermentation}.

\subsection{GMP as engineering discipline}

Good manufacturing practice is often taught as paperwork. The better
engineering reading is that GMP is a memory and control system for
processes whose failures may not be visible at the end of the run. Batch
records, change control, deviation reports, validated cleaning, operator
training, and material traceability exist because a contaminated or
misprocessed biological product may look normal while carrying a hidden
hazard. The quality system is a control loop around a process whose
state cannot be fully observed in real time.

This chapter does not train the reader as a GMP operator. It gives the
engineering reason for the discipline: in a biological process, the
product's history is part of the product.

\section{Cell-culture vs microbial vs plant systems}\pathtag{standard}

The host is a manufacturing platform. The choice among bacteria, yeast,
filamentous fungi, mammalian cells, plant cells, plants, insects, and
cell-free systems is a process decision as much as a biological one.

\engterm{Bacterial systems}. \emph{E.\ coli} grows quickly, reaches high
cell densities, is genetically tractable, and is cheap to feed. It is a
strong platform for enzymes, peptides, non-glycosylated proteins,
metabolites, and pathway-engineered small molecules. Its weaknesses are
equally engineering-relevant: inclusion bodies, endotoxin, limited
post-translational modification, codon and folding problems, and strong
selection against burdensome products.

\engterm{Yeast and fungi}. \emph{Saccharomyces cerevisiae},
\emph{Pichia pastoris}, and filamentous fungi sit between bacteria and
mammalian cells. They are eukaryotic, tolerate industrial fermentation,
secrete many products, and can perform some post-translational
processing. They also bring glycosylation patterns that may differ from
human patterns, morphology problems in filamentous growth, and oxygen
and mixing demands that can dominate scale-up.

\engterm{Mammalian cells}. CHO and related mammalian-cell platforms are
used for many therapeutic proteins because they fold, assemble, and
glycosylate proteins in ways acceptable for human therapy. They grow
slowly, require expensive media, are shear-sensitive, and demand tight
control of metabolites such as lactate and ammonia. A mammalian-cell
process is often justified by product quality rather than by ease of
manufacture.

\engterm{Plant and algal systems}. Plant cells, whole plants, and algae
offer photosynthetic or plant-specific production routes, large surface
area, and some low-cost cultivation possibilities. Their engineering
constraints are light delivery, slow growth, seasonal or batch
variability, containment, extraction, and product heterogeneity. A
photobioreactor is a light-transfer device before it is a tank.

\engterm{Cell-free systems}. A cell-free system uses extracted or
reconstituted biochemical machinery outside a living cell. It removes
the burden of keeping a whole organism alive and can simplify toxic
products or rapid prototyping. It also replaces cellular maintenance
with expensive reagents, short reaction lifetimes, and a different
scale-up problem. Cell-free synthesis is best read as a specialised
manufacturing route, not as a universal replacement for cells.

\section{Downstream processing}\pathtag{standard}

The reactor makes a broth, not a finished product. Downstream processing
turns that broth into material with a defined identity, purity, potency,
stability, and container. The train may include cell removal, cell
disruption, clarification, capture chromatography, precipitation,
filtration, polishing chromatography, viral clearance, concentration,
buffer exchange, formulation, sterile filtration, filling, inspection,
and storage \cite[ch.~14]{text:shuler-kargi-bioprocess}.

\subsection{Intracellular and extracellular product}

An extracellular product leaves the cell and appears in the medium. The
first separation removes cells from liquid. An intracellular product
requires harvesting cells and breaking them open, which releases DNA,
host-cell proteins, lipids, endotoxin in Gram-negative bacteria, and
proteases. The difference changes the whole plant. A secreted product
can be dilute but cleaner. An intracellular product can be concentrated
but dirty.

\subsection{Yield through the train}

Downstream recovery is multiplicative. A train with five steps at 90\%
yield each has total yield
\[
0.9^5 = 0.59.
\]
At 80\% each, it has
\[
0.8^5 = 0.33.
\]
That arithmetic is why an apparently small improvement in a capture
step can be more valuable than a larger increase in reactor titer. The
process engineer asks where each gram is lost, and whether the lost gram
is product, impurity, or material that protects the product from damage.

\subsection{Purity, potency, and comparability}

For a therapeutic protein, the product is not only the amino-acid
sequence. Glycosylation, folding state, aggregation, clipping, oxidation,
host-cell protein profile, DNA carryover, endotoxin, and formulation
state can affect safety and efficacy. The process defines the product.
Changing the host, media, reactor scale, purification resin, hold time,
or formulation can change the product enough that comparability becomes
an engineering and regulatory question.

\section{Worked example: insulin manufacture}\pathtag{standard}

Recombinant human insulin is a useful worked example because it connects
the molecular design of Chapter~\ref{vol06:ch09} to the process design
of this chapter. Early recombinant insulin work expressed chemically
synthesised human-insulin genes in \emph{E.\ coli}
\cite{paper:goeddel1979-insulin}. Modern processes vary by producer and
product, but the engineering structure is stable: construct a production
strain, grow it under controlled conditions, express an insulin
precursor or chain product, recover and purify the protein, process it
to the active form, polish impurities, formulate, and fill.

We will not claim a current commercial process. We will design a toy
process whose numbers are explicit and reviewable.

\subsection{A toy mass balance}

Suppose the target is $1.0\,\text{kg}$ of purified insulin-equivalent
product. Assume:
\[
\text{final recovery from culture to purified product} = 0.40,
\]
and an achieved product titer in culture of
\[
1.0\,\text{g}/\text{L}.
\]
The required culture volume is
\[
V = \frac{1000\,\text{g}}{0.40 \times 1.0\,\text{g}/\text{L}}
= 2500\,\text{L}.
\]
If the plant uses $500\,\text{L}$ production batches, five successful
batches are required. If the batch rejection rate is 10\%, the expected
number of batches attempted is $5/0.9 \approx 5.6$. The half-batch in
that arithmetic is not a real batch; it is the planning signal that a
campaign of five batches has poor margin.

Now include downstream yield. Suppose the train has four major steps:
cell harvest or clarification, capture, conversion or refolding, and
polishing/formulation. If their yields are 0.90, 0.80, 0.75, and 0.75,
then total yield is
\[
0.90 \times 0.80 \times 0.75 \times 0.75 = 0.405.
\]
The assumed 40\% recovery is therefore not a decorative parameter. It is
the product of four specific engineering performances.

\subsection{What the example teaches}

The reactor titer matters, but the process does not end at harvest.
Double the titer and hold downstream recovery fixed, and the required
culture volume halves. Improve the weakest downstream step from 75\% to
90\%, and total recovery rises from 40.5\% to 48.6\%, a 20\% gain in
purified output at the same reactor titer. Reduce batch rejection from
10\% to 2\%, and campaign planning changes again. The process optimum
is distributed across strain, reactor, purification train, quality
system, and scheduling.

The example also shows why biomanufacturing belongs in Volume VI rather
than only in Volume VIII or XI. The machine is the cell plus the vessel
plus the purification train plus the quality system. The living phase is
part of the machine.

\section{Failure: contamination, scale-up loss, batch failures}\pathtag{core}

Biomanufacturing fails in ways that look familiar from the rest of
engineering, and in ways specific to living systems.

\engterm{Contamination}. A contaminating organism can consume substrate,
kill the production organism, release proteases, change pH, produce
toxins, or quietly introduce an impurity profile that appears only in
late analytics. In microbial production, a phage can collapse a culture
rapidly. In mammalian culture, mycoplasma or adventitious virus can
threaten a cell bank or production campaign. The response is layered:
cell-bank qualification, raw-material control, sterile boundary design,
environmental monitoring, in-process analytics, and a deviation system
that treats anomalies as information.

\engterm{Scale-up loss}. A process that works in a shake flask may fail
in a stirred tank because oxygen transfer, mixing time, pH control,
foam, heat removal, or shear changed. The laboratory run may have had
one environment; the production cell sees a loop through zones. The
symptom is often lower titer, altered product quality, or unstable
metabolism rather than an obvious mechanical failure.

\engterm{Genetic and phenotypic drift}. A production strain is under
selection. If product formation burdens growth, non-producing or
low-producing variants can outgrow the desired population. Plasmids can
be lost, promoters can mutate, copy number can shift, epigenetic or
phenotypic states can change, and cell-line productivity can decay. The
engineering countermeasure is to define the cell bank, passage number,
selection pressure, genetic stability test, and end-of-production
criterion.

\engterm{Batch rejection}. A batch can be rejected even if the reactor
looked healthy. A product-quality attribute may sit outside
specification; a filter-integrity test may fail; a temperature excursion
may invalidate a hold; a record may reveal that the material history is
not reconstructable. The rejection is expensive because the process has
already consumed media, vessel time, operator time, quality-control
capacity, and downstream resources. A rejected biological batch is a
material loss and an information loss: the plant must learn whether the
failure was a one-off excursion or a sign that the process model is
wrong.

\begin{principle}[The batch record is part of the product]
In biomanufacturing, a vial is acceptable only if its material history
is acceptable. The batch record, environmental record, cell-bank record,
deviation record, and analytical record are therefore part of the
engineered object. A product whose history cannot be reconstructed is
not a product the engineer can release.
\end{principle}

\section{Project}\pathtag{core}

\begin{project}[Ferment a small batch and close the mass balance]
The reader performs a household-scale fermentation and treats it as a
bioreactor.

\textbf{Track}. Household fermentation plus analysis.
\textbf{Hazard class}: low. Use a standard food-safe recipe. Use clean
glassware or food-grade containers. Do not seal a gas-producing
fermentation in a rigid closed vessel. Discard the product if there is
visible mould, putrid odour, unexpected colour, gas pressure in a sealed
container, or any doubt about safety.

\textbf{Allowed systems}. Yogurt, sauerkraut, kimchi, sourdough starter,
or a sugar-yeast CO$_2$ fermentation whose product is not consumed.

\textbf{Specification}.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
\item Draw the process boundary. State every input mass: water, milk,
  cabbage, salt, sugar, starter, inoculum, and container tare.
\item Record temperature, time, pH if available, visible gas formation,
  mass loss if measurable, and qualitative state at least once per day.
\item Write a mass balance for the run. For a CO$_2$-producing
  fermentation, estimate the CO$_2$ produced from the mass loss or from
  stoichiometry. For yogurt or lactic fermentation, estimate acid
  production from pH or from literature values and state the uncertainty.
\item Identify the limiting substrate, expected product, expected waste,
  and principal contamination routes.
\item Compare the observed time course to a simple growth or conversion
  model. State which parameter you would need to measure to improve the
  model.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Deliverable}. A 6- to 10-page engineering notebook report with
the boundary drawing, raw data table, mass balance, uncertainty
estimate, failure-mode discussion, and a one-page reflection on which
parts of the living process were observable and which were hidden.
\end{project}

\section{Exercises}

\subsection*{Calculation}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
A batch culture starts with $S_0 = 20\,\text{g}/\text{L}$ glucose and
has biomass yield $Y_{X/S} = 0.45\,\text{g}/\text{g}$. Ignore
maintenance and product formation. Estimate the maximum biomass
concentration increase.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A chemostat has volume $2.0\,\text{L}$ and feed flow
$0.60\,\text{L}/\text{h}$. Compute the dilution rate. If
$\mu_{\max}=0.50\,\text{h}^{-1}$, is washout possible at saturating
substrate?
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
For Monod parameters $\mu_{\max}=0.8\,\text{h}^{-1}$ and
$K_S=0.10\,\text{g}/\text{L}$, compute the growth rate at
$S=0.05$, $0.10$, $1.0$, and $10\,\text{g}/\text{L}$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A reactor has $k_La = 180\,\text{h}^{-1}$, $C^\ast =
7.0\,\text{mg}/\text{L}$, and a dissolved-oxygen setpoint
$C_L = 2.0\,\text{mg}/\text{L}$. Compute the oxygen-transfer rate in
$\text{mg}/(\text{L}\cdot\text{h})$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A $500\,\text{L}$ culture produces $0.8\,\text{g}/\text{L}$ product at
harvest. Downstream recovery is 35\%. Compute purified product per
batch and the number of successful batches needed for $1.0\,\text{kg}$.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Derivation}\pathtag{standard}

\begin{exercise}
Derive the nonzero steady-state biomass and substrate concentrations
for a chemostat with Monod growth and biomass yield $Y_{X/S}$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Starting from the batch equations with $\mu=\mu_{\max}$ constant and no
maintenance, derive $X(t)$ and $S(t)$ until the substrate is exhausted.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Derive the relation between oxygen uptake rate and heat release using
an assumed heat release of $14\,\text{MJ}/\text{kg O}_2$. Express the
answer in $\text{kW}$ for an arbitrary OUR in
$\text{mol}/(\text{L}\cdot\text{h})$ and volume in litres.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
For a sterilisation process with decimal reduction time $D$, derive the
time required for an $n$-log reduction. Apply it to $D=2\,\text{min}$
and $n=12$.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Estimation}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the time for a $1\,\text{L}$ well-mixed culture at
$X=5\,\text{g}/\text{L}$ to consume the dissolved oxygen if aeration
stops. State your assumed oxygen solubility and specific uptake rate.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the heat load of a $10{,}000\,\text{L}$ aerobic reactor whose
oxygen uptake is $0.05\,\text{mol}/(\text{L}\cdot\text{h})$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate how many $2000\,\text{L}$ batches are needed to produce
$10\,\text{kg}$ purified product if the culture titer is
$2\,\text{g}/\text{L}$ and total recovery is 50\%.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the salt concentration in a sauerkraut fermentation made from
$1.5\,\text{kg}$ cabbage and $30\,\text{g}$ salt. Express the result
as percent mass fraction and state the main uncertainty.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Simulation}\pathtag{mastery}

\begin{exercise}
Simulate a fed-batch reactor with Monod growth and a feed rate chosen to
hold substrate near $0.2\,\text{g}/\text{L}$. Plot $X$, $S$, and
reactor volume over time.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Simulate a chemostat after a step increase in dilution rate from
$0.2$ to $0.6\,\text{h}^{-1}$. Use Monod kinetics and identify whether
the culture reaches a new steady state or washes out.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Build a two-zone scale-up model in which cells circulate between an
oxygen-rich zone and an oxygen-poor zone. Compare predicted product
formation to a single well-mixed model with the same average oxygen.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Monte Carlo a five-step downstream process whose step yields are
normally distributed with means 0.90, 0.85, 0.80, 0.85, 0.90 and
standard deviation 0.03, truncated to $[0,1]$. Report the distribution
of total recovery.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Design}\pathtag{standard}

\begin{exercise}
Design a bench-scale aerobic microbial run to produce an intracellular
enzyme. Specify reactor volume, feed composition, oxygen-transfer
target, harvest criterion, and first two downstream steps.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Design a mammalian-cell process for a secreted protein at concept
level. Specify batch or perfusion mode, cell-retention strategy if any,
principal quality attributes, and the scale-up variable you would
protect first.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Design a sterile sampling procedure for a small bioreactor. Draw the
sterile boundary and identify every operation that crosses it.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Design a household fermentation experiment that can distinguish
temperature-limited from substrate-limited fermentation. State the two
experimental arms, the measured variables, and the expected signatures.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Judgement}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
A production run reaches the expected titer but fails a late
purity specification. State three hypotheses, one upstream, one
downstream, and one analytical, and the evidence you would seek for
each.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A scale-up team proposes constant geometric similarity from
$2\,\text{L}$ to $2000\,\text{L}$ and no other criterion. State why
this is incomplete and name the failure mode you would protect first
for a fast aerobic bacterial process.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A chemostat used for production has run stably for 30 days, but product
titer is drifting downward while biomass remains constant. State three
biological explanations and two process explanations.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
An operator finds that a batch record is incomplete for a hold-time
step, but all final analytical tests pass. Explain why release is still
an engineering decision rather than a purely analytical one.
\end{exercise}
