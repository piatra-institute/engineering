% hQ/2 rising, total the sum with a flat minimum at Q* = sqrt(2KD/h).
% Chapter numbers K=80, D=50000, h=4 give Q* approx 1414 and
% C* approx 5657. To keep pgf arithmetic small, work in normalised
% units: let q = Q/1000 (batches in thousands) and costs in thousands
% of currency. Then ordering = KD/Q = 4000/Q_thousand ... rescale:
% ordering(q) = 4/q  (thousands), holding(q) = 2q (thousands),
% total(q) = 4/q + 2q, minimum at q* = sqrt(2) approx 1.414, C* = 5.657.
% Plot: q in [0.2, 3.4] mapped plotx = 2.4*q; ploty = cost*0.65.

% Ordering cost 4/q, ploty = (4/q)*0.65, plotx = 2.4*q -> q = plotx/2.4

  (\x, {(4/(\x/2.4))*0.65});

  {ordering $KD/Q$};

\draw[thick, engamber, dashdotted] plot[domain=0:8, samples=2]

% Total cost = 4/q + 2q
\draw[very thick, engblue] plot[domain=0.48:8, samples=140]
  (\x, {((4/(\x/2.4)) + 2*(\x/2.4))*0.65});

\caption[The economic-order-quantity cost and its flat minimum]{The
annual inventory cost $C(Q) = KD/Q + hQ/2$ as the sum of a falling
ordering cost (red) and a rising holding cost (amber). The total (blue)
has a single minimum at $Q^{*} = \sqrt{2KD/h}$, where the two cost
halves are equal and $C(Q^{*}) = \sqrt{2KDh}$. The minimum is flat:
the cost is quadratic in the relative batch error near $Q^{*}$, so
doubling the batch to $2Q^{*}$ raises the total by only a quarter. The
flatness is the optimisation face of the small quadratic remainder near
a smooth minimum, and it is why round-number batch policies survive in
practice.}
