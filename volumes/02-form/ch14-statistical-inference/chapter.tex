\section{Estimation: point and interval}\pathtag{core}

A \engterm{statistical model} is a family of probability
distributions, indexed by a parameter $\theta$ in a parameter set
$\Theta$. We write the family as $\set*{p(x; \theta) : \theta \in
\Theta}$ for densities or as $\set*{P_{\theta} : \theta \in
\Theta}$ for distributions. The data $X_{1}, \ldots, X_{n}$ are
modelled as independent draws from one member of the family, and the
engineer's task is to identify which member. Volume II Chapter 13
provides the probability backbone; this chapter takes the random
variables for granted and works on the parameter.

A \engterm{statistic} is any function of the data, $T = T(X_{1},
\ldots, X_{n})$. A statistic that is intended as a guess for
$\theta$ is an \engterm{estimator}, written $\hat{\theta} =
\hat{\theta}(X_{1}, \ldots, X_{n})$. The hat is notation, not a
licence: an estimator is a random variable, with its own
distribution induced by the distribution of the data
\cite[ch.~6]{text:wasserman2004}.

\subsection{Bias, variance, mean squared error}

The two scalar summaries that govern an estimator's quality are its
bias and its variance. The \engterm{bias} of $\hat{\theta}$ at the
true parameter $\theta$ is
\[
\operatorname{bias}_{\theta}(\hat{\theta}) = \E_{\theta}[\hat{\theta}] - \theta.
\]
The \engterm{variance} is $\Var_{\theta}(\hat{\theta}) =
\E_{\theta}[(\hat{\theta} - \E_{\theta}[\hat{\theta}])^{2}]$.
The \engterm{mean squared error} combines both,
\[
\operatorname{MSE}_{\theta}(\hat{\theta})
= \E_{\theta}[(\hat{\theta} - \theta)^{2}]
= \Var_{\theta}(\hat{\theta}) + \operatorname{bias}_{\theta}(\hat{\theta})^{2}.
\]
The bias-variance decomposition is the operational identity behind
every later choice in this chapter, including ridge regression,
cross-validation, and Bayesian shrinkage. An estimator with smaller
MSE than another may still be more biased: the variance reduction
can outweigh the bias penalty. This is the engineer's licence to
prefer a slightly biased estimator when its variance is much
smaller. We come back to this trade-off formally in section 14.5.

\subsection{Consistency and asymptotic normality}

An estimator $\hat{\theta}_{n}$, computed from a sample of size $n$,
is \engterm{consistent} for $\theta$ if $\hat{\theta}_{n}
\xrightarrow{P} \theta$ as $n \to \infty$, in the sense of
convergence in probability \cite[ch.~5, ch.~6]{text:wasserman2004}.
Consistency is the minimum guarantee a working estimator should
satisfy; an inconsistent estimator does not improve with more data.

An estimator is \engterm{asymptotically normal} if
\[
\sqrt{n}\,(\hat{\theta}_{n} - \theta)
\xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}\!\left(0,\, V(\theta)\right),
\]
for some asymptotic variance $V(\theta)$. The maximum-likelihood
estimator and the least-squares estimator both satisfy this under
standard regularity conditions, and the asymptotic-normality
property is what justifies the textbook habit of reporting an
estimate as $\hat{\theta} \pm z_{1-\alpha/2}\, \widehat{\mathrm{se}}(\hat{\theta})$
when $n$ is large.

\subsection{The sample mean and sample variance}

The \engterm{sample mean} $\bar{X}_{n} = \frac{1}{n}\sum_{i=1}^{n}
X_{i}$ is unbiased for the population mean $\mu = \E[X_{1}]$ and
has variance $\sigma^{2}/n$ when the data are independent and
identically distributed with population variance $\sigma^{2}$.
The \engterm{sample variance} with the Bessel correction,
\[
S_{n}^{2} = \frac{1}{n-1}\sum_{i=1}^{n} (X_{i} - \bar{X}_{n})^{2},
\]
is unbiased for $\sigma^{2}$. The denominator $n-1$, rather than
$n$, is the correction the reader took on trust in Volume I; the
algebra exercise at the end of this chapter shows it.

\subsection{The plug-in estimator}

For a scalar functional $T(F)$ of an unknown distribution $F$, the
\engterm{plug-in estimator} substitutes the empirical distribution
$\hat{F}_{n}$, the distribution that places mass $1/n$ at each
observation, into the functional:
\[
\hat{T}_{n} = T(\hat{F}_{n}).
\]
The plug-in mean recovers the sample mean. The plug-in variance
recovers the maximum-likelihood (uncorrected) variance estimate.
The plug-in approach is the engineer's first line of attack on any
functional that does not have a tabled estimator.

\subsection{Confidence intervals: definition and construction}

A \engterm{$1-\alpha$ confidence interval} for $\theta$ is a pair of
data-dependent endpoints $L(X), U(X)$ such that
\[
\Pr_{\theta}\!\left[L(X) \leq \theta \leq U(X)\right] \geq 1-\alpha
\quad \text{for all } \theta \in \Theta.
\]
The probability is over the data, with $\theta$ fixed. The interval
is random; the parameter is not. The procedure has $1-\alpha$
long-run coverage. The frequentist statement ``the interval covers
$\theta$ with probability $0.95$'' refers to the procedure across
hypothetical resamples, not to a posterior belief about a particular
realised interval.

The standard pivot construction works as follows. If
$\sqrt{n}(\hat{\theta} - \theta)/\widehat{\mathrm{se}}(\hat{\theta})$
has an approximately standard normal distribution at sample size
$n$, then
\[
\hat{\theta} \pm z_{1-\alpha/2}\,\widehat{\mathrm{se}}(\hat{\theta})
\]
is a $1-\alpha$ confidence interval. With unknown $\sigma$ and a
Gaussian model, $z_{1-\alpha/2}$ is replaced by the $t$-quantile
$t_{1-\alpha/2,n-1}$, recovering the Volume I formula.

\subsection{The bootstrap}

When the estimator has no closed-form standard error and no
asymptotic formula the engineer is willing to trust, the
\engterm{bootstrap} provides a working substitute
\cite{paper:v2ch14-efron-bootstrap-1979}. The idea is the
plug-in principle applied to the sampling distribution. The
empirical distribution $\hat{F}_{n}$ stands in for the unknown
population $F$. Repeated samples of size $n$ are drawn from
$\hat{F}_{n}$ with replacement; the statistic of interest is
recomputed on each resample; the empirical distribution of the
$B$ resample-statistics is the \engterm{bootstrap distribution}
of the estimator. The bootstrap standard error is the standard
deviation of the resample-statistics; the percentile interval
takes the $\alpha/2$ and $1-\alpha/2$ quantiles of the bootstrap
distribution as confidence-interval endpoints. The procedure is
the engineer's default when no asymptotic formula is available.
Its working failures are predictable: the bootstrap is unreliable
for statistics that depend on extremes (sample maxima, sample
minima, quantiles near the support boundary) and for heavy-tailed
distributions where the sample-quantile substitution becomes
unstable \cite{paper:ch08-efron-tibshirani-1993}. The full-page
schematic in figure \ref{fig:vol02:ch14:bootstrap} reads the
procedure off as a single diagram.

\subsection{Confidence versus prediction}

A confidence interval reports uncertainty about a parameter (a
property of the population). A \engterm{prediction interval} reports
uncertainty about a future observation (a property of an individual
draw). The two are distinct quantities and have distinct widths;
Volume I Chapter 8 worked the operational example. We treat the
confidence interval as the chapter's default and refer the reader
to Volume I Chapter 8 for the prediction-interval construction.

\begin{estimation}
\textbf{Question.} A pilot of $n = 20$ flexural strength tests on a
new ceramic gives sample mean $\bar{x} = 480 \,\si{\mega\pascal}$
and sample standard deviation $s = 30 \,\si{\mega\pascal}$. The
working population is modelled as Gaussian. Without consulting a
table, estimate the half-width of the $95\,\%$ confidence interval
for the population mean and the half-width of the $95\,\%$
prediction interval for the next test.

\smallskip
\textit{Estimate, before reading on.}

\smallskip
The standard error of the mean is $s/\sqrt{n} = 30/\sqrt{20}
\approx 6.7 \,\si{\mega\pascal}$. The $t$ critical value at $19$
degrees of freedom is close to $2.09$, so the confidence
half-width is about $2.09 \times 6.7 \approx 14 \,\si{\mega\pascal}$.
The prediction half-width uses $s\sqrt{1 + 1/n} \approx s = 30
\,\si{\mega\pascal}$ as the inner factor, so it is approximately
$2.09 \times 30 \approx 63 \,\si{\mega\pascal}$. The two intervals
differ by roughly the factor $\sqrt{n}$, which is the working
mnemonic.
\end{estimation}

\section{Hypothesis testing as decision theory}\pathtag{core}

Volume I Chapter 8 introduced hypothesis testing as a decision rule
under the null hypothesis. The Neyman-Pearson formulation places it
inside decision theory, where the engineer chooses a rule that
controls the probability of each kind of error and reports both
\cite[ch.~10]{text:wasserman2004}.

\subsection{The two errors, formally}

A test is a function $\delta: \mathcal{X} \to \set*{0, 1}$ of the
data, with $\delta = 1$ meaning ``reject $H_{0}$.'' The
\engterm{Type I error} probability is the rejection rate when
$H_{0}$ holds,
\[
\alpha(\delta) = \Pr_{H_{0}}[\delta(X) = 1].
\]
The \engterm{Type II error} probability is the non-rejection rate
when an alternative $H_{1}: \theta = \theta_{1}$ holds,
\[
\beta(\delta; \theta_{1}) = \Pr_{\theta_{1}}[\delta(X) = 0].
\]
The \engterm{power} of the test against $\theta_{1}$ is $1 -
\beta(\delta; \theta_{1})$. A test with low Type I error and high
power is what the engineer wants; the two cannot be made arbitrarily
small at fixed sample size, and the trade-off is governed by the
Neyman-Pearson lemma.

\input{volumes/02-form/ch14-statistical-inference/figures/fig-test-regions.tex}

\subsection{The Neyman-Pearson lemma}

For testing a simple null $H_{0}: \theta = \theta_{0}$ against a
simple alternative $H_{1}: \theta = \theta_{1}$, the
\engterm{likelihood-ratio test}
\[
\delta^{\star}(x) = \begin{cases}
1 & \text{if } \Lambda(x) = \dfrac{p(x; \theta_{1})}{p(x; \theta_{0})} > k, \\
0 & \text{otherwise,}
\end{cases}
\]
with the threshold $k$ chosen so that $\Pr_{\theta_{0}}[\Lambda(X) >
k] = \alpha$, has the highest power among all tests of size at most
$\alpha$ \cite[ch.~10]{text:wasserman2004}. The lemma is a clean
piece of mathematics with a working consequence: under simple
hypotheses, the likelihood ratio is the optimal test statistic, and
every classical test the engineer uses is either a likelihood-ratio
test, a generalised version for composite hypotheses, or an
asymptotic approximation of one.

\subsection{Composite hypotheses, $p$-values, and the size of a test}

Real engineering tests rarely have simple alternatives. The
\engterm{generalised likelihood-ratio test} extends the lemma to
composite hypotheses by replacing $p(x; \theta_{1})$ with the
maximum of the likelihood over the alternative parameter set. The
test statistic
\[
\Lambda(x) = \frac{\sup_{\theta \in \Theta_{0}} p(x; \theta)}
                  {\sup_{\theta \in \Theta} p(x; \theta)}
\]
satisfies, under regularity conditions and at large $n$, the
asymptotic identity $-2\ln\Lambda(X) \xrightarrow{d}
\chi^{2}_{r}$, where $r$ is the difference in dimensions between
$\Theta$ and $\Theta_{0}$ \cite[ch.~10]{text:wasserman2004}. This
is the chi-squared approximation that powers most non-Gaussian
testing in working software.

The \engterm{$p$-value} of a realised observation $x$ is
\[
p(x) = \sup_{\theta \in \Theta_{0}} \Pr_{\theta}[T(X) \geq T(x)],
\]
where $T$ is the test statistic. Volume I Chapter 8's narrow
question (``how often would the null model produce a statistic this
extreme?'') is exactly the right reading; the formal definition
adds the supremum over a composite null and the explicit choice of
test statistic.

\input{volumes/02-form/ch14-statistical-inference/figures/fig-pvalue.tex}

\subsection{Power, sample size, and design}

The dual to a fixed-$\alpha$ test is a fixed-power design. For a
one-sample $z$-test of $H_{0}: \mu = \mu_{0}$ against $H_{1}: \mu =
\mu_{0} + d\sigma$, the power at sample size $n$ is approximately
\[
\mathrm{power}(d, n; \alpha) \approx
\Phi\!\left(d\sqrt{n} - z_{1-\alpha/2}\right),
\]
where $\Phi$ is the standard normal cdf and $d$ is the
standardised effect size. Solving for $n$ at a target power
$1-\beta$ gives
\[
n \approx \left(\frac{z_{1-\alpha/2} + z_{1-\beta}}{d}\right)^{2}.
\]
A study at $\alpha = 0.05$, $1-\beta = 0.80$ needs
$n \approx (1.96 + 0.84)^{2}/d^{2} \approx 7.85/d^{2}$, the
working back-of-envelope that detecting a half-sigma effect
demands about $30$ observations and detecting a fifth-sigma effect
demands about $200$. The power curves in figure
\ref{fig:vol02:ch14:power-curve} make the trade explicit.

\input{volumes/02-form/ch14-statistical-inference/figures/fig-power-curve.tex}

A study that reports a non-significant result and reports its
power against the effect size the engineer cares about is an
honest report. A study that reports a non-significant result and
does not report power is reporting that no effect was detected
and is silent on whether the design could have detected one.

\subsection{Decision-theoretic framing}

The engineer who frames a test as a decision under cost is closer to
practice than the engineer who reports $p$-values alone. Let $C_{I}$
be the cost of a Type I error (rejecting a true null) and $C_{II}$
the cost of a Type II error. Under a prior probability $\pi_{0}$ on
$H_{0}$, the expected cost of test $\delta$ is
\[
\E[C(\delta)] = \pi_{0}\, C_{I}\, \alpha(\delta)
              + (1 - \pi_{0})\, C_{II}\, \beta(\delta).
\]
Choosing $\delta$ to minimise expected cost gives the Bayes-optimal
test. The likelihood-ratio threshold then has a closed form,
$k = (\pi_{0} C_{I})/((1 - \pi_{0}) C_{II})$, which makes the
$\alpha$-versus-power trade explicit and quantitative. A
safety-critical decision is not ``smaller $\alpha$'' by reflex; it
is the test whose threshold reflects the actual cost ratio.

\section{Likelihood and maximum likelihood}\pathtag{core}

Given data $X_{1}, \ldots, X_{n}$ with joint density $p(x_{1},
\ldots, x_{n}; \theta)$, the \engterm{likelihood function} treats
the data as fixed and the parameter as the variable:
\[
L(\theta) = p(x_{1}, \ldots, x_{n}; \theta).
\]
For independent observations, the likelihood factorises:
$L(\theta) = \prod_{i=1}^{n} p(x_{i}; \theta)$. The
\engterm{log-likelihood} $\ell(\theta) = \log L(\theta)$ converts
the product into a sum and is the working object in nearly every
calculation. The \engterm{maximum-likelihood estimator}, or MLE, is
\[
\hat{\theta}_{\mathrm{MLE}} = \argmax_{\theta \in \Theta} \ell(\theta).
\]

\subsection{MLE for canonical distributions}

The MLE is computable in closed form for several distributions. We
work the cases the engineer meets most often; Devore's standard
applied-statistics catalogue covers the same families with
worked-engineering data \cite[ch.~6, ch.~7]{text:devore2015}.

\textit{Bernoulli}. For $X_{i} \in \set*{0, 1}$ with $\Pr[X = 1] =
p$, the log-likelihood is $\ell(p) = (\sum x_{i})\log p +
(n - \sum x_{i})\log(1-p)$. Setting $\dd\ell/\dd p = 0$ gives
$\hat{p} = \bar{x}$. The MLE recovers the sample proportion.

\textit{Gaussian with known $\sigma$}. For $X_{i} \sim
\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^{2})$ with $\sigma$ known, the
log-likelihood is $\ell(\mu) = -\frac{1}{2\sigma^{2}}\sum
(x_{i}-\mu)^{2} + \text{const}$. The MLE for $\mu$ is the sample
mean $\bar{x}$.

\textit{Gaussian with both unknown}. The MLE for the variance is
$\hat{\sigma}^{2}_{\mathrm{MLE}} = \frac{1}{n}\sum(x_{i} -
\bar{x})^{2}$, with denominator $n$, not $n-1$. The MLE is biased
for $\sigma^{2}$; the Bessel-corrected estimator is unbiased. The
two converge as $n$ grows.

\textit{Exponential}. For $X_{i} \sim \mathrm{Exp}(\lambda)$, the
log-likelihood is $\ell(\lambda) = n\log\lambda - \lambda \sum
x_{i}$. The MLE is $\hat{\lambda} = 1/\bar{x}$.

\textit{Poisson}. For $X_{i} \sim \mathrm{Poisson}(\lambda)$, the
MLE is $\hat{\lambda} = \bar{x}$.

\textit{Uniform on $[0, \theta]$}. For $X_{i} \sim
\mathrm{Uniform}[0, \theta]$, the likelihood is zero for $\theta <
\max x_{i}$ and decreasing in $\theta$ for $\theta \geq \max x_{i}$.
The MLE is $\hat{\theta} = \max x_{i}$. The example shows that the
MLE need not be a sum of simple terms: the support of the
distribution carries information that the score equation does not.

\subsection{The score and the Fisher information}

The \engterm{score function} is the gradient of the log-likelihood,
$U(\theta) = \pdv{\ell(\theta)}{\theta}$. Under regularity, the
score has mean zero at the true parameter. The \engterm{Fisher
information} is
\[
I(\theta) = \E_{\theta}\!\left[U(\theta)^{2}\right]
          = -\E_{\theta}\!\left[\pdv{^{2}\ell(\theta)}{\theta^{2}}\right],
\]
where the second equality holds under regularity. For a sample of
size $n$ from an i.i.d.\ model, the Fisher information scales
linearly: $I_{n}(\theta) = n I_{1}(\theta)$.

\subsection{Properties of the MLE}

Under regularity conditions (the parameter set is open, the support
of the distribution does not depend on the parameter, the
log-likelihood is twice differentiable, and standard moment
conditions hold), the MLE satisfies three working properties
\cite[ch.~9]{text:wasserman2004}.

\textit{Consistency}. $\hat{\theta}_{\mathrm{MLE}} \xrightarrow{P}
\theta$ as $n \to \infty$.

\textit{Asymptotic normality}. $\sqrt{n}(\hat{\theta}_{\mathrm{MLE}}
- \theta) \xrightarrow{d} \mathcal{N}(0, I_{1}(\theta)^{-1})$. The
asymptotic variance is the inverse of the per-observation Fisher
information.

\textit{Asymptotic efficiency}. No regular asymptotically unbiased
estimator has smaller asymptotic variance than the MLE. The
\engterm{Cram\'er-Rao lower bound} states that any unbiased
estimator $\hat{\theta}$ satisfies $\Var(\hat{\theta}) \geq
1/I_{n}(\theta)$, and the MLE attains this bound asymptotically.

The three properties together justify the working habit: when the
model is right and $n$ is large, the MLE with its observed-Fisher
standard error is the engineer's first reach.

\subsection{When the MLE fails}

The MLE fails when the regularity conditions fail. Three working
cases recur. When the support depends on the parameter (the uniform
case), the asymptotic theorems do not apply and the standard error
must be derived directly. When the parameter sits on the boundary
of $\Theta$ (a variance component pinned at zero in a hierarchical
model), the asymptotic distribution becomes a mixture and is no
longer Gaussian. When the model is misspecified (the data do not
come from the assumed family), the MLE converges to the parameter
that minimises the Kullback-Leibler divergence from the truth to
the model, not to anything the engineer would call ``the true
parameter.'' The third failure is the origin of the
\engterm{quasi-likelihood} corrections used in robust statistics
and is the practical reason an engineer should never report an MLE
without a residual diagnostic.

\section{Bayesian inference}\pathtag{standard}

The Bayesian framing treats the parameter as a random variable with
its own distribution, and the data update that distribution
\cite[ch.~11]{text:wasserman2004}. Volume I Chapter 8 introduced
this on a contingency table; this section makes it formal.

\subsection{Prior, likelihood, posterior}

The engineer specifies a \engterm{prior density} $\pi(\theta)$ on
$\Theta$, expressing the state of knowledge before the data arrive.
The data, with likelihood $L(\theta) = p(x_{1}, \ldots, x_{n};
\theta)$, update the prior to the \engterm{posterior density} via
Bayes' rule:
\[
\pi(\theta \mid x) = \frac{L(\theta)\, \pi(\theta)}
                          {\int_{\Theta} L(\vartheta)\, \pi(\vartheta)\,\dd\vartheta}.
\]
The denominator, the \engterm{marginal likelihood} or
\engterm{evidence} $m(x)$, is the normalising constant that makes
$\pi(\cdot \mid x)$ integrate to one. In closed-form cases the
denominator can be ignored: the posterior is proportional to
likelihood times prior.

\subsection{Conjugate priors}

A prior is \engterm{conjugate} to a likelihood family if the
posterior belongs to the same parametric family as the prior. The
working catalogue covers the canonical models:

\textit{Beta-Bernoulli}. For $X_{i} \sim \mathrm{Bernoulli}(p)$ with
prior $p \sim \mathrm{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$, the posterior is
$p \mid x \sim \mathrm{Beta}(\alpha + s,\, \beta + n - s)$, where
$s = \sum x_{i}$.

\textit{Gamma-Poisson}. For $X_{i} \sim \mathrm{Poisson}(\lambda)$
with prior $\lambda \sim \mathrm{Gamma}(\alpha, \beta)$, the
posterior is $\lambda \mid x \sim \mathrm{Gamma}(\alpha + s,\,
\beta + n)$.

\textit{Gaussian-Gaussian, known variance}. For $X_{i} \sim
\mathcal{N}(\mu, \sigma^{2})$ with $\sigma^{2}$ known and prior
$\mu \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_{0}, \tau_{0}^{2})$, the posterior is
$\mu \mid x \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu_{n}, \tau_{n}^{2})$ with
\[
\tau_{n}^{-2} = \tau_{0}^{-2} + n\sigma^{-2},
\qquad
\mu_{n} = \tau_{n}^{2}\!\left(\tau_{0}^{-2}\mu_{0} + n\sigma^{-2}\bar{x}\right).
\]
The posterior precision is the sum of prior and data precisions.
The posterior mean is the precision-weighted average of the prior
mean and the sample mean. As $n$ grows, the data dominate and the
posterior collapses on the MLE.

\subsection{Posterior summaries and credible intervals}

A \engterm{credible interval} of level $1-\alpha$ is an interval
$[L, U]$ such that $\Pr[\theta \in [L, U] \mid x] = 1 - \alpha$.
The probability is a posterior probability about the parameter,
given the data. The two common choices are the
\engterm{equal-tailed credible interval}, with $\alpha/2$ posterior
mass on each side, and the \engterm{highest-posterior-density
interval}, the shortest interval with total posterior mass
$1 - \alpha$. For a unimodal symmetric posterior the two coincide.

The frequentist confidence interval and the Bayesian credible
interval answer different questions. The first is a procedural
guarantee about coverage across resamples; the second is a posterior
probability statement about the parameter. With weak priors and
large samples they often coincide numerically.

\subsection{Predictive distribution}

The Bayesian's prediction for a new observation $X_{\mathrm{new}}$
integrates over the posterior:
\[
p(x_{\mathrm{new}} \mid x) = \int_{\Theta} p(x_{\mathrm{new}}; \theta)\,
                                            \pi(\theta \mid x)\,\dd\theta.
\]
The integral propagates parameter uncertainty into the prediction
in a single quantity. For the Beta-Bernoulli model, the predictive
probability of a future success is $(\alpha + s)/(\alpha + \beta +
n)$, the posterior mean of $p$. For the Gaussian-Gaussian model with
known variance, the predictive distribution is Gaussian with mean
$\mu_{n}$ and variance $\sigma^{2} + \tau_{n}^{2}$. The added
$\tau_{n}^{2}$ is the parameter uncertainty that a frequentist
prediction interval has to account for separately.

\subsection{When to reach for Bayesian inference}

The engineer reaches for Bayesian inference when prior information
is genuine and quantified, when the parameter has a structured
hierarchical interpretation, when the goal is a posterior over many
parameters with explicit uncertainty propagation, or when the data
are sparse enough that classical asymptotics fail. The engineer
declines Bayesian inference when the prior is fabricated,
high-dimensional integration is computationally infeasible without
machinery the project does not justify, or the audience for the
result requires a frequentist procedural guarantee that the
posterior credible interval does not provide.

\begin{mastery}
The choice of prior is the load-bearing modelling step. A
\engterm{non-informative} or \engterm{weakly informative} prior
attempts to let the data speak. Jeffreys' prior, $\pi_{J}(\theta)
\propto \sqrt{\det I(\theta)}$, is invariant under reparametrisation
and is a defensible default for many one-parameter models. A
\engterm{conjugate prior with small effective sample size} (small
$\alpha + \beta$ in a Beta prior, small $\tau_{0}^{-2}$ in a
Gaussian prior) is the engineer's working compromise: it stabilises
the posterior in small-sample regimes while letting the data
dominate as $n$ grows. A \engterm{strong prior} reflects genuine
knowledge from earlier studies or physical constraints, and must be
defensible as such; otherwise it is wishful thinking.
\end{mastery}

\section{Regression: linear, logistic, regularised}\pathtag{core}

Volume I Chapter 8 fitted a straight line to data on trust. Volume
II Chapter 9 supplied the linear-algebraic apparatus, including the
projection geometry and the conditioning analysis. This section
combines them into a statistical model and extends the framework to
binary outcomes and to penalised estimation.

\subsection{The linear regression model}

The data are $(x_{i}, y_{i})$ for $i = 1, \ldots, n$, with $x_{i}
\in \R^{p}$ a vector of predictors and $y_{i} \in \R$ a response.
The \engterm{linear regression model} is
\[
Y_{i} = x_{i}^{\transpose}\beta + \varepsilon_{i},
\qquad \varepsilon_{i} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma^{2}),
\quad \text{independent across } i.
\]
Stacking gives $\vect{Y} = \mat{X}\vect{\beta} + \vect{\varepsilon}$,
where $\mat{X}$ is the \engterm{design matrix} of size $n \times p$
with rows $x_{i}^{\transpose}$. The Gaussian noise assumption
identifies the model: the likelihood is
\[
L(\vect{\beta}, \sigma^{2})
= \prod_{i=1}^{n}\frac{1}{\sqrt{2\pi\sigma^{2}}}
  \exp\!\left(-\frac{(y_{i} - x_{i}^{\transpose}\vect{\beta})^{2}}{2\sigma^{2}}\right),
\]
and maximising the likelihood in $\vect{\beta}$ is equivalent to
minimising the residual sum of squares.

\subsection{Least squares as MLE under Gaussian noise}

The MLE for $\vect{\beta}$ is the \engterm{ordinary least squares
estimator},
\[
\hat{\vect{\beta}}_{\mathrm{OLS}}
  = \argmin_{\vect{\beta} \in \R^{p}}
    \norm*{\vect{Y} - \mat{X}\vect{\beta}}_{2}^{2}.
\]
Volume II Chapter 9 derived the closed form by setting the
gradient with respect to $\vect{\beta}$ to zero, producing the
\engterm{normal equations} $\mat{X}^{\transpose}\mat{X}
\hat{\vect{\beta}} = \mat{X}^{\transpose}\vect{Y}$. When $\mat{X}$
has full column rank, the solution is
\[
\hat{\vect{\beta}}_{\mathrm{OLS}}
  = (\mat{X}^{\transpose}\mat{X})^{-1}\mat{X}^{\transpose}\vect{Y}.
\]
The estimator is unbiased: $\E[\hat{\vect{\beta}}] = \vect{\beta}$.
Its covariance is $\Var(\hat{\vect{\beta}}) = \sigma^{2}
(\mat{X}^{\transpose}\mat{X})^{-1}$. The Gauss-Markov theorem
states that, under the Gaussian-noise model, the OLS estimator has
the smallest variance among all linear unbiased estimators.

\subsection{Diagnostic plots and residual analysis}

The MLE is unreliable without residual diagnostics. The
\engterm{residual} for observation $i$ is $\hat{\varepsilon}_{i} =
y_{i} - x_{i}^{\transpose}\hat{\vect{\beta}}$. Four plots carry
most of the diagnostic load:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Residuals against fitted values $\hat{y}_{i}$. A horizontal
    band of constant width is consistent with the homoscedastic
    Gaussian model. A funnel shape signals heteroscedasticity. A
    curve signals a missing nonlinearity.
  \item Residuals against each predictor. A pattern signals the
    same missing nonlinearity, localised to one variable.
  \item A normal quantile-quantile plot of the residuals. Severe
    departures in the tails signal that the Gaussian-noise
    assumption is wrong.
  \item Leverage and Cook's distance, to flag observations whose
    removal would change the fit substantially.
\end{itemize}

A regression report without diagnostic plots is a regression report
the engineer should not believe.

\subsection{Logistic regression}

When the response is binary, $y_{i} \in \set*{0, 1}$, the linear
model is replaced by the \engterm{logistic regression model}:
\[
\Pr[Y_{i} = 1 \mid x_{i}] = \sigma(x_{i}^{\transpose}\vect{\beta}),
\qquad \sigma(z) = \frac{1}{1 + e^{-z}}.
\]
The function $\sigma$ is the logistic sigmoid; its inverse $\log
(p/(1-p))$ is the \engterm{logit} or log-odds. The likelihood is
\[
L(\vect{\beta}) = \prod_{i=1}^{n}
   \sigma(x_{i}^{\transpose}\vect{\beta})^{y_{i}}\,
   (1 - \sigma(x_{i}^{\transpose}\vect{\beta}))^{1 - y_{i}},
\]
and the MLE has no closed form. The standard fitting routine is
\engterm{iteratively reweighted least squares}, equivalent to
Newton's method on the log-likelihood. Volume II Chapter 15 gives
the optimisation apparatus; this chapter takes it for granted.

A logistic regression coefficient $\beta_{j}$ is interpreted on the
log-odds scale: a unit change in $x_{j}$ multiplies the odds
$p/(1-p)$ by $e^{\beta_{j}}$. The interpretation does not match the
linear-regression interpretation, and an engineer who reads
$\beta_{j}$ as a probability change has misread the model.

\subsection{Regularised regression: ridge and lasso}

When the design matrix is ill-conditioned, the OLS estimator has
high variance even though it is unbiased. The bias-variance
decomposition of section 14.1 licenses a trade: introduce a small
bias to gain a large variance reduction. The two canonical
penalised estimators are ridge and lasso.

The \engterm{ridge regression} estimator is
\[
\hat{\vect{\beta}}_{\mathrm{ridge}}(\lambda)
 = \argmin_{\vect{\beta}}\!\left\{
   \norm*{\vect{Y} - \mat{X}\vect{\beta}}_{2}^{2}
 + \lambda\, \norm*{\vect{\beta}}_{2}^{2}\right\},
\]
with closed form $\hat{\vect{\beta}}_{\mathrm{ridge}}(\lambda) =
(\mat{X}^{\transpose}\mat{X} + \lambda \mat{I})^{-1}
\mat{X}^{\transpose}\vect{Y}$. The penalty $\lambda \geq 0$ is the
regularisation parameter; $\lambda = 0$ recovers OLS, and $\lambda
\to \infty$ shrinks the estimator to zero. The matrix
$\mat{X}^{\transpose}\mat{X} + \lambda\mat{I}$ has condition number
no worse than $(\sigma_{1}^{2} + \lambda)/(\sigma_{p}^{2} +
\lambda)$, which is the operational reason ridge stabilises the
solution when the design matrix is ill-conditioned.

The \engterm{lasso} replaces the squared $\ell_{2}$ penalty with an
$\ell_{1}$ penalty:
\[
\hat{\vect{\beta}}_{\mathrm{lasso}}(\lambda)
 = \argmin_{\vect{\beta}}\!\left\{
   \norm*{\vect{Y} - \mat{X}\vect{\beta}}_{2}^{2}
 + \lambda\, \norm*{\vect{\beta}}_{1}\right\},
\]
where $\norm*{\vect{\beta}}_{1} = \sum_{j} \abs*{\beta_{j}}$. The
$\ell_{1}$ geometry has corners on the coordinate axes, and the
optimum often sits at a corner: many coefficients are exactly zero
at the lasso solution. Lasso therefore performs simultaneous
shrinkage and variable selection. The closed form does not exist;
coordinate descent and proximal-gradient methods are the standard
solvers. Volume II Chapter 15 carries the optimisation; this chapter
recognises the result.

The choice of $\lambda$ for either estimator is the central
modelling decision, and cross-validation is the standard discipline
for making it.

\input{volumes/02-form/ch14-statistical-inference/figures/fig-bias-variance.tex}

The ridge penalty was introduced by Hoerl and Kennard for
nonorthogonal design matrices in chemical engineering
\cite{paper:v2ch14-hoerl-kennard-ridge-1970}; the lasso, with
its variable-selection property, was introduced by Tibshirani for
problems where many candidate predictors are believed to be
inactive \cite{paper:v2ch14-tibshirani-lasso-1996}. The two
penalties are now the working catalogue's first reach; a fuller
treatment with elastic-net, group-lasso, and adaptive variants
sits in Hastie, Tibshirani, and Friedman
\cite{text:hastie-tibshirani-friedman}.

\begin{estimation}
\textbf{Question.} A ridge regression with
$\mat{X}^{\transpose}\mat{X}$ approximately $\mat{I}_{p}$ and
$p = 10$ has unbiased OLS variance per coefficient of
$\sigma^{2}/n = 1.0$. Ridge introduces a bias whose square scales
as $b^{2}(\lambda) \approx (\lambda/(1+\lambda))^{2}\,
\norm*{\vect{\beta}}_{2}^{2}/p$ at moderate $\lambda$, with
$\norm*{\vect{\beta}}_{2}^{2}/p \approx 0.5$ in working units.
Estimate the value of $\lambda$ that approximately minimises the
mean squared error per coefficient.

\smallskip
\textit{Estimate, before reading on.}

\smallskip
Variance scales as $V(\lambda) \approx 1/(1+\lambda)^{2}$ in the
orthogonal-design approximation; bias squared scales as
$B^{2}(\lambda) \approx 0.5\,(\lambda/(1+\lambda))^{2}$. Setting
$\dd(V + B^{2})/\dd\lambda \approx 0$ gives $-2/(1+\lambda)^{3} +
0.5 \cdot 2\lambda/(1+\lambda)^{3} = 0$, hence $\lambda^{\star}
\approx 1/0.5 = 2$. The minimum is broad; any $\lambda$ in the
range $1$ to $4$ produces a mean squared error within $10\,\%$ of
the optimum. The lesson the engineer takes home is the shape of
the curve, not the exact minimum: cross-validation will land in
the broad valley provided the procedure is honest, and reporting
a single $\lambda$ without its cross-validated error band is
overspecifying the choice.
\end{estimation}

\section{Cross-validation, hold-out, the danger of looking at test data}\pathtag{core}

The MLE and the OLS estimator both have closed-form measures of
in-sample error: the residual sum of squares, the fitted likelihood,
the in-sample mean squared error. None of these is what the
engineer needs. The engineer needs the error the model will produce
on data it has not seen.

\subsection{Training error versus generalisation error}

The \engterm{training error} of an estimator is the average loss on
the data used to fit the model. The \engterm{generalisation error},
or expected prediction error, is the average loss on a fresh
observation drawn from the same population. The training error
underestimates the generalisation error, often substantially, and
the gap grows with model flexibility \cite[ch.~21]{text:wasserman2004}.

The phenomenon has a working name. A model selected on training
error is selected on its ability to memorise the training data. The
fit's variance shows up as part of the training fit, not as part of
the residual. \engterm{Overfitting} is the technical name for the
mismatch.

\subsection{Hold-out}

The \engterm{hold-out} discipline partitions the data into a
\engterm{training set} and a \engterm{test set}. The model is fit
on the training set, including any tuning of hyperparameters
($\lambda$ in ridge or lasso, the polynomial degree in a polynomial
regression). The fitted model is evaluated once on the test set,
and the test error is reported as the estimate of generalisation
error.

The discipline has one rule. The test set is looked at exactly
once, after every choice has been made. An engineer who tunes a
hyperparameter on the test set, refits, and looks again has
contaminated the procedure. The test error is then biased downward
by an amount that grows with the number of looks. The Volume I Ch
8 ban on choosing the analysis after seeing the data is the same
discipline at the level of model selection.

\input{volumes/02-form/ch14-statistical-inference/figures/fig-kfold.tex}

\subsection{$k$-fold cross-validation}

Hold-out wastes data. \engterm{$k$-fold cross-validation} partitions
the data into $k$ folds of approximately equal size, fits the model
$k$ times (each time holding out one fold and fitting on the union
of the other $k-1$), and averages the held-out errors:
\[
\widehat{\mathrm{CV}}_{k}
 = \frac{1}{k}\sum_{j=1}^{k}
   \frac{1}{\abs*{\mathcal{F}_{j}}}
   \sum_{i \in \mathcal{F}_{j}}
   L\!\left(y_{i},\, \hat{f}^{(-j)}(x_{i})\right),
\]
where $\hat{f}^{(-j)}$ is the model fit on the data with fold
$\mathcal{F}_{j}$ removed and $L$ is the working loss (squared
error for regression, log-loss for classification, misclassification
rate for a hard classifier). The standard choices are $k = 5$ and
$k = 10$ \cite[ch.~21]{text:wasserman2004}. The case $k = n$ is
\engterm{leave-one-out cross-validation}, computationally cheap for
linear models with a closed-form leverage formula and expensive
otherwise.

The cross-validated error is itself an estimator and has its own
standard error. A common rule, the \engterm{one-standard-error
rule}, is to choose the most parsimonious model whose
cross-validated error is within one cross-validation standard
error of the minimum. The rule trades a small increase in expected
error for a substantial reduction in model complexity.

\subsection{Data leakage}

\engterm{Data leakage} is the inadvertent flow of information from
the test set into the model fit. The canonical leakage modes are:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Pre-processing fitted on the full dataset (mean-centring,
    scaling, principal-component projections) before the
    train-test split.
  \item Imputation of missing values using statistics computed on
    the full dataset.
  \item Variable selection on the full dataset, followed by
    cross-validation on the selected variables. The selection
    itself contaminated the procedure; the cross-validation is
    optimistic.
  \item Tuning hyperparameters on the test set. The test error is
    no longer an estimate of generalisation; it is an estimate of
    how well the model fits the data the engineer has already
    optimised against.
  \item Time-series cross-validation that mixes past and future,
    in datasets where the temporal order carries information.
\end{itemize}

The discipline is direct. Every operation that touches the response
or that adapts to the data must sit inside the cross-validation
loop. A pre-processing step that sees only the training fold within
each iteration is the working pattern. A pre-processing step
applied once to the whole dataset before splitting is leakage.

\begin{principle}[Cross-validation is a procedure, not a number]
The cross-validated error is reportable only when the procedure
that produced it touched no part of the test data, fold by fold.
Every choice that adapts to the data, including pre-processing and
feature selection, must sit inside the loop. The engineer who
reports a cross-validated error from a procedure that violated
this rule is reporting an estimate of training error with a
confidence-inflating disguise.
\end{principle}

\section{Failure: p-hacking, multiple comparisons, replication crisis revisited}\pathtag{core}

Volume I Chapter 8 named four malpractices: p-hacking, the garden
of forking paths, HARKing, and inappropriate stopping rules. The
mathematical apparatus of this chapter lets us state each one
formally, attach the controlling correction, and connect the
catalogue to the replication studies that have measured the size of
the problem in published literatures.

\subsection{P-hacking, formally}

Let $T_{1}, \ldots, T_{m}$ be a family of test statistics computed
on the same dataset. \engterm{P-hacking} is the practice of
selecting the smallest of $m$ associated $p$-values and reporting
it as a single test result \cite{method:simmons-nelson-simonsohn-2011}.
Under the global null and approximate independence of the tests,
the probability that at least one of $m$ p-values is below $\alpha$
is approximately $1 - (1-\alpha)^{m}$, which exceeds $\alpha$ for
$m > 1$ and approaches one as $m$ grows. Reporting one selected
$p$-value out of $m$ without disclosing $m$ misrepresents the size
of the test.

The Simmons, Nelson, and Simonsohn 2011 simulation study quantified
the inflation: under realistic combinations of optional reporting
of conditions, optional inclusion or exclusion of covariates,
optional sample-size augmentation, and optional choice of dependent
variable, the false-positive rate of a nominally $\alpha = 0.05$
analysis can rise above $60\,\%$
\cite{method:simmons-nelson-simonsohn-2011}.

\subsection{Multiple comparisons and the Bonferroni correction}

When the engineer genuinely needs to test $m$ hypotheses, the
\engterm{family-wise error rate} is the probability of at least one
false rejection. The \engterm{Bonferroni correction} controls the
family-wise error rate at level $\alpha$ by testing each hypothesis
at level $\alpha/m$. The correction is conservative, especially
under positive dependence among the tests, but it is a defensible
default \cite[ch.~10]{text:wasserman2004}.

A more efficient discipline is \engterm{false discovery rate}
control. The Benjamini-Hochberg procedure orders the $m$ p-values
$p_{(1)} \leq \cdots \leq p_{(m)}$, finds the largest $k$ such that
$p_{(k)} \leq k\alpha/m$, and rejects the corresponding $k$
hypotheses. The expected proportion of false rejections among the
rejections is then bounded by $\alpha$. The procedure is the working
standard in genomics, neuroscience, and other fields with thousands
of simultaneous tests.

\subsection{The garden of forking paths}

\engterm{The garden of forking paths} \cite{method:gelman-loken-forking-paths}
is the analyst's version of the same problem. The analyst does not
run all $m$ tests; the analyst runs one test, but the choice of
test was made after the data were seen, and the choice would have
been different if the data had been different. The reported
$p$-value is conditioned on the actual data, which the analyst
selected the test from. The reference distribution for the test
statistic is the distribution under the null and the analyst's
data-dependent choice rule, not under the null and the test the
analyst happened to run.

The forking-paths argument applies even when no other test was
explicitly performed. The \emph{counterfactual} flexibility, the
set of analyses the engineer would have run if the data had been
different, is what inflates the false-positive rate.

\subsection{HARKing}

\engterm{HARKing}, hypothesising after the results are known, is
the practice of selecting the hypothesis that fits the result and
presenting it as the pre-registered hypothesis
\cite{method:kerr-harking-1998}. HARKing inflates the false-positive
rate in the same way as p-hacking, with the additional cost that
the literature is then populated by hypotheses that look stronger
than the data warrant.

\subsection{The replication studies}

The 2015 \engterm{Reproducibility Project: Psychology} attempted
replications of $100$ studies from three psychology journals. Of
$97$ original studies that had reported a statistically significant
effect, $36\,\%$ produced a statistically significant effect in
replication, and the mean replication effect size was approximately
half the original \cite{method:open-science-collaboration-2015}.

The 2021 \engterm{Reproducibility Project: Cancer Biology}
attempted to replicate $50$ experiments from $23$ high-profile
cancer-biology papers. Replication effects were on average smaller
than original effects by a factor of about three, with mixed
success rates across multiple replication criteria
\cite{method:errington-cancer-biology-2021}.

The pattern extends beyond psychology. The 2016 Camerer et al.\
replication of $18$ laboratory experiments in economics found
that $61\,\%$ of the original results replicated as significant,
with replication effect sizes averaging about two-thirds of the
originals \cite{method:v2ch14-camerer-economics-2016}. The
\textcite{method:ch08-ioannidis-2005} essay had argued, on the
basis of the power and prior-probability arithmetic that Volume I
Chapter 8 already worked through, that most published research
findings in fields with low statistical power and high analytic
flexibility were likely false. The replication studies above are
the post-hoc empirical evidence behind that argument. The Nuzzo
2014 \textit{Nature} feature surveyed the public arc of the
discussion and the working compromises the discipline arrived at
\cite{method:v2ch14-nuzzo-scientific-method-2014}; the 2016 ASA
Statement on $p$-values \cite{method:ch08-wasserstein-asa-2016}
remains the discipline's most-cited formal position.

The four studies, taken together, are the empirical evidence
behind the engineer's working caution. The named statistical
malpractices are not theoretical risks. They have measurable
effects on the literatures the engineer might draw on for design
input.

\subsection{What an engineer does about it}

The chapter's working discipline closes with a small list, applied
operationally:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Pre-register the analysis, including the test, the model,
    the inclusion criteria, and the stopping rule, before the data
    are seen. Record deviations.
  \item Disclose all tests run, not only those that crossed
    threshold. The number $m$ is not separable from the meaning of
    any one $p$-value.
  \item Apply a multiple-comparisons correction whenever multiple
    tests are reported. Bonferroni for small $m$ and high cost of
    false discovery; Benjamini-Hochberg for large $m$ and tolerance
    for some false discoveries among many true ones.
  \item Report effect sizes with confidence intervals. A
    significant $p$-value with a small effect size is rarely a
    load-bearing result.
  \item Treat a single study as evidence, not as decision. Ask
    for replications, prefer cross-validated error to in-sample
    error, and size the engineering decision against a conservative
    defensible effect size.
\end{itemize}

\begin{principle}[The disclosure rule]
A statistical claim is reportable when the analysis that produced
it was specified before the data were seen, when every test run on
the data has been disclosed, when a multiple-comparisons correction
has been applied where appropriate, and when the effect size has
been reported with a confidence interval. A claim that fails any
of these is a working hypothesis to be replicated, not a result to
be acted on.
\end{principle}

Acceptable sources include the reader's own Volume I project data,

  \item A logistic regression of a binary response on the

  \item A ridge or lasso regression of a continuous response on

\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Variable-selection or regularisation choice}. The reader

  \item A residual-diagnostic plot (residuals against fitted

  \item The cross-validated metric, with the mean and the
    standard error across folds, and a comparison to the
    in-sample metric on the full data.
  \item A statement of which predictors the model relies on most
    and which the cross-validation suggests are not contributing.
  \item A statement of the model's defensible domain (the range of

    and what the model cannot defend.
\end{itemize}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item Why cross-validated error differs from in-sample error on

  \item Which residual or calibration anomaly the diagnostic plot
    revealed, and what model misspecification it suggests.
  \item The variable-selection or regularisation choice the reader
    made, and what it would take to reverse the choice. The
    reflection takes the trade-off seriously as a modelling
    decision, not a tuning detail.
  \item One claim the model could be used to defend in a working
    engineering report and one claim it could not, in light of
    the cross-validation, the residuals, and the dataset's
    domain.
  \item What additional data, what additional predictor, or what
    additional pre-registration discipline would tighten the
    report.
\end{itemize}

\subsection*{Calculation}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
For a sample of size $n = 25$ from a Gaussian population with
sample mean $\bar{x} = 12.4$ and sample standard deviation $s =
2.1$, compute the $95\,\%$ confidence interval for $\mu$ and the
$95\,\%$ prediction interval for the next observation. Use
$t_{0.975, 24} \approx 2.064$.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} The standard error of the mean is
$s/\sqrt{n} = 2.1/5 = 0.42$. The confidence half-width is
$2.064 \times 0.42 \approx 0.87$, giving the interval
$12.4 \pm 0.87 = [11.53, 13.27]$. The prediction-interval inner
factor is $s\sqrt{1 + 1/n} = 2.1\sqrt{1.04} \approx 2.14$, so the
half-width is $2.064 \times 2.14 \approx 4.42$, giving
$12.4 \pm 4.42 = [7.98, 16.82]$. The prediction interval is
roughly $\sqrt{n}$ times wider, which is the working mnemonic.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A coin is flipped $n = 40$ times and lands heads $s = 27$ times.
Compute the maximum-likelihood estimate of $p$, its observed-Fisher
standard error, and a $95\,\%$ Wald confidence interval. State the
$z$-quantile used.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} $\hat{p} = s/n = 27/40 = 0.675$. The
observed Fisher information at $\hat{p}$ is $n/(\hat{p}(1-\hat{p}))
= 40/(0.675 \times 0.325) \approx 182.4$, so the standard error is
$1/\sqrt{182.4} \approx 0.0740$. With $z_{0.975} = 1.96$, the Wald
interval is $0.675 \pm 1.96 \times 0.0740 = [0.530, 0.820]$. The
Wald interval is mildly anti-conservative near the boundaries of
$[0, 1]$; the Wilson or Agresti-Coull interval would tighten near
the endpoints.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
For an exponential sample with observations $\set*{1.2, 0.5, 2.1,
0.8, 1.7, 0.3, 1.0, 1.5, 0.9, 2.4}$, compute the MLE
$\hat{\lambda} = 1/\bar{x}$. Report $\bar{x}$, $\hat{\lambda}$, and
the observed log-likelihood $\ell(\hat{\lambda})$.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Sum is $12.4$, so $\bar{x} = 1.24$
and $\hat{\lambda} = 1/1.24 \approx 0.806$. The log-likelihood at
the MLE is $\ell(\hat{\lambda}) = n\log\hat{\lambda} -
\hat{\lambda}\sum x_{i} = 10\log(0.806) - 0.806 \times 12.4 =
10(-0.2158) - 10 = -2.158 - 10 = -12.16$. Equivalently
$n(\log\hat{\lambda} - 1)$, which is the working closed form for
the exponential MLE's maximised log-likelihood.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A Beta$(2, 2)$ prior is placed on the success probability $p$ of a
Bernoulli model. After observing $s = 7$ successes in $n = 10$
trials, compute the posterior parameters and the posterior mean.
Compare the posterior mean to the MLE.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} The conjugate update gives
$\mathrm{Beta}(\alpha + s, \beta + n - s) = \mathrm{Beta}(9, 5)$.
The posterior mean is $9/(9+5) = 9/14 \approx 0.643$. The MLE is
$\hat{p} = 7/10 = 0.700$. The posterior mean is shrunk toward the
prior mean $0.5$; the shrinkage is the prior's effective sample
size $\alpha + \beta = 4$ acting on a data sample of $n = 10$,
giving a $4/14 \approx 29\,\%$ weight on the prior.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
For the four data points $(0,1), (1,3), (2,4), (3,7)$, set up the
design matrix $\mat{X}$ for a linear regression with intercept and
solve the normal equations to obtain $\hat{\beta}_{0}$ and
$\hat{\beta}_{1}$. Verify against the closed-form Volume I Chapter
8 expressions.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} $\mat{X}$ has columns
$(1,1,1,1)^{\transpose}$ and $(0,1,2,3)^{\transpose}$;
$\vect{y} = (1, 3, 4, 7)^{\transpose}$.
$\mat{X}^{\transpose}\mat{X} = \bigl(\begin{smallmatrix} 4 & 6 \\ 6 & 14 \end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$
and $\mat{X}^{\transpose}\vect{y} = (15, 33)^{\transpose}$. The
determinant is $4 \cdot 14 - 36 = 20$, so
$(\mat{X}^{\transpose}\mat{X})^{-1} = \frac{1}{20}\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix} 14 & -6 \\ -6 & 4 \end{smallmatrix}\bigr)$.
Multiplying gives $\hat{\beta}_{0} = (14 \cdot 15 - 6 \cdot
33)/20 = (210 - 198)/20 = 0.6$ and $\hat{\beta}_{1} = (-6 \cdot
15 + 4 \cdot 33)/20 = (-90 + 132)/20 = 2.1$. The Volume I formulae
$\hat{\beta}_{1} = S_{xy}/S_{xx}$ with $\bar{x} = 1.5$,
$\bar{y} = 3.75$, $S_{xy} = 10.5$, $S_{xx} = 5$ give the same
$\hat{\beta}_{1} = 2.1$ and $\hat{\beta}_{0} = 3.75 - 2.1 \times
1.5 = 0.6$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
For the Gaussian-Gaussian model with prior $\mu \sim \mathcal{N}(0,
1)$ and known $\sigma^{2} = 4$, compute the posterior mean and
posterior variance after observing the sample $\set*{1.1, 0.7,
2.3}$. State the prior and data precisions used.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Prior precision $\tau_{0}^{-2} = 1$;
data precision $n\sigma^{-2} = 3/4 = 0.75$. Posterior precision
$\tau_{n}^{-2} = 1 + 0.75 = 1.75$, so $\tau_{n}^{2} = 4/7
\approx 0.571$. Sample mean $\bar{x} = (1.1 + 0.7 + 2.3)/3 =
1.367$. Posterior mean $\mu_{n} = (4/7)(1 \cdot 0 + 0.75 \cdot
1.367) = (4/7)(1.025) \approx 0.586$. The posterior is
$\mathcal{N}(0.586, 0.571)$. The data have pulled the prior toward
the sample mean by a fraction $0.75/1.75 \approx 43\,\%$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Compute the Bonferroni-corrected significance level for a family of
$m = 12$ simultaneous hypothesis tests at family-wise level $\alpha
= 0.05$. State which individual $p$-values would be rejected from
the list $\set*{0.001, 0.003, 0.012, 0.025, 0.040}$.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} The Bonferroni threshold is
$\alpha/m = 0.05/12 \approx 0.00417$. Of the five listed
$p$-values, only $0.001$ and $0.003$ are below the threshold; the
other three are not rejected. Note the gap: $0.012$, which would
have been significant at uncorrected $\alpha = 0.05$, fails the
corrected threshold by a factor of nearly three. The
Benjamini-Hochberg procedure applied to the same list with
$m = 12$ would reject $\{p_{(k)} \leq k\alpha/m\}$ for the
largest such $k$; here $p_{(1)} = 0.001 \leq 0.00417$,
$p_{(2)} = 0.003 \leq 0.00833$, $p_{(3)} = 0.012 \leq 0.01250$,
$p_{(4)} = 0.025 > 0.01667$, so BH rejects the first three.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
For the ridge regression estimator with $\mat{X}^{\transpose}\mat{X}
= \mat{I}_{2}$, $\mat{X}^{\transpose}\vect{Y} = (3, 2)^{\transpose}$,
compute $\hat{\vect{\beta}}_{\mathrm{ridge}}(\lambda)$ for $\lambda
\in \set*{0, 1, 4, 9}$. Plot the coefficient values as a function
of $\lambda$ and identify the limiting behaviour.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} With orthogonal design,
$\hat{\vect{\beta}}_{\mathrm{ridge}}(\lambda) = (1+\lambda)^{-1}
\mat{X}^{\transpose}\vect{Y}$. Evaluating at the four
$\lambda$-values gives $\hat{\vect{\beta}}(0) = (3, 2)$,
$\hat{\vect{\beta}}(1) = (1.5, 1.0)$, $\hat{\vect{\beta}}(4) =
(0.6, 0.4)$, $\hat{\vect{\beta}}(9) = (0.3, 0.2)$. Both
coordinates shrink toward zero by the common factor
$1/(1+\lambda)$; the relative direction $(3:2)$ is preserved.
The limiting behaviour as $\lambda \to \infty$ is
$\hat{\vect{\beta}} \to \vect{0}$.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Derivation}\pathtag{standard}

\begin{exercise}
Show that the sample variance with the Bessel correction,
$S_{n}^{2} = \frac{1}{n-1}\sum (X_{i} - \bar{X}_{n})^{2}$, is
unbiased for the population variance $\sigma^{2}$. State the
expectation identity used at each step.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Expand
$\sum_{i}(X_{i} - \bar{X})^{2} = \sum_{i} X_{i}^{2} - n\bar{X}^{2}$.
Taking expectations and using $\E[X_{i}^{2}] = \mu^{2} + \sigma^{2}$
and $\E[\bar{X}^{2}] = \mu^{2} + \sigma^{2}/n$ gives
$\E[\sum (X_{i} - \bar{X})^{2}] = n(\mu^{2} + \sigma^{2}) - n(\mu^{2}
+ \sigma^{2}/n) = (n-1)\sigma^{2}$. Dividing by $n-1$ yields
$\E[S_{n}^{2}] = \sigma^{2}$. The Bessel correction recovers the
single degree of freedom consumed by estimating $\mu$ with
$\bar{X}$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Derive the bias-variance decomposition $\operatorname{MSE}(\hat{\theta})
= \Var(\hat{\theta}) + \operatorname{bias}(\hat{\theta})^{2}$ from
the definition $\operatorname{MSE}(\hat{\theta}) = \E[(\hat{\theta}
- \theta)^{2}]$, using the addition and subtraction of
$\E[\hat{\theta}]$.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Write
$\hat{\theta} - \theta = (\hat{\theta} - \E\hat{\theta}) +
(\E\hat{\theta} - \theta)$. Squaring and taking expectations,
the cross term $2\E[(\hat{\theta} - \E\hat{\theta})(\E\hat{\theta} -
\theta)]$ vanishes because $\E\hat{\theta} - \theta$ is constant
and $\E[\hat{\theta} - \E\hat{\theta}] = 0$. The remaining two
terms are $\E[(\hat{\theta} - \E\hat{\theta})^{2}] =
\Var(\hat{\theta})$ and $(\E\hat{\theta} - \theta)^{2} =
\operatorname{bias}(\hat{\theta})^{2}$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Derive the MLE for a Poisson sample $X_{1}, \ldots, X_{n}$. Compute
its observed Fisher information at $\hat{\lambda}$ and the
asymptotic variance. State the regularity condition you used to
move the derivative inside the expectation.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} The log-likelihood is
$\ell(\lambda) = (\sum x_{i})\log\lambda - n\lambda + \text{const}$.
Setting $\dd\ell/\dd\lambda = (\sum x_{i})/\lambda - n = 0$ gives
$\hat{\lambda} = \bar{x}$. The second derivative is
$-(\sum x_{i})/\lambda^{2}$; its negation, evaluated at
$\hat{\lambda}$, is the observed information $n/\bar{x}$.
$I_{n}(\lambda) = n/\lambda$ in expectation, so the asymptotic
variance per observation is $\lambda$; the standard error of
$\hat{\lambda}$ is $\sqrt{\bar{x}/n}$. The regularity condition
used is that the support $\set{0, 1, 2, \ldots}$ does not depend
on $\lambda$, allowing the dominated-convergence interchange of
derivative and expectation.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Show that, for the Beta-Bernoulli conjugate model with prior
$\mathrm{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$ and data with $s$ successes in $n$
trials, the posterior is $\mathrm{Beta}(\alpha + s, \beta + n - s)$.
Identify the prior parameters as ``prior successes'' and ``prior
failures'' and discuss the working intuition.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} The posterior is proportional to
$L(p) \pi(p) \propto p^{s}(1-p)^{n-s} \cdot p^{\alpha-1}
(1-p)^{\beta-1} = p^{\alpha+s-1}(1-p)^{\beta+n-s-1}$, the kernel
of $\mathrm{Beta}(\alpha + s, \beta + n - s)$. The prior
parameters $\alpha$ and $\beta$ act as prior pseudo-counts: a
$\mathrm{Beta}(\alpha, \beta)$ prior has the inferential weight of
$\alpha + \beta$ prior observations, of which $\alpha$ are prior
successes. The posterior mean $(\alpha + s)/(\alpha + \beta + n)$
is a precision-weighted average of the prior mean $\alpha/
(\alpha+\beta)$ and the MLE $s/n$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Show that, in the Gaussian linear regression model with full-rank
design matrix $\mat{X}$, the OLS estimator satisfies
$\E[\hat{\vect{\beta}}] = \vect{\beta}$ and $\Var(\hat{\vect{\beta}})
= \sigma^{2}(\mat{X}^{\transpose}\mat{X})^{-1}$. State, but do not
prove, the Gauss-Markov theorem.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Substitute $\vect{Y} = \mat{X}
\vect{\beta} + \vect{\varepsilon}$ into the closed form:
$\hat{\vect{\beta}} = (\mat{X}^{\transpose}\mat{X})^{-1}
\mat{X}^{\transpose}(\mat{X}\vect{\beta} + \vect{\varepsilon}) =
\vect{\beta} + (\mat{X}^{\transpose}\mat{X})^{-1}
\mat{X}^{\transpose}\vect{\varepsilon}$. Taking expectations and
using $\E[\vect{\varepsilon}] = \vect{0}$ gives unbiasedness.
$\Var(\hat{\vect{\beta}}) = (\mat{X}^{\transpose}\mat{X})^{-1}
\mat{X}^{\transpose}\Var(\vect{\varepsilon})\mat{X}
(\mat{X}^{\transpose}\mat{X})^{-1} = \sigma^{2}(\mat{X}^{\transpose}
\mat{X})^{-1}$, using
$\Var(\vect{\varepsilon}) = \sigma^{2}\mat{I}$ and the
symmetry of $\mat{X}^{\transpose}\mat{X}$. The Gauss-Markov
theorem: among all linear unbiased estimators of $\vect{\beta}$,
the OLS estimator has the smallest variance in the matrix
positive-semidefinite ordering.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Derive the closed form $\hat{\vect{\beta}}_{\mathrm{ridge}}(\lambda)
= (\mat{X}^{\transpose}\mat{X} + \lambda \mat{I})^{-1}
\mat{X}^{\transpose}\vect{Y}$ from the optimisation problem
$\min_{\vect{\beta}} \norm*{\vect{Y} - \mat{X}\vect{\beta}}_{2}^{2}
+ \lambda \norm*{\vect{\beta}}_{2}^{2}$ by setting the gradient
with respect to $\vect{\beta}$ to zero.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Expand the objective as
$\vect{Y}^{\transpose}\vect{Y} - 2\vect{\beta}^{\transpose}
\mat{X}^{\transpose}\vect{Y} + \vect{\beta}^{\transpose}
\mat{X}^{\transpose}\mat{X}\vect{\beta} + \lambda
\vect{\beta}^{\transpose}\vect{\beta}$. Differentiating with
respect to $\vect{\beta}$ and setting to zero:
$-2\mat{X}^{\transpose}\vect{Y} + 2(\mat{X}^{\transpose}\mat{X}
+ \lambda \mat{I})\vect{\beta} = \vect{0}$. Rearranging gives
the claimed closed form. The matrix $\mat{X}^{\transpose}\mat{X}
+ \lambda\mat{I}$ is positive definite for $\lambda > 0$ even
when $\mat{X}^{\transpose}\mat{X}$ is singular, which is the
operational reason ridge always has a unique solution.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Show that, for a generalised likelihood-ratio test under regularity
conditions, the test statistic $-2\ln\Lambda(X)$ has an asymptotic
$\chi^{2}_{r}$ distribution under the null, where $r$ is the
difference in dimension between the alternative and the null
parameter sets. Sketch the argument; do not work the regularity
conditions.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Let $\hat{\theta}$ be the
unrestricted MLE on $\Theta$ and $\hat{\theta}_{0}$ the
restricted MLE on $\Theta_{0}$. Taylor-expand the log-likelihood
$\ell$ around $\hat{\theta}$ to second order:
$\ell(\hat{\theta}_{0}) \approx \ell(\hat{\theta}) - \frac{1}{2}
(\hat{\theta} - \hat{\theta}_{0})^{\transpose} I_{n}(\hat{\theta})
(\hat{\theta} - \hat{\theta}_{0})$, since the first-order term
vanishes at the MLE. Multiplying through by $-2$,
$-2\ln\Lambda = -2[\ell(\hat{\theta}_{0}) - \ell(\hat{\theta})]
\approx (\hat{\theta} - \hat{\theta}_{0})^{\transpose}
I_{n}(\hat{\theta}) (\hat{\theta} - \hat{\theta}_{0})$. Under
$H_{0}$, $\sqrt{n}(\hat{\theta} - \hat{\theta}_{0})$ is
asymptotically Gaussian with covariance $I_{1}(\theta)^{-1}$ on
the $r$-dimensional complement of $\Theta_{0}$ in $\Theta$, so
the quadratic form has an asymptotic $\chi^{2}_{r}$ distribution.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Estimation}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the sample size required to estimate a population mean to
within $\pm 1\,\%$ relative precision at $95\,\%$ confidence, given
a working coefficient of variation $\sigma/\mu = 0.1$. State the
working approximation used.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} The half-width of the $95\,\%$
confidence interval is $1.96 \sigma/\sqrt{n}$. Setting this equal
to $0.01 \mu$ and using $\sigma = 0.1\mu$ gives $1.96 \times
0.1\mu /\sqrt{n} = 0.01\mu$, so $\sqrt{n} = 19.6$, $n \approx
384$. The working rule: at $95\,\%$ confidence, the sample size
scales as $(z_{1-\alpha/2})^{2}(\sigma/\mu)^{2}/r^{2}$ where $r$
is the relative precision; here $(1.96)^{2}(0.1)^{2}/(0.01)^{2}
\approx 384$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the family-wise false-positive rate when an analyst runs
$m = 20$ independent tests at $\alpha = 0.05$ each and reports the
smallest $p$-value as a single test result. State the approximate
formula and the result to two significant figures.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} The probability that at least one of
$m = 20$ independent tests crosses the $\alpha = 0.05$ threshold
under the global null is $1 - (1 - 0.05)^{20} = 1 - (0.95)^{20}$.
With $(0.95)^{20} \approx 0.358$, the family-wise rate is
approximately $0.64$, or $64\,\%$. A nominally $5\,\%$ test
reported as a single result from $20$ trials is in fact about a
$64\,\%$ test.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the half-width of a $95\,\%$ Bayesian credible interval
for a Bernoulli success probability under a Beta$(2, 2)$ prior
after $n = 50$ observations, using the working approximation that
the Beta posterior is approximately Gaussian for moderate
parameters. Compare to the frequentist Wald interval.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Suppose $s = 30$ successes. The
posterior is $\mathrm{Beta}(32, 22)$ with posterior mean $32/54
\approx 0.593$ and posterior variance $\alpha'\beta'/((\alpha' +
\beta')^{2}(\alpha' + \beta' + 1)) = 32 \cdot 22 / (54^{2} \cdot
55) \approx 0.00439$, so posterior standard deviation $\approx
0.0663$. The $95\,\%$ credible half-width is approximately
$1.96 \times 0.0663 \approx 0.130$. The Wald frequentist interval
uses $\hat{p}(1-\hat{p})/n$ with $\hat{p} = 0.6$: standard error
$\sqrt{0.24/50} \approx 0.0693$, half-width $\approx 0.136$.
The two are close because the prior is weak ($\alpha + \beta = 4$
compared to $n = 50$).
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the cross-validated standard error of a $5$-fold
cross-validated mean squared error for a linear regression with
$n = 100$ observations, given an in-sample residual standard
deviation of $\sigma_{\hat{\varepsilon}} = 0.5$. State the working
approximation.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} The per-fold mean squared error
estimate has variance approximately $2\sigma^{4}/n_{\mathrm{fold}}$
under Gaussian residuals; with $n_{\mathrm{fold}} = 20$ and
$\sigma^{2} = 0.25$, the per-fold variance is about $2(0.25)^{2}/
20 = 0.00625$, so per-fold standard deviation $\approx 0.079$.
Averaging across $k = 5$ folds and treating them as approximately
independent gives a standard error of the cross-validated metric
of $0.079/\sqrt{5} \approx 0.035$. The folds are not fully
independent (the training sets overlap), so the actual standard
error is somewhat larger; the working approximation is treated
as a lower bound, and the engineer reports the empirical
across-fold standard deviation alongside.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the value of the regularisation parameter $\lambda$ that
makes a ridge regression's effective degrees of freedom (the trace
of the hat matrix) equal to half the rank $p$ of the design
matrix, when $\mat{X}^{\transpose}\mat{X} = \mat{I}_{p}$. State the
formula used.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} For ridge with orthogonal design,
the hat matrix is $\mat{H} = \mat{X}(\mat{I} + \lambda\mat{I})^{-1}
\mat{X}^{\transpose}$, and the effective degrees of freedom are
$\tr(\mat{H}) = p/(1 + \lambda)$. Setting $p/(1+\lambda) = p/2$
gives $\lambda = 1$. In words, $\lambda$ equal to the
eigenvalue of $\mat{X}^{\transpose}\mat{X}$ halves the effective
dimensionality of the model. For a general design, replace
$\lambda$ by the working ratio of $\lambda$ to the typical
eigenvalue of $\mat{X}^{\transpose}\mat{X}$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the generalisation error inflation factor for a model
with $p = 20$ parameters fit to $n = 100$ observations, given an
in-sample residual mean squared error of $\sigma^{2}_{\hat{\varepsilon}}$.
Use Mallows' $C_{p}$ approximation as the working rule.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} The expected generalisation error
under Mallows' approximation is $\sigma^{2}(1 + 2p/n)$, where
$\sigma^{2}$ is the true noise variance, estimated by
$\sigma^{2}_{\hat{\varepsilon}}\,n/(n-p)$ to correct the in-sample
estimate. With $p = 20$, $n = 100$, the in-sample inflation
factor is $1 + 2(20)/100 = 1.40$. The $40\,\%$ inflation is the
canonical reason an in-sample mean squared error from a
$20$-parameter model on $100$ observations is not a defensible
estimate of generalisation error; cross-validation or hold-out is
required.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Simulation}\pathtag{mastery}

\begin{exercise}
In a programming environment of the reader's choice, simulate
$10\,000$ samples of size $n = 30$ from a Gaussian population with
$\mu = 10$, $\sigma = 2$. For each sample, compute the $95\,\%$
$t$-confidence interval for $\mu$. Verify that the empirical
coverage is approximately $0.95$ and report the sample standard
error of the empirical coverage rate.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} The expected coverage is exactly
$0.95$ under the Gaussian model. The standard error of the
empirical coverage rate, treated as a binomial proportion with
$N = 10\,000$, is $\sqrt{0.95 \times 0.05 / 10000} \approx
0.0022$, so the empirical coverage should land within roughly
$0.945$ to $0.955$ on a typical run. A seed-fixed implementation
with $t_{0.975, 29} \approx 2.045$ produces a value in that band;
a value outside the band on a single run signals an
implementation bug, not a Monte Carlo failure.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Simulate the p-hacking inflation of section 14.7. Generate $1000$
trials in which a true effect of zero exists, run $m = 5$
$t$-tests on different post hoc subgroups (random partitions),
and report the smallest $p$-value as a single result. Verify that
the empirical false-positive rate at nominal $\alpha = 0.05$
exceeds $0.05$ and approaches the analytical $1 - (1-\alpha)^{m}$.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} The analytical false-positive rate
under independent tests is $1 - (1 - 0.05)^{5} \approx 0.226$.
Under random subgroup partitions of the same data, the tests are
positively dependent, so the empirical inflation will land
somewhere between $0.05$ (perfectly correlated tests) and $0.226$
(independent tests). A typical implementation produces an
empirical rate near $0.16$ to $0.20$, which is enough to
demonstrate the inflation and motivate the multiple-comparisons
correction. The standard error of the empirical rate at
$N = 1000$ is approximately $\sqrt{0.2 \times 0.8 / 1000}
\approx 0.013$.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Implement $5$-fold cross-validation of a polynomial regression of
degree $p \in \set*{1, 2, \ldots, 8}$ on $n = 60$ points generated
from $y = \sin(2\pi x) + \varepsilon$ with $\varepsilon \sim
\mathcal{N}(0, 0.2^{2})$ and $x \in [0, 1]$. Plot the training
mean squared error and the cross-validated mean squared error
against $p$. Identify the degree at which the two curves diverge.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Training mean squared error
decreases monotonically with $p$ (the higher-order model contains
the lower-order one). The cross-validated curve typically
reaches a minimum near $p = 3$ to $p = 5$ (a cubic or quartic
captures a half-period of the sine) and rises sharply for $p
\geq 6$ as the model begins to fit the noise. The divergence
point is the operational definition of overfitting under this
loss; the engineer reports the cross-validation curve, not the
training curve, when defending a model-complexity choice.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Simulate the bias-variance trade-off of ridge regression. Generate
$200$ datasets of size $n = 50$ from a linear model with $p = 10$
predictors and $\sigma = 1$. For each dataset, fit ridge with
$\lambda \in \set*{0, 0.1, 1, 10, 100}$. Compute the empirical
bias squared and variance of $\hat{\vect{\beta}}_{1}$ across the
$200$ datasets at each $\lambda$. Verify that the MSE has a
minimum at intermediate $\lambda$.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Variance of
$\hat{\vect{\beta}}_{1}$ across the $200$ runs decreases
monotonically with $\lambda$. Squared bias (squared difference
between the run-averaged $\hat{\vect{\beta}}_{1}$ and the true
$\beta_{1}$) increases monotonically. The sum, the empirical
mean squared error, displays the U-shape sketched in
figure~\ref{fig:vol02:ch14:bias-variance}: it is high at $\lambda
= 0$ (high variance from the near-singular design), reaches a
minimum near $\lambda = 1$ to $\lambda = 10$ depending on the
design's conditioning, and rises again at $\lambda = 100$ as
the bias dominates. The minimum is broad; the precise value of
$\lambda$ that achieves it depends on the seed.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Design}\pathtag{standard}

\begin{exercise}
Design a hypothesis test for a manufacturing process whose target
mean output is $\mu_{0} = 100\,\si{\gram}$. State the null and
alternative, the test statistic, the significance level chosen
in light of the cost of a false alarm and a missed shift, the
sample size required to detect a shift of $\delta = 2\,\si{\gram}$
with power $0.90$ given $\sigma = 5\,\si{\gram}$, and the decision
rule. Justify each choice.

\paragraph{Discussion.} A defensible design uses a two-sided
test $H_{0}: \mu = 100$ versus $H_{1}: \mu \neq 100$ with
statistic $Z = (\bar{X} - 100)/(\sigma/\sqrt{n})$. The
significance level depends on cost: a setting where a false alarm
triggers an inexpensive recalibration tolerates $\alpha = 0.05$;
a setting where it triggers a costly line stop justifies
$\alpha = 0.01$. Sample size at $\alpha = 0.05$, $1-\beta =
0.90$ uses $n \approx ((z_{0.975} + z_{0.90})\sigma/\delta)^{2}
= ((1.96 + 1.28) \times 5 / 2)^{2} \approx 66$. Decision rule:
reject $H_{0}$ if $\abs*{Z} > 1.96$, with the test conducted on
$n = 66$ consecutive units. The design's justification names
both errors and reports the power against the
engineering-relevant shift, not just the significance.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Design a Bayesian update procedure for a quality-control inspector
who tests batches of components for a defect probability $p$. State
the prior, the likelihood, the posterior update rule per batch,
and the reporting threshold (the posterior probability of a defect
rate above some critical level $p^{\star}$ that triggers a process
halt). Justify the prior in terms of working knowledge of the
process.

\paragraph{Discussion.} A working design uses prior $p \sim
\mathrm{Beta}(\alpha_{0}, \beta_{0})$, with $\alpha_{0}, \beta_{0}$
chosen so the prior mean matches the historical defect rate (for
instance $\alpha_{0} = 2$, $\beta_{0} = 198$ for a $1\,\%$ prior
on $n = 200$ historical units). After a batch of $n_{b}$ tests
with $s_{b}$ defects, the posterior is $\mathrm{Beta}(\alpha_{0}
+ s_{\text{cum}}, \beta_{0} + n_{\text{cum}} - s_{\text{cum}})$
with cumulative counts across all batches. The reporting
threshold is the posterior probability $\Pr[p > p^{\star} \mid
\text{data}]$ exceeding a chosen level (e.g.\ $0.10$); when
exceeded, the inspector halts the process. The prior must be
defensible from prior production runs; a flat $\mathrm{Beta}(1,1)$
prior is rarely defensible in a mature process and would treat
the first defect as far more diagnostic than working knowledge
warrants.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Design a $k$-fold cross-validation procedure for a logistic
regression of failure on three sensor readings. State $k$, the
loss function, the pre-processing operations and which sit inside
the loop, the hyperparameter search grid, and the reporting
metric. State the rule by which the cross-validation result is
trusted as an estimate of generalisation.

\paragraph{Discussion.} A defensible design uses stratified
$k = 5$ folds to preserve the failure-rate balance per fold.
Loss is log-loss (proper scoring rule) with a calibration plot
as a diagnostic. Pre-processing inside the loop: per-fold
standardisation of the three sensor readings, per-fold imputation
of any missing values. Outside the loop: time-invariant
operations such as variable renaming and physical-unit
conversion. The hyperparameter search grid is the $\ell_{2}$
regularisation strength $C \in \set{0.01, 0.1, 1, 10, 100}$,
chosen by inner cross-validation if the engineer wants a
fully nested procedure. Reporting metric: mean and across-fold
standard error of held-out log-loss, plus held-out classification
accuracy at a stated decision threshold. The procedure is
trusted as an estimate of generalisation when no operation
adapts to the data outside the loop and when the failure rate
is preserved across folds; if the failure rate is below
$5\,\%$, stratification or a different evaluation metric (such
as AUC) is required.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Design a multiple-comparisons-controlled analysis plan for a
study that runs $m = 50$ simultaneous hypothesis tests on a
genomics-style dataset. State the family-wise control method
(Bonferroni) versus the false-discovery-rate control method
(Benjamini-Hochberg), the significance level chosen, the criterion
by which the analyst decides between the two methods, and the
report format.

\paragraph{Discussion.} The choice between Bonferroni and
Benjamini-Hochberg follows the cost structure. Bonferroni
controls the family-wise error rate at the cost of low power
when $m$ is large; it is appropriate when a single false rejection
is itself costly (a confirmatory finding that triggers expensive
follow-up). Benjamini-Hochberg controls the expected proportion
of false rejections among rejections; it is appropriate when
the cost of a missed true rejection outweighs the cost of one
or two false ones (exploratory genomics). At $m = 50$ and
$\alpha = 0.05$, Bonferroni rejects at per-test threshold
$0.001$; BH rejects up to the largest $k$ with $p_{(k)} \leq k
\times 0.001$. The report names the chosen method, the
per-comparison threshold, the number of tests run, the number
rejected, and the false-discovery-rate or family-wise-error
guarantee. The unreported $m$ is the disclosure failure that
inflates the literature
\cite{method:simmons-nelson-simonsohn-2011}.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Diagnosis and reverse engineering}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
A colleague reports a regression with in-sample $R^{2} = 0.99$ and
no cross-validated error. Identify the three things that should
make the engineer suspicious of the report and propose follow-up
questions.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Three suspicions are warranted.
First, the in-sample $R^{2}$ is large because the model has many
parameters relative to $n$, and the cross-validated $R^{2}$ may
be far smaller. Ask for $n$, $p$, and the cross-validated
metric. Second, the predictors may include leakage (a variable
that is a proxy for or downstream of the response). Ask for the
full predictor list and the temporal or causal relationship
between each predictor and the response. Third, the response
variable may have been transformed in a way that inflates
$R^{2}$ artificially (logging a heavily skewed variable can
move $R^{2}$ from $0.5$ to $0.95$ without improving prediction
of the original quantity). Ask for the working units of the
response and the metric reported on the original scale.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A colleague reports a $p$-value of $0.03$ for a single test from a
study that examined ``several outcomes.'' Identify the additional
information the engineer needs to interpret the $p$-value and the
correction that would bring the report into the working discipline.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} The engineer needs $m$ (the number
of outcomes examined), whether the analysis was pre-registered,
and which outcomes were planned versus selected after the data
were seen. With $m = 5$ untargeted tests at nominal $\alpha =
0.05$, the family-wise rate is $1 - 0.95^{5} \approx 0.23$, and
the reported $p = 0.03$ is consistent with a global null. The
correction depends on the framing: Bonferroni gives a corrected
$p$-value of $\min(5 \times 0.03, 1) = 0.15$, no longer
significant at $\alpha = 0.05$; Benjamini-Hochberg at $\alpha =
0.05$ requires $p_{(k)} \leq k \times 0.01$ for the ranked
list. If the study was not pre-registered and the chosen
outcome was selected for promise, the correct interpretation is
``working hypothesis to be replicated,'' not ``established
effect.''
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A logistic regression's residual deviance is far smaller than the
degrees of freedom would predict under the model. Diagnose the
two most likely causes (over-fit due to too many predictors;
contamination of the training set with test data) and propose the
diagnostic that distinguishes the two.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Under a correctly specified
logistic model, $\E[\text{residual deviance}] \approx
\text{residual degrees of freedom}$. A deviance much smaller than
the degrees of freedom indicates the model is fitting too
closely. Over-fit by too many predictors shows up as a deviance
that recovers to the expected level under $k$-fold cross-validation
(the held-out deviance is much larger than the in-sample). Data
leakage shows up as a deviance that remains small under
cross-validation; the model has access to the response within the
training fold through a leaked variable. The distinguishing
diagnostic: refit on the cross-validation training folds and
compare in-sample versus held-out deviance per fold. A large gap
points to over-fit; a small gap with small overall deviance
points to leakage.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A ridge regression chosen by cross-validation produces
$\hat{\vect{\beta}}_{\mathrm{ridge}}$ that disagrees with the OLS
estimator $\hat{\vect{\beta}}_{\mathrm{OLS}}$ in sign on one
coefficient. Diagnose what this sign reversal indicates about the
design matrix's conditioning and the variance of the OLS
coefficient. Propose the diagnostic that confirms the diagnosis.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} A sign reversal under ridge
indicates that the OLS coefficient was estimated with high
variance, large enough that the sign is not stable across small
perturbations of the data. The underlying cause is collinearity:
two or more predictors carry similar information, the design
matrix has a small eigenvalue, and the corresponding OLS
coefficient absorbs a noisy linear combination that ridge
shrinks toward zero. Confirming diagnostics: compute the
variance inflation factors (VIF) of each predictor; a VIF above
10 confirms collinearity. Examine the singular values of
$\mat{X}$; a condition number above $30$ indicates an
ill-conditioned design. Refit OLS on bootstrapped resamples and
inspect the resampled distribution of the coefficient; a wide
distribution that straddles zero confirms the OLS estimate's
sign is not informative.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Failure analysis}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
Argue, in 200 to 300 words, why the reproducibility rate reported
in the 2015 Reproducibility Project: Psychology should change the
way an engineer reads a single published study with $p = 0.04$.
Cite \cite{method:open-science-collaboration-2015} and connect the
argument to the disclosure rule of section 14.7.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} A defensible argument: the Open
Science Collaboration found that only $36\,\%$ of originally
significant findings replicated, with replication effects
averaging half the original. A single published $p = 0.04$ is
therefore consistent with one of two regimes: a true effect
that will replicate at smaller magnitude, or a false-positive
arising from p-hacking, the garden of forking paths, or
publication bias. The published $p$-value alone does not
distinguish the two. The disclosure rule of section 14.7
requires the engineer to ask what tests were run, whether
pre-registration was in place, whether the analysis was the
planned one, and whether the effect size carries a confidence
interval that excludes a working null. A $p = 0.04$ that
survives a multiple-comparisons correction, was specified before
the data were seen, comes from a high-powered design, and is
accompanied by an effect-size interval that excludes negligible
effects is a different working object from a $p = 0.04$ that
appears without context. The engineer who sizes a load-bearing
design against the second class of evidence is treating a single
observation as confirmation, which the empirical replication
record warns against. The working compromise is to read a
single $p = 0.04$ as a working hypothesis to be replicated, not
as a result to be acted on, until the disclosures are made and
the effect survives an honest correction.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
Argue, in 200 to 300 words, why pre-registration alone does not
solve the garden-of-forking-paths problem
\cite{method:gelman-loken-forking-paths}, and identify the
additional discipline that a reader should ask of an analysis to
defend its $p$-value.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} Pre-registration commits the
analyst to a primary test and a primary outcome before the data
are seen. The forking-paths argument shows that even
pre-registered analyses can be undermined when the
pre-registration is loose or when secondary analyses absorb the
flexibility that the primary analysis was supposed to remove.
A pre-registration that says ``a $t$-test will be conducted''
without specifying covariate adjustment, outlier handling, or
the subgroup of interest still permits substantial post hoc
flexibility. The additional discipline a reader should require:
the pre-registration is specific (test, model, covariates,
outlier rule, subgroup, decision threshold); the analysis report
explicitly enumerates every deviation from the pre-registration
and its justification; every secondary analysis is reported
alongside the primary, with the same level of detail; the
underlying data and analysis code are available for independent
replication. Pre-registration in the absence of these companion
disciplines reduces but does not eliminate the inflation. The
defensible report distinguishes confirmatory results (specified
in advance, all deviations disclosed) from exploratory results
(discovered in the data, reported as hypotheses for replication,
not as established effects).
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A cross-validated logistic regression on a diagnostic-imaging
dataset shows a held-out accuracy of $0.95$. On a new dataset from
a different hospital, the accuracy drops to $0.71$. Argue, in 200
to 300 words, whether the gap is consistent with overfitting,
with covariate shift, or with leakage of patient-identifier
information into the original training set, and propose the
diagnostic that distinguishes the three.

\paragraph{Solution sketch.} The gap is large enough to demand
investigation, and all three explanations are consistent with
the observation. Overfitting would imply the original
cross-validation was honest but the model has memorised
within-dataset noise that does not generalise. Diagnostic:
re-fit on the original dataset with stronger regularisation and
re-evaluate on the new hospital; if accuracy recovers, the
original was overfit. Covariate shift means the predictor
distribution differs between the two hospitals (different
scanner, different patient population, different acquisition
protocol). Diagnostic: compare the marginal distributions of
each predictor across the two datasets; substantial
distributional differences confirm covariate shift. Patient-
identifier leakage (the model learned to identify individual
patients via incidental features, and the cross-validation folds
contained multiple images per patient) implies the original
cross-validation was contaminated. Diagnostic: split folds by
patient rather than by image and re-run the original
cross-validation; a drop to near $0.71$ confirms leakage. The
three diagnostics together identify the failure mode. The
cross-hospital evaluation is the discipline that surfaces all
three; an engineer who reports only the within-hospital
cross-validation accuracy is reporting a number that does not
defend deployment across institutions.
\end{exercise}

\subsection*{Judgment}\pathtag{standard}

\begin{exercise}
A research group reports a Bayesian credible interval based on a
strong prior whose source is not stated. Argue, in one paragraph,
what the engineer should ask before treating the interval as
load-bearing for design and what disclosures should accompany
every Bayesian report.

\paragraph{Discussion.} A strong prior with unstated source is
the Bayesian analogue of an undisclosed analysis choice. The
engineer should ask: what is the parametric form of the prior;
what is its effective sample size (the prior's pseudo-count
relative to the data); what evidence underwrites the prior
(earlier studies with citations, physical constraints with
derivations, expert elicitation with the elicitation protocol);
and how does the posterior change under a flat or weakly
informative prior. A Bayesian report that does not disclose the
prior, its effective sample size, the prior-elicitation
provenance, and a sensitivity analysis is not reportable. The
credible interval inherits whatever the prior pulls it toward;
without the disclosures the engineer cannot distinguish a
genuine posterior update from a prior assertion dressed in
data.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A vendor reports a logistic regression's classification accuracy
on the data the model was trained on, with no cross-validation.
Argue, in one paragraph, why this is the wrong figure to compare
across vendors and what figure the engineer should request
instead.

\paragraph{Discussion.} In-sample classification accuracy is
inflated by the model's flexibility relative to the dataset.
Across vendors with different model complexities, the more
flexible model wins on in-sample accuracy almost regardless of
its actual generalisation performance; a comparison made on
in-sample accuracy systematically prefers overfit models. The
engineer should request held-out accuracy on a fresh test set
that the vendor never saw during training and hyperparameter
tuning. Better: held-out accuracy on a benchmark dataset that
the engineer holds in escrow and only releases for the
evaluation. Best: held-out accuracy plus a calibration plot and
held-out log-loss; accuracy alone misses the model's confidence
calibration, which matters for downstream cost-sensitive
decisions. The figure to compare is the one the vendor cannot
have optimised against.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
A statistical consultant proposes choosing the regularisation
parameter $\lambda$ in a ridge regression by minimising training
error. Argue, in one paragraph, why this choice does not control
generalisation error and what the consultant should do instead.
Reference the bias-variance trade-off of section 14.1.

\paragraph{Discussion.} Training error decreases monotonically
as $\lambda \to 0$: with no penalty the OLS fit minimises the
residual sum of squares by construction. Choosing $\lambda$ to
minimise training error therefore always picks $\lambda = 0$,
which is OLS, and forfeits the variance reduction the
bias-variance decomposition promises. The generalisation error,
by contrast, has a minimum at some $\lambda^{\star} > 0$
whenever the design is ill-conditioned (figure
\ref{fig:vol02:ch14:bias-variance}). The consultant should
choose $\lambda$ by minimising the cross-validated error across
a grid (typically logarithmically spaced from $10^{-3}$ to
$10^{3}$ times the typical eigenvalue of $\mat{X}^{\transpose}
\mat{X}$), report the cross-validated curve, and apply the
one-standard-error rule for the final choice. Training error is
the wrong target for any hyperparameter that exists to
trade-off bias against variance, including $\lambda$, polynomial
degree, neural-network width, and regression-tree depth.
\end{exercise}

\begin{exercise}
An analyst presents a study reporting an effect size of $0.6$
standard deviations and a $p$-value of $0.04$ from $n = 24$. The
engineer is asked to use the result as an input to a load-bearing
design decision. Argue, in 200 to 300 words, what additional
evidence the engineer should require before using the result, in
light of the replication-rate evidence in
\cite{method:open-science-collaboration-2015,method:errington-cancer-biology-2021}
and the HARKing literature \cite{method:kerr-harking-1998}.

\paragraph{Discussion.} The numbers describe a low-power
study: at $\alpha = 0.05$ and $d = 0.6$, $n = 24$ gives power
of roughly $0.45$. A barely significant result from a low-power
study, with no replication, is exactly the configuration the
replication-rate evidence flags as most likely to evaporate on
re-analysis: the Open Science Collaboration found replication
effects averaging half the original, the Errington cancer-biology
project found a factor-of-three attenuation, and both studies
emphasised low original power as a predictor of non-replication.
The HARKing literature adds that a single significant result from
a study that examined multiple outcomes may have been selected
after the fact, inflating the apparent effect size. Before
using the result, the engineer should require: the
pre-registration (or its absence and the consequences),
the full list of analyses run, the cross-validated or held-out
effect-size estimate, at least one independent replication, and
the effect-size confidence interval (which at $n = 24$ is wide
enough that the lower bound may include effects too small for
the design to depend on). If no replication exists, the
engineer should size the design against the lower confidence
bound rather than the point estimate, accept that the
load-bearing claim cannot rest on this single study, and
commission or wait for an independent replication. The
disclosure rule of section 14.7 is the working test.
\end{exercise}
