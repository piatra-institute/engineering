\caption[Minibatch variance and cost against batch size]{The two
curves an engineer balances when choosing a minibatch size. The
gradient variance (solid) falls as $\sigma^{2}/b$: doubling the batch
halves the variance, an exact $1/b$ law from independent sampling. On
the variance-per-flop ledger this is a wash, because the arithmetic
cost of a batch gradient rises in proportion to $b$. The decisive
curve is the wall-clock cost per step on a modern accelerator
(dashed), which stays nearly flat while the batch is too small to
saturate the arithmetic units and only rises once the hardware is
fully occupied. The working batch size (roughly $32$ to $512$ in 2026
practice) sits at the knee where the cost per step is still cheap but
the variance has already dropped by one to two orders of magnitude.
The batch size is a hardware-utilisation decision wearing the costume
of a statistics decision.}
\label{fig:vol02:ch15:sgd-variance}
