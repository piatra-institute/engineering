\begin{scope}[shift={(0,0)}]
  \draw[->, thick] (-0.2, 0) -- (5.2, 0) node[right, font=\scriptsize] {$x$};

  % f(x) = x + 1/x, sampled; scale y by 0.55 to fit.
  \draw[engblue, very thick, samples=80, domain=0.28:4.6]
    plot ({\x}, {0.55*(\x + 1/\x)});
  \fill[engorange] (1.0, {0.55*2}) circle (1.8pt);
  \node[engorange, anchor=south west, font=\scriptsize] at (1.05, {0.55*2+0.05})

  \node[anchor=north, font=\scriptsize] at (2.6, -0.35)

\end{scope}

\begin{scope}[shift={(7.2,0)}]

\caption[The log transform that convexifies a geometric
program]{The change of variables that turns a geometric program into a
convex program. A posynomial such as $f(x) = x + x^{-1}$ (left) is not
convex in $x$: the second derivative $2x^{-3}$ is positive here, but a
general posynomial with several monomials need not be convex in the
original variables. Substituting $y = \log x$, so $x = e^{y}$, sends
every monomial $c\,x^{a}$ to $c\,e^{a y}$ and the whole posynomial to a
sum of exponentials of affine functions, which is convex in $y$ (right).
The minimum at $x^{*} = 1$ becomes the minimum at $y^{*} = 0$ of the
convex bowl $e^{y} + e^{-y} = 2\cosh y$. The transform is exact and
reversible, so a geometric program in any number of variables, however
nonconvex it looks in the design variables, is a convex program in the
log-variables and solves in polynomial time. Recognising that a cost
model is a posynomial is the whole difficulty; the solver follows.}
