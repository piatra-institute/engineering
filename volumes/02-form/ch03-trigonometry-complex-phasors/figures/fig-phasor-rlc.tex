\caption[Series RLC phasor diagram and time-domain
waveforms]{Series RLC circuit at $50\,\si{\hertz}$ with
$R = 100\,\si{\ohm}$, $L = 100\,\si{\milli\henry}$,
$C = 10\,\si{\micro\farad}$, $V_{m} = 10\,\si{\volt}$, well
below the resonance frequency
$\omega_{0}/(2\pi) = 1/(2\pi\sqrt{LC}) \approx 159\,\si{\hertz}$.
Upper panel: phasor diagram in the complex plane. The source
phasor $\tilde V_{s}$ defines the real axis. The current phasor
$\tilde I$ leads the source by $70.8^{\circ}$ because the
capacitive reactance dominates the inductive reactance at this
frequency; $\tilde V_{R}$ is parallel to $\tilde I$, $\tilde
V_{L}$ leads $\tilde I$ by $90^{\circ}$, and $\tilde V_{C}$ lags
$\tilde I$ by $90^{\circ}$. The vector chain
$\tilde V_{R} + \tilde V_{L} + \tilde V_{C}$ closes at $\tilde
V_{s}$, as Kirchhoff's voltage law requires. The capacitor
voltage is larger in magnitude than the source ($10.46$ V versus
$10$ V): a common feature of below-resonance operation and not an
arithmetic error. Lower panel: the same data as time-domain
waveforms over two periods of the source. The current peaks
$70.8^{\circ}$ before the source; the capacitor voltage trails
the current by $90^{\circ}$, putting it ahead of the source by
$70.8^{\circ}-90^{\circ}=-19.2^{\circ}$. The phasor picture
displays the phase relations geometrically; the time-domain
picture displays them as the lead and lag visible to an
oscilloscope.}
\label{fig:vol02:ch03:phasor-rlc}
\end{figure}
