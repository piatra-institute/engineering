% iteration exploits. A shift mu near an eigenvalue turns the nearest
% eigenvalue into the dominant one of the shifted-inverse matrix.

\caption[Shift-and-invert spectral transformation]{Inverse iteration
runs the power method on $(\mat{A}-\mu\mat{I})\inv$, whose eigenvalues
are $1/(\lambda_i-\mu)$. The transformation (blue) has a pole at the
shift $\mu = 4.6$ (dashed), so an eigenvalue close to the shift is
mapped to a large-magnitude image and becomes the dominant eigenvalue
of the shifted-inverse matrix. For the spectrum $\{1,3,5,7\}$ the
eigenvalue $\lambda = 5$, nearest the shift, is magnified to $2.5$,
larger in magnitude than any other image, so power iteration on the
shifted inverse converges to its eigenvector. Moving the shift toward
a different eigenvalue retargets the method to that one, which is why
an engineer who wants the mode nearest a known resonance sets $\mu$
to that resonance and lets the pole do the selection. The near-
singularity of $\mat{A}-\mu\mat{I}$ that a shift close to an
eigenvalue creates is exactly the effect that accelerates convergence,
not a numerical hazard to avoid.}
\label{fig:vol02:ch10:inverse-iteration-target}
\end{figure}
