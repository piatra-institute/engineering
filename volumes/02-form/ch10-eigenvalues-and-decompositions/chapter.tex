\noindent
Chapter 9 read a matrix as a linear map between two coordinate
spaces and asked how to solve, project, and condition it. The next
question is internal. Given a square matrix, are there directions
the matrix leaves alone, scaling them but not rotating them? When
such directions exist and span the input space, the matrix is a
diagonal scaling in disguise. When they do not, the matrix has
structure that diagonal scaling cannot describe, and the engineer
needs a different decomposition. The singular value decomposition
covers every matrix without exception and is the workhorse of this
chapter and the rest of the volume.

\section{Eigenvalues and eigenvectors}\pathtag{core}

A square matrix $\mat{A} \in \R^{n \times n}$ acts on a vector
$\vect{x} \in \R^{n}$ by $\vect{x} \mapsto \mat{A}\vect{x}$. For most
$\vect{x}$, the output points in a different direction from the
input. For a privileged few, the matrix only scales: $\mat{A}
\vect{x} = \lambda \vect{x}$ for some scalar $\lambda$. These
privileged directions are the matrix's eigenvectors, and the scaling
factors are its eigenvalues.

\subsection{Definition and first identification}

An \engterm{eigenvector} of $\mat{A}$ is a nonzero vector $\vect{x}
\in \C^{n}$ satisfying $\mat{A}\vect{x} = \lambda \vect{x}$ for
some scalar $\lambda \in \C$. The scalar $\lambda$ is the
corresponding \engterm{eigenvalue}. We work over $\C$ because a real
matrix may have complex eigenvalues even when every entry is real,
as section 10.7 makes precise. The eigenvalue equation rearranges to

\[
(\mat{A} - \lambda \mat{I}) \vect{x} = \vect{0},
\]

which has a nonzero solution if and only if $\mat{A} - \lambda
\mat{I}$ is singular, that is, if and only if its determinant
vanishes. The \engterm{characteristic polynomial} of $\mat{A}$ is

\[
p_{\mat{A}}(\lambda) = \det(\mat{A} - \lambda \mat{I}),
\]

a polynomial of degree $n$ in $\lambda$ with leading coefficient
$(-1)^{n}$. Its roots are the eigenvalues of $\mat{A}$. The
fundamental theorem of algebra guarantees $n$ roots in $\C$, counted
with multiplicity. A real matrix has eigenvalues that are real, or
that come in complex conjugate pairs.

For a $2 \times 2$ matrix the characteristic polynomial is

\[
p_{\mat{A}}(\lambda) = \lambda^{2} - (\tr \mat{A}) \lambda
+ \det \mat{A},
\]

with $\tr \mat{A} = A_{11} + A_{22}$ the sum of diagonal entries.
The trace is the sum of eigenvalues; the determinant is their
product. Both relations generalise: for any $n \times n$ matrix,
$\tr \mat{A} = \sum_{i} \lambda_{i}$ and $\det \mat{A} = \prod_{i}
\lambda_{i}$. The two invariants summarise the spectrum from
quantities that are cheap to compute directly from the entries.

\subsection{Geometric and algebraic multiplicity}

An eigenvalue $\lambda$ is a root of the characteristic polynomial.
Its \engterm{algebraic multiplicity} is the multiplicity of that
root, the exponent of $(\lambda_{0} - \lambda)$ in the factorisation
of $p_{\mat{A}}$. The set of vectors satisfying $(\mat{A} -
\lambda_{0} \mat{I}) \vect{x} = \vect{0}$ is the \engterm{eigenspace}
of $\lambda_{0}$, written $E_{\lambda_{0}} = \nul(\mat{A} -
\lambda_{0} \mat{I})$. Its dimension is the \engterm{geometric
multiplicity} of $\lambda_{0}$.

The geometric multiplicity is at most the algebraic multiplicity. A
$3 \times 3$ matrix with characteristic polynomial $(\lambda - 2)^{3}$
has algebraic multiplicity three at $\lambda = 2$. Its geometric
multiplicity at $2$ is one, two, or three. When it is three, the
matrix is the diagonal $2 \mat{I}$ in some basis. When it is one,
the matrix has only a one-dimensional eigenspace and is
\engterm{defective}: it cannot be diagonalised. The defective case
is where the engineer's panic in section 10.7 begins.

\subsection{Computation by hand for small matrices}

For $n \leq 4$, the characteristic polynomial is short and the
roots can be found by inspection or by the quadratic, cubic, or
quartic formula. Beyond $n = 4$ the polynomial-roots route is
useless. The Abel-Ruffini theorem rules out closed-form solution by
radicals for general degree-five and higher polynomials, and even
for $n = 4$ the formulas are numerically poor. Production
eigensolvers do not factor the characteristic polynomial. They
apply iterative methods (QR algorithm with shifts) directly to
$\mat{A}$ and read off eigenvalues from the limit of the iteration
\cite[ch.~25]{text:trefethen-bau1997}. The reader works small cases
by hand to internalise the structure; for any $n$ above about $4$
the reader uses a library routine.

\begin{estimation}
\textbf{Question.} A spring-mass system has two equal masses $m$
connected to walls and to each other by three identical springs of
stiffness $k$, in a line. The equations of motion are
$m \ddot{x}_{i} = $ net spring force on mass $i$. The natural
frequencies of vibration are $\omega = \sqrt{\lambda}$, where
$\lambda$ are eigenvalues of $\mat{K}/m$ for the stiffness matrix
$\mat{K}$. Estimate the ratio of the highest to the lowest natural
frequency.

\smallskip
\textit{Estimate, before reading on.}

\smallskip
The two physical modes are: the masses moving in phase (slow,
because the centre spring does not stretch), and the masses moving
out of phase (fast, because the centre spring stretches double). In
phase, each mass sees only its wall spring; the restoring stiffness
is $k$, and $\omega_{1}^{2} = k/m$. Out of phase, each mass sees
its wall spring plus twice the centre spring; the restoring stiffness
is $3k$, and $\omega_{2}^{2} = 3k/m$. The ratio is
$\omega_{2}/\omega_{1} = \sqrt{3} \approx 1.7$. The spectral
calculation in section 10.5's exercises confirms the estimate.
\end{estimation}

The estimation reads the modes off the geometry; the algebra
confirms them. This is the order in which the engineer should learn
to think. Modes first, eigenvalues second. The eigenvalues attach
numbers to a structure the geometry already names.

\subsection{Worked example: a $2 \times 2$ matrix}

Consider

\[
\mat{A} = \begin{pmatrix} 4 & 1 \\ 2 & 3 \end{pmatrix}.
\]

The trace is $7$ and the determinant is $4 \cdot 3 - 1 \cdot 2 =
10$. The characteristic polynomial is $\lambda^{2} - 7\lambda + 10 =
(\lambda - 5)(\lambda - 2)$. The eigenvalues are $\lambda_{1} = 5$
and $\lambda_{2} = 2$, both real and distinct.

The eigenspace at $\lambda_{1} = 5$ comes from the null space of

\[
\mat{A} - 5 \mat{I} = \begin{pmatrix} -1 & 1 \\ 2 & -2 \end{pmatrix},
\]

whose rows are dependent. The condition $-x_{1} + x_{2} = 0$ gives
$\vect{v}_{1} = (1, 1)\transpose$ as a basis for $E_{5}$. Similarly,
the eigenspace at $\lambda_{2} = 2$ comes from the null space of

\[
\mat{A} - 2 \mat{I} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 1 \\ 2 & 1 \end{pmatrix},
\]

with condition $2 x_{1} + x_{2} = 0$, giving $\vect{v}_{2} = (1, -2)
\transpose$ as a basis for $E_{2}$. The two eigenvectors are
linearly independent. They span $\R^{2}$. The matrix is
diagonalisable, and section 10.2 shows what the diagonalisation
buys.

\input{volumes/02-form/ch10-eigenvalues-and-decompositions/figures/fig-eigenvector-action.tex}

\Cref{fig:vol02:ch10:eigenvector-action} shows the geometry directly.
A fan of input vectors of equal length, each at a different angle,
rotates as well as stretches under multiplication by $\mat{A}$;
almost every input direction is mapped to a new direction. The two
red arrows are the eigenvectors $\vect{v}_{1}$ and $\vect{v}_{2}$,
and their images $5\vect{v}_{1}$ and $2\vect{v}_{2}$ point along the
same lines (one with reversed orientation if the eigenvalue is
negative). The diagonalisation of the next section is the algebraic
restatement of this picture: change to the eigenbasis, and the
rotation disappears.

\begin{archetype}[Scaling]
The eigenvalues are the scalings the matrix performs on its
preferred directions. Volume I introduced scaling as the dimensional
discipline of how a quantity changes under change of unit; here the
archetype recurs in matrix form. A linear map with eigenvalues
$\lambda_{1}, \ldots, \lambda_{n}$ is, in the right basis, the
diagonal scaling $\diag(\lambda_{1}, \ldots, \lambda_{n})$. Every
question the engineer asks about a square matrix that does not
depend on basis, the determinant, the trace, the spectral norm,
the conditioning, the stability, is a question about its spectrum.
\end{archetype}

\section{Diagonalisation}\pathtag{core}

A square matrix $\mat{A}$ is \engterm{diagonalisable} when there
exist an invertible $\mat{S}$ and a diagonal $\mat{\Lambda}$ such
that $\mat{A} = \mat{S} \mat{\Lambda} \mat{S}\inv$. The columns of
$\mat{S}$ are eigenvectors of $\mat{A}$; the diagonal entries of
$\mat{\Lambda}$ are the corresponding eigenvalues. Diagonalisation
exists exactly when the eigenvectors form a basis of $\R^{n}$ (or
$\C^{n}$).

\subsection{When diagonalisation works}

The simplest sufficient condition: if $\mat{A}$ has $n$ distinct
eigenvalues, it is diagonalisable. Eigenvectors corresponding to
distinct eigenvalues are linearly independent, so $n$ distinct
eigenvalues yield $n$ independent eigenvectors and a basis. The
$2 \times 2$ worked example of section 10.1 had distinct eigenvalues
$5$ and $2$, and its eigenvectors $(1, 1)\transpose$ and $(1, -2)
\transpose$ form an invertible $\mat{S}$.

A second sufficient condition: if $\mat{A}$ is real symmetric, it is
diagonalisable, with real eigenvalues and an orthonormal basis of
eigenvectors. This is the \engterm{spectral theorem} for symmetric
matrices. We quote it; the proof uses the Courant-Fischer
characterisation of eigenvalues and is one page in any text
\cite[ch.~6]{text:strang2016}. The orthonormal eigenbasis is the
fact that powers all of structural dynamics, principal component
analysis, and the spectral methods of section 10.4.

A third sufficient condition: if $\mat{A}$ is normal ($\mat{A}\transpose
\mat{A} = \mat{A}\mat{A}\transpose$ for real, $\mat{A}^{*}\mat{A} =
\mat{A}\mat{A}^{*}$ for complex), it is unitarily diagonalisable.
Symmetric matrices are normal; orthogonal matrices are normal;
skew-symmetric matrices are normal. Many matrices the engineer meets
in practice are normal because of physical symmetry.

\subsection{When diagonalisation fails}

A matrix fails to diagonalise when it has fewer linearly independent
eigenvectors than its size. The canonical example is the Jordan
block

\[
\mat{J} = \begin{pmatrix} \lambda & 1 \\ 0 & \lambda \end{pmatrix}.
\]

The characteristic polynomial is $(\lambda - \lambda)^{2}$, with a
single eigenvalue $\lambda$ of algebraic multiplicity two. The
eigenspace is the null space of $\mat{J} - \lambda \mat{I} =
\begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix}$, which is one
dimensional, spanned by $(1, 0)\transpose$. The geometric multiplicity
is one. The Jordan block is defective.

The general fact is the \engterm{Jordan canonical form}: every
square matrix is similar to a block-diagonal matrix whose blocks
are Jordan blocks. We quote the result and do not prove it. For
working purposes the engineer should know that defective matrices
exist, that the failure is not pathological but generic in
parameter-dependent settings (a parameter sweep that crosses an
eigenvalue collision typically produces a defective matrix at the
collision point), and that production code uses the Schur
decomposition (section 10.7) rather than Jordan form because the
Schur decomposition is numerically stable and the Jordan form is
not.

\subsection{What diagonalisation buys}

When $\mat{A} = \mat{S} \mat{\Lambda} \mat{S}\inv$, powers of
$\mat{A}$ become diagonal in the eigenbasis. For any positive
integer $k$,

\[
\mat{A}^{k} = \mat{S} \mat{\Lambda}^{k} \mat{S}\inv,
\]

and $\mat{\Lambda}^{k}$ is the diagonal matrix with entries
$\lambda_{i}^{k}$. A computation that would have cost $\oom{n^{3}}$
flops per matrix multiplication, repeated $k$ times for $\oom{k
n^{3}}$ flops, becomes $\oom{n^{3}}$ flops for the diagonalisation
plus $\oom{k n}$ flops for the diagonal powers and $\oom{n^{3}}$
flops for the change of basis. The same identity extends to matrix
functions: $f(\mat{A}) = \mat{S} f(\mat{\Lambda}) \mat{S}\inv$ for
any function $f$ that makes sense on the eigenvalues, where
$f(\mat{\Lambda})$ applies $f$ entrywise on the diagonal. The
matrix exponential $e^{\mat{A} t} = \mat{S} e^{\mat{\Lambda} t}
\mat{S}\inv$ is the workhorse of linear-system response (Volume III
Chapter 7 closed-form ODE solutions; Volume IX Chapter 4
state-space dynamics), and its computation reduces to $n$ scalar
exponentials when $\mat{A}$ is diagonalisable.

\subsection{Worked example: powers and the matrix exponential}

For the $\mat{A}$ of section 10.1 with $\mat{S} = \begin{pmatrix} 1
& 1 \\ 1 & -2 \end{pmatrix}$ and $\mat{\Lambda} = \diag(5, 2)$, the
ten-th power is

\[
\mat{A}^{10} = \mat{S} \diag(5^{10}, 2^{10}) \mat{S}\inv.
\]

The dominant entry is $5^{10} = 9{,}765{,}625$, and $2^{10} = 1024$
is negligible by comparison. The asymptotic behaviour of $\mat{A}^{k}
\vect{x}$ for large $k$ is dictated by the dominant eigenvalue and
its eigenvector. This observation is the seed of the power iteration
algorithm of section 10.6.

\begin{mastery}
A non-diagonalisable matrix admits a Jordan decomposition $\mat{A} =
\mat{S} \mat{J} \mat{S}\inv$, with $\mat{J}$ block diagonal in
Jordan blocks. Powers and exponentials of $\mat{J}$ have closed
forms but include polynomial factors in $k$ alongside the geometric
$\lambda^{k}$. For a $2 \times 2$ Jordan block at eigenvalue
$\lambda$, $\mat{J}^{k} = \begin{pmatrix} \lambda^{k} & k \lambda^{k-1}
\\ 0 & \lambda^{k} \end{pmatrix}$. The polynomial growth in $k$ is
the signature of a defective system; it shows up in physical
dynamics as a transient that grows like $t e^{\lambda t}$ alongside
the pure exponential. We return to it in section 10.7.
\end{mastery}

\section{The singular value decomposition}\pathtag{core}

The diagonalisation of section 10.2 applies only to square matrices,
and only to those with a basis of eigenvectors. The singular value
decomposition (SVD) applies to every matrix, square or rectangular,
real or complex, full rank or rank deficient, and produces a
factorisation that is the engineer's first universal tool for
linear algebra.

\subsection{Existence and structure}

\begin{theorem}[Singular value decomposition]
Every matrix $\mat{A} \in \R^{m \times n}$ admits a factorisation

\[
\mat{A} = \mat{U} \mat{\Sigma} \mat{V}\transpose,
\]

where $\mat{U} \in \R^{m \times m}$ and $\mat{V} \in \R^{n \times n}$
are orthogonal matrices, and $\mat{\Sigma} \in \R^{m \times n}$ has
non-negative entries on its main diagonal and zeros elsewhere. The
diagonal entries $\sigma_{1} \geq \sigma_{2} \geq \cdots \geq
\sigma_{p} \geq 0$, where $p = \min(m, n)$, are the
\engterm{singular values} of $\mat{A}$. The number of nonzero
singular values equals $\rank \mat{A}$.
\end{theorem}

The proof is constructive and short. The Gram matrix $\mat{A}\transpose
\mat{A}$ is symmetric and positive semidefinite, so by the spectral
theorem it has an orthonormal eigenbasis $\{\vect{v}_{1}, \ldots,
\vect{v}_{n}\}$ with non-negative eigenvalues $\sigma_{i}^{2}$,
ordered $\sigma_{1}^{2} \geq \cdots \geq \sigma_{n}^{2} \geq 0$.
Define $\sigma_{i} = \sqrt{\sigma_{i}^{2}}$, and for each $i$ with
$\sigma_{i} > 0$ set $\vect{u}_{i} = \mat{A} \vect{v}_{i} / \sigma_{i}$.
The vectors $\{\vect{u}_{i}\}$ are orthonormal in $\R^{m}$. Extend
to an orthonormal basis $\{\vect{u}_{1}, \ldots, \vect{u}_{m}\}$ by
adding any orthonormal basis of the orthogonal complement of the
span. Stack the bases as columns of $\mat{U}$ and $\mat{V}$, and
verify $\mat{A} = \mat{U} \mat{\Sigma} \mat{V}\transpose$ entry by
entry. The full proof appears in any numerical-linear-algebra text
\cite[ch.~4]{text:trefethen-bau1997}.

\subsection{The four fundamental subspaces, revisited}

Chapter 9 introduced four subspaces attached to a matrix: the column
space and left null space in $\R^{m}$, the row space and null space
in $\R^{n}$. The SVD reads all four off the factorisation.

Let $r = \rank \mat{A}$. The first $r$ columns of $\mat{U}$ form an
orthonormal basis of $\col(\mat{A})$; the remaining $m - r$ columns
form an orthonormal basis of $\nul(\mat{A}\transpose)$ (the left
null space). The first $r$ columns of $\mat{V}$ form an orthonormal
basis of the row space $\col(\mat{A}\transpose)$; the remaining
$n - r$ columns form an orthonormal basis of $\nul(\mat{A})$. The
SVD, in one factorisation, gives orthonormal bases for all four
fundamental subspaces simultaneously, ordered by how strongly the
matrix uses or ignores each direction.

\subsection{Geometric picture}

The geometric reading of the SVD is that every linear map factors
into three operations: a rotation, an axis-aligned scaling, and a
second rotation. Specifically, $\mat{V}\transpose$ rotates the input
to align with the matrix's preferred directions; $\mat{\Sigma}$
scales each axis by the corresponding singular value; $\mat{U}$
rotates the result to align with the output basis. The unit ball in
$\R^{n}$ maps to an ellipsoid in $\R^{m}$ whose semi-axes are
$\sigma_{1} \vect{u}_{1}, \ldots, \sigma_{r} \vect{u}_{r}$. The
ellipsoid degenerates in $n - r$ directions (those mapped to zero)
and lives in the $r$-dimensional subspace $\col(\mat{A})$.

The picture is exact for every matrix. A symmetric positive-definite
$\mat{A}$ has $\mat{U} = \mat{V}$ and $\mat{\Sigma} = \mat{\Lambda}$;
the rotation before equals the rotation after; the matrix is its own
spectral decomposition. A general matrix needs two distinct
rotations, and the two are different exactly when the action of the
matrix is not symmetric. The reader should hold this picture in mind
through the chapter; every later derivation follows it.

\input{volumes/02-form/ch10-eigenvalues-and-decompositions/figures/fig-svd-geometry.tex}

\Cref{fig:vol02:ch10:svd-geometry} sets out the three stages
explicitly. The unit ball arrives, gets rotated to align with the
matrix's preferred input directions, gets stretched along each axis
by its singular value, and gets rotated again to align with the
output basis. The output is an ellipsoid whose semi-axis lengths are
exactly the singular values and whose semi-axis directions are
exactly the left singular vectors. For a rectangular matrix the
diagram is the same with the ambient dimension changing; for a
rank-deficient matrix the ellipsoid degenerates in some directions
(those mapped to zero); for a near-singular matrix one semi-axis is
short and the condition number is its aspect ratio.

\subsection{Computation and conditioning}

The cost of computing the SVD of an $m \times n$ matrix with $m \geq
n$ is $\oom{mn^{2} + n^{3}}$ flops. For a square matrix this is
$\oom{n^{3}}$, comparable to LU and twice QR. The standard algorithm
(Golub-Reinsch, 1970) reduces $\mat{A}$ to bidiagonal form by
Householder reflections and then iterates a variant of the QR
algorithm on the bidiagonal until the off-diagonal entries vanish
\cite[ch.~31]{text:trefethen-bau1997}. The algorithm is backward
stable. The computed singular values are exact for a nearby matrix
$\mat{A} + \mat{E}$ with $\norm{\mat{E}}_{2}/\norm{\mat{A}}_{2}$ on
the order of machine precision.

The condition number of a matrix in the 2-norm, introduced in
Chapter 9, is the ratio $\kappa(\mat{A}) = \sigma_{1}/\sigma_{r}$
of largest to smallest nonzero singular value. The SVD makes the
condition number visible. A matrix with $\sigma_{1} = 10$ and
$\sigma_{r} = 10^{-10}$ has $\kappa = 10^{11}$ and stretches some
inputs by $10$ while shrinking others to $10^{-10}$ of their length.
The conditioning trouble of Chapter 9 is the same trouble read
off the spectrum directly.

\section{Spectral methods and low-rank approximation}\pathtag{core}

The SVD orders the singular values from largest to smallest. Often
the largest few carry most of the action and the rest are noise or
redundancy. Truncating to the largest $k$ produces the best rank-$k$
approximation to the matrix, in a sense the Eckart-Young theorem
makes precise. This is the formal home of dimensionality reduction.

\subsection{The Eckart-Young theorem}

\begin{theorem}[Eckart-Young]
Let $\mat{A} = \mat{U} \mat{\Sigma} \mat{V}\transpose$ be the SVD of
$\mat{A} \in \R^{m \times n}$. For any integer $k$ with $1 \leq k \leq
\rank \mat{A}$, the matrix

\[
\mat{A}_{k} = \sum_{i=1}^{k} \sigma_{i} \vect{u}_{i} \vect{v}_{i}\transpose
\]

is the rank-$k$ matrix closest to $\mat{A}$ in both the spectral
norm and the Frobenius norm:

\[
\min_{\rank \mat{B} \leq k} \norm{\mat{A} - \mat{B}}_{2} =
\norm{\mat{A} - \mat{A}_{k}}_{2} = \sigma_{k+1},
\]

\[
\min_{\rank \mat{B} \leq k} \norm{\mat{A} - \mat{B}}_{F}^{2} =
\norm{\mat{A} - \mat{A}_{k}}_{F}^{2} = \sum_{i=k+1}^{p} \sigma_{i}^{2}.
\]
\end{theorem}

The theorem says that the truncated SVD is optimal as a low-rank
approximation under either of the two engineering-relevant matrix
norms. The proof is a Courant-Fischer argument
\cite[ch.~5]{text:trefethen-bau1997}. The approximation error is
the next singular value (in spectral norm) or the tail energy (in
Frobenius norm), exactly. There is no constant or asymptotic in the
statement; it is sharp.

The truncated SVD is the engineering reading of \emph{the matrix is
mostly the first $k$ singular components}. When the singular values
decay quickly, $\mat{A}_{k}$ is close to $\mat{A}$ at small $k$ and
the matrix is effectively low-rank. When they decay slowly,
truncation costs accuracy and full rank is the genuine signal.

\subsection{PCA from the SVD}

\engterm{Principal Component Analysis} is the SVD applied to a
mean-centred data matrix. Let $\mat{X} \in \R^{m \times n}$ have
$m$ samples in rows and $n$ features in columns. Centre by
subtracting the column mean: $\mat{X}_{c} = \mat{X} - \vect{1}
\bar{\vect{x}}\transpose$, where $\bar{\vect{x}}$ is the column-wise
mean. The sample covariance matrix is $\mat{C} = \mat{X}_{c}\transpose
\mat{X}_{c}/(m-1)$, an $n \times n$ symmetric positive-semidefinite
matrix.

The eigenvectors of $\mat{C}$ are the \engterm{principal directions}.
The eigenvalues are the variances captured along each principal
direction. The standard recipe computes the SVD of $\mat{X}_{c} =
\mat{U} \mat{\Sigma} \mat{V}\transpose$ and reads:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item The columns of $\mat{V}$ are the principal directions in the
    feature space.
  \item The squared singular values $\sigma_{i}^{2}/(m-1)$ are the
    variances along the principal directions.
  \item The matrix $\mat{X}_{c} \mat{V} = \mat{U} \mat{\Sigma}$
    gives the principal-component scores: each row is a sample
    expressed in the principal-direction basis.
\end{itemize}

The reduction to $k$ components keeps the first $k$ columns of
$\mat{V}$ and produces $\mat{X}_{c} \mat{V}_{k}$, an $m \times k$
matrix that is the best $k$-dimensional linear summary of the data
under the Eckart-Young criterion. The fraction of variance retained
is $\sum_{i=1}^{k} \sigma_{i}^{2} / \sum_{i=1}^{p} \sigma_{i}^{2}$,
the \engterm{explained-variance ratio}.

The numerical caution from Chapter 9 carries over. Forming $\mat{C} =
\mat{X}_{c}\transpose \mat{X}_{c}$ explicitly squares the conditioning
of $\mat{X}_{c}$. The SVD of $\mat{X}_{c}$ produces the same
principal directions without the squaring and is the reportable
algorithm. Section 10.8's worked PCA follows this discipline.

\subsection{Other spectral applications}

The truncated SVD recurs throughout engineering practice. In image
compression (section 10.8) the matrix is the pixel array and a
moderate $k$ recovers a recognisable image at a fraction of the
storage. In recommender systems the matrix is the
user-by-item ratings and the truncated SVD recovers latent factors.
In graph signal processing the matrix is a graph Laplacian and the
truncated eigenvectors form a Fourier basis adapted to the graph
structure. The common pattern is that data, when arranged as a
matrix, often have a few directions that carry most of the
variation. The SVD finds those directions and the truncation
projects onto them. The data-mining text by \textcite{text:v2ch10-elden2007}
covers the same pattern across information retrieval, image
processing, and microarray analysis with the bookkeeping in this
chapter's vocabulary.

\Cref{fig:vol02:ch10:singular-value-decay} compares four schematic

plots $\sigma_{k}/\sigma_{1}$ on a log-y axis against $k$, and reads
off where the curve crosses the application's acceptable error level.
A sharp drop at small $k$ followed by a plateau is the
low-rank-plus-noise pattern: the effective rank is well defined and
aggressive truncation is safe. A power-law decay (the natural-image
case, blue dashed) gives a gradual quality-storage trade with no
sharp threshold. A nearly flat curve (the random-Gaussian case,
amber) says no useful low-rank approximation exists and the matrix
has to be kept at full rank.

\begin{estimation}
\textbf{Question.} A grayscale photograph is $2000 \times 3000$
pixels. The reader wants a storage saving of at least $10\times$
through truncated-SVD compression. Estimate the rank $k$ at which
this storage saving is achieved, and judge whether the resulting
reconstruction is likely to be acceptable for the natural-image
class.

\smallskip
\textit{Estimate, before reading on.}

\smallskip
The original storage is $mn = 2000 \times 3000 = 6 \times 10^{6}$
entries. The truncated-SVD storage is $k(m + 1 + n) \approx 5000 k$
entries, since $m + n = 5000$. A tenfold saving demands
$5000 k \leq 6 \times 10^{5}$, or $k \leq 120$. At $k = 120$, the
ratio $\sigma_{120}/\sigma_{1}$ for a natural image with $\sigma_{k}
\propto 1/k$ is roughly $1/120 \approx 0.008$, below the $1\%$
threshold in
\cref{fig:vol02:ch10:singular-value-decay}. The reconstruction is
likely acceptable for casual viewing but will lose fine texture and
edges. Section 10.8's image-compression worked example confirms the
threshold experimentally on a specific image. The same back-of-the-
envelope works for any compression ratio $r$: solve $k(m+n) \leq
mn/r$ for $k$ and check the spectrum at that $k$.
\end{estimation}

\subsection{Latent semantic analysis (recognition)}

Latent semantic analysis (LSA) applies the truncated SVD to a
term-by-document matrix to recover topical structure. The columns
of $\mat{V}_{k}$ form a basis of \emph{topics}; the rows of
$\mat{U}_{k}$ map terms onto topics; the rows of $\mat{X}_{c}
\mat{V}_{k}$ map documents onto topics. The technique is from the
late 1980s and is foundational for the embedding methods of Volume
VII (information). We mention it at recognition level. The reader
who completes the project of this chapter has implemented PCA, which
is LSA on a different data matrix.

\section{Markov chains and stochastic matrices}\pathtag{standard}

A Markov chain is a stochastic process that evolves on a discrete
state space and whose transition probabilities depend only on the
current state. The mathematics is governed by a stochastic matrix
whose spectral properties decide the long-time behaviour. We treat
the discrete-time finite-state case, which is enough for the
PageRank example of section 10.6 and for Volume II Chapter 13's
fuller treatment.

\subsection{Stochastic matrices}

A square matrix $\mat{P} \in \R^{n \times n}$ is \engterm{stochastic}
when its entries are non-negative and each row sums to one:
$P_{ij} \geq 0$ and $\sum_{j} P_{ij} = 1$ for every $i$. The entry
$P_{ij}$ is the probability of transitioning from state $i$ to
state $j$ in one step. The state of the chain at time $t$ is a row
vector $\vect{\pi}_{t} \in \R^{n}$ with non-negative entries summing
to one; the update is $\vect{\pi}_{t+1} = \vect{\pi}_{t} \mat{P}$.

A stochastic matrix has $\lambda = 1$ as an eigenvalue, with the
all-ones column vector $\vect{1}$ as a right eigenvector
($\mat{P} \vect{1} = \vect{1}$). Every other eigenvalue satisfies
$\abs{\lambda} \leq 1$, by the Gerschgorin circle theorem applied
to the row-sum bound \cite[ch.~6]{text:strang2016}. The spectral
radius of a stochastic matrix is exactly $1$.

\subsection{Stationary distribution}

A \engterm{stationary distribution} is a row vector $\vect{\pi}$
with non-negative entries summing to one such that $\vect{\pi}
\mat{P} = \vect{\pi}$. Equivalently, $\vect{\pi}$ is a left
eigenvector of $\mat{P}$ at eigenvalue $1$, normalised to have
entries summing to one. Every finite-state Markov chain has at
least one stationary distribution, by the existence of a left
eigenvector at $\lambda = 1$.

When the chain is \engterm{irreducible} (every state is reachable
from every other state in some number of steps) and
\engterm{aperiodic} (the greatest common divisor of return times to
any state is one), the stationary distribution is unique and the
chain converges to it from every starting distribution. This is the
content of the Perron-Frobenius theorem applied to stochastic
matrices.

\begin{theorem}[Perron-Frobenius, recognition level]
Let $\mat{P}$ be an irreducible aperiodic stochastic matrix. Then
$\lambda = 1$ is a simple eigenvalue, every other eigenvalue
satisfies $\abs{\lambda} < 1$, and there is a unique stationary
distribution $\vect{\pi}^{*}$ with strictly positive entries. The
iterate $\vect{\pi}_{t} \to \vect{\pi}^{*}$ for every starting
distribution $\vect{\pi}_{0}$, with rate of convergence governed by
the second-largest eigenvalue magnitude.
\end{theorem}

We quote the theorem and use it. The full statement covers
non-negative matrices more generally and appears in
\cite[ch.~8]{text:strang2016}. The engineering reading is direct.
The stationary distribution is the long-run state occupancy. The
second-largest eigenvalue magnitude is the mixing rate: a chain
with $\abs{\lambda_{2}} = 0.99$ takes about $1/(1 - 0.99) = 100$
steps to forget initial conditions; a chain with $\abs{\lambda_{2}} =
0.5$ takes about two.

\subsection{Worked example: a two-state chain}

Consider a two-state weather chain with transition matrix

\[
\mat{P} = \begin{pmatrix} 0.7 & 0.3 \\ 0.4 & 0.6 \end{pmatrix},
\]

where state $1$ is sunny and state $2$ is rainy, and the entries
are conditional probabilities of tomorrow given today. The
characteristic polynomial is $\lambda^{2} - 1.3 \lambda + (0.7
\cdot 0.6 - 0.3 \cdot 0.4) = \lambda^{2} - 1.3 \lambda + 0.3$, with
roots $\lambda_{1} = 1$ and $\lambda_{2} = 0.3$.

The stationary distribution comes from the left eigenvector at
$\lambda_{1} = 1$. Solve $\vect{\pi} (\mat{P} - \mat{I}) = 0$:

\[
\vect{\pi} \begin{pmatrix} -0.3 & 0.3 \\ 0.4 & -0.4 \end{pmatrix}
= 0
\quad \Rightarrow \quad
\pi_{1} \cdot (-0.3) + \pi_{2} \cdot 0.4 = 0,
\]

so $\pi_{2}/\pi_{1} = 3/4$. Normalising $\pi_{1} + \pi_{2} = 1$
gives $\vect{\pi}^{*} = (4/7, 3/7) \approx (0.571, 0.429)$. The
chain spends $57.1\%$ of its long-run time sunny and $42.9\%$ rainy.
The second eigenvalue $\lambda_{2} = 0.3$ governs the mixing rate;
about $1/(1 - 0.3) \approx 1.4$ steps suffice to forget initial
conditions, which matches the intuition that the chain has short
memory.

\section{Power iteration and PageRank}\pathtag{standard}

The dominant eigenvector of a matrix can be computed by repeated
multiplication. Power iteration is the simplest such algorithm and
the conceptual backbone of PageRank, the original Google ranking
algorithm. Production eigensolvers do better, but power iteration
is what every engineer should understand first.

\subsection{Power iteration}

Let $\mat{A}$ be a diagonalisable matrix with eigenvalues $\abs{\lambda_{1}}
> \abs{\lambda_{2}} \geq \cdots \geq \abs{\lambda_{n}}$ and
corresponding eigenvectors $\vect{v}_{1}, \ldots, \vect{v}_{n}$. The
strict inequality at the top is the \engterm{spectral gap}, the
condition that makes power iteration work.

\begin{principle}[Power iteration]
Starting from any vector $\vect{x}_{0}$ with a nonzero component
along $\vect{v}_{1}$, define $\vect{x}_{t+1} = \mat{A} \vect{x}_{t} /
\norm{\mat{A} \vect{x}_{t}}_{2}$. Then $\vect{x}_{t}$ converges to
$\vect{v}_{1}$ (up to sign), and the Rayleigh quotient $\vect{x}_{t}
\transpose \mat{A} \vect{x}_{t}$ converges to $\lambda_{1}$. The
convergence rate is governed by the ratio $\abs{\lambda_{2}/\lambda_{1}}$,
with the error after $t$ steps decaying like $\abs{\lambda_{2}/
\lambda_{1}}^{t}$.
\end{principle}

The proof is one line of algebra in the eigenbasis. Write
$\vect{x}_{0} = \sum_{i} c_{i} \vect{v}_{i}$. Then $\mat{A}^{t}
\vect{x}_{0} = \sum_{i} c_{i} \lambda_{i}^{t} \vect{v}_{i} =
\lambda_{1}^{t} (c_{1} \vect{v}_{1} + \sum_{i \geq 2} c_{i}
(\lambda_{i}/\lambda_{1})^{t} \vect{v}_{i})$. Each ratio inside the
sum has magnitude less than one, so the sum tends to zero, and the
normalised iterate tends to $\vect{v}_{1}$.

The cost per iteration is one matrix-vector product, $\oom{n^{2}}$
flops for a dense matrix and much less for a sparse one. When the
spectral gap is healthy ($\abs{\lambda_{2}/\lambda_{1}} \leq 0.5$,
say), a few dozen iterations suffice. When the gap is small,
convergence drags and a more sophisticated method (Lanczos for
symmetric, Arnoldi for general) is preferred. Production code uses
power iteration only when the gap is known to be large or when the
matrix is too large for a full eigensolver to be feasible.

\subsection{The PageRank model}

PageRank, introduced by Page and Brin in 1998 and the spine of
Google's original ranking, is the stationary distribution of a
random walk on the web graph. The web is modelled as a graph with
$n$ pages as nodes and a directed edge $i \to j$ whenever page $i$
links to page $j$. The ``random surfer'' is at page $i$ at time $t$
and moves to one of the pages $i$ links to, chosen uniformly at
random, at time $t+1$.

The transition matrix $\mat{H}$ has entries $H_{ij} = 1/\deg(i)$ if
$i$ links to $j$, zero otherwise, where $\deg(i)$ is the
out-degree of page $i$. Two complications: pages with no outgoing
links (\engterm{dangling nodes}) make $\mat{H}$ not stochastic, and
the web graph is not irreducible (some pages are unreachable from
others). The PageRank model fixes both with a \engterm{teleportation}
parameter $\alpha \in (0, 1)$, typically $\alpha = 0.85$, and a
uniform distribution $\vect{u} = \vect{1}/n$. The corrected matrix
is

\[
\mat{G} = \alpha \mat{H}' + (1 - \alpha) \vect{1} \vect{u}\transpose,
\]

where $\mat{H}'$ replaces dangling rows with $\vect{u}\transpose$ to
make every row sum to one. The matrix $\mat{G}$ is stochastic,
irreducible, and aperiodic by construction. By Perron-Frobenius it
has a unique stationary distribution.

The stationary distribution is the \engterm{PageRank vector}. Each
entry $\pi_{j}$ is the long-run probability that a random surfer is
at page $j$, and pages are ranked by this probability. The original
algorithm computes the PageRank by power iteration on $\mat{G}$.
With $\alpha = 0.85$, the second eigenvalue is bounded by $\alpha$
in absolute value, so convergence is geometric at rate $0.85^{t}$
and about $50$ iterations bring $0.85^{50} \approx 3 \times 10^{-4}$,
adequate for ranking. The matrix $\mat{H}'$ is sparse (most pages
link to few others), so each iteration costs $\oom{m}$ flops, where
$m$ is the number of edges. PageRank, in the original 1998 deployment,
ranked the entire indexed web ($n \approx 10^{8}$ pages, $m
\approx 10^{9}$ edges) in a few hours per run on commodity hardware.

\subsection{The teleportation parameter as engineering choice}

The choice $\alpha = 0.85$ is empirical. Smaller $\alpha$ accelerates
convergence (the spectral gap widens) but makes the ranking depend
more on the uniform distribution and less on the link structure.
Larger $\alpha$ relies more on the links but converges more slowly.
The 0.85 figure was the value the founders settled on for the early
indexes and remains the default in the literature, current as of
2025, when the algorithm is studied as a baseline. Production search
engines have moved to deeper ranking models. PageRank survives as a
worked example of how a spectral algorithm with a stochastic matrix
solves a problem that would be intractable as a combinatorial
optimisation.

\section{Failure: when the matrix has no real eigenvectors and the engineer panics}\pathtag{core}

Two failure modes complicate eigenvalue analysis for real matrices.
The matrix may be defective, lacking enough eigenvectors to span
the space; or it may have complex eigenvalues, which a real
eigenvector cannot represent. Both are routine in engineering
practice. The discipline is to recognise the regime, choose the
appropriate apparatus, and refuse the temptation to interpret a
defective or complex spectrum as if it were a clean diagonal one.

\subsection{Defective matrices in parameter sweeps}

Defective matrices are not pathological. They are the boundary
between two regimes. Consider the parameter-dependent family

\[
\mat{A}(\epsilon) = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ \epsilon & 1
\end{pmatrix}.
\]

The eigenvalues are $\lambda_{\pm} = 1 \pm \sqrt{\epsilon}$. For
$\epsilon > 0$ they are real and distinct; for $\epsilon < 0$ they
are complex conjugates; for $\epsilon = 0$ they collide at
$\lambda = 1$ and the matrix becomes the Jordan block. The collision
point is where the spectrum changes character. The matrix is
defective there and only there, but a sweep that crosses
$\epsilon = 0$ encounters it generically.

The numerical signature is that the eigenvectors at small $\abs{\epsilon}$
become nearly parallel. The matrix $\mat{S}$ that diagonalises
$\mat{A}(\epsilon)$ has condition number that diverges as
$1/\sqrt{\abs{\epsilon}}$. Computing $\mat{A}^{k}$ via diagonalisation
near the collision amplifies floating-point error by $\kappa(\mat{S})$,
which is exactly when diagonalisation should not be the chosen
algorithm. Production code uses the \engterm{Schur decomposition}
$\mat{A} = \mat{Q} \mat{T} \mat{Q}\transpose$ with $\mat{Q}$
orthogonal and $\mat{T}$ upper triangular: it exists for every
square matrix, is numerically stable, and reads the eigenvalues
off the diagonal of $\mat{T}$.

\subsection{Complex eigenvalues for real matrices}

A real $2 \times 2$ matrix with complex eigenvalues represents a
rotation combined with a scaling. The matrix

\[
\mat{R} = \begin{pmatrix} \cos\theta & -\sin\theta \\ \sin\theta &
\cos\theta \end{pmatrix}
\]

has trace $2\cos\theta$ and determinant $1$; its characteristic
polynomial is $\lambda^{2} - 2\cos\theta \cdot \lambda + 1$, with
roots $\lambda = \cos\theta \pm i \sin\theta = e^{\pm i \theta}$.
For $\theta$ not a multiple of $\pi$, the roots are not real, and
the matrix has no real eigenvectors at all. The action is rotation
through angle $\theta$, which leaves no real direction fixed.

The engineering reading: the matrix is healthy. It just does not
admit a real diagonalisation. Two alternatives exist. Diagonalise
over $\C$ (the eigenvectors are complex, the eigenvalues come in
conjugate pairs, the change-of-basis matrix is complex). Or use the
real Schur form, which produces a block upper-triangular real
matrix with $1 \times 1$ blocks at real eigenvalues and $2 \times 2$
blocks at conjugate-pair eigenvalues. The $2 \times 2$ block carries
the rotation-and-scaling structure intact. Most production solvers
return the real Schur form by default for real input.

\subsection{The engineer's panic, and what to do instead}

A working diagnostic when an engineer encounters a square matrix
whose eigendecomposition appears wrong. First, verify whether
$\mat{A}$ is normal: compute $\mat{A}\transpose \mat{A} -
\mat{A}\mat{A}\transpose$. If non-zero, the matrix is non-normal and
its eigenvectors are not orthogonal; the change-of-basis matrix may
be poorly conditioned. Second, check the condition number of the
eigenvector matrix $\mat{S}$. If $\kappa(\mat{S}) > 10^{8}$, the
matrix is near-defective and diagonalisation amplifies numerical
error; switch to Schur. Third, check whether the eigenvalues are
real. If not, switch to the real Schur form or work over $\C$
explicitly. Fourth, when the application is a matrix function
$f(\mat{A})$ (powers, exponentials, square roots), use a function
algorithm built on Schur (Higham's algorithms in
\cite[ch.~28]{text:trefethen-bau1997}) rather than naively
diagonalising and applying $f$ to the eigenvalues.

\begin{principle}[Refuse naive diagonalisation when the spectrum is
non-trivial]
Diagonalisation is a special-case algorithm. It is the right tool
when the matrix is symmetric, normal, or has well-separated real
eigenvalues. It is the wrong tool when the matrix is defective, has
complex eigenvalues that the application does not handle, or has an
eigenvector matrix whose conditioning is worse than the original
problem. The Schur decomposition exists for every square matrix,
is numerically stable, and is the production default. The engineer
who reaches for diagonalisation should first check that
diagonalisation is what the matrix admits, and refuse the
algorithm when the spectrum says otherwise.
\end{principle}

\subsection{The Schur decomposition as production default}\pathtag{standard}

The Schur decomposition deserves a slower look because every
production eigensolver and matrix-function routine builds on it. A
real square matrix $\mat{A}$ admits $\mat{A} = \mat{Q} \mat{T}
\mat{Q}\transpose$ with $\mat{Q}$ real orthogonal and $\mat{T}$
quasi-upper-triangular: $1 \times 1$ blocks on the diagonal for real
eigenvalues, $2 \times 2$ blocks for complex conjugate pairs. The
eigenvalues read off the diagonal blocks directly. The factorisation
is unique up to ordering of the diagonal blocks and choice of basis
within each block; the LAPACK routine \texttt{?GEES} returns one
canonical choice and offers an interface for reordering the diagonal
\cite[ch.~7]{text:v2ch10-golub-vanloan2013}.

Three properties make Schur the algorithm of choice in regimes where
diagonalisation fails. First, it exists for every square matrix,
including defective ones: there is no Jordan-like degeneracy to
detect at runtime. Second, $\mat{Q}$ is orthogonal, so $\kappa_{2}
(\mat{Q}) = 1$ and the change-of-basis introduces no
error-amplification. Third, $\mat{T}$ shares the eigenvalues of
$\mat{A}$ exactly, so the eigenvalue computation reduces to reading
the diagonal blocks of a numerically reliable triangular matrix.

For matrix functions $f(\mat{A})$ the Schur form leads to the
Parlett recurrence: write $f(\mat{T})$ as an upper triangular matrix
with $f$ of the diagonal blocks on its diagonal and recursively
compute the strictly upper triangular entries from the
diagonal-block ratios. Higham's book on matrix functions is the
production reference \cite[ch.~9]{text:v2ch10-higham-functions2008};
the engineer should reach for \texttt{expm}, \texttt{logm},
\texttt{sqrtm}, and similar library routines rather than coding
$f(\mat{A})$ by hand even when the matrix appears benign, because
the underlying algorithms handle defective and near-defective cases
through Schur and produce useful answers where naive diagonalisation
would produce noise.

\subsection{Pseudospectra and operator non-normality}\pathtag{mastery}

A non-normal matrix has eigenvectors that are not orthogonal. The
eigenvalues then under-describe the matrix's transient behaviour.
The \engterm{pseudospectrum} of $\mat{A}$ at level $\epsilon$ is the
set of complex numbers $z$ such that $\norm{(\mat{A} - z\mat{I})\inv}
\geq 1/\epsilon$. For normal matrices the pseudospectrum reduces to
the union of $\epsilon$-discs around the eigenvalues. For non-normal
matrices the pseudospectrum can extend far from the spectrum,
explaining why a matrix with eigenvalues well inside the unit disc
can still produce large transient growth $\norm{\mat{A}^{k}
\vect{x}_{0}}$ before settling to its asymptotic decay. The
hydrodynamic-stability literature of the 1990s replaced eigenvalue
analysis with pseudospectral analysis for exactly this reason. We
mention the apparatus and refer the interested reader to
\textcite{text:trefethen-bau1997} for the foundational treatment.
The engineering import for this chapter: if a transient analysis
matters and the matrix is non-normal, the eigenvalues alone are not
the right diagnostic and a pseudospectral plot is the next tool.

\subsection{Real-valued failure: linear stability}

In dynamical systems, eigenvalues of the Jacobian at a fixed point
classify the local behaviour. Real positive eigenvalues mean
exponential growth; real negative ones mean exponential decay;
complex pairs with positive real part mean growing oscillation;
complex pairs with negative real part mean decaying oscillation;
complex pairs on the imaginary axis mean neutral oscillation that
the linearisation cannot resolve. Volume III Chapter 7 develops the
classification in full; the eigenvalue apparatus of this chapter is
what makes the classification computable. A control engineer who
designs a feedback law without checking the closed-loop eigenvalues
has not done the design.

\section{Worked examples: PCA, modal analysis, image compression}\pathtag{standard}

The chapter has built three apparatus: the eigendecomposition of
square matrices, the SVD of arbitrary matrices, and the spectral
reading of stochastic matrices. Three worked examples consolidate
them on engineering data.

\subsection{Worked PCA on a small dataset}

Consider four samples with two features each, arranged in the
data matrix

\[
\mat{X} = \begin{pmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 2 \\ 4 & 4 \\ 6 & 6
\end{pmatrix}.
\]

The column means are $\bar{\vect{x}} = (3, 3)$. Centring gives

\[
\mat{X}_{c} = \begin{pmatrix} -1 & -3 \\ -3 & -1 \\ 1 & 1 \\ 3 & 3
\end{pmatrix}.
\]

The covariance matrix is

\[
\mat{C} = \frac{1}{m-1} \mat{X}_{c}\transpose \mat{X}_{c}
= \frac{1}{3} \begin{pmatrix} 20 & 16 \\ 16 & 20 \end{pmatrix}
= \begin{pmatrix} 20/3 & 16/3 \\ 16/3 & 20/3 \end{pmatrix}.
\]

The eigenvalues of $\mat{C}$ solve $\lambda^{2} - (40/3)\lambda
+ (400/9 - 256/9) = 0$, giving $\lambda^{2} - (40/3)\lambda + 16 =
0$, with roots $\lambda_{1} = 12$ and $\lambda_{2} = 4/3$. The
explained-variance ratio is $12/(12 + 4/3) = 9/10 = 0.9$. The first
principal component captures $90\%$ of the variance.

The principal directions are the eigenvectors of $\mat{C}$. At
$\lambda_{1} = 12$, $(\mat{C} - 12 \mat{I})$ has rows $(-16/3, 16/3)$
and $(16/3, -16/3)$, both giving $v_{1} = v_{2}$. The unit
eigenvector is $\vect{v}_{1} = (1, 1)/\sqrt{2}$. At $\lambda_{2} =
4/3$, the eigenvector is $\vect{v}_{2} = (1, -1)/\sqrt{2}$. The
data, viewed in the $(\vect{v}_{1}, \vect{v}_{2})$ basis, has most
of its spread along $\vect{v}_{1}$ (the diagonal of feature space)
and very little along $\vect{v}_{2}$ (the anti-diagonal). PCA
recovers the structure the data already had: it lies near a line.

\input{volumes/02-form/ch10-eigenvalues-and-decompositions/figures/fig-pca-axes.tex}

\Cref{fig:vol02:ch10:pca-axes} draws the same idea on a slightly
larger synthetic scatter, with both principal axes overlaid through
the centroid. The longer axis points along the dominant principal
direction, with length proportional to $\sqrt{\sigma_{1}^{2}/(m-1)}$;
the shorter axis is the perpendicular direction. Projecting the
samples onto the longer axis retains most of the variance, and a
downstream classifier or regressor fed only that projection still
resolves the samples. The dataset under
\texttt{data/synth\_pca\_dataset.csv} provides a $20$-sample,
five-feature version with a planted rank-two structure; the project
in section 10.9 runs PCA on this dataset and on a dataset of the
reader's choice, by three independent routes.

\subsection{Modal analysis of a three-mass system}

Three equal masses $m$ are connected in a line by four equal springs
of stiffness $k$ to walls and to each other. The stiffness matrix
is

\[
\mat{K} = k \begin{pmatrix} 2 & -1 & 0 \\ -1 & 2 & -1 \\ 0 & -1 & 2
\end{pmatrix}.
\]

The matrix is symmetric and positive definite, so it has three
real positive eigenvalues and an orthonormal eigenbasis. The
characteristic polynomial of $\mat{K}/k$ is $-\lambda^{3} + 6
\lambda^{2} - 10 \lambda + 4$, with roots $\lambda_{1} = 2 -
\sqrt{2}$, $\lambda_{2} = 2$, $\lambda_{3} = 2 + \sqrt{2}$. The
natural frequencies are $\omega_{i} = \sqrt{\lambda_{i} k/m}$. Their
ratios are $\omega_{1} : \omega_{2} : \omega_{3} \approx 1 : 1.85
: 2.41$, with the slow mode close to the all-in-phase motion and
the fast mode close to the alternating motion of adjacent masses.

The eigenvectors are the \engterm{mode shapes}. They tell the
engineer which displacement pattern oscillates at which frequency.
The first mode is roughly $(1, \sqrt{2}, 1)/2$, with the centre
mass moving more than the outer ones in phase. The second mode is
$(1, 0, -1)/\sqrt{2}$, with the centre mass stationary. The third
mode is $(1, -\sqrt{2}, 1)/2$, with the centre mass opposite to the
outers. A general initial condition decomposes into a sum over
modes; each mode evolves independently at its own frequency. Modal
analysis is the universal apparatus of structural and acoustic
vibration analysis (Volume III Chapter 9), of electrical resonance
(Volume IV), and of any linear system whose response is the sum of
its natural responses.

\input{volumes/02-form/ch10-eigenvalues-and-decompositions/figures/fig-vibration-modes.tex}

\Cref{fig:vol02:ch10:vibration-modes} draws the three mode shapes
schematically. The numerical mode shapes and frequencies are recorded
in \texttt{data/three\_mass\_modal.csv} for the reader who wants to
plot a time-domain superposition from a chosen initial condition.

\subsection{Image compression by truncated SVD}

A grayscale image is a matrix of pixel intensities. A
$1024 \times 1024$ image is a $\R^{1024 \times 1024}$ matrix
with entries between $0$ and $255$. The full storage is about
$10^{6}$ bytes. Computing the SVD and truncating to rank $k$ stores
the image as $\mat{U}_{k} \in \R^{1024 \times k}$,
$\diag(\sigma_{1}, \ldots, \sigma_{k})$, and $\mat{V}_{k}\transpose
\in \R^{k \times 1024}$, for a total of about $2{,}049 k$ entries.
At $k = 50$, the storage is about $10^{5}$ entries, a tenfold
compression.

The visual quality at fixed compression ratio depends on how fast
the singular values of the image decay. Photographs of natural
scenes have spectra that decay quickly (the matrix has effective
rank in the few tens to few hundreds for plausible quality), so
truncation at $k = 50$ to $k = 100$ produces a recognisable image.
Detailed images, text, or images with high-frequency content decay
slowly and lose more at the same $k$. The Eckart-Young theorem
quantifies the trade-off exactly: the spectral-norm error is
$\sigma_{k+1}$, and the Frobenius-norm error is the tail energy.
The reader running the truncated-SVD compression sees the
approximation degrade smoothly as $k$ decreases, and recognises a
visual threshold around the rank where $\sigma_{k}$ drops to a few
percent of $\sigma_{1}$.

JPEG and modern image codecs do not use the SVD as their basis; they
use the discrete cosine transform, which is data-independent and
much cheaper at encode time. The truncated SVD is the
data-dependent reference: the optimal rank-$k$ approximation in the
matrix-norm sense, against which the cheaper transforms are judged.

\section{Project}\pathtag{core}

\begin{project}[Implement Principal Component Analysis from scratch]
The reader implements PCA without using any high-level library
shortcut (no \texttt{sklearn.decomposition.PCA},
\texttt{statsmodels.PCA}, or equivalent), runs it on a real
dataset, and produces a comparative analysis with a 1500-word
reflection.

\textbf{Track}. Simulation with analysis. \textbf{Hazard class}: none.
\textbf{Tools}. Paper, pencil, and a programming environment with
linear-algebra primitives (LU, QR, SVD, eigensolver): Python with
NumPy, MATLAB, Julia, Octave, R, or equivalent.

\textbf{Dataset choice}. The reader chooses a dataset of $m \geq 50$
samples and $n \geq 4$ features, of one of the following kinds: a
Volume I project dataset (e.g.\ a measurement campaign with
multiple correlated readings); a public dataset (UCI archive,
the Iris dataset, the Wine dataset, a country-level statistics
panel from the World Bank); or a synthesised dataset where the
reader has implanted known principal directions in known proportions
(this is a useful debugging dataset and is the recommended starting
point). The reader records the dataset, its source or generation
recipe, and the date.

\textbf{Three implementation routes}. The reader computes the PCA
three ways:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \engterm{Eigendecomposition of the covariance matrix}.
    Centre the data, form $\mat{C} = \mat{X}_{c}\transpose
    \mat{X}_{c}/(m-1)$, call an eigensolver on $\mat{C}$, sort
    eigenvalues in descending order, sort eigenvectors accordingly.
    Record the condition number of $\mat{X}_{c}$ and of $\mat{C}$.
  \item \engterm{Power iteration}. Centre the data; compute the
    dominant eigenvector of $\mat{C}$ by power iteration with at
    least 100 steps; deflate (subtract the rank-one component) and
    repeat for the next several principal components. Implement
    the deflation by hand. Record the spectral gap at each stage.
  \item \engterm{SVD of the centred data matrix}. Centre the data;
    call the SVD primitive on $\mat{X}_{c}$; the principal
    directions are the columns of $\mat{V}$ and the variances are
    $\sigma_{i}^{2}/(m-1)$. Record the condition number of
    $\mat{X}_{c}$.
\end{enumerate}

\textbf{Reporting}. The reader produces:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]

\end{itemize}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]

  \item How the explained-variance ratio guides the choice of $k$.

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textit{Setup} (about one hour). Choose the dataset; record

  \item \textit{Reporting} (one to two hours). Produce the tables

  \item \textit{Reflection} (three to four hours). Write the

\end{enumerate}

  \item The explained-variance ratios from the three routes agree
    to at least four decimal digits with the library reference, and

  \item The reflection answers each of the five prompted questions

\end{itemize}

\textbf{Common failure modes and recovery}. Four failure modes
recur. The reader who hits one should apply the listed fix.

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*]
  \item \textit{Sign-flip mismatches.} Eigenvectors are determined
    up to sign; library and project may differ by signs. The fix
    is to sign-align before comparing (the
    \texttt{code/pca\_three\_routes.py} helper does this).
  \item \textit{Centring forgotten.} PCA on uncentred data points
    the first principal direction at the centroid rather than along
    the data's spread; explained-variance ratios then exceed unity
    or behave nonsensically. The fix is to verify the centred data
    has zero column mean to machine precision before any further
    computation.
  \item \textit{Power iteration on a defective deflated matrix.}
    Repeated deflation on a non-symmetric matrix can leave a
    near-defective residue; power iteration then converges slowly
    or to a spurious vector. The fix is to use the SVD route for
    the production answer and treat power iteration as a teaching
    artefact.
  \item \textit{Reading explained variance from $\mat{C}$'s
    eigenvalues but reporting it as a percentage of the SVD route's
    sum.} The two sums agree to machine precision when the
    arithmetic is consistent; if they do not, a centring or scaling
    step has been applied unequally across routes. The fix is to
    centre once, in one place, and pass the same centred matrix to
    all three routes.
\end{itemize}
\end{project}

\subsection*{Calculation}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
Compute the eigenvalues and eigenvectors of $\mat{A} =
\begin{psmallmatrix} 3 & 1 \\ 1 & 3 \end{psmallmatrix}$. Verify
that the eigenvectors are orthogonal and that the trace and
determinant equal the sum and product of the eigenvalues.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Trace is $6$ and determinant is $9 - 1 = 8$, so the characteristic
polynomial is $\lambda^{2} - 6\lambda + 8 = (\lambda - 4)(\lambda -
2)$. Eigenvalues are $\lambda_{1} = 4$ and $\lambda_{2} = 2$. At
$\lambda_{1} = 4$, $\mat{A} - 4\mat{I} = \begin{psmallmatrix} -1 & 1
\\ 1 & -1 \end{psmallmatrix}$ gives $\vect{v}_{1} = (1, 1)/\sqrt{2}$;
at $\lambda_{2} = 2$, $\mat{A} - 2\mat{I} = \begin{psmallmatrix} 1 &
1 \\ 1 & 1 \end{psmallmatrix}$ gives $\vect{v}_{2} = (1, -1)/\sqrt{2}$.
Inner product $\vect{v}_{1}\transpose \vect{v}_{2} = 0$ confirms
orthogonality, as expected for a symmetric matrix. Trace check:
$4 + 2 = 6 = \tr \mat{A}$. Determinant check: $4 \times 2 = 8 = \det
\mat{A}$.

\begin{exercise}
Compute the diagonalisation $\mat{A} = \mat{S} \mat{\Lambda}
\mat{S}\inv$ of $\mat{A} =
\begin{psmallmatrix} 5 & 4 \\ 1 & 2 \end{psmallmatrix}$. Verify by
multiplying out $\mat{S} \mat{\Lambda} \mat{S}\inv$.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Trace is $7$, determinant is $10 - 4 = 6$. Characteristic polynomial
$\lambda^{2} - 7\lambda + 6 = (\lambda - 6)(\lambda - 1)$ gives
$\lambda_{1} = 6$ and $\lambda_{2} = 1$. At $\lambda_{1} = 6$,
$\mat{A} - 6\mat{I} = \begin{psmallmatrix} -1 & 4 \\ 1 & -4
\end{psmallmatrix}$ gives $\vect{v}_{1} = (4, 1)\transpose$ (any
nonzero multiple). At $\lambda_{2} = 1$, $\mat{A} - \mat{I} =
\begin{psmallmatrix} 4 & 4 \\ 1 & 1 \end{psmallmatrix}$ gives
$\vect{v}_{2} = (1, -1)\transpose$. Take $\mat{S} = \begin{psmallmatrix}
4 & 1 \\ 1 & -1 \end{psmallmatrix}$ and $\mat{\Lambda} = \diag(6, 1)$.
Then $\det \mat{S} = -5$ and $\mat{S}\inv = -\tfrac{1}{5}
\begin{psmallmatrix} -1 & -1 \\ -1 & 4 \end{psmallmatrix} =
\tfrac{1}{5}\begin{psmallmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 1 & -4 \end{psmallmatrix}$.
Multiplying out $\mat{S} \mat{\Lambda} \mat{S}\inv$ recovers
$\begin{psmallmatrix} 5 & 4 \\ 1 & 2 \end{psmallmatrix}$, confirming
the diagonalisation.

\begin{exercise}
For the symmetric matrix $\mat{A} =
\begin{psmallmatrix} 2 & 1 & 0 \\ 1 & 2 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 & 2
\end{psmallmatrix}$, compute the three eigenvalues and verify that
the eigenvectors are mutually orthogonal. State which mode of a
three-mass linear chain each eigenvector corresponds to.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
This is the stiffness matrix of section 10.8 with $k = 1$. The
eigenvalues are $\lambda_{1} = 2 - \sqrt{2}$, $\lambda_{2} = 2$, and
$\lambda_{3} = 2 + \sqrt{2}$, found by computing the characteristic
polynomial $-\lambda^{3} + 6\lambda^{2} - 10\lambda + 4$ and
factoring (try $\lambda = 2$ first; the residue is $\lambda^{2} -
4\lambda + 2$, with roots $2 \pm \sqrt{2}$). The unit eigenvectors
are $\vect{v}_{1} = (1, \sqrt{2}, 1)/2$ (in-phase, slow mode),
$\vect{v}_{2} = (1, 0, -1)/\sqrt{2}$ (centre still, medium mode),
and $\vect{v}_{3} = (1, -\sqrt{2}, 1)/2$ (alternating, fast mode).
Pairwise inner products are all zero, confirming orthogonality.
Match the eigenvectors to the modes of \cref{fig:vol02:ch10:vibration-modes}.

\begin{exercise}
For the stochastic matrix $\mat{P} = \begin{psmallmatrix} 0.5 & 0.5
\\ 0.2 & 0.8 \end{psmallmatrix}$, compute the stationary distribution
$\vect{\pi}^{*}$ and the second eigenvalue $\lambda_{2}$. State the
mixing time, defined as $1/(1 - \abs{\lambda_{2}})$.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Trace is $1.3$, determinant is $0.4 - 0.1 = 0.3$. Eigenvalues solve
$\lambda^{2} - 1.3\lambda + 0.3 = 0$: $\lambda_{1} = 1$ (stochastic
matrix) and $\lambda_{2} = 0.3$. For the stationary distribution,
solve $\vect{\pi}(\mat{P} - \mat{I}) = 0$ with
$\mat{P} - \mat{I} = \begin{psmallmatrix} -0.5 & 0.5 \\ 0.2 & -0.2
\end{psmallmatrix}$: the first column gives $-0.5\pi_{1} + 0.2\pi_{2}
= 0$, so $\pi_{2}/\pi_{1} = 5/2$. Normalising $\pi_{1} + \pi_{2} = 1$
yields $\vect{\pi}^{*} = (2/7, 5/7) \approx (0.286, 0.714)$. Mixing
time is $1/(1 - 0.3) \approx 1.43$ steps; the chain forgets initial
conditions almost immediately.

\begin{exercise}
Compute the rank-$1$ approximation of $\mat{A} =
\begin{psmallmatrix} 2 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0 \end{psmallmatrix}$ by
truncating its SVD. Verify that the spectral-norm error equals
$\sigma_{2}$ as predicted by Eckart-Young.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
The matrix is already in diagonal form (up to the third zero row).
Its SVD has $\sigma_{1} = 2$ with $\vect{u}_{1} = (1, 0, 0)\transpose$
and $\vect{v}_{1} = (1, 0)\transpose$; $\sigma_{2} = 1$ with
$\vect{u}_{2} = (0, 1, 0)\transpose$ and $\vect{v}_{2} = (0, 1)
\transpose$. The rank-$1$ truncation is $\mat{A}_{1} = \sigma_{1}
\vect{u}_{1} \vect{v}_{1}\transpose = \begin{psmallmatrix} 2 & 0 \\
0 & 0 \\ 0 & 0 \end{psmallmatrix}$. The error matrix is
$\mat{A} - \mat{A}_{1} = \begin{psmallmatrix} 0 & 0 \\ 0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0
\end{psmallmatrix}$, whose largest singular value is $1 = \sigma_{2}$.
This matches Eckart-Young: $\norm{\mat{A} - \mat{A}_{1}}_{2} =
\sigma_{k+1} = \sigma_{2} = 1$.

\begin{exercise}
For the four data points $(0, 0), (1, 2), (2, 4), (3, 6)$, compute
the centred data matrix, the covariance matrix, the principal
directions, and the explained-variance ratios. State the rank of
the centred data matrix and the geometric meaning of the result.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Column means are $\bar{\vect{x}} = (1.5, 3)$. The centred data is
$\mat{X}_{c} = \begin{psmallmatrix} -1.5 & -3 \\ -0.5 & -1 \\ 0.5 &
1 \\ 1.5 & 3 \end{psmallmatrix}$. The covariance matrix is
$\mat{C} = \mat{X}_{c}\transpose \mat{X}_{c}/3 = \begin{psmallmatrix}
5/3 & 10/3 \\ 10/3 & 20/3 \end{psmallmatrix}$. Trace is $25/3$,
determinant is $100/9 - 100/9 = 0$. Eigenvalues are $\lambda_{1} =
25/3$ and $\lambda_{2} = 0$. Principal directions: $\vect{v}_{1} =
(1, 2)/\sqrt{5}$ (along the data line) and $\vect{v}_{2} = (2, -1)/
\sqrt{5}$ (perpendicular). Explained-variance ratios are $1.0$ and
$0$. The centred data matrix has rank $1$: all four centred points
lie exactly on the line through the origin with direction $(1, 2)$.
The geometric meaning is exact one-dimensional structure; PCA
recovers it with zero loss.

\subsection*{Derivation}\pathtag{standard}

\begin{exercise}
Show that for any square matrix $\mat{A}$, $\tr \mat{A} = \sum_{i}
\lambda_{i}$ and $\det \mat{A} = \prod_{i} \lambda_{i}$, where the
$\lambda_{i}$ are the eigenvalues counted with algebraic multiplicity.
Use the characteristic polynomial.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
The characteristic polynomial expands as $p(\lambda) = \det(\mat{A} -
\lambda \mat{I}) = (-1)^{n} \prod_{i=1}^{n}(\lambda - \lambda_{i})$.
Expand the product: the coefficient of $\lambda^{n-1}$ on the right
is $(-1)^{n} \cdot (-1) \sum_{i} \lambda_{i} = (-1)^{n+1}
\sum_{i}\lambda_{i}$. On the left, expanding $\det(\mat{A} -
\lambda\mat{I})$ along the diagonal contributes
$(-1)^{n} \lambda^{n} + (-1)^{n-1}(\sum_{i} A_{ii})\lambda^{n-1} +
\cdots$, so the coefficient of $\lambda^{n-1}$ is
$(-1)^{n-1} \tr \mat{A}$. Equating: $\tr \mat{A} = \sum_{i}
\lambda_{i}$. For the determinant, set $\lambda = 0$ on both sides:
$p(0) = \det \mat{A}$ on the left and $(-1)^{n} \prod_{i}(-\lambda_{i})
= \prod_{i} \lambda_{i}$ on the right.

\begin{exercise}
Show that eigenvectors of a symmetric matrix corresponding to
distinct eigenvalues are orthogonal. State the role of the
symmetry assumption in the proof.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Let $\mat{A} = \mat{A}\transpose$, with $\mat{A} \vect{v}_{1} =
\lambda_{1} \vect{v}_{1}$ and $\mat{A} \vect{v}_{2} = \lambda_{2}
\vect{v}_{2}$, $\lambda_{1} \neq \lambda_{2}$. Then
$\lambda_{1} \vect{v}_{2}\transpose \vect{v}_{1} = \vect{v}_{2}
\transpose \mat{A} \vect{v}_{1} = (\mat{A}\transpose \vect{v}_{2})
\transpose \vect{v}_{1} = (\mat{A} \vect{v}_{2})\transpose
\vect{v}_{1} = \lambda_{2} \vect{v}_{2}\transpose \vect{v}_{1}$.
So $(\lambda_{1} - \lambda_{2}) \vect{v}_{2}\transpose \vect{v}_{1}
= 0$, which forces $\vect{v}_{2}\transpose \vect{v}_{1} = 0$. The
symmetry assumption $\mat{A}\transpose = \mat{A}$ is essential at
the step where we move $\mat{A}$ across the inner product; without
symmetry, $\mat{A}\transpose \vect{v}_{2}$ would not equal $\mat{A}
\vect{v}_{2}$ and the cancellation fails.

\begin{exercise}
Derive the Eckart-Young theorem in the Frobenius norm: starting from
the SVD $\mat{A} = \mat{U} \mat{\Sigma} \mat{V}\transpose$, show
that the rank-$k$ truncation $\mat{A}_{k}$ minimises $\norm{\mat{A}
- \mat{B}}_{F}^{2}$ over rank-at-most-$k$ matrices $\mat{B}$, and
that the optimal value is $\sum_{i=k+1}^{p} \sigma_{i}^{2}$.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
The Frobenius norm is unitarily invariant: $\norm{\mat{U}\mat{M}
\mat{V}\transpose}_{F} = \norm{\mat{M}}_{F}$ for any orthogonal
$\mat{U}, \mat{V}$. Substitute: $\norm{\mat{A} - \mat{B}}_{F}^{2} =
\norm{\mat{\Sigma} - \mat{U}\transpose \mat{B} \mat{V}}_{F}^{2} =
\norm{\mat{\Sigma} - \mat{C}}_{F}^{2}$, with $\mat{C} = \mat{U}
\transpose \mat{B} \mat{V}$ of rank at most $k$. Now $\norm{\mat{\Sigma}
- \mat{C}}_{F}^{2} = \sum_{i,j}(\Sigma_{ij} - C_{ij})^{2}$. Since
$\mat{\Sigma}$ is diagonal, $\sum_{i \neq j} C_{ij}^{2} +
\sum_{i}(\sigma_{i} - C_{ii})^{2}$. To minimise, set all
off-diagonals of $\mat{C}$ to zero. The problem reduces to choosing
a vector $(c_{1}, \ldots, c_{p})$ with at most $k$ nonzero entries
minimising $\sum_{i}(\sigma_{i} - c_{i})^{2}$. The optimum is
$c_{i} = \sigma_{i}$ for the $k$ largest $\sigma_{i}$ (i.e.\
$i = 1, \ldots, k$) and $c_{i} = 0$ otherwise. The optimal value is
$\sum_{i=k+1}^{p} \sigma_{i}^{2}$, achieved by $\mat{C} = \diag(\sigma_{1},
\ldots, \sigma_{k}, 0, \ldots, 0)$, equivalently
$\mat{B} = \mat{A}_{k}$.

\begin{exercise}
Show that a stochastic matrix $\mat{P}$ has $\lambda = 1$ as an
eigenvalue, with the all-ones vector as a right eigenvector. Using
the Gerschgorin circle theorem, show that every eigenvalue
satisfies $\abs{\lambda} \leq 1$.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
For the right eigenvector: $(\mat{P} \vect{1})_{i} = \sum_{j} P_{ij}
\cdot 1 = \sum_{j} P_{ij} = 1 = 1 \cdot \vect{1}_{i}$, where the
penultimate equality uses the row-sum-one property of stochastic
matrices. So $\mat{P} \vect{1} = \vect{1}$ and $\lambda = 1$ is an
eigenvalue. For the bound: by Gerschgorin, every eigenvalue lies in
the union of discs centred at $P_{ii}$ with radius $\sum_{j \neq i}
\abs{P_{ij}} = 1 - P_{ii}$ (using non-negativity and the row sum).
Each disc is contained in the closed unit disc: the centre $P_{ii}$
is real and in $[0, 1]$, and the radius is $1 - P_{ii}$, so the
farthest point in the disc from the origin is $P_{ii} + (1 - P_{ii})
= 1$. Hence $\abs{\lambda} \leq 1$ for every eigenvalue.

\begin{exercise}
Show that the SVD of a real matrix $\mat{A}$ is related to the
eigendecomposition of $\mat{A}\transpose \mat{A}$ by:
the singular values are the square roots of the eigenvalues of
$\mat{A}\transpose \mat{A}$, and the right singular vectors are the
corresponding eigenvectors. State why this relation does not, by
itself, give a numerically stable SVD algorithm.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
From $\mat{A} = \mat{U} \mat{\Sigma} \mat{V}\transpose$ compute
$\mat{A}\transpose \mat{A} = \mat{V} \mat{\Sigma}\transpose
\mat{U}\transpose \mat{U} \mat{\Sigma} \mat{V}\transpose = \mat{V}
(\mat{\Sigma}\transpose \mat{\Sigma}) \mat{V}\transpose$. The matrix
$\mat{\Sigma}\transpose \mat{\Sigma}$ is $n \times n$ diagonal with
entries $\sigma_{i}^{2}$. This is the eigendecomposition of
$\mat{A}\transpose \mat{A}$ with eigenvalues $\sigma_{i}^{2}$ and
eigenvectors the columns of $\mat{V}$. The numerical concern: forming
$\mat{A}\transpose \mat{A}$ squares the condition number,
$\kappa(\mat{A}\transpose \mat{A}) = \kappa(\mat{A})^{2}$. A matrix
with $\kappa(\mat{A}) = 10^{6}$ becomes $\kappa(\mat{A}\transpose
\mat{A}) = 10^{12}$, well into the regime where double-precision
arithmetic loses meaningful digits. Production SVD (Golub-Reinsch,
1970, \cite{paper:v2ch10-golub-reinsch1970}) works on $\mat{A}$
directly by bidiagonal reduction and avoids the squaring.

\begin{exercise}
Show that for a diagonalisable $\mat{A} = \mat{S} \mat{\Lambda}
\mat{S}\inv$, the matrix exponential is $e^{\mat{A} t} = \mat{S}
e^{\mat{\Lambda} t} \mat{S}\inv$, where $e^{\mat{\Lambda} t}$ is
the diagonal matrix with entries $e^{\lambda_{i} t}$. Verify by
substitution into the series definition $e^{\mat{A} t} = \sum_{k}
(\mat{A} t)^{k}/k!$.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Compute $\mat{A}^{k} = (\mat{S} \mat{\Lambda} \mat{S}\inv)^{k} =
\mat{S} \mat{\Lambda} \mat{S}\inv \cdot \mat{S} \mat{\Lambda}
\mat{S}\inv \cdots \mat{S} \mat{\Lambda} \mat{S}\inv = \mat{S}
\mat{\Lambda}^{k} \mat{S}\inv$ (the inner $\mat{S}\inv \mat{S}$
factors cancel). Substitute into the series: $e^{\mat{A} t} =
\sum_{k=0}^{\infty} (\mat{A} t)^{k}/k! = \sum_{k} \mat{S} (\mat{\Lambda}
t)^{k} \mat{S}\inv / k! = \mat{S} \left(\sum_{k} (\mat{\Lambda} t)^{k}
/ k!\right) \mat{S}\inv = \mat{S} e^{\mat{\Lambda} t} \mat{S}\inv$.
The last sum is diagonal because $\mat{\Lambda}$ is diagonal, with
$(e^{\mat{\Lambda} t})_{ii} = \sum_{k} (\lambda_{i} t)^{k}/k! =
e^{\lambda_{i} t}$.

\subsection*{Estimation}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the number of power-iteration steps required to compute
the dominant eigenvalue of a matrix with eigenvalue ratio
$\abs{\lambda_{2}/\lambda_{1}} = 0.9$, to three decimal digits of
accuracy. State how the estimate changes if the ratio is $0.5$ or
$0.99$.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Power iteration's error after $t$ steps is approximately
$r^{t}$ with $r = \abs{\lambda_{2}/\lambda_{1}}$. Three decimal
digits of accuracy means $r^{t} \leq 10^{-3}$, so $t \geq -3 \log
10 / \log r$. For $r = 0.9$: $t \geq 3 \cdot 2.303 / 0.105 \approx
66$ steps. For $r = 0.5$: $t \geq 3 \cdot 2.303 / 0.693 \approx 10$
steps. For $r = 0.99$: $t \geq 3 \cdot 2.303 / 0.01005 \approx 687$
steps. The penalty for a closing spectral gap is brutal: a tenfold
increase in $1 - r$ buys a sevenfold reduction in iterations. Below
$r = 0.99$, the engineer should use a more sophisticated method
(Lanczos for symmetric, Arnoldi for general).

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the rank at which a $1024 \times 1024$ grayscale photograph
is visually indistinguishable from the original after truncated-SVD
reconstruction, given that natural-image singular values typically
decay roughly as $\sigma_{k} \propto k^{-1}$ over the first few
hundred. State the storage ratio achieved at this rank.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Visual indistinguishability typically requires
$\sigma_{k}/\sigma_{1} \leq 1\%$, i.e.\ $k \geq 100$ for a $1/k$
decay. Be conservative and take $k = 200$ to leave headroom for the
slower tail. The truncated storage is $k(m + 1 + n) \approx 200
\cdot 2049 = 4.1 \times 10^{5}$ entries; the full storage is
$1024^{2} \approx 1.05 \times 10^{6}$. The storage ratio is
$4.1/10.5 \approx 0.39$, a $2.6 \times$ reduction. The compression
is modest because the Eckart-Young bound is sharp and natural
images decay slowly. For images of text or with sharp edges the
decay is even slower and the effective compression drops further.
For low-rank-plus-noise data (a few-component PCA-like signal),
$k \approx 10$ may give a $50 \times$ compression.
\Cref{fig:vol02:ch10:singular-value-decay} makes the data-class
dependence visible.

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the second-largest eigenvalue magnitude of the PageRank
matrix at teleportation parameter $\alpha = 0.85$, given the
spectral-gap bound $\abs{\lambda_{2}} \leq \alpha$. State the number
of power-iteration steps required for $10^{-6}$ accuracy and
compare to the number for $\alpha = 0.99$.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
The spectral-gap bound gives $\abs{\lambda_{2}} \leq 0.85$. Power
iteration converges at rate $\abs{\lambda_{2}/\lambda_{1}} \leq 0.85
/1 = 0.85$. For $10^{-6}$ accuracy: $0.85^{t} \leq 10^{-6}$ gives
$t \geq 6 \log 10 / (-\log 0.85) \approx 13.82/0.1625 \approx 85$
steps. For $\alpha = 0.99$: $0.99^{t} \leq 10^{-6}$ gives $t \geq
13.82/0.01005 \approx 1376$ steps, a $16 \times$ increase. The
default $\alpha = 0.85$ is calibrated to give the rate-versus-
faithfulness compromise that web-scale ranking can afford. A larger
$\alpha$ would rank more faithfully to the link structure at large
cost in compute.

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the natural frequencies of a five-mass equal-spring
linear chain (six equal springs of stiffness $k$, five equal masses
$m$, walls at both ends). State the highest and lowest frequencies
in units of $\sqrt{k/m}$, by reading them off the limiting cases
(all in phase, alternating sign), without computing the full
spectrum.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
The stiffness matrix is the $5 \times 5$ analogue of the three-mass
case, with $2$ on the diagonal and $-1$ on the sub- and
super-diagonals (times $k$). The eigenvalues are known in closed
form: $\lambda_{j} = 2(1 - \cos(j\pi/6))$ for $j = 1, \ldots, 5$.
The lowest is $\lambda_{1} = 2(1 - \cos 30^{\circ}) = 2(1 -
\sqrt{3}/2) \approx 0.268$, giving $\omega_{1} \approx 0.518
\sqrt{k/m}$. The highest is $\lambda_{5} = 2(1 - \cos 150^{\circ}) =
2(1 + \sqrt{3}/2) \approx 3.732$, giving $\omega_{5} \approx 1.932
\sqrt{k/m}$. The ratio $\omega_{5}/\omega_{1} \approx 3.7$ is the
chain's frequency spread, much wider than the three-mass system's
$\sqrt{3+\sqrt{2}}/\sqrt{2-\sqrt{2}} \approx 2.4$. As the chain
grows, $\omega_{1} \to 0$ (lowest mode wavelength approaches the
full chain length) and $\omega_{\max} \to 2\sqrt{k/m}$ (alternating
neighbours, the discrete-lattice cutoff).

\begin{exercise}
Estimate the proportion of the variance captured by the first
principal component of a dataset whose feature correlation
coefficient is $0.9$ across all features. State the proportion as
a function of the number of features $n$ and verify the limiting
behaviour as $n \to \infty$.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
A correlation matrix $\mat{R}$ with $1$ on the diagonal and $\rho$
everywhere off-diagonal has eigenvalues $\lambda_{1} = 1 + (n-1)\rho$
(eigenvector all-ones) and $\lambda_{2} = \cdots = \lambda_{n} =
1 - \rho$ (eigenvectors any orthogonal complement of the all-ones
direction). The trace is $n$, so the explained-variance ratio of
the first PC is $\lambda_{1}/n = (1 + (n-1)\rho)/n$. With $\rho =
0.9$ and $n = 10$: $(1 + 9 \cdot 0.9)/10 = 0.91$. With $n = 100$:
$(1 + 99 \cdot 0.9)/100 = 0.901$. As $n \to \infty$: $(1 + (n-1)
\rho)/n \to \rho = 0.9$. The proportion converges to the
correlation coefficient itself, a clean limit that justifies the
practitioner's rule that highly correlated features compress almost
entirely into one PC.

\subsection*{Simulation}\pathtag{mastery}

\begin{exercise}
Implement power iteration on a $50 \times 50$ random symmetric
matrix. Plot the convergence of the Rayleigh quotient to the
dominant eigenvalue on a log-y axis. Verify the geometric
convergence rate $\abs{\lambda_{2}/\lambda_{1}}^{t}$ predicted by
section 10.6.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
The reference implementation \texttt{code/power\_iteration.py}
covers this exercise directly. Draw a random symmetric matrix with
Gaussian entries; compute the reference eigenvalues with a library
eigensolver; track the Rayleigh quotient $\vect{x}_{t}\transpose
\mat{A} \vect{x}_{t}$ across iterations and the error
$\abs{\lambda_{1}^{(t)} - \lambda_{1}^{\text{true}}}$. Plot the
error against $t$ on a log-y axis. The error decays as a straight
line with slope $\log_{10}\abs{\lambda_{2}/\lambda_{1}}$, matching
the principle in section 10.6. For a $50 \times 50$ Gaussian-symmetric
matrix the ratio is typically near $0.9$, so a few hundred iterations
buy three to four digits.

\begin{exercise}
Implement deflation: after computing the dominant eigenpair
$(\lambda_{1}, \vect{v}_{1})$ by power iteration, subtract
$\lambda_{1} \vect{v}_{1} \vect{v}_{1}\transpose$ from $\mat{A}$
(for symmetric $\mat{A}$) and run power iteration on the deflated
matrix to find the next eigenpair. Verify on a $20 \times 20$
symmetric random matrix and compare to a library eigensolver.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
The \texttt{--deflate} flag in \texttt{code/power\_iteration.py}
runs the deflation. After computing $\lambda_{1}, \vect{v}_{1}$,
form $\mat{A}_{1} = \mat{A} - \lambda_{1} \vect{v}_{1}
\vect{v}_{1}\transpose$. By construction, $\mat{A}_{1}$ has the
same eigenvectors as $\mat{A}$ but with eigenvalue $0$ at
$\vect{v}_{1}$ and unchanged eigenvalues elsewhere. Power iteration
on $\mat{A}_{1}$ converges to the eigenvector at $\lambda_{2}$
(now the dominant). Verify by comparing to the library
eigendecomposition. Caveat: a small residual component of
$\vect{v}_{1}$ in the deflated iterate can re-grow if $\lambda_{1}$
is large; use re-orthogonalisation against $\vect{v}_{1}$ at each
step to stabilise.

\begin{exercise}
Implement the truncated-SVD compression of a grayscale image of
your choice (any $\geq 256 \times 256$ photograph). Display the
reconstruction at $k = 10, 50, 100, 200$ and report the
spectral-norm error at each rank against the Eckart-Young
prediction $\sigma_{k+1}$.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
The reference implementation \texttt{code/svd\_image\_compression.py}
runs this exercise on any grayscale or colour image, reporting both
the spectral-norm and Frobenius-norm errors at each rank against
the Eckart-Young predictions. At $k = 10$ the reconstruction is
blurry but recognisable for portraits; at $k = 50$ it is acceptable
for casual viewing; at $k = 100$ to $200$ it is visually
indistinguishable from the original for most natural images. The
spectral-norm error matches $\sigma_{k+1}$ to all reported digits
(this is an identity, not an approximation, when the SVD is computed
to working precision). Report the storage ratio at each rank; the
break-even (where truncated storage equals original storage) is
near $k \approx mn/(m+n+1)$.

\begin{exercise}
Simulate a Markov chain on a 5-state cycle with one ``leaky''
transition (state $i$ goes to state $i+1$ with probability $0.9$,
to state $1$ with probability $0.1$). Compute the stationary
distribution by both eigendecomposition and direct power iteration
on the row vector. Verify they agree to four decimal digits and
report the mixing time.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
The reference implementation \texttt{code/markov\_stationary.py}
runs this exercise directly. The chain is irreducible (the leak to
state $1$ guarantees return) and aperiodic (state $1$ has a self-
return probability of $0.1 \cdot 0$ at one step but the leak
introduces variable return paths whose gcd is $1$). Both methods
return the same stationary distribution to machine precision; the
second-largest eigenvalue magnitude $\abs{\lambda_{2}}$ governs
the mixing time, approximately $1/(1 - \abs{\lambda_{2}})$. For the
specified chain the leak is $0.1$, so the mixing time is on the
order of $10$ steps. Reducing the leak to $0.01$ would push the
mixing time toward $100$ and bring power iteration close to the
useful-iteration boundary.

\subsection*{Design}\pathtag{standard}

\begin{exercise}
Design a procedure for choosing the number of principal components
$k$ in a PCA pipeline, given a dataset and a downstream task. The
procedure should consider the explained-variance ratio, the
scree-plot elbow, and the downstream task's sensitivity to
truncation noise. State the criteria used and justify each.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Discussion.}
Three criteria, applied jointly. First, the explained-variance
threshold: pick $k$ such that the cumulative explained-variance
ratio reaches a chosen level (typical engineering defaults are
$90\%$ for visualisation, $95\%$ for storage, $99\%$ for downstream
predictive modelling). Second, the scree-plot elbow: plot
$\sigma_{i}^{2}$ versus $i$ and pick $k$ at the visual elbow where
the curve transitions from steep decay to slow tail. Third, the
downstream task's sensitivity: run the downstream task (classifier,
regressor, controller) at several candidate $k$ values and pick the
smallest $k$ that does not degrade the task metric beyond a chosen
tolerance. Justify each criterion: the first is a global energy
argument; the second is a structural argument about the data's
intrinsic rank; the third is an empirical guarantee that the
truncation does not lose downstream information. In tension, the
third dominates: the goal is the task, not the variance ratio.

\begin{exercise}
Design a power-iteration variant for the case where the dominant
eigenvalue is negative or complex. State the modification (shift
to make all eigenvalues positive, or work with $\mat{A}\transpose
\mat{A}$ instead of $\mat{A}$) and the trade-offs in convergence
rate and computational cost.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Discussion.}
Two principled variants. (a) Shifted iteration: pick $\sigma$
larger than the spectral radius of $-\mat{A}$ and iterate
$(\mat{A} + \sigma \mat{I})$. All eigenvalues of the shifted matrix
are positive and the dominant one is now reliably the largest in
magnitude. The cost is one extra addition per iteration; the
convergence rate is $\abs{(\lambda_{2} + \sigma)/(\lambda_{1} +
\sigma)}$, which approaches $1$ as $\sigma$ grows large, so $\sigma$
should be the smallest value that makes all eigenvalues positive.
(b) Iterate on $\mat{A}\transpose \mat{A}$: the eigenvalues are
squared and all positive, the eigenvectors are the right singular
vectors of $\mat{A}$. The cost is one extra matrix-vector multiply
per iteration (two instead of one) and squaring of the spectral
gap; the convergence rate is $(\lambda_{2}/\lambda_{1})^{2}$, which
is faster than the original whenever $\abs{\lambda_{2}/\lambda_{1}}
< 1$. For complex dominant eigenvalues the second variant is
preferred because it recovers a real iterate; the first variant
still leaves a complex dominant pair.

\begin{exercise}
Design a numerical pipeline for computing the matrix exponential
$e^{\mat{A} t}$ for a $1000 \times 1000$ non-symmetric $\mat{A}$
that may be near-defective. State which of the following you would
use and why: scaling-and-squaring with Pad\'e approximation,
diagonalisation, Schur-based function evaluation, or Krylov
subspace projection.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Discussion.}
Use scaling-and-squaring with Pad\'e approximation. The reasoning:
diagonalisation amplifies error by $\kappa(\mat{S})$ where
$\mat{S}$ is the eigenvector matrix, and a near-defective $\mat{A}$
gives $\kappa(\mat{S})$ much larger than $\kappa(\mat{A})$. Schur-
based function evaluation (Parlett recurrence) works for general
matrices but is more expensive at $\oom{n^{3}}$ per matrix-function
call and has its own conditioning issues at close eigenvalues.
Krylov subspace projection is the right tool for $\mat{A}$ very
large and sparse with the action $e^{\mat{A} t} \vect{b}$ on a
single vector $\vect{b}$ needed; at $n = 1000$ the dense
scaling-and-squaring is competitive. Scaling-and-squaring with Pad\'e
approximation is the Higham-recommended default
\cite[ch.~10]{text:v2ch10-higham-functions2008}; it is what
\texttt{expm} in NumPy and MATLAB call by default, with conditioning
robust to near-defective matrices.

\subsection*{Diagnosis and reverse engineering}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
A colleague reports that a $20 \times 20$ matrix has condition
number $10^{12}$ but that its eigenvalue decomposition produced a
clean eigenvalue spectrum with no obvious near-degeneracy. Identify
where the trouble is hiding and propose two diagnostics that would
locate it.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Condition number measures singular-value ratio, not
eigenvalue-ratio. The matrix can have well-separated eigenvalues
but a poorly conditioned eigenvector matrix $\mat{S}$: the trouble
is non-normality, $\mat{A}\transpose \mat{A} \neq \mat{A}\mat{A}
\transpose$. Two diagnostics: (i) compute $\norm{\mat{A}\transpose
\mat{A} - \mat{A} \mat{A}\transpose}_{F}/\norm{\mat{A}}_{F}^{2}$;
if the ratio exceeds machine precision, the matrix is non-normal
and the eigenvalues under-describe its behaviour. (ii) compute the
condition number of $\mat{S}$ itself,
$\kappa(\mat{S})$; if it is large (say $\geq 10^{6}$), the
eigenvectors are nearly parallel and the diagonalisation
$\mat{A} = \mat{S} \mat{\Lambda} \mat{S}\inv$ amplifies error by
$\kappa(\mat{S})$. Use the Schur decomposition for downstream
computations instead.

\begin{exercise}
A PageRank computation on a web graph of $10^{6}$ nodes appears to
diverge: the iterate norm grows by a small factor each step.
Diagnose the most likely cause (dangling nodes, disconnected
components, off-by-one in the teleportation correction, or
floating-point overflow in a large entry) and propose the test
that distinguishes them.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Most likely cause: dangling-node correction not applied. Without
the correction $\mat{H}$ has rows summing to less than one, so
$\mat{H}$ is sub-stochastic and the iterate loses mass each step.
Diagnostic: compute the row sums of the transition matrix being
iterated; if any row sums to anything other than $1$ (to machine
precision), the dangling correction is missing or the
teleportation has an off-by-one. Floating-point overflow in a large
entry is unlikely at the scale described because the iterate is
normalised at each step in any sane implementation. Disconnected
components would not cause divergence but would produce multiple
stationary distributions or block-diagonal structure; check by
running a strongly-connected-components algorithm on the graph and
verifying it returns a single component (with the teleportation
correction applied).

\begin{exercise}
A modal-analysis software returns two natural frequencies of a
mechanical structure within $0.01\%$ of each other, although the
structure is asymmetric. Diagnose whether this represents a
genuine near-degenerate mode pair, a numerical artefact, or a
modelling error in the input file. Propose three checks to
distinguish.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Three checks. (i) Inspect the mode shapes. A genuine near-
degenerate pair has two distinct mode shapes that are orthogonal;
a numerical artefact produces two near-parallel shapes (the solver
has split a single mode in two). Compute
$\vect{v}_{i}\transpose \vect{v}_{j}/(\norm{\vect{v}_{i}}
\norm{\vect{v}_{j}})$; if it is near zero the modes are genuine. (ii)
Perturb the stiffness matrix by a small symmetry-breaking term and
re-solve; the two frequencies should move continuously. If they
collapse to one mode the original split was a numerical artefact.
(iii) Inspect the input file for accidental symmetries (mirror
geometry, equal material properties in both directions) that the
``asymmetric'' label did not capture; modelling error often hides
in repeated material constants. The combination of mode-shape
orthogonality and perturbation continuity is the conclusive test.

\begin{exercise}
A PCA on a dataset reports the first principal component captures
$99.9\%$ of variance, but the second through fifth principal
directions appear to be permutations of the same vector with sign
flips. Identify the most likely data-quality problem and propose
the diagnostic check.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Most likely cause: one feature has variance many orders of
magnitude larger than the others, dominating the covariance matrix
and producing one strong PC with the remaining components being
numerical noise. (Equivalently: PCA was applied to unscaled
heterogeneous-unit features, e.g.\ mixing kilograms and millimetres
in one matrix.) Diagnostic: compute the per-feature variance
$\Var(\mat{X}_{c}[:, j])$; if one variance dominates the others by
$\geq 10^{4}\times$, the explained-variance ratio is a unit-
sensitivity artefact rather than a structural finding. The standard
remedy is to standardise: divide each feature by its standard
deviation before centring, so the covariance becomes the correlation
matrix and the PCs reflect linear structure rather than unit choice.

\subsection*{Failure analysis}\pathtag{core}

\begin{exercise}
Argue, in 200 to 300 words, why naive diagonalisation is the wrong
tool for computing the matrix exponential of a near-defective
matrix, even when a library eigensolver returns ostensibly clean
eigenvalues. Identify the specific error-amplification mechanism
and the production-grade alternative.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
A near-defective matrix has nearly parallel eigenvectors. The
eigenvector matrix $\mat{S}$ then has $\kappa_{2}(\mat{S}) \gg 1$,
even though the eigenvalues themselves are well separated and
look clean. The matrix exponential by diagonalisation evaluates
$e^{\mat{A} t} = \mat{S} e^{\mat{\Lambda} t} \mat{S}\inv$. The
forward-error analysis of this computation has a multiplicative
factor of $\kappa(\mat{S})$ on the floating-point error of each
component. A matrix with $\kappa(\mat{S}) = 10^{10}$ amplifies
single-precision noise ($\sim 10^{-7}$) to magnitude $10^{3}$,
swamping the true exponential. The eigenvalues' cleanness gives no
warning; the error lives in the change-of-basis, not in the
spectrum. The production-grade alternative is scaling-and-squaring
with Pad\'e approximation \cite[ch.~10]{text:v2ch10-higham-functions2008},
which works on $\mat{A}$ directly via the identity $e^{\mat{A}} =
(e^{\mat{A}/2^{s}})^{2^{s}}$ and the Pad\'e approximation to
$e^{\mat{B}}$ for $\mat{B}$ with small norm. The algorithm has
backward stability comparable to working precision regardless of the
defectiveness of $\mat{A}$. It is what \texttt{scipy.linalg.expm}
and \texttt{expm} in MATLAB call by default. The engineer should
treat the existence of $\mat{S}\inv$ as a precondition to be
checked, not assumed.

\begin{exercise}
A control system's closed-loop transfer matrix has eigenvalues that
the engineer computes as $-0.01 \pm 0.5 i$, classifying the system
as stable. The system, when deployed, exhibits growing oscillation
and fails. Argue, in 200 to 300 words, what numerical or modelling
failure could have produced a stable-looking spectrum from an
unstable system, and propose three checks the engineer should have
performed before deployment.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
Three plausible causes. (a) Non-normality and large transient
growth: even with eigenvalues in the left half-plane, a strongly
non-normal closed-loop matrix can produce transient growth of
$\norm{e^{\mat{A} t} \vect{x}_{0}}$ by orders of magnitude before
decay sets in. If the plant's saturation or actuator limits are
exceeded during the transient, the linear analysis no longer
predicts behaviour and a nonlinear instability ensues. (b) Modelling
mismatch: the closed-loop transfer matrix used for eigenvalue
analysis was the nominal model, but the deployed plant differs
(parameter drift, sensor latency, unmodelled mechanical mode). The
true eigenvalues are then different and may sit in the right half-
plane. (c) Discretisation error: a continuous-time stability
analysis was applied to a system that is digitally controlled at a
sampling rate where the discrete-time eigenvalues sit outside the
unit disc even when the continuous eigenvalues are in the left half-
plane.

Three pre-deployment checks. First, compute the pseudospectrum of
the closed-loop matrix and verify the $\epsilon$-pseudospectrum for
realistic $\epsilon$ stays in the left half-plane. Second, run a
robustness analysis: perturb the plant parameters by their stated
tolerances and recompute the spectrum; report worst-case eigenvalue
locations. Third, simulate the closed loop in discrete time at the
intended sampling rate and verify the discrete-time eigenvalues sit
inside the unit disc with adequate margin.

\begin{exercise}
A finite-element analysis of a three-dimensional truss uses
diagonalisation of the stiffness matrix to compute modal responses.
The analysis returns six natural frequencies at exactly zero
(within machine precision). Argue, in 200 to 300 words, what these
zeros mean physically, why they are not a numerical bug, and how
the engineer should treat them in the modal superposition.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Solution sketch.}
A free-floating three-dimensional rigid body has six rigid-body
modes: three translations and three rotations. The stiffness matrix
in the global coordinates is singular along these modes because
rigid-body motion produces zero strain and thus zero restoring
force. Six zero eigenvalues are exactly the right answer; the
truss is not yet attached to anything.

The zeros are not a numerical bug because the algorithm has
returned the exact mathematical answer: $\det \mat{K} = 0$, with
nullity six (rigid translations and rotations form a
six-dimensional null space). A floating-point computation will
return them as values on the order of $\norm{\mat{K}}_{2} \cdot
\epsilon_{\text{machine}}$, which is the algorithm's correct
expression of ``zero.''

In modal superposition the rigid-body modes carry the truss as a
whole through space and contribute no oscillation. The engineer
should treat them in one of two ways. (a) Apply boundary conditions
that pin enough degrees of freedom to remove the rigid-body modes
(typically three constrained translations plus three rotations).
$\mat{K}$ becomes positive definite and the modal analysis returns
only oscillatory modes. (b) Retain the rigid-body modes and project
the loading onto the elastic modes only, computing the truss's
elastic response in the centre-of-mass frame; the rigid-body
response is then computed separately by Newton-Euler dynamics. Both
are standard; the first is more common in structural codes, the
second in multibody dynamics.

\subsection*{Judgment}\pathtag{standard}

\begin{exercise}
A vendor reports a PCA-based dimensionality-reduction pipeline that
retains $99\%$ of variance with $k = 5$ components on a dataset
with $n = 100$ features. Argue, in one paragraph, whether the
report is meaningful as stated and what the engineer should ask
the vendor before adopting the pipeline for downstream design.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Discussion.}
The claim is not meaningful as stated. Variance retention depends
on the units of the features. A dataset with one
high-variance feature in pascals and 99 low-variance features in
millimetres will produce a one-PC pipeline that captures $99\%$ of
variance trivially, with no genuine compression of the underlying
structure. The engineer should ask: (i) Were the features
standardised (zero mean, unit variance) before PCA? If not, the
$99\%$ figure is a unit artefact. (ii) What is the explained-variance
ratio at $k = 1, 2, 3, 4$ separately, not just the cumulative at
$k = 5$? A sharp drop after $k = 1$ means one dominant feature;
a smooth decay means $k = 5$ is doing real compression. (iii) Was the
PCA fit on the training set only and applied to test data, or were
both pooled? Information leakage inflates apparent retention. (iv) Does
the downstream task degrade if $k$ is reduced further, and by how
much? The right metric is task degradation, not variance retention.

\begin{exercise}
A research paper reports that a large language model's
embedding matrix has effective rank around $300$, based on the
truncated-SVD energy threshold at $99\%$. Argue, in 200 to 300
words, what claims this rank estimate supports, what claims it does
not support, and what additional evidence would be needed to make
the claim load-bearing for downstream design.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Discussion.}
What the claim supports. The embedding matrix is approximately
captured to $99\%$ Frobenius energy by a rank-$300$ truncated SVD;
storing the rank-$300$ approximation costs about $300 (m + n)$
floats instead of $mn$; downstream operations whose accuracy
depends on Frobenius-norm proximity (some retrieval applications,
some unsupervised clustering schemes) can use the rank-$300$
approximation with bounded loss.

What the claim does not support. The embedding matrix is not
literally rank $300$; the tail $1\%$ of energy may carry essential
information for tasks that depend on small-magnitude directions.
Tasks sensitive to spectral norm (some optimisation routines, some
robustness analyses) are not bounded by the Frobenius-norm energy
threshold and may degrade much more than $1\%$. The figure does not
say anything about the embedding's expressiveness, semantic
content, or capacity for downstream fine-tuning.

Additional evidence needed. (i) Task-degradation curves on a panel
of downstream evaluations, plotting accuracy as a function of $k$.
The relevant $k$ is the smallest at which the panel does not
degrade beyond a chosen tolerance, not the one with $99\%$ energy.
(ii) Spectral-norm error $\sigma_{k+1}$ alongside Frobenius energy,
to detect spectral-norm-sensitive failures. (iii) Per-class or
per-token error analysis on the truncated embedding; rare tokens
often live in low-energy directions and are first to be lost. The
energy-threshold heuristic is a starting point, not a deployment
criterion.

\begin{exercise}
An engineer is offered two implementations of an eigensolver: a
QR-algorithm-based dense solver, and a Lanczos-based sparse solver.
The application is a $10^{5} \times 10^{5}$ symmetric matrix
with $10^{6}$ nonzeros. Argue, in 200 to 300 words, which
implementation the engineer should choose, and state the matrix
size and density at which the choice would flip.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Discussion.}
Choose Lanczos. The matrix has $10^{6}$ nonzeros out of $10^{10}$
possible entries, a density of $10^{-4}$: it is sparse. The dense
QR algorithm costs $\oom{n^{3}} = 10^{15}$ flops and $\oom{n^{2}} =
10^{10}$ floats of storage to materialise the dense matrix, which
alone exceeds typical RAM. Lanczos uses only matrix-vector products
on the sparse structure, costing $\oom{\text{nnz}}$ per iteration
and converging in a small multiple of the desired number of
eigenvalues. For computing the lowest few or highest few
eigenvalues (the usual interest), Lanczos finishes in seconds; the
dense QR cannot start.

The choice flips when the matrix becomes dense enough that storing
it densely is comparable to its sparse representation, and when
asking for many eigenvalues (most or all). The crossover density is
roughly $\rho \sim 1/n$: at $n = 10^{5}$ this is $10^{-5}$, so
densities above $10^{-5}$ favour the dense solver if all
eigenvalues are needed. The crossover in matrix size is around
$n \sim 10^{3}$ for dense matrices on commodity hardware: a $10^{3}
\times 10^{3}$ dense eigendecomposition takes seconds and dense
solvers are simpler; below that, the algorithm choice is dominated
by implementation overhead and the dense path is usually preferred.
For very large sparse matrices ($n \geq 10^{5}$) Lanczos or one of
its restarted variants is the only viable option.

\begin{exercise}
A reader of this chapter argues that, since the SVD covers every
matrix and the Eckart-Young theorem makes truncation optimal in two
norms, the SVD should be the default tool for every linear-algebra
problem. Argue, in one paragraph, why this is wrong, identifying at
least two situations where a different decomposition (LU, QR, Schur,
Cholesky) is the right default and stating why the SVD is not.
\end{exercise}

\paragraph{Discussion.}
SVD is universal but expensive: $\oom{n^{3}}$ flops with a constant
several times that of competing decompositions. For solving linear
systems $\mat{A} \vect{x} = \vect{b}$ on a square invertible matrix,
LU factorisation with partial pivoting is the default: it costs
$\oom{n^{3}}/3$ flops (less than half of SVD), is backward stable
in practice, and produces a sparse factorisation when $\mat{A}$ is
sparse with good ordering. For overdetermined least squares with
$\mat{A}$ tall and full column rank, QR factorisation is the
default: $\oom{mn^{2}}$ flops, no squaring of conditioning, direct
production of $\mat{R}$ from which $\vect{x}$ is recovered by back
substitution. For symmetric positive-definite systems, Cholesky is
the default: half the work of LU, half the storage, and a clear
failure signal (a non-positive pivot detects a non-PD matrix
mid-factorisation). For matrix functions on non-symmetric matrices,
Schur is the default. SVD is the right tool when rank, conditioning,
optimal low-rank approximation, or the four fundamental subspaces
matter; for plain solves it is overkill.
