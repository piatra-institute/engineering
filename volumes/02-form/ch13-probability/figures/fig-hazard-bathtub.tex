\caption[Hazard-rate shapes and the bathtub curve]{The hazard rate
$h(t)=f(t)/S(t)$ is the instantaneous failure rate of a unit that has
survived to age $t$, and its shape selects the model. Left: the Weibull
hazard $h(t)=k\,t^{k-1}$ falls for shape $k<1$ (infant mortality), is
constant for $k=1$ (the memoryless exponential, the only law with flat
hazard), and rises for $k>1$ (wear-out). Right: the reliability bathtub
curve is three hazard regimes stitched together, a falling early phase in
which weak units are culled, a flat useful-life floor over which the
exponential model is valid, and a rising wear-out phase. The exponential
is the right model only in the flat middle; a constant-hazard assumption
applied across the early or late regime misreads the failure rate in the
direction the curve bends. Source: computed by the authors from the
Weibull hazard formula.}
\label{fig:vol02:ch13:hazard-bathtub}
